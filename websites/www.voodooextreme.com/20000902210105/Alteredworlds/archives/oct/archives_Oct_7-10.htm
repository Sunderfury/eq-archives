More System Shock
Looks like Ken Levine is really letting it all hang out with this System Shock info. No arguements coming from me. - Sacrates

In a game like Shock 2, story is a critical concern. I've spent a good deal of time developing the storyline for the game, with the goals that it is (1) entertaining, (2) consistent and coherent with the first title and (3) an enhancement to the player's gaming experience.

Storyline is a fine line to walk in a computer game. I think the story in Shock 1 worked so well because it rarely _forced_ the player to sit there and watch it. The player picked up most of the story from interactive logs, and they could continue playing while they listened to these bits of narrative. The story didn't block the gameplay. I'm personally not a fan of games that force you along a story path at the expense of gameplay. Compare a Sierra style adventure to Shock... Sierra games are strictly linear... you follow a singular path, pre-specified by the designer from start to finish. Shock has certain pre-specced points you have to hit (the laser, etc.) but HOW you achieve this and WHAT you do in between is what counts. The story embellishes the gameplay, it does not dictate it.

But, it is still quite important. I'm currently preparing an internal document for the development team that details the events between Shock 1 and 2. Many of these details won't actually be present in the game, but they will help inform the team of the game world they are working in.

My background is in "normal" literature, screenplays, books, etc. (I was a dramatic writing major back in the day), so we've looked at many sci-fi and non-sci fi references in the development of the Shock storyline. The thing to remember, however, is we are an RPG and not a FMV or Sierra game. Therefore, the player has a lot of say in how the character develops. Hence, we're not so much as proscribing a charater as letting the game subscribe to the character the player develops. It's a challenging task...

No BG DVD
Damn! Looks like they won't be making a Baldur's Gate for DVD now it seems. Come on guys, we're gonna have to convert sooner or later so let's be pioneers and try something new. I am personally tired of seeing grainy postage stamp sized video using about 10 cd's! Anyway, here is the smack straight from Captain Jack - Sacrates

I apologize in advance that I am sending this out over the bulletin board and not on the web site home page, but our webmasters have left for the day and I promised some folks an answer on this question before the end of Friday.

We are no longer considering a DVD for the launch of Baldur's Gate.

Basically there were a lot of risks / costs involved that were simply not justified given the minimal benefit our DVD version would offer fans. I apologize for any confusion, but some times these decisions cannot be made until very far into the development timeline. I feel we need to bring this decision to a close once and for all simply because we owe it to all of you to let you know what is happening on Baldur's Gate NOW, and not when the product finally starts to show up in stores.

Though I am posting this note and served as the catalyst for this decision, it is the combined decision of many different people. To be totally open and honest about the reasoning behind the decision, I have posted below the email that I sent out earlier today to bring this all to a close:

After talking with as many people as possible, I agree that we need to cancel consideration of a Baldur's Gate DVD. Rationale:

1) There would be no value-added DVD features in the product. It would simply be the 5 Baldur's Gate CD's dumped on a DVD. The only value it would offer consumers would be the convenience of not having to swap CD's.

2) The installed base of DVD machines is still low. If lucky, we would sell X units (removed for confidentiality reasons) of DVD product. Because of low quantities, our variable product costs would be high, especially if numerous production runs were required due to uncertainty around the numbers.

3) Low retail excitement. Retailers are not planning to get fully behind DVD software titles until mid/late 1999. Most stores do not have DVD software sections. There are issues/costs associated with stocking, shipping and tracking two separate SKU's.

4) Potential risk to ship dates. A DVD version would only make sense if released in conjunction with the CD version (else DVD owners would just buy the CD). An additional SKU would complication and possibly delay the release of the title.

Given minimal consumer benefit and high potential risks, I recommend we cancel any consideration of a DVD title for initial release.

If no one has any issues, I will remove the DVD from the web site and post a short explanation of our rationale...

In many ways this was a very tough decision to make. Though it will in no way impact the quality of Baldur's Gate, I know many of you own DVD drives and were hoping to use them with Baldur's Gate (I've been reading all of your posts). I hope you all understand where we are coming from on this. If you have any questions or comments, you can post them here or send me an email...

On Monday we will post this news on the home page and remove the DVD reference.

Holy BG Info
Wow...there's a TON of new info on the Developer's Corner BG message board, and as always, the ever vigilante guys at Baldur's Gate Chronicles have organized it into easily digestible chunks. All this info is from Scott Grieg, Lead Programmer at Bioware. - Bowmeister

First, a shwack of info on your options when you finally get BG and go to install that bad boy.

There has been no official word because we don't know yet. Since we are still waiting for some sounds and music, I can't give you an exact install size. It should be somewhere between 250 and 300 MB for a minimum and 2.5 to 3 gigs for a maximun. When we get the rest of the resources in the game, there might be some extra space on all of the CDs. If that is true, we can reduce the min install by placing more redundant information on each CD. It does however look like a fairly tight fit since we have taken a lot of care to minimize the amount of CD swapping. As far as install options there are min, recommended and custom.

The recommended install is only about a 100 megs more than the min and includes everything but background graphics and movies.

The custom install lets you install as many of the remaining parts that you have the hard drive space for. The components are organized by CD so you install entire CDs to minimize swapping. I would recommend that you install the entire CD5. This contains, although just barely, the actual city of Baldur's Gate.

There are also some additional install configurations that are available but not directly supported by the install package. That is, you will have to make some manual changes to the ini file, but these will be documented in the readme.txt file.

If you don't have the drive space on one partition, you can copy the entire CD to another drive and have the game pretend that it is that particular CD.

If you have multiple CD drives, you can tell the game that you want CDs 1,3,5 on one drive and 2,4 on another.

The same thing goes if you have a network setup, although we are finding our CD drives, faster than our network connections.

Second is a post called "Avoiding the Daggerfall Syndrome", or more accurately, how to stop players from running from store to store shoplifting their way to riches.

There is some random chance involved you will not ever be able to steal from stores that are way to hard for your current skill level, no matter how many times that you reload. Conversely, if your skill is much higher than the store difficulty. But don't worry, any store that has really good items, also has a very high difficulty.

If you are caught and guards are summoned, there are several things you can do to avoid being summarily executed on the spot.

First you can try hiding in shadows until the guards get bored and go away. This can be really nerve racking when they are searching all of the rooms for you.

Second, you can try to fight them. This really isn't an option early in the game, but as you get better, and have more party members, this becomes an option.

Thirdly, you can try to charm them. Ahh, the good old "Charm Person" spell. Although you had better run before it wears off and they become really pissed off at you.

And lastly, you can try and bribe them. Depending on your party repution and charisma value, you can bribe the guards. Just remember that some guards won't be bribeable and if you bribe too much, your reputation will suffer. And, of course, if your reputation gets too bad, guards and bounty hunters will start looking for you.

As far as the "barrel-trick", you can't move the barrels, and some of the guards will be spell casters.

Thirdly is a short blurb that can put you to rest about all the walkthroughs popping up all over the net.

Any walk through of the first chapter will be mostly moot by the time the game comes out. James and the rest of the design team have been sacrificing many hours of sleep to pack more subplots and fun (if you consider being waylaid by nasties fun), into all of the areas. The change over the last month has been really quite amazing. They have really brought the world to life.

So just remember when you're poisoned, sleep-deprived, half-dead party members get ambushed just yards from safety, it's JAMES you want to send the flame mail to, not me ;)

And finally, a short blurb on the multiple language releases of Baldur's Gate that will follow the initial release.

No promises, but we are hoping to have the first of the localized versions done before christmas. We have about 70% of the translation done, but since there is soooo much text in the game, it might not be feasible to finish this before christmas. The first localized versions will be French, German, Spanish and Italian. Rumor has it that the French, German and Spanish versions will also have translated verbal dialoge too.

BG Random Bits
Baldur's Gate Chronicles have another installment of their Random Bits of Information™ up. As always, lots of good info. - Bowmeister

NPCs make no choices regarding anything to do with advancement. Once they join the party, how they develop is up to you. BG will be about constructing an effective party, even though only one character is really "yours." The only time an NPC is removed from your control is plot related events, or if they fight with other NPC party members, or if they disagree with how you lead (alignment conflicts). NPCs are yours to control, within the limits of their alignments. They recognize you as leader, and do as you dictate.

Again, you can use any member of the party as you see fit. A thief that steals a town blind can do so, but such acts will effect your party reputation a bit. The reputation is not just how outsiders view you, but how the party views itself as well. (besides, in a world with divination spells, how does anyone get away with anything? :) As for any plots that involve singular characters, the experience for individual actions is always divided up between the party as a whole.

I think you will find that having the auto-pause activated every round will be rather tedious. I find that it really disrupts the flow of the game. Usually, I play in real-time, with the auto-pause set to go on if a weapon breaks. That is not to say that I don't pause the game quite often. Once you have more than 2 members in your party and are fighting multiple or large opponents, pausing just becomes sound battle management. I just don't see a need in doing so every round though. Fighters retain the last target they were directed to attack, and if you have selected from one of our default behavior scripts, they will even switch targets once through with one, chase others down or switch to bows, or just wait and guard weaker members of the party. Clerics can be scripted to do the above, as well as automatically heal members that fall below a certain percentage of HP, and Mages can be directed to launch their spells and attack or just get the hell out of the way :) Thieves can automatically try to backstab, or be told to stay at range. There are more variations, especially with the spellcasters, but these are the basics. Again, I would not advise letting the scripts do everything, and taking control of every action is quite challenging and fun with a full party. Turn the AI off and have at 'er, but I still don't think you will need to pause every round on the round. Sometimes you'll need to pause MORE, often less.

Oh, and yes, you will have to hit spacebar to unpause after autopause has kicked in. There is no way to judge when to automatically do this, as you can make infinite changes to your orders while paused. You aren't using up "movement points" or some such, as the game is still real-time. The characters will still only do the immediate action you have directed, but you can change your mind what that should be if you are still paused.

Yes, you can transfer your main character from singleplayer into multiplayer. There is a handy little "export" button on his character sheet, which saves him to a separate file outside of your saved game. This will require a bit of suspension of disbelief though, as the HOST becomes the "main character" in multiplayer games. He can assign control of that character to whomever, but there is always just one character that the story is "about." As for items, everything comes with you, though the host can restrict items, experience, or stats that enter the game.

Yet Even More BG Advice
Luke Kristjanson of Bioware is shelling out some more tid bits on the game that everyone wants. Maybe we should save all of these periodic advice posts and save $15.00 on the inevitable hint book:) MadBard

on creating all six party members: You will miss sooo much by creating all the characters. First, your party of all created will all use the default voice files, and while there will be a couple to choose from, they are all intentionally generic. You can go to the trouble of creating more lively ones, but the characters will still be devoid of "personality overrides" and behaviors such as moral failures and berserk furies. Sure it might be more convenient to play with automatons that never question you, but what fun is there in that? Second, the NPCs interact with each other. Some will seem fine with each other at first, but slowly build up to outright fighting within the group. Others may take exception to how you lead and run out at inopportune moments, or try and stab you in the back. Inconvenient? yes, but lots of fun too! Basically, the NPCs are not just static quest-generating meatsacks that you pick up for no reason. They will change with the lineup, and as well each has a special ability or talent that only they can do. If you are going to play multiplayer, play with other players and experience their personalities. If you are going to play by yourself, get some NPCs for the same reason. Sure there are a few quests as well, but thay are just the gravy.

Inside Black Isle
Here is a post by Greg Peterson of Interplay's role playing division Black Isle over at the BG Backroom Board. Lots of detail on the game development process at BI. MadBard

on how Black Isle works: I think the challenge we have is that there are many different types of RPGer's in the world, and we would like to be able to design games for most of them. Not everyone wants a 200 hour game. Not everyone wants to pay for it OR wait for it (how long has Baldur's Gate been in development versus Fallout2?). Our approach is to design different games for different RPG audiences - which is a departure from other companies who try to design one game to please everyone, and end up disappointing everyone to some extent. As we design these games, we want people to clearly understand that they are different, and more importantly, HOW they are different. We all know game companies whose products start to feel very similar after a while. We are striving to make Fallout2, Baldur's Gate, Planescape:Torment, and all our other products very different games. If we hit on a combination or title that strikes a chord with fans, we plan on coming out with additional products in the same line until folks 'vote' with their dollars and tell us it's time to move on... What this means is that if Baldur's Gate is successful, plan on a lot of Baldur's Gate products that are all 100% loyal to what Baldur's Gate is all about... One of the major commitments Interplay has made in the recent year has been to totally reorganize around product genres which is a fancy way of saying 'let the RPGer's work on nothing but RPG's." Black Isle Studios was created to totally focus on RPG's - to the total exclusion of everything else. Everyone who works here is every bit as much of a PRG gamer as you are (hard to believe). Our necks are on the line to prove to Interplay that we know what we're doing. The reason why I hang out on these bulletin boards as much as I do is because you are the people that will make it happen for us... so it pays to listen. So we are NOT trying to water down RPG's or something evil or sinister. We are trying to come up with a good mix of products that people give us 5 stars for -- while letting each product be very different from the other. I think we take a lot of heat for the corporate 'profit motive' when in fact I think a focus on profits is a good thing. When we hit a product dead-on in terms of designing something that everyone goes nuts over, we get the signal loud and clear "do more stuff like this!" One thing that hopefully you will give us credit for (as least for as long as Black Isle has been around). We don't design the product one way, and try to sell it another. We are being very careful to let people know that Planescape is NOT another Baldur's Gate -- and I guarantee you that there will be many that think it is better (even with 40 hours of gameplay to play through to the finish once (and even WE don't know how long it will REALLY take - the game isn't even close to being done yet...)). on being different vs trying to please everyone: I hear you about the interviews. I have read them all. It is a tough line we walk - we want people to clearly hear how we feel the game will be different, but we don't want to put one game "up" by putting another game (or gamers) "down". For example, we had an interesting discussion on my first day here at Black Isle. I didn't like the Fallout2 ad. To me, it didn't say anything about what the game stood for or how it was different. It was too cluttered. So I asked people "how would you describe this game to your friends?'' The response was "Fallout2 is the RPG for big boys" so then we spent time trying to figure out an intriguing way to say that. In the end, Krys Card, our PR manager, came up with the line "A Classic RPG (minus the faeries, spells and other crap)" which we thought really captured the feel and 'attitude' of Fallout2. So we ran with it. The point is, I like spells. Everyone likes spells. Half the time we talk about BG or Planescape we are focusing on how cool the spells will be. But there are no spells in Fallout2 -- it is not that type of game. We were not intending to belittle all the classic RPGers, or the spellcasters (god forbid - I generally play one) but we thought it was an important point to show how Fallout2 is a different KIND of RPG experience. Two things I try to avoid 1) Knocking the competition. Diablo II is going to be a great game. I can't wait for it to come out. I am going to buy it and play it and hope that it is awesome. Diablo II is NOT Baldur's Gate though. I want our games to be so good that they become the standard by which all other games are judged. Even now people are saying things like "it is a lot like Fallout, only..." I try to keep game comparisons within Black Isle. "It is like BG, but more internal development focused" for example. 2) Talking about ourselves. (Which is what I've been doing on this post, but you guys asked for it). I am not happy with our current BG ad - the one that says "The Most Anticipated RPG of the Year". To me that is marketing fluffery. It tells me nothing about the game or why we should buy other than "MY, we're cool..." It is one thing if we have actual reviews to quote - that is helpful for gamers to know who is saying what. But for BG we should be saying something like "The ultimate AD&D experience as never before seen on a PC" which is how I describe BG to my core gaming buddies (we need to figure a cooler way to say this though). Expect a new ad soon... Finally, back to the interviews. It is very difficult to do these - trust me. You have to sound like you're chatting and that you're not reading from a script, while at the same time not making some sort of verbal slip that is immediately quoted all over the world like "halflings suck". These guys are software designers, not news anchors. Imagine some of your friends if they were dropped in front of a microphone and asked to pontificate on a game they were really proud of... after the fact they'd probably read the transcript and say "when did I say THAT!" Think of it less as a corporate spokesperson that is speaking from "on high", and more like a friend and you'll probably smile a little more. If one of your friends said something that you thought was out of line or simply wrong you'd say "dude! You're totally off-base!" and that would be the beginning of an interesting (and fun) exchange. So keep it in perspective. (Of course, I can't necessarily explain Chris Avellone's aversion to halflings & elves... we all have friends that have personal 'biases'. I think he feels that they are overused to the point of being... well, uninteresting... It is a point of considerable kidding here at Black Isle)
