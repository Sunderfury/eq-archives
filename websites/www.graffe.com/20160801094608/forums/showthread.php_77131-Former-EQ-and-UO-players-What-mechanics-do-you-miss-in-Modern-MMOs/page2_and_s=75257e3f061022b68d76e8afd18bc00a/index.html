    Re: Former EQ and UO players: What mechanics do you miss in Modern MMOs?

    Originally Posted by Delores Mulva
    WoW is trying to move away from the "races play radically differently" thing, though. I think they're mildly concerned about players feeling that they picked the "wrong" race and that their character is permanently gimped as a result. But WoW places the game ahead of the worldbuilding, so that shouldn't come as a surprise.
    IMO, the way most games are designed regarding skills is idiotic and nonsensical in every possible perspective, whether that be from the POV of role playing or technical aspects; it does not have any advantages over alternatives either. In effect, the only reason to do so is inertia and artificial diversity, additionally it makes even hypothetically being able to achieve the much masturbated over "balance" unachievable in the same way that segregation's equal but separate doctrine is unachievable and noted as such.

    A much, much better system, one that has benefits in all aspects of gameplay whether online or single player is to have no classes, no races, no levels. Everything is achievable through skill and grinding, which also enables dynamic roleplaying/role assumption. Rift improved upon previous attempts through its Soul System which enabled a player to switch between roles quickly; however it did not take the idea all the way and eliminate skill segregation entirely. I much prefer that new skills are obtained via resource trading or fighting mobs, and it is up to the player to decide how to compose the role for a given situation.

    One thing we see in very mature games like EQ1 and WoW that add new features every expansion, particularly ones that impact gameplay, is that it becomes a complete clusterfuck, not only in terms of mudflation, but also in terms of delineation becoming less discrete and many of the skills/resources merging together; from a design perspective the cognitive load is much higher too with many more variables to take into account. I also refute the notion that a FFA system leads to less fun or competitiveness. As an example, in WoW's professions aside from some negligible cases, anyone can obtain anything and anyone can learn anything and everyone is in competition with one another for resources and BOE skills.

    The result is that initial differentiation early in an expansion is sometimes tremendously beneficial, I had exclusivity on 2 out of 3 of the JC recipes in Cataclysm for instance, thanks to cross faction hustling and dropping hundreds of thousands of gold on them, and I ended up profiting within a week and long term a factor of ten in revenue from those alone, however all of that was decisions I had to make on my own and decisions that influenced others. It is a much more realistic world that way. An example from EQ1 are the BOE/Rep spells/skills or the raid dropped spells/skills from WoW's Classic/TBC, those were tremendously valuable in some cases and gave people something to work towards.

    This design also removes all of the interclass drama from the game and all the associated bureaucracy for developers trying to homogenize how good certain classes/skills are, it removes the need or usefulness of multi-classing as well. In general, it is just a better design that generalizes much better, everyone wins.

    Making new content is also much easier too as well as developing a way for advanced players to work effectively with newbie players as adjusting comparative powers is much more trivial thanks to the vastly reduced number of variables to take into account, and with professions/improvements in expansions like Enchanting/Jewelcrafting/Alternate Advancement/Alchemy, all of those can simply improve variables that already existed in our initial design rather than adding yet another layer to balance/dither with.

    Re: Former EQ and UO players: What mechanics do you miss in Modern MMOs?

    I miss faction - not bullshit grind faction for neat trinket faction. I miss "This city is now friendly and you can have access to meaningful quests here" faction.

    You want to be the best <class>? You have to make friends, do quests, then betray those friends all for the sake of power. Once you're done, half the world probably hates you, but damn if you didn't have an experience.

    Also, fuck realm vs realm "Factions". Horde/Alliance shit was stupid when WoW did it (WC3 killed the entire pretense for this existing outside of NPC factions anyway). There is 1 experience that I got that were worth dealing with cross faction crap. The Blades of Wrath conquest of Orgrimmar. That's it, and while it was great as an ego boost, (40 people vs the entire enemy faction was kinda neat) it just served to show how ridiculous raiding gear was vs not-raiding gear.

    -------------

    I miss real, meaningful quests that made you actually explore the game. Unlike many others I do not think the existence of Allakhazam and friends ruined the ability of these to perform their function. The entire point of commonplace, complicated quests is to force an allakhazam-style website to exist, and to get players talking about them. Because then players are TALKING ABOUT THEM. That's how you make a real MMORPG.

    The staff of the wheel quest will forever be the single greatest quest design in MMORPG history at this rate. Why? Because you didn't have a recommended level and a minimum level for the gear. If you were good enough, enjoy being overpowered as hell! Because balance is bad for gaming.

    -------------

    I miss when games understood that balance was the direct opposite of good PVE. Old-school PVE is about massive situational imbalance. PVP requires a lot more balance, and the two cannot coexist. The original ice comet was not balanced. It was fucking terrifying. And that was OK.

    The quest for balance is actually a quest to remove frustration from gaming. It's the same idea that leaves fisher price invisible walls in dungeons instead of having 2000DPS lava just fucking kill you. It's why HP totals inflate faster than DPS as a general rule. It's why DPS is fantastic almost universally in all vanilla games, and then Pure DPS cedes ground to everyone being a different kind of CC class over time. Playstyles all converge on high HP don't die while we kill the bad guy, because standard gaming strategy for most PVE tactics converges on what works consistently, and "Kill it before it kills me" is not consistent. "Don't allow it to have a snowball's chance in hell to kill me" works all the time.

    -------------

    Corollary to the above - I miss fast pacing. Note that this isn't about twitch vs non-twitch gameplay.

    In EQ everything was slower. Casting times were slower, attack cycles were slower, and the tick rate was slower. This was server limited. This was also a *Good Thing*(TM).

    Because EQ's gameplay was actually faster than WoW's, in that you had a semi turn-like structure to the game. I got quad hit, I have about 1.2 seconds to do something before I get quad hit again. Incoming "bad shit" was a series of steps climbing the ladder to death, and if you got out of the bad situation, you stopped at the last step in the ladder. Unlike in WoW, where you have a much more linear progression of the bad. What that ends up meaning is you end up getting killed by nicks and cuts so you have to have a dedicated healer or the party is fucked.

    You see this in tabletop as well. More skilled D&D groups focus on the mechanics that give them consequence ladders rather than escalators, and then bring the minimum amount of restoration focused abilities to the table to keep them alive so that they can focus on doing the story and killing monsters (I.E. the Dungeons and Dragons) rather than playing Medics and Montages.

    That being said, I feel the need to address the idea that EQ also had terrible downtime. A little downtime is fine - 5-10 minutes every 30 minutes is about right. 5 minutes per pull was stupid. Why did this develop in EQ? Because of the inevitable progression to "I can't die to this" tactics and the lack of an out-of-combat mechanic (arguably WoW's single most useful contribution to the genre). Doing shit the boring way gets it done all the time. I will counter the impending argument that this was the ideal method of playing eq - this was how shitty players played eq. Good groups who knew a dungeon would bring enough DPS to kill shit before the healer had to bring out recovery, because recovery was for downtime. Active time was about Offense vs Defense. They'd chainpull for about 30 minutes and take a 5 minute break.

    30/5 timing is important. You will see this fall out in every genre and in most workflows. (sometimes extending to 60min/ 10 min timing if everyone is exceptionally focused on the thing being done.) This is the pacing of most League of Legends games, most Starcraft games, and is basically how long people stay active on counterstrike servers. Anything an MMO can do to get to 30/5 or 60/10 is good. You may notice, if you remember, that at their relevant levels, Breeze, Clarity, and Clarity II made a group of casters typically sit relatively close to 30/5 and 60/10. (Longer at higher levels). This may have been a mistake, but it is arguably a core reason why EQ had days where you felt like everything went right.

    ----------------

    Speaking of clarity:

    I miss Buffs. I miss spirit of wolf, I miss crack dealer enchanters, I miss the economy of player generated drug distribution networks. Because that was a shadow economy that didn't have the option of using an auction house and it was good for the game.

    -----------------

    So why don't we have the above anymore? Because what people like me liked is not profitable. It doesn't appeal to the broadest possible playerbase because it only appeals to people who want to take gaming to raid-level mastery. And that's fine. If I want that experience I'll go to Shards of Dalaya and play that experience. It's right in my happy place, and it's got a niche community of people that have my attitude. Everyone on that server knows we're an outlier, and we don't begrudge WoW and the rest of the MMO companies for making decisions that are good for their needs.

    If, however, you looking for the perfected EQ experience? Go play SoD. I guaruntee it's 99.5% of what you wanted every time you wax nostalgic for the game.
