    Re: What are you reading now?

    Thought about making a new thread but figured here was as good a place as any to ask for some advice!

    Due to my schedule when I'm out to sea I have to be in bed within an hour after I get off work or I'll end up roped into more work that goes on during the day. As a result I've been getting a lot of reading done. I'm pretty much out of things that I wanted to read that I can think of and looking for suggestions. Here's what I've got from the last couple months:

    Everything by Chuck Palahniuk except Fight Club
    World War Z - Max Brooks (I like zombie stuff but this was poorly written)
    Good Omens - Terry Pratchett and Neil Gaiman (amazing)
    American Gods - Neil Gaiman (good but very dark)
    Warrior of the Light - Paulo Coelho (only read cause I liked the Alchemist and was disappointed)
    Thief of Time - Terry Pratchett (good but not into fantasy much anymore)
    Slaughterhouse Five - Kurt Vonnegut
    Slapstick - Kurt Vonnegut

    I got kinda burned out on Palahniuk for now. Mostly if anybody knows anything I'm interested in more like his two non-fiction works (Fugitives and Refugees, and Stranger than Fiction). Fugitives and Refugees is a sort of travel guide for Portland Oregon drawing attention to the weirdo type things to do in the city which I found extremely fascinating.

    I was extremely disappointed with Vonnegut. I liked the concepts that he was aiming for in what I read, but I found most of his characters completely unlikable and the tone of both of his books that I read was just irritating.

    Currently reading The Screwtape Letters by CS Lewis.
    Next up will be Gentlemen of the Road by Michael Chabon. Unfortunately the Mysteries of Pittsburgh isn’t available on nook or I'd have started with that.

    I'm trying to stay away from fantasy. Much as I enjoyed Pratchett I just cant get into fantasy novels the way I could before; they don’t hold my attention anymore. Something similar maybe to the movie Push could be cool. I just want a little more realism than typical fantasy stuff has to it.

    Also I fell in love with the miniseries Generation Kill, and was considering reading that although usually I try to stay away from books that I've already seen the movie version of.

    Any suggestions more than welcome and I am limited for the most part to things I can get on the nook because mail ordering books is minimum 2-3 weeks delivery time. If something is definitely worth ordering that way though I'm not opposed to it.

    Re: What are you reading now?

    I finally read Dune (Frank Herbert). We own the movie, watched it last week, and I got the hankerin'. It's a top-notch sci-fi novel.

    As a followup, I read through the summaries of the following books on Wikipedia. Yeah, I'm not reading any of those. I'm going to stop at just the first book, but I'm also going to recommend it to everyone I can. The rest? It starts to get really preach-y. Pun intended.

    Also, continuing to read The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire (abridged, Edward Gibbons), a very famous work of historical recounting. In fact, it set the standard for historical documentation. If you haven't tried it, at least read the first few chapters. Not a good sleeping book - every single sentence is warfare on your brain. However, the knowledge contained in it is amazing.

    Reading 'The Wild Trees' by Richard Preston.

    http://www.richardpreston.net/books/wt.html

    The deep redwood canopy is a vertical Eden filled hanging gardens of ferns, reefs of lichens, small animals, and all sorts of plants, including thickets of huckleberry bushes and small trees actually growing on the branches of giant redwoods. There are massive redwood limb systems fused into flying buttresses and carved into “fire caves.” Thick layers of soil sitting on limbs harbor animal and plant life unknown to science. Humans move through the deep canopy suspended on ropes, far out of sight of the ground, knowing that the price of a small mistake may be a plunge to one's death.

    Preston account of this world, by turns terrifying, moving, and fascinating, is an adventure story told in novelistic detail by a master of nonfiction narrative. Preston became an expert tree climber, and learned the techniques of super-tall tree climbing to tell the story in THE WILD TREES—the story of the fate of the world's most splendid forests and of the imperiled biosphere itself.
