    Re: Anita Sarkeesian vs the Cybermob

    Originally Posted by Wool
    Men like to think that they are needed to protect women because it makes them feel better about themselves, but at the cost of the woman's own feelings. Why should a woman feel weak just so that a man can feel strong?
    This is an important point which I think deserves emphasis. One of the benefits of privilege is the ability to be blind to its impact on your life and on others. You can see this in action in many of the threads on feminism, on LGBT rights, etc. in virtually any forum you might choose: generally white, always cisgendered, nearly universally heterosexual men acting as self-appointed bastions of rationality battered by a rising tide of angry, oppressed Others. Appeals to common principles (free speech! equality!), to shared ideals (color blind society!), to civility (stop being so angry!) come fast and furious in these discussions, and no one will ever convince them that the points made by angry, oppressed Others are as important or deserve as much consideration as their own problems or their own cherished beliefs. This dynamic is to be expected! MRAs, having never been in a position where they had to arrange their lives around Not Being Raped, don't have particularly analogous experience to inform their responses when women complain about specific aspects of male behavior, or point out that rape is much more prevalent than anyone really wants to think about. That the very fact that they don't have to arrange their lives around Not Being Raped is an aspect of the privilege they possess never seems to occur to MRAs. Again, there's nothing particularly surprising about this fact but it clearly feeds into the dynamic in a lot of these discussions.

    In the past, Goladus has ascribed certain behaviors/trends to a lack of positive male gender roles (which, as best as I can tell, he blames largely on feminism), and I personally think that many of his arguments in threads that touch on feminism can be explained by that belief. If his preferred solution to specific problems is, in effect, to reinforce traditional gender roles (whatever the reason), that fact provides a pretty good explanation for his opposition to eroding the narratives which underlie male privilege. Arguing that the "Damsel in Distress" trope is actually just an example/extension of healthy relationship dynamics is perfectly in keeping with the position he has taken in previous threads. The implied answer to your question is therefore pretty simple: women should feel weak so that men can feel strong because that's largely in keeping with traditional gender roles, and he believes that the problems he is specifically concerned with can be traced to the weakening/devaluing of traditional male gender roles. Personally, I don't find arguments along those lines to be particularly persuasive
