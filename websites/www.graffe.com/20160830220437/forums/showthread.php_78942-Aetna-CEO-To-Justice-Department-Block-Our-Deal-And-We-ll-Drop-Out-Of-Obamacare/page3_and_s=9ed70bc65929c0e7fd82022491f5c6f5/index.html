    • sub-specialists are limited everywhere because there just aren't many people that need them (even out of a population of half a million). When you live within 2 hours of Toronto (and I'm guessing you are from the London area), you probably have access to dozens of them, even if it does involve a bit of a drive down the 401.

    • Doctor's salaries in Canada have risen about 30% (constant dollars) in the last decade or so, resulting in far fewer doctors moving to the US. Specialists generally make between 250-300k here (sub-specialists another 50-200k above that), although family docs might make about half that. For the most part, we really aren't losing many doctors to the US, and we are gaining some from other countries to balance that out.
    • Two whole hours? For valve replacement? That's unthinkable! That's also an issue of geography, not healthcare. (Also, quit whining - my father had the same surgery a few years back, and had to drive 8 hours for his. If you are Canadian, you know you have to drive long distances because it's a big fucking country so the major cities are far apart.)
    • Hospital food in Canada does, indeed, suck. So, yeah - major healthcare goal missed there.
    • Survival rates for most serious illnesses are similar in the US and Canada. If you get something super-rare, and require ultra-specialized care, then yeah - definitely better off in the US (as long as you can afford it, of course). But that's due to a greater population base supporting more specialization, and not a product of the organizational system.

    Re: Aetna CEO To Justice Department: Block Our Deal And We'll Drop Out Of Obamacare

    Originally Posted by Quelian
    Extended health care has no context in the US, that's worth exploring with further discussion. What's that actually mean?
    As a general statement, extended health is anything beyond that covered by the universal plan. What that actually means varies from country to country. Example: in the UK, dental is covered, save cosmetic treatments that aren't clinically necessary. In Canada, only the sorts of non-elective dental surgeries performed in a hospital are covered, which is why insurance companies offer dental as part of extended packages. One of the first things the US would have to determine is what procedures, drugs etc. would be covered by a government plan. If you took the easy path and said "Medicare coverage is now universal", you'd have about the same dental coverage as Canada, with the same opportunities for insurance companies.

    The area that would bring up the most problems would be drugs. Drugs are expensive, even in nations that cap prices and encourage generics. You also have experimental treatment drugs, and drugs that are on the borderline between health-related and elective, like Viagra. (As someone who administered an extended health plan, I'll tell you right now: if a plan covers Viagra, almost every man on the plan will take advantage.) I'm not sure that the US government has the tax income to fund prescription drugs as part of a UHC plan, and I really don't think the political will exists to raise taxes to pay for such a plan. That would be another opportunity for private insurance to pick up the slack.

    Originally Posted by Shatter
    IMO there should be a balance of government funding and paying split, but comparing US health care to Canada's, I prefer to be in the USA because if I get seriously ill, I stand a much better chance of survival here than I do in Canada
    Back in the early 90's, my grandfather had a heart attack while at his snowbird retreat in California. My family paid $10,000 out of pocket to air ambulance him from California to Manitoba because of how stupidly expensive the hospital was there. They charged him $10 for one tablet of aspirin. Today, a quarter century later, you can buy aspirin at an American Wal-Mart for $0.05 each. You might survive the medical emergency, but you'll be killed by the medical debt.
