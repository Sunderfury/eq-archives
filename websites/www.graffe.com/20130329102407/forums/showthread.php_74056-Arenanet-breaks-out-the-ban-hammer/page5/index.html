    They were not exploiting the gem store or anything you can get within the gem store. Access to said perks was merely a consequence of abusing flaws in the game to inflate their supply of gold. I don't know why people brought it up, other than to give an example of the kind of damage they could cause with the exploit.

    I've spent 0 dollars on the gem store. Nothing found in the store has anything to do with skill. As I said, it's stuff like bank slots, extra character slots, vanity pets, dyes for your armor. It is an alternative to a subscription. Would it be morally acceptable to circumvent paying a subscription in WoW?

    At no point do you spend real money to create an uber item out of thin air. All you can do is trade purchased gems with another player for gold. You could then buy the weapons with the gold, but the other player also gets something in return.

    Now, one of the key things with a game economy to understand, is that when people exploit a game mechanic, it devalues the gold and items that enter the system normally. This can eventually lead to a situation in which normal players are not able to benefit from the same trading system.

    So for example. Let's say it cost 10 gems to buy a chef's hat for your character. Let's also say the normalized conversion rate has 10 gems as costing 1 gold, and that your average player will have 10 gold. They could very easily get the chef's hat without hurting their enjoyment of the game.

    Now let's say someone had found a way to get 300 gold a day by taking advantage of a flaw in the game. Gold floods the system, and loses it's value. Now it costs 100 gold to buy 10 gems, and the person playing the game normally can no longer afford his chef's hat.

    Maybe not the best example, but you get the idea. This sort of havok on a game's economy is not uncommon.
