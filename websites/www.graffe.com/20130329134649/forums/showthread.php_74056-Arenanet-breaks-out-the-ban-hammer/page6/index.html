    Re: Arenanet breaks out the ban hammer

    Originally Posted by Alikat Astrae
    Pretty sure they were talking a lot about selling stuff for gold to buy gems to buy bank slots 'n' such.
    You take the gold and convert it into Gems. Then you use the gems to buy the bank slots.

    Gems can be bought for cash or you can convert gold into gems. The issue is that there isn't a set ratio for converting gold to gems. It's calculated based on demand (the more demand to convert gold into gems, the worse the ratio is). Right now there isn't a whole lot of gold in the game so the ratios are very favorable for conversion. As people accumulate gold the ratios will become tighter.

    Now with people, basically, creating gold out of thin air, they can use that gold and convert it into gems. This causes the ratio to, artificially, get worse for other people.

    And, again for those who haven't played GW2, there is NO pay to win model in Guild Wars 2. The advantages are, primarily, cosmetic. There are boosts available for gems but you can also obtain them without spending gems or gold (as drops). Also, these boosts (such as an experience booster) aren't as good as they sound. The experience booster gives extra experience for killing mobs. However, killing mobs is the least rewarding thing you can do to gain experience. Each mob only gives a tiny amount of experience which is drastically eclipsed by such things as dynamic events, crafting and exploration. Grinding mobs would, probably, be the worst way to level in this game.
    There are other worlds than these.

    The distinction is that every one of those trades, save the first one for the gems themselves, is a transaction with another player. Usually in a pay to win system you pay real money and your uber items spawn out of thin air. The only person that benefits is the original buyer (and the company selling it of course).

    In this system, you trade gems for gold. The person trading the gold gets gems in return. You then trade the gold for an item. The person trading the item gets gold in return. It becomes the basis for an economy.

    Originally Posted by Dru
    When I have found things mispriced in grocery and department stores I am usually sold the item at the listed price and then they quickly send someone to change it. Unfortunately RL analogies suck because it was a computer selling the item; not a real person.
    Exactly my point, the RL parallels just don't work, hence I didn't go that route. In the case of the misprinted item, most places would just give you the one item at that price. If it was an unpriced item with a sign indicating price, most would just let you buy a few and take the sign down and their losses would self-limit thanks to supply on hand. If the item was unpriced and rang up via UPC scanner and you got a better price due to a data entry error, they would pretty much be selling them at the lower price until they fixed it, and they would likely pull stock off the shelves to the store room or just ignore it depending on the amount of discrepancy. If there were one register malfunctioning such that it scanned a lower price, they'd shut that one down after the customer being rang up had completed their transaction. But in no case would they call the cops and accuse the customer of shoplifting unless the customer was actually defrauding them (i.e. had tampered with the price tag or sign or register).
