    Re: Game Programming

    I was thinking about this the other day during one of our code reviews. Where I work, my team has weekly code and design reviews and the other team members can ask questions and learn and our manager and lead developer can usually pick apart what we did and tell us how it could be done better. That's a good thing. Except that we do not often get a do over because of time constraints and by the time we are reviewing our code, it most likely is already in QA for testing prior to deployment so its too late to make any changes.

    Our applications were originally written several years ago. Over time, we add new functionality or small enhancements, fix defects or make changes due to compliance reasons. What we do not do is rewrite the old code to be better unless it is to take a portion of the code with a specific purpose and function and put that functionality in a library or service. Basically it exists, and even though it may be outdated or better ways of doing the same thing are available, it works. It would cost a small fortune in terms of development hours and more important quality assurance hours to 'fix everything to be better code' or even to write an entirely new application.

    In the business world, it is just not feasible. Creating something entirely new, that does not have to interface with any 'old' code, applications or services is a rarity in the business world because of the cost.

    I know a few guys I was in school with that wanted to program games. They never finished school. They got a job writing code for some independent consultant (not gaming) who finished their education with experience instead. However, that experience did not include any best practices. No design. No documentation. No code reviews. No project management or planning. No steady paycheck. The guy really took advantage of these kids. They wanted me to come join them. After a few interviews the guy had me come in to try me out.

    He worked out of his apartment. There was a mattress laying on the living room floor where he had long tables set up for the computers. He wanted me to code the front end layout of a web page that would populate data from a database into a form for a customized page of his 'application' that he sells to his clients. He didn't like my clean, clear code using divs and css to handle the layout. No he wanted to use a nasty nested html table that was nothing but a jumple of crap and he removed all my commenting. I seen right away what he was doing. You see, he sells his application to his customers. But then if they need any changes down the line, they more or less have to hire him to maintain it because its coded so badly and without commenting no one else would understand it.

    When he paid me for the day, he wrote me a check for a couple hundred dollars (the check was a blank check, as in no company name or anything... a starter check for a new bank account), it was a bad omen. The check cleared, but I felt like I got off lucky. Glad I never heard from him again. He was creepy.

    So yeah, these kids learned, but did not necessarily learn the right way. And to think they traded their education for that... I can't even imagine what would happen if they ever do seriously try to get into game programming now.

    Really, there is probably a hundred reasons why game code is the way it is. Sometimes tho, it is just downright scary.

    Originally Posted by Star-Demon
    If I wanted to teach someone programming I'd teach them first how to actually complete a project.

    I would not start with C++, that's for goddamned sure.
    C++ would have been much better off had the ISO taken the approach Joe Armstrong (one of the designers of Erlang at Ericsson) or and less strictly that of Go (via gofmt) which is to only implement a new language feature if it can sufficiently replace an existing one and in the relevant release remove that language feature from Erlang. The result is that despite Erlang being very old and extremely battled tested with its standard libraries the language has not grown into a behemoth that very, very, very few people can grok and Erlang reminds a very small language on par with ANSI C89.

    That the meme exist and happens to be true that a team learns a new version of C++ with every project is a tragic design failure IMO not a benefit. Rust is in early days but hopefully will avoid that approach. I have yet to see a programming language developed by committee that did not eventually turn into a sprawling hydra that backstabs its users in the worst possible ways. A side effect is that implementing a fully compliant compiler from scratch is a monumental effort in comparison to the point of being near impossible as evidenced by no formally verified compiler existing for one of the most prevalent industrial languages in most every context (one exist as a WIP though that has me hyped but I expect it to never be completed due to insufficient funding long term).

    I was thinking about this thread after I made the previous post and I think a lot of industrial practices simply don't apply to game programming from a financial POV. The only people who benefit from shipping code that is not a clusterfuck (aside from the game engines that will be reused for years) are the people who own the relevant platforms that will have to deal with all of the bugs that enable people to use them for implementing homebrew code which is in and of itself rarely significant financially. So, I don't think this will ever actually happen unless it is a no effort situaton due to tooling improvements, the necessary financial incentives simply don't exist even for large game publishers as they don't have to deal with any of the fiscal downsides.

    And game developers have little incentive to improve their corporate culture due to having an endless queue to pull fresh bodies from, so the quality of life improvement for maintainers is not something they need care about. It's a bad cycle for professionals inexplicably compelled to subject themselves to abuse.

    Re: Game Programming

    I interview a lot of candidates fresh out of school, and I'll be honest, I typically put zero faith in them actually understanding anything about good programming practices - mostly because academia typically does a poor job at developing them. There might be some basics, like maybe you're entire java application shouldn't be crammed inside of public static void main in hundreds upon hundreds of lines of code, but some of the "best practices" I've heard candidates parrot at me makes me wish I could reach out and slap their professors (My favorite one that comes up a lot is that variable names should only be one character long - x, y, i, etc).

    Projects are typically stand alone, in which students will write a pile of code and then it'll be discarded, and won't be developed with again, so students never have a chance to work with code and see how trade offs they've made for the sake of "getting it done" can bite them in the ass later. There's no chance to practice developing maintainable code. When you're working with systems that are so broad and expansive, that tends to be problematic because no one person can keep it all in their head. No one ever wants to work with that "one system" where the attitude that's taken is always "Don't worry about refactoring it, just get it working" because the code is such an unmaintainable mess that no one is sure that the new version actually works the same as the old version.

    And it's a catch-22 - the only way you really learn to write maintainable code is by working with the same code consistently for awhile so you can see the pain that comes from your trade offs and what worked out really well. There tends to be so little emphasis on that in academia that students don't get much practical attempts at doing it. Consequently, most companies don't want to take the risk because there can be very real financial impacts to shoddily-written code. There's a huge disconnect between software programming in the industry and computer science in academia and there's been comparatively little effort to bridge it. Schools tend to not want to do it because there's so much more to computer science that just writing code and companies tend to not want to do it because training is expensive and (ironically) risky.

    Re: Game Programming

    I'd certainly agree that school can't do much to give you an excellent preparation for the breadth and scope of most software projects, particularly older legacy systems that have had years of features tacked on. But I certainly think there are certain ways to approach projects that can at least give students a better foundation for preparing to tackle such environments that what many (but not all) programs do at all. Looking back, one of my best beneficial classes in prepping me for it was actually my operating systems class. The topical knowledge has cropped up useful from time to time, but the real gain I got from that class was our project work. Each project layered on more and more work on our last project - very much an iterative development cycle for it. Sure, it wasn't anything fancy, but you got to see how your previous decisions in design impacted your next set of decisions - how sudden changes in requirements forced you to revisit previous decisions.

    When I sit down with candidates and ask them to describe their experience with their coursework, I rarely hear similar project set ups. In classes where such an approach would be feasible, it really feels like students are cheated by not having their coursework laid out in such a manner.

    It's somewhat ironic that my software engineering and development class did the absolute least to prepare for actually writing software.
