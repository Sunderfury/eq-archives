    Re: New Star Wars trailer out

    Originally Posted by Nerkahia
    We've had this debate ad nauseum, but again, if you release a formal announcement stating that the Expanded Universe collection is NO LONGER CANON, then by definition, it once was.

    From: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Wars_canon

    "With the creation of new Star Wars novels in early 1991 with Timothy Zahn's Heir to the Empire, the publishing department at Lucas Licensing set out with a new publishing strategy. Instead of stand-alone books where the events of one did not influence another, the new novels would tie into a continuous storyline. In the words of Lucas Licensing's managing editor Sue Rostoni, "Our goal is to present a continuous and unified history of the Star Wars galaxy, insofar as that history does not conflict with, or undermine the meaning of Mr. Lucas's Star Wars saga of films and screenplays."

    Every EU book published since 1991 went through a strict approval process by Lucas Licensing.

    = CANON.
    No, it does not. The fact that the novels were supposed to form part of "a continuous storyline" merely means they tried to keep that alternate universe internally consistent within itself. So no, the books were never declared canon, they were merely written and edited in ways to avoid contradictions with each other and with whatever Lucas did previously.

    But again, this is worth repeating: George Lucas himself, long before the Disney sale, said the books are not "canon" for the universe he depicted on film. The only true definition of "canon" can come from the guy who created the whole thing in the first place.

    There are other precedents for this. The Marvel Universe, for example, and the Marvel Cinematic Universe are different continuities. And that's OK too.

    No. "...are now officially non-canonical" only states that if they were non-canonical previously, it was not official. The statement on the Star Wars web site announcing the change makes that interpretation the most likely:

    http://www.starwars.com/news/the-leg...rns-a-new-page

    Originally Posted by Excerpt
    While Lucasfilm always strived to keep the stories created for the EU consistent with our film and television content as well as internally consistent, Lucas always made it clear that he was not beholden to the EU. He set the films he created as the canon. This includes the six Star Wars episodes, and the many hours of content he developed and produced in Star Wars: The Clone Wars. These stories are the immovable objects of Star Wars history, the characters and events to which all other tales must align.
    That makes it very clear that EU stuff was not canon; it could be contradicted or deleted on the whim of George Lucas. Six films plus Clone Wars equals all the canonical writings in the Star Wars multiverse. It's also worth noting that all the other quotes you pulled were reporters interpreting what was released on the site, which means their beliefs about what was and was not canon colour how they reported the event. The only canonical document on what is and is not Star Wars canon is the press release, and it's very unambiguous.

    There's a missing set of commas in the prologue scroll.

    Spoiler for article:

    There's a missing pair of commas, first found by Mic's sharp-eyed branded content editor, Joel Topcik. The crawl reads, "With the support of the Republic, General Leia Organa leads a brave resistance. She is desperate to find her brother Luke and gain his help in restoring peace and justice to the galaxy."

    It should read, "She is desperate to find her brother, Luke, and gain his help in restoring peace and justice to the galaxy."

    The commas are necessary because "Luke" is not essential to the meaning of the sentence — "brother" is. His name is unnecessary information, as Leia has only one brother, meaning it should be offset by commas.

    "Without the offset commas, they're suggesting that it's necessary to specify which brother," Mic's senior copy editor Kaitlyn Jakola explained. So in all likelihood, this is just a mistake.

    But what if it isn't?

    Anakin Skywalker, also known as the late Darth Vader, and his wife, Padmé Amidala, only had two children: Luke and Leia. Padmé died in childbirth, as seen in Star Wars Episode III: Revenge of the Sith, so it's impossible for them to have another sibling from the same parents. It is possible, however, that they have a half-sibling.



    Or, maybe it's just because they're trying to list two major characters...

    Or maybe it's just to make the damn thing fit on screen.
