    Re: Hijacking the Hugos?

    I am of the same two minds as when you guys first started arguing about Orson Scott Card. Generally speaking, I do not care about an author's politics, except insofar as it obviously affects the work itself. If there are bigoted metaphors and language at use, then sure I can see it lessening my enjoyment of the book, but otherwise I'm probably not going to notice.

    I can empathize with someone feeling passionately enough about something for it to affect their judgement, but I am not sure I can relate to it. Everyone has their own line of course, but I don't think about it that much.

    That guy cited as complaining about Korra's writers sounds like a real piece of shit for example, but when looking for books to read I don't research the authors to make sure they are someone I would like. Fact of the matter is, most human beings are horribly flawed in some way, and if I worried about this stuff too much I would never accept anybody.


    As for the issue itself, like with GamerGate I can understand people liking a thing and wanting more of the thing they like, but I don't understand the entirely absolutist demands that everything has to be one thing or another, with no compromise whatsoever. This juvenile belief that people are taking your ball away from you. Conversely, i think people are overly antagonistic towards people who want things that aren't in vogue anymore.

    Having said that, the provided argument about the supposed problem that prompted all this seems a bit off to me. Maybe I'm not reading enough and politicizing really is getting out of hand, but most of the sci-fi I've read written decades ago had all the things they are complaining about, so the claim that science fiction is becoming "too liberal" makes zero sense to me. It's actually bewildering.

    I'm not sure if science fiction is really getting more progressive as much as people on both sides are becoming more polarizing in their actions and beliefs. It isn't enough to be inclusive anymore, no, you have to stamp "the enemy" out. I sort of think the divide between us is self imposed and exaggerated to a needless degree.


    Originally Posted by QuelianSpyralshot
    The whole purpose of art is to comment on the human experience. By definition art is entwined with politics, because politics are a consequence of the human experience.

    You cannot judge a work of art in a vacuum. Without personal experience you have no tools to judge it with.
    What's good for the goose is good for the gander. I'm not saying you are wrong, but this line of reasoning justifies the actions of people you disagree with as well. If the author's politics become an integral part towards accepting a work of fiction, then of course people with different beliefs are going to start butting heads like this.

    Re: Hijacking the Hugos?

    Originally Posted by Melcar
    Are you being deliberately obtuse? I'm talking about judging "Gulliver's Travels" by reading "A Modest Proposal."



    I almost never know anything about the author before I read a book. Hell I almost never know anything about any author after I read the book. Really I just don't care?
    Generalizing Quelian's assertion, which I happen to agree with although don't necessarily think it pertains to the intent of the Hugo award or that the rubric can reasonably be applied with the same year, Quelian is stating that for a non-trivial subset of literature, especially that which describes a future type of organization and its consequences, analyzing the relevant literature without also taking into context what the author is responding to results in comprehending the literature's effects being difficult to impossible, and it necessarily is true that any possible non-vacuous rubric factors in the literature's effects directly or indirectly. As some other examples where this premise holds, The Federalist Papers are not sensible without considering The Anti-Federalist Papers and the social zeitgeist the authors experienced, similarly Martin Luther's Ninety-Five Theses lose much of their substance without taking into account the Catholic Church's state at the point in time of Luther's publication, Augustine's City of God is incomprehensible without a comprehension of Rome's hysteria which Augustine is arguing against.

    A non-literary example is The Wire. While with respect to enjoyment there are other films and shows that I enjoy more, however The Wire remains number one in my ranking of all-time great shows because its social utility is unprecedented in significance and its execution in messaging the utility is without equal.

    Similarly, while I enjoy ten or more comedians more than Bill Hicks or George Carlin, Hicks' and Carlins' arguments resound so strongly that they are the most important comedians in modern times IMO despite finding comedians like Bill Burr, Hannibal, Dave Chappelle, Dmetri Martin, and Chelsea Handler much funnier.

    HOWEVER, in the context of the Hugo's, waiting sufficiently long that the literature can be ranked in an austere and detached manner is not the Hugo's ambitionn despite that approach leading to a more sound ranking. So, as Dmitry says, the manipulation is not wrong, manipulating poorly is what is wrong; being both a sophist and incompetent at demagoguery is simply embarrassing to witness and shameful to attempt so crudely. In essence, if you are going to foment outcomes, at least do so in a plausible manner that respects people's intelligence; doing otherwise concedes ignorance and incompetence of the trade craft, which is never acceptable in any context.
