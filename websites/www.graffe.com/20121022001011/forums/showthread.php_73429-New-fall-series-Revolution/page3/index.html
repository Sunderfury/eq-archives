    That is a terrible example. Thor, and any good fiction, has a set of premises the reader/viewer is asked to accept for the purposes that the story can be told. Under this suspension of belief the world is created and the actors do whatever in it according to the limitations of that world. In the Revolution pilot episode, you are asked to accept that some event occurred and from at that point in time all power was turned off, e.g. a world wide black out. Further, Revolution asks that you accept that not only was all power turned off, but the method by which this occurred was such that it did not effect the chemical and electric reactions necessary for humans to simultaneously dissociate and cease at once. Alright, that seems pretty reasonable. However, Revolution goes on to ask you to not only to believe that some event happened, but the effects of that event were such that Maxwell's equations are no longer true (there is no electric current). And, during the initial event at least laws of motion were no longer true, so those are very significant premises for the world to have, but again its the setting of the environment and the story so okay, it's pretty lazy but what's next? Where the embarrassingly lazy, Stephanie Meyeresque, goat-whore script eats its own tail and continues on as the zombie of Terry Schiavo is when not only are all the actions in the fiction world incongruent with the premises, but the concluding money shot is of a wireless power source that powers a computer that is networked. So, the show that has both Favreau and Abrams involved with it can not stay consistent with its own plot devices and world for 43 minutes, and you expect quality to come from such a production where no one involved could be bothered to pay attention to significant details for less than an hour? No, it's trite and an embarrassingly poor showing from at least two names that had previously been associated with quality. Revolution could and should have came up with an environment with greater explanatory power and at the minimum consistent with itself on major setting elements instead of adorning polished fecal spheres with whore dust and strobe lights.

    Re: New fall series: Revolution

    An airplane isn't going to autorotate like that upon system failure. If it was a helicopter it would be plausible.
    A Plane isn't going to "autorotate" at all - that's strictly a helicopter term, and it's used when the helo loses power, the rotor will continue to spin in the wind - that's what "auto-rotation" is referring to". The wind through the main rotor coupled with a little forward momentum can create just enough lift in the helo to bring it safely to the ground.

    Now what can happen in an aircraft though - is one wing can stall before the other (in an aircraft stall refers to the point where the wings no longer provide enough lift to keep the aircraft in the air, not like a car where stall refers to the engine cut out.) So if one wing is suddenly providing more lift then the other, what happens is the stalling wing drops, the other wing rises - causing a spin. (Which is what I assume you were trying to refer to as autorotation).
    You can see that if you've ever been to an airshow and watched them do snap rolls -- a snap roll is where one wing stalls - it rapidly falls, the other wing pulls up and over, and the plane spins.
