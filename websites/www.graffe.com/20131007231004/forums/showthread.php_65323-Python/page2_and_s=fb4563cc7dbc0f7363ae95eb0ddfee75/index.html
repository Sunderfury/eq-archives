    So... the best way to compare programming languages is by analogy to cars. Lisp is a whole family of languages, and can be broken down approximately as follows:

    I can sympathize with Ikeya's allergy to parentheses at least due to how it is introduced without the requisite tools that exploit the elegant syntax to reorganize the AST in a way that is complete zen compared to any non-lisp language or even just illuminate what is balanced and formatting the code properly.

    A proclamations that Scheme looks like a line of parentheses in practice shows that the instructor did not take the time to introduce even a basic tool suitable to work with the language, it would be like trying to work with Java using ed alone. The reason I know this is that any competent environment emulates the way emacs formats lisp code and its pretty printer makes explicit only what is important while separating the components cleanly.

    And I can sympathize and even agree that scheme is unsuited to maintain and iterate upon very complex systems for the most part, especially prior to R7RS where fragmentation in scheme implementations entailed difficulty in taking into account different implementation specific details and where before R4RS scheme as a general entity stagnated.

    However, for the purpose of introducing a concept itself rather than how a concept is implemented and associated caveats particular to the language Scheme is exceptionally suitable, and its suitability for developing massive, kafkaesque systems at Oracle has not a damn thing to do with its suitability for teaching the concepts required to do so.

    It is a difference of learning a field with the least friction possible versus learning an element of that field and hoping that that irrelevantly minute knowledge translates exactly to other elements which the person who had learned the information robustly would have understood trivially.

    After reading SICP it was depressing to see how unambitious popular languages were and how ill suited they were for expressing ideas abstractly and allowing the computer to negotiate the rest, even just the first 50 pages was soul crushing disappointment at how much power in utility to humans had been sacrificed for so little gain in computing at the cost of forcing humans to think like the machine the code is ran upon.

    Little Schemer and Seasoned Schemer further validated how idiotic the way CS (not software engineering, which is a separate degree that exists as a side effect of the poor education of CS primarily but benefits from being different even disregarding that as CS is about mathematics not building systems) is taught and the suitability of Scheme for the task of teaching concepts early on, even more complex tasks (compilers in this case) are suitable for explaining with Scheme as the Lisp in Small Pieces book reveals.

    However, Scheme is insufficient (although possible if the instructor wanted to be extremely thorough in expressing an understanding of how the underlying concepts are implemented for things Scheme does not include due to its absurdly potent metaprogramming faculties, but personally I would not go that route.

    Instead I would utilize the text Concepts, Techniques, and Models of Computer Programming as it follows the pedagogy the aforementioned texts employ even though it does not use Scheme as its vehicle for doing so and the text is one of the best written texts I have seen thus far, Little Schemer is likely number one at present due to how damn fun and unorthodox it is.

    The way algorithms is taught is really idiotic as well, confusing implementation details with the concept and then often abandoning languages which allow actually seeing the implementation first hand for a weird state of abstraction that satisfies neither case and should be taught entirely mathematically in the same way that some of the lambda calculus and category theory books are which ends up being much more robust as well as instilling numeracy instead of contributing to the rampant innumeracy among programmers, a state which is obscenely ironic.

    At least now there exists non-emacs environments that are proficient scheme environents such as DrRacket which is popular for that purpose, and it has some features that are not possible in emacs at present too.

    Everyone who has tried ocaml has had nothing but nice things to say about it, usually saying that it had just enough mutation to never feel like the language got in there way.

    Haskell's type system is the reason I will likely abandon common lisp in the future, as I really loved it when I went through http://learnyouahaskell.com/ It has a really nice tool chain too that is the same across all editors more or less.

    Unlike any lisp (even with annotations), Haskell is super fast too (on the llvm based compiler) with excellent metaprogramming facilities that allow for it to be useful in even esoteric situations like real time embedded software (http://hackage.haskell.org/package/atom) and it retains a quality REPL along the way.

    Another advantage is the quality of its quick check implementation (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QuickCheck) that makes tests way less of a pain in the ass and a time consuming process to inspire confidence that the computer is doing what I think it is doing; a faculty that further catches bugs on top of the huge amount the compiler itself kills.

    Combined with excellent concurrency and parallelism packages along with the very rich community and documentation, it seems like this could be a killer platform sufficiently agile for adapting to future needs that do not feel tacked on.

    Haskell even has continuations (http://hackage.haskell.org/packages/...onad-Cont.html) that are not a total hack that junk up the language.

    And Erlang's OTP which is a main cause of Erlang's unparalled stability has a maturing analogue in the form of Cloud Haskell (http://www.haskell.org/haskellwiki/Cloud_Haskell).

    I think I just convinced myself to throw my limited free time into mastering Haskell instead of putting off grokking the GHC developer info (http://ghc.haskell.org/trac/ghc/) in earnest.

    Surprisingly reading language specifications is not as obscenely hard as I had anticipated and it is tremendously insightful if you want to know how something actually is instead of how it reportedly is and greatly reduces ambiguity trying to figure it out from further removed sources then running into WTF moments when running into more esoteric cases.

    Before actually trying it I thought it would require a doctorate in CS specializing in programming languages to understand.

    While not required, I think it is way the fuck easier to understand monads if you read the an actual math text that covers them, plus they cover a lot more useful stuff that is used in Haskell too that the text will contain.

    I read/watched several articles/videos on them and it did not really click, when I read the actual theory on them in a category theory text written for mathematicians it was like oh, so that is what they are.

    Trying to explain them with an implementation of them is way too hard to pull off.

    (untyped) Lambda calculus topics are a lot easier this way too, and the dover pubication texts are really short (a strong indicator of the text quality IME, especially in math) and cheap too for that (as an example).

    IME reading an applied intro text of a subject then reading the comprehensive theory of the subject has been the most successful way to grasp the information.

    That is just what works for me, what works for you is likely different, especially if you do not find reading manuals bearable.

    Another example validating the utility of Haskell:

    Originally Posted by Research Paper
    Haskell threads provide a key, lightweight concurrency abstraction to simplify the programming of important network applica- tions such as web servers and software-defined network (SDN) controllers. The flagship Glasgow Haskell Compiler (GHC) introduces a run-time system (RTS) to achieve a high-performance multicore implementation of Haskell threads, by introducing effective components such as a multicore scheduler, a parallel garbage collector, an IO manager, and efficient multicore memory allocation. Evaluations of the GHC RTS, however, show that it does not scale well on multicore processors, leading to poor performance of many network applications that try to use lightweight Haskell threads. In this paper, we show that the GHC IO manager, which is a crucial component of the GHC RTS, is the scaling bottleneck. Through a series of experiments, we identify key data structure, scheduling, and dispatching bottlenecks of the GHC IO manager. We then design a new multicore IO manager named Mio that eliminates all these bottlenecks. Our evaluations show that the new Mio manager improves realistic web server throughput by 6.5x and reduces expected web server response time by 5.7x. We also show that with Mio, McNettle (an SDN controller written in Haskell) can scale effectively to 40+ cores, reach a throughput of over 20 million new requests per second on a single machine, and hence become the fastest of all existing SDN controllers.
    The research paper is pretty approachable for the layman: http://haskell.cs.yale.edu/wp-conten...askellworkshop
