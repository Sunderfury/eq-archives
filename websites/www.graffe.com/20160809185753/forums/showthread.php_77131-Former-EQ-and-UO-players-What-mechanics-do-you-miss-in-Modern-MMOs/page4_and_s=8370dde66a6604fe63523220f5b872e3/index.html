    Re: Former EQ and UO players: What mechanics do you miss in Modern MMOs?

    Originally Posted by Goladus
    Downtime is a tricky word when it comes to videogames, and MMOs in particular. What constitutes downtime?

    A game typically has several different phases, and meta-phases. In a dungeon, you'll might have a cycle of:

    1. Explore
    2. Move
    3. Combat
    4. Recover

    If you think of uptime as basically how much time you spend in combat, then a dungeon where time is divided equally between those four phases will seem to have 25% uptime and 75% downtime. But healing up, figuring out where to go next, and then actually going there is not really downtime. It might represent an unbalanced emphasis on less interesting parts of the game, but not all of it is downtime. In fact I think a more complete description of the cycle would look like this.

    1. Explore
    2. Move
    3. Combat preparation
    4. Combat
    5. Loot
    6. Recover
    There is no reason for step 5, that is trivially scriptable or better yet AOE looting which I am a tremendous fan of. One and two are the same steps; three is of use in so far as it is battle planning, after knowledge is acquired though an experienced team should not have three, aside from perhaps acquiring consumables etc., IMO this is exactly where bots should be involved in the game play experience. It is simply beyond absurd that machines are not used in any game outside of EVE that I can think of; it is a failure of imagination and disrespectful. Six, also should be unnecessary, leaving us with moving our characters, combat, and recovery (in some sense, inordinate downtime, especially one subject to the same pattern is simply dull and offensive to humanity).

    [QUOTE=GoladusPace, of course, can be a different thing depending on what you measure. If pace is measured in cycles per hour, it doesn't matter if you have 10 or 30, if the ratios are the same the combat uptime will be the same. And you can see there are many different ways the combat ratio might drop:

    * Combat might incur high costs relative to your available resources, increasing recovery time.
    * Combat might be high-risk and complicated, requiring extra planning.
    * Enemies might be sparse, meaning more time spent exploring and moving.
    * Enemies might be high-risk but die quickly, decreasing combat time.[/QUOTE]
    The ratio should be 1 for elite gameplay, if it is not even hypothetically achievable that is indicative of terrible design. A player's time should be entirely consumed doing things humans are exceptional at and are fun, not benigh, pointless bureacracy as is the norm now and reprensible. The ratio of reward to uptime should exceed 1 for elite gameplay though, think of AOE grinding in EQ as a case which realizes this through elite team work and creativity at the cost of extreme risk.

    Originally Posted by Goladus
    I observed an interesting phenomenon in EQ, where groups optimized themselves to maximize battle time for most efficient melee DPS output (on the plausible hypothesis that this will optimize XP gain). In the highest (battle) uptime groups, I noticed boredom, mental fatigue or a combination of both. This was offset only by the drive to keep doing it for the experience rewards. Boredom was a bigger factor when combat was uninteresting, mental fatigue was a bigger factor when combat was more challenging and engaging, but the overall experience lacked sufficient variety.

    How this worked:

    The "explore and move" steps were trimmed by players as much as possible. Either you sat in one place and pulled, or moved through a dungeon you'd already explored many times (since XP gain was so slow). Movement in EQ also had a significant opportunity cost in that you could not rest/meditate and move at the same time. WoW, with its instanced dungeons and faster XP gain, did not suffer from this as much.
    IMO this is only true for some cases, because people have not adopted a classless design, many classes are ez-mode, boring, and suitable only for the lowest skilled players. BM Hunters in TBC are examples of this, a class entirely automatable. Contrast this with AOE healing in Sunwell which was immense fun and a white knuckle experience that required exacting management and flawless execution or it was an entire wide wipe and subesquently being called out by the GM for sucking.

    [QUOTE=GoladusAfter hundreds of days /played, preparation evolved from tactical planning and creative use of abilities, items, and other resources to application of one or two standard strategies for the vast majority of encounters. All that was left in the preparation phase was routine tasks that felt more like a hassle than part of the game, like buffing and re-arranging equipment, and developers wisely added abilities that reduced the frustration of re-casting buffs. I am ignorant of this but would not be surprised at all to learn that modern MMOs let players define equipment sets (either as built-in functionality or add-on UI modules) and other ways to streamline inventory management.[/QUOTE]
    Extensive scripting removes the totality of anything which fits a pattern, player should only be concerned with metagame; think of starcraft where players manage many units at a macro and micro level. The only factors here are individual skill and creativity, there is no bureacracy involved and meaningless, pointless, entirely benign leg work involved. It has a great social aspect as well. Enabling player's to create content and utilize it at whatever difficulty the want removes boredom and retreading. This is something that pen and paper games or procedural muds implement perfectly.

    Originally Posted by Goladus
    With tactical challenges neutralized by these standard methods, actual combat tended to get very repetitive; especially after hundreds of days /played. The primary distinction of the combat phase is the risks, costs, and chance. Exploring and movement technically have risk and chance as well, but generally speaking the risk of exploration is merely unexpected combat, so the risk still winds up here anyway. This is where the gameplay is most dynamic. Note that "risk of losing" doesn't have to be the only risk. In a game with resources, risk of losing resources than you hadn't planned on counts.
    True of MMOs, symptomatic of terrible design and developers having contempt for players.

    Originally Posted by Goladus
    Players find the optimal configuration of group strength vs. enemy output to reduce recovery time to nothing. Recovery activities usually involve deliberate avoidance of risk due to the post-combat state of vulnerability. As part of a healthy cycle, this is fine since players need some mental recovery time as well, the constant stress of combat risk is, I believe, what leads to the mental fatigue. In EQ is the amount of recovery activity that involves nothing but sitting on your ass waiting for mana. This obviously sucks. In theory, "active recovery" abilities (such as cannibalize) help this, but most recovery abilities (including heals) tend to be highly deterministic requiring no skill, so after enough days /played they're going to be such a hassle that you'll miss being able to just wait a few minutes. Ultimately, however, for combat costs be meaningful, there needs to be some method for the player to actually pay those costs.     Assuming such a game existed which enabled scripting extensively, and say hypothetically it also achieved the 1 ratio for uptime and > 1 for reward, that almost necessarily entails removing opportunities for extensive socializing, which is perhaps an unintended consequence EQ's insane downtime had; that EQ's social dynamic was its best quality has been noted over and over again. The last point re: loot is one that is most interesting to tackle I think. Do we allow players to create whatever loot they want, make stats of no consequence and only cosmetc, or what? This has the most opportunity for a creative solution I think. I am in favor of a deterministic system whatever that is, given how frequently RNG systems decide to shit upon my soul; cosmically unlikely outcomes plague me with impossible frequency in games which do not.
