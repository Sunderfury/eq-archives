    Re: Heart Attack

    Thanks for posting this Nerkahia. In December of 2014 I had bad chest discomfort and went in for testing. They wound up finding a couple of blockages and were able to stent one. The other one couldn't be stented but a pressure wire revealed 90% blood throughput and it was deemed "physiologically irrelevant." Since then I have been on Cholesterol and Blood pressure meds in addition to my Type II diabetes medication.

    I have bad heart genetics on both sides of the family - Mother and Father both had heart surgeries before they were 55. I am 42 now. Weight has been a big problem for me my whole life. Back in December of 2014 I was 275 lbs. I got down to 240, and have crept back up to 248. I stopped drinking ANYTHING except water, which I know has helped a ton. I drink around 10 full glasses of water a day. Salt is my major weakness as well - I still need to kick that habit.

    The meds and exercise have helped. My latest labs last month show an A1C of 5.5, not even pre-diabetic. Cholesterol is down to 120, with a bad cholesterol of only 51. BP is 110/70. Still, I worry all the time now. I still have a long way to go and a lot of weight to lose. I need to learn to eat without salt, which is going to be HARD.

    I appreciate hearing others are going through what I have in a way. I've never smoked in my life (both my parents did) so at least I have that. But it seems the genetics is the big factor and there I got dealt a bad hand. Still, ultimately my own eating and lifestyle habits landed me here, so I have to get back on the horse and resume my weight loss.
    The more I learn the more I realize I have yet to learn.

    Re: Heart Attack

    Originally Posted by Nerkahia
    No apologies necessary. I agree with your characterization of the stakes.

    And thank you for all the recommendations! I will definitely look into several of those options should things get more difficult or I lapse.

    I've tried to quit once or twice before without much success. I'm finding after having a heart attack however it has been less difficult so far. They had me on patches the first 3 days. I told them I didn't even need anymore on the 4th day when they let me go home and haven't used one since.

    The frustrating thing I'm struggling with at the moment is the frequent fatigue and the difficulty focusing for sustained periods. It frustrates me because I have quit smoking, I should have better circulation than I've had in years, so I expect to have lots of energy. But I do not.

    I don't know if it's a side effect of the medications or from quitting smoking but I suspect it's a combination from both.
    I am not really surprised by that, it would be more surprising if you did not experience that. Consider that even disregarding the other chemicals in mass produced tobacco cigarettes that nicotine alone is a minor stimulant. Now that you do not have that in your system all the time, you don't have that minor upper modulating your energy that you can continually use. Nicotine has a half-life of around 2 hours iirc.

    On the energy front, I would talk to your doctor about getting a modafinil or armodafinil prescription. My father uses modafinil as it is the only ADD/ADHD medication that he can use on account of being the only one that does not affect the cardiovascular system. I have a thread on that on Graffe's.

    It is the only one I have taken that does not make me feel tweaked out and like an awake zombie with daily usage.

    There are some other supplements that can help with that that my doctor recommended but they do not have as much of an affect as modafinil or armodafinil do, very minute and I would not take them without consluting your heart doctor beforehand.

    With respect to meats, I also eat the ones mentioned down thread, I get them at Sam's Club. They are pretty good. I don't eat bread ever but I like rolling them up around some cheese.

    It is going to take you a year or more before you get anywhere near full strength judging by how my grandfather and father responded after their cardiovascular events, one was a double bypass surgery the other was getting a stem(?) in place, both of them were life long smokers who were able to quit afterwards cold turkey.

    I was lucky to quit much earlier in life, also cold turkey, but like I said it was incredibly difficult. What worked for me was basically ascribing hatred towards cigarettes, I think I accidentally caused my brain to develop a psychosomatic response to cigarette smoke though as I now get a massive migraine anytime I smell it, *shrug*.

    Good luck. I am going to be pissed at you if we read another death bed thread on Graffe's because you did not take this serious.

    Re: Heart Attack

    Another thing you may experience as a result of your heart medications is getting really bloated if you do not walk everyday and really watch what you eat, while kind of emasculating it is probably a good idea to have your wife watch all of your food intake so that you are not overeating massively on accident.

    Getting as much exercise as you can do every day is going to make a big difference in your recovery one to three years from now. You won't be able to lift weights for a very long time from now but you can walk at a low pace every day and build up to longer walks. Doing it with your wife will be a big help sticking to that schedule. Even something as simple as opening a pickle jar will be difficult.

    Another thing that my grandfather went through was getting really depressed from his medications that he was on for months until he was able to get off of them, so you might want to prepare your wife for that possibility as it was really hard on my grandmother not understanding that being from a different generation where people didn't talk about mental health at all and my grandfather being from the generation of men who were expected to be stoic buttresses regardless of what wave was thrashing their walls.

    If you can, having a psychiatrist to talk about this with may help a lot because it's difficult for even people close to you to understand what you are going through and why your personality has changed so much despite your trying to embody the stereotypical male archetype that we are expected to behave as where men cry on the inside and don't show any emotion ever.
