    Those professors can teach at a facility that is not a research university, even without potential prestige that would still be the case as they will always underperform compared to their peers who do due to grants paying bills and paying for new department facilities. And, you have neglected the utility that research provides to its students who take advantage of it.

    Students who take advantage of research opportunities or working as a research assistant get to work on real world problems, gain experience with applying for funding, reading research papers (something EVERY student should be able to do and the only way they can access current materials in their field without a 5 to 30 year latency), and writing published research papers.     John Hopkins wisely requires its undergraduate students to do undergraduate research. Competency in applying for funding alone and dealing with navigating the labyrinthian bureacracy associated with the process pays dividends when acting as a professional outside of university.

    Re: Hijacking the Hugos?

    Originally Posted by Deabn
    You might enjoy Richard Feynman's account of sitting on a committee to review school books for California public schools.
    That was entertaining. As one of the two students on the committee, I tried to focus on how well something was presented (ease of learning), number of examples (ease of learning), cost (including if used copies were available), and accuracy. Accuracy was rarely a problem, but clarity usually was. The professors on the committee liked books that were much more technical but went more in-depth, giving them the chance to teach more if time permitted, and they didn't care what the book cost, so us being there (there were two of us) really helped the students in those classes get something useful.

    We didn't exactly get fruit baskets, but the publishers made sure we got free copies of the books and knew we could call them at any time with questions. And the numbers make it easy to understand why: 300 students in an introductory class, books retailed (at the time) for $100 each. $30,000 retail, one class, with each semester having at least one class, so about $100,000 per year per class, this back in the early 90's. I've heard about how much money textbook companies can get from getting a state contract, so I'm not surprised that they'd pull out all the stops to win those deals. I've also heard how political some of the committees are, like the biology textbook debates.

    Re: Hijacking the Hugos?

    Originally Posted by kaisann
    I highly recommend visiting File770's Hugo round-ups if you're looking for a complete picture of what's being said as opposed to a summary, most of which are (unsurprisingly) biased. Much like Gamergate, the slow build to the current level of animosity is pretty hard to summarize - both sides have bad actors, and both sides believe the other is primarily responsible for the escalation. In other words, you can find examples of reprehensible rhetoric on both sides, some of which was used as justification for the next round of reprehensible rhetoric.

    Unlike Gamergate, however, Sad and Rabid Puppies both have clear owners, and it's pretty straightforward to demolish the actual, traceable original arguments they made. George R.R. Martin expended thousands of words on this effort, and his blog is well worth a read.
    The most interesting common thread between Gamergate and Puppygate is the speed and degree to which the established powers ceded the moral high ground in the face of ethical criticism, regardless as to the authenticity of that criticism. I followed gamergate from the beginning, and gave give you a nice succint summary:

    On the one hand, you are right that the origins of gamergate are murky and involve shit-slinging on both sides. Zoe Quinn and her personal twitter army versus /v/ and /pol/ and such (The TFYC stuff was going on around this time). Tumblr activists, "SJWs" were also aligned opposite /v/ and /pol/. Eron Gjoni's story of relationship abuse at Zoe's hands gave the 4channers ammo to go after Zoe, the SJWs, and the media they hated. In response to this, Zoe used her connections in the media and DMCA-takedowns to try and suppress discussion. This held the brewing tropical shitstorm at bay for a week or more. This naturally pissed off a whole pile of new people, and the "sleeps around for favorable coverage" angle attracted the attention of various pickup/mens forums outside the jurisdiction of Reddit, The Escapist, and Youtube. I believe there was even censorship at 4chan but my memory about the timeline on that is foggy. I know the 4chan exodus to 8chan didn't show up until later.

    During all of this, the gaming media was completely silent. There was no coverage at all, about anything related to Zoe Quinn or the allegations of ethical violations. Many people were calling for some kind of coverage of the scandal. You might think that the gaming media was taking the moral high ground here, that they were refusing to stoop to coverage of such base internet drama. Nevermind that we're talking about Gawker Media here, but at the time that's what I thought they were going to do. They were going to batten down the hatches, shore up their ethics policies, and militantly avoid any appearance of impropriety until the shitstorm blew itself out (where "blowing itself out" means the average redditor gets bored and goes back to /r/Games while /v/ and /pol/ and Tumblr go back to sniping at each other about social justice). They were going to hold themselves above the fray.

    Instead, for some mind-boggling reason, the gaming media decided to go on the offensive. They launched the infamous "Gamers are dead" articles, as a direct "Fuck You" to all the readers clamoring for coverage of the Zoe Quinn scandal. You can quibble over the degree of actual coordination involved there, but that's entirely irrelevant. The dead gamer articles were the nuclear bombs that transformed the routine tropical shitstorm into the Radioactive Cat 5 Hurricane we now call #GamerGate. That's when r/KotakuInAction's membership exploded. That's when Brietbart journalist Milo Yiannopoulos went digging for dirt and found the GameJournoPros mailing list. His Brietbart expose was inflammatory and full of speculative suggestions not remotely supported by the evidence. But that didn't matter. The Gaming Media had already established that they didn't care about truth, objectivity, or professionalism anyway. They'd already declared this to be a propaganda war. That's when feminist critics Christina Hoff Sommers and Cathy Young got involved. That's when #GamerGate caught the attention of other independent and conservative publications, who saw #GamerGate was standing tall in the face of a left-wing propganda onslaught and knew they had nothing to lose by giving them fair or positive coverage (and obviously inserting their own biases and talking points).

    So yes, you can describe a pattern of escalation that became a huge controversy. But not all sides are equal. The anons on 4chan are anons on 4chan. They have nothing but their words, ideas, and a simple imageboard to share them (also twitter and youtube). They have no respectability. They have no inherent credibility, no well-funded, highly-trafficked podiums to deliver their message. They are not trained journalists. No one expects objectivity, no one expects thoroughness-- at least not from any one individual. Respectable people don't share links to 4chan on facebook, but they won't hesitate to share links to Kotaku, Polygon, NPR, The NY Times, The Guardian, or Buzzfeed. Scoff all you want about the "respectability" of clickbait sites like Buzzfeed-- they are very successful. They have huge audiences. Maybe people claim not to take them seriously, but they still read. They're big enough that even GamerGate couldn't bring them down entirely. But damage was done and GamerGate doesn't appear to be going away any time soon.

    And so it was with the Hugo Awards. The puppies were outsiders. They were smaller in number, held no positions of power or influence in the system, and had no real reach beyond their personal fanbase (IOW they didn't have the kind of connections it takes to get stories covered in the mainstream media). The events happened over a longer timeframe, and there hasn't been a "flash point" as dramatic and game-changing as the "Dead Gamer" blitz, but there have been a number of similarities with regards to the establishment leveraging power and influence to attack perceived threats instead of on protecting the integrity and reputation of the award.
