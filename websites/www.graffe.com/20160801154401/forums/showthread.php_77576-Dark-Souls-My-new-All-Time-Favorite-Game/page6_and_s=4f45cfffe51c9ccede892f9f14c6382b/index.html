    Re: Dark Souls: My (new) All-Time Favorite Game.

    Here's an overview of Dark Souls vs Bloodborne, though I disagree with a few of the points about DS1:

    http://www.primagames.com/games/bloo...th-becomes-you

    Combat Speed

    In Dark Souls, speed is not a commonly used term. If you try to finish off a group of enemies too quickly, they'll almost certainly end up taking you down with one or two attacks. You need to approach each enemy encounter with patience, and sometimes run away to separate the enemies and make it easier to combat them. Slow and steady wins the race in Dark Souls, as even fights against basic foes can take awhile because you're constantly dodging or trying to move around to their back side to score critical blows.

    That's not the case when it comes to Bloodborne. Combat is much faster, and while you'll still need a heavy dose of strategy to get through most of the battles, you can't take your time and expect to win. Your character moves faster, enemies move faster and your dodging ability and general movement speed are also a bit quicker. If you don't take down enemies quickly, you'll be overrun.
    I think he overstates the case quite a bit with Dark Souls, you can certainly play fast if you are skilled enough, but in general he's right. Patience is huge in DS1.

    Offense vs. Defense

    When it comes to Dark Souls, defense wins battles. Whether you're a light-weight warrior who swings quickly and dodges even faster, or a heavy-weight combatant who uses a shield and giant axe, you're almost always playing defensively. It's not long before you have a shield that absorbs 100 percent physical damage, or you've learned how to dodge attacks with relative ease to the point where you don't even need a shield. No matter the case, defense comes first. You dodge or block, then you attack. It's rare to see players take the initiative in Dark Souls.

    Bloodborne is an entirely different matter. The new Regain System completely changes the logic on offensive vs. defensive play. If you take a hit in Dark Souls, you need to use a healing item to replenish that lost health. However, the Regain System in Bloodborne allows players to regain lost health by performing a spot-on counterattack. The more precise you are with counterattacking, the easier it will be to regain that lost health. However, if you're not precise and attempt to wildly counterattack with no rhyme or reason, you won't regain your lost health and will instead take additional hits during fast-paced battles.
    This one is absolutely true (about Dark Souls). While you certainly can play offensively, defense-first is usually the way to go (except maybe in PvP).

    Weapon Variety

    There are a large number of weapons in Dark Souls. They all have different strengths and weakness, and you can upgrade them to improve the weapons or add magic abilities. However, despite the large array of weaponry, players rarely switch weapons unless they want to completely change their play style. Rarely are there situations in which it's better to use a two-handed great axe over a one-handed broadsword. You find a weapon you like and then you stick to that weapon type for the majority of the game.

    In Bloodborne, weapons have multiple forms. This helps to keep things fresh, and forces players to learn how to use their weaponry. For example, changing the form of some weapons will alter the reach and speed of the item. In the short form, the weapon attacks much faster but inflicts far less damage compared to the slower but longer reaching form. You can also change weapon form mid-combo, which counts as an attack and helps to keep your offense moving quickly. Knowing how and when to change weapon forms is one of the big keys to a successful offense in Bloodborne.
    This is the only part that does seem a bit misinformed. I switched weapons all the time in DS1, just not usually mid-battle, and then only because of weight dynamics (secondary weapon slots are best filled with lightweight spell catalysts/talismans/etc. rather than alternate weapons).

    The "pick a weapon and stick with it" mentality seems like a cultural thing and something that the game just doesn't explicitly discourage. Fact is though, there are plenty of situations where certain weapons will be far more effective than others. Tall enemies with lots of space between their legs can be harder to hit with a spear or rapier thrust, for example. But wide swinging weapons may get blocked by narrow passageways. Some enemies are resistant to damage types (eg skeletons are strong against thrusting, weak against striking... armored foes tend to be strong against slashing, etc.). Add to the ability to add lightning and fire to weapons, and there will be various situations on individual playthroughs where you'll want to swap out to a secondary weapon for this reason. A mage with enchanted weapons, for example, will want to switch to a lightning or chaos weapon against magic-resistant golems. So there's lots of incentives to mix and match and try different combinations.

    Re: Dark Souls: My (new) All-Time Favorite Game.

    Spears are fast, dex-oriented weapons. They function similar to rapiers but are stronger, slower, and don't get the same backstab/riposte bonuses. One neat thing about spears (and rapiers) is that you can attack without lowering your shield. You burn through stamina like crazy, but it can be very useful against some enemies. Spears, with the fast attack speed, are also pretty good for pre-empting an enemy and hitting them before they hit you.

    Polearms (halberds, scythes) are definitely worth a look at somepoint. The downsides of halberds/scythes is that you stagger on miss (like axes), they tend to have narrow hitboxes, they are fairly easy to block with shields, they don't do a lot of poise drain, and their "strong attacks" are more about having a different hit profile than dealing extra damage. The upsides though: fast, powerful light attacks with great range. So long as you don't miss, halberds and scythes are some of the highest DPS weapons in the game.

    Some exceptions-- The Black Knight Halberd's strong attack is more like a typical weapon compared to the weak attack. The "Great Scythe" strong attack is fast one, the weak attack is the slow one. The strong attack still takes more stamina, but for this reason the Great Scythe is easily one of the most popular Dex weapons in the game.

    Something else I discovered is that while curved swords (falchion, scimitar) have a slight delay for their 1-handed weak attacks, there is no delay for the 2-handed attacks. For most weapons, 2-handed attacks are slightly slower. This makes some sense, though, since curved swords don't get a strength bonus for 2-handing like a longsword would.

    It's challenging and rewarding. The whole premise is that you are going to die a lot. But very few deaths are "cheap" and most can be avoided.
