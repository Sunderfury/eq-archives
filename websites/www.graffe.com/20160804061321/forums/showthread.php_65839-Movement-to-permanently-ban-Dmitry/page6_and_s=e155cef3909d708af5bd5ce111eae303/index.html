    Re: Movement to permanently ban Dmitry

    Well, evidently, Dmitry has been perma-banned. His account is now listed as "locked" (though, I do not really know what this means, so perhaps an administrator can shed light in informing the rest of us).

    Firstly, I think that Dmitry's greatest problem was that he was never really interested in discussion. Most of his points, as Delores rightly suggests, were made in furtherance of a basic bigoted view: that the forums were somehow being controlled by a "gay cadre", a conspiratorial censorship board that prevented critical discussions of Homosexuality. When he stopped being interested in discussing the merits or flaws of controversial views and started being principally interested in disrupting the forums to prove his point and convince others of his legitimacy, he was penchantly guilty of forum abuse, and for that, I think suspension of his account to whatever degree (temporary or permanent) to prevent this abuse from being propagated further was both justified and necessary.

    Secondly, I think there is a legitimate case to be made for prohibition of content. If some content is deemed too "dangerous" for discussion, it should be identified up-front so that users know the boundaries of permissible discussion. As Claud mentioned elsewhere, people have said many crazy things on this forum, and I have found some of the best conversations stemmed from shock-language that provoked thought and exchange of views. But, I also acknowledge that as this board's demographic changes over the years, people of varying emotional and intellectual capacities constitute its new pluralistic segments, and so topics that, while objectionable and offensive, may have once been considered legitimate may now be viewed as too disruptive by their very definition.

    If criticism of homosexuals is a forbidden topic here because it is considered bigoted, then it should be outright banned. To simply forbid "homophobia" is far too subjective a measure, because there are legitimate discussions to be had about the Gay Rights Movement and its sociopolitical consequences which some discussants would consider "homophobic", but other perfectly reasonable people would consider legitimate points that, even offensive, would be worthy of further exploration.
