    Re: Fallout 4

    These are not games you play for a linear experience, but to wander around and explore for hours on end.
    Maybe it's just my MMO background, but I've found that linear is the opposite of what I like. Some of these shooters are so linear it feels like you're going down a train track. Open world or semi open world mature content is the best for me, and I think more and more people. Single player MMO's basically. The main quest line is kind of a rope that keeps you on course but you're constantly off on all sorts of side adventures which boost your powers and make the harder encounters more doable. Most of Bethesda's games fall under that category, Far Cry, GTA, Elder Scrolls, to a lesser extent Dark Souls, Dragon Age, Bioshock, The Witcher, although I'd classify the latter few as semi open world. Semi-open world can be more easily scripted and equally fun if done well, but sometimes they go overboard. See Metro Last Night and ME3.

    Re: Fallout 4

    I don't remember them all exactly. They are frequent and minor enough to more or less be ignored beyond that single moment. Things you come to expect from Bethesda, yet less severe than they were known for in the past.

    Incorrect video cropping during conversations, AI pathing bugs, object geometry bugs, etc. I've had looted bodies that never disappear, and then have bodies disappear while I'm clearing a raider base before I can loot them. I've seen cars flying through the air, and brahmin running like crazy into the distance. AI behavior acts strange sometimes. Early on I discovered that if you save a settler from raiders and then talk to them, you can convince them to join your settlement. This hasn't worked since then, as now whenever I save them they just haul ass into the distance. I once had a bunch of these guys milling about Sanctuary until I realized I could give them orders in the workshop mode despite them not being citizens of any of my towns.

    Etc etc. The list could go on as I experience and re-remember them, but you get the point.


    As an aside, now that I'm further into the game and understand it a little more, I've become frustrated with just how many perk points crafting and settlements take up. At this rate I'll beat the game without a single perk in combat. Some of this stuff seems like it should be base functionality.

    All the people saying it doesn't matter because there is no level cap are fooling themselves. There may not be a hard cap but there most certainly is a soft one. While playing the game normally you will inevitably be in a certain level range after going through x amounts of content. The only way around this is to grind like a maniac or cheat.

    Anyway, going to reserve judgement until I'm further in and see just how much I am able to get that I wanted in my build. I made the mistake early on of spreading my stat points out evenly since you can level them up as perks now, without realizing just how much of an investment getting anything interesting was going to be without speccing that way from the start.

    Re: Fallout 4

    I've been playing this pretty heavily, and finally beat it. Or should I say, I burnt out hard building settlements, doing sidequests, and exploring, so mainlined through the rest of the story as quickly as possible. I sort of get the impression you are supposed to beat it first, and then do all that other stuff, but oh well.


    It had plenty of flaws, but overall was a pretty great game. As someone who replayed New Vegas directly before this, I think I can say that it's easy to take for granted just how much of an improvement this was. The one thing I think New Vegas probably still did better was the writing. While this was arguably better than what Fallout 3 did, it still seemed pretty simplistic at times. The answer to every problem is to murder everyone, and even things like speech checks are more often than not illusory and simply a way to get extra exp.

    I think what I liked the most about this game was the world itself. They did a great job designing the Commonwealth. This is probably the best I've seen a game have landmarks in the distance that you use to figure out where you are in the world. It's not just that mountain the distance anymore, but something immediately recognizable. The sense of scale accomplished as a result is impressive. Even just getting lost in Boston itself can be overwhelming at times.


    P.S. My concerns over perk points were completely unfounded. Not only did Preston keep giving me unwanted quests to save towns well after I already saved them, but just building settlements gave me a ridiculous amount of exp.

    Re: Fallout 4

    Automatron - a fairly decent DLC with a story that'll keep you busy for a few days if you're not rushing things. The robot companions are nice, but fully upgraded feel overpowered. My buddy did the Minutemen Castle quest on survival mode, and his robot had the mirelurk queen down to 1/4 health before he'd gotten around to engaging her. The new raider faction is interesting, but not super well fleshed out. Their armor seems to suck though; it really weighs a ton and I'm not sure how it fits in with good ol' heavy combat armor.

    Survival Mode 2.0 - I'll pass; not my cup of tea at all. No more fast traveling? Well, say goodbye to a settlement network, which is one of the main attractions the game holds for me. No sense building up a settlement out in East Podunk when you'll never make it back there to defend it in time when it's inevitably attacked. I'm all for more difficult gameplay, up to the point where parts of it are made so difficult that you cannot access other parts of the game.

    Wasteland Workshop - I've just gotten this installed, and I've spent about 0 hours playing Pokémon with it. I've got to say though, the gunner trap is hilarious. But on the bright side, there are more concrete building options so I won't have to build all my settlements out of foundation blocks. I just started a new int specialist playthrough and the first thing I've built after the standard wooden barracks so far is a high throne for Mama Murphy to sit in up on her fancy chair. I'm thinking that the Abernathys will be getting an acropolis-style temple built to worship me as god-emperor relatively soon, after I spend some more time studying RL near eastern temple layouts. Maybe I'll put in a gift shop and assign Lucy to it so she stops getting 'kidnapped' by raiders. I'm not here to judge her pro-bondage lifestyle, but after about the fifth time 'rescuing' her from whatever raider she ran off with this time it gets old.

    Far Harbor - I love Nick's quests, so I'm really looking forward to this one. Reading the previews I'm not sure it sounds like a mystery, but we'll have to wait and see.

    In general - I'm in my fourth official playthrough right now, I'm FINALLY going to plug my nose and side with the Institute this time around. I've been putting off allying with them, mainly because their objectives seem warped and I find it practically impossible to ally myself with slavers. Couldn't do it in Paradise Falls, couldn't do it with the Legion, can't do it now. Father is an asshole, and his entire society needs to burn. That stated, since I've already played through the other three factions' endings I'm stuck with this playthrough by default I guess. We'll see how long I keep going before just deciding to rush the main quest. It's a fun and great game but it hasn't really hooked me like Skyrim did, where I didn't mind going through a bazillion different playthroughs. Maybe it's the linearity of the whole thing. Maybe it's the garbage collector role. Maybe it's the way the economy breaks just as soon as you can mass-produce Jet. I'm not really sure. Guess I could set some goals like 'get all bobbleheads and magazines' or 'get 2 settlements to 100' but why do that when there are other gaming options out there? Still, overall it's an amazing, fun fallout/minecraft/halflife mélange of a game.
