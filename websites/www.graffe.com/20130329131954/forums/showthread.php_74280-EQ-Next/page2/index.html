    Re: EQ Next...

    Originally Posted by DmitrytheWizzy
    Things I like that will never come back:

    1. Real Death Penalties
    2. First Person MMOs
    3. Obscure difficult quests
    4. Camps
    5. Real group content
    6. Slow levelling

    I know that I am not in the majority enough to ever see that kind of a game again. It makes me really sad.
    1 - yes, I'd like at least *some* penalty in death. EQ was a little harsh at first, but death had a meaning.

    2 - YES!

    3 - difficult, yes, obscure no. Sure, everything is spoiled on the web now, but I want a nice difficult quest that is solvable from in-game lore/items

    4 - not a big fan of camps.

    5 - yeah, that would be nice, but a lot of games still have that don't they?

    6 - slower leveling yes. EQ slowness no thanks. I played pretty much non-stop from about a month after release on 2 characters. Neither of them hit the level cap pre-kunark, in fact none of my characters in EQ ever hit the level cap at whatever the current level cap was. Leveling in SWToR felt about right, maybe a little fast. GW2 is hella fast. WoW pre-expansions felt about right, haven't played it since the first expansion though, so no idea now. EQ2's base leveling felt about right, but there were so many boosters to leveling (double/triple weekends, etc.) that you ended up leveling quicker than I'd like.

    I want a nice, deep game, with a huge world that rewards me for exploring it (EQ, no matter where you went, there was usually something there). Lots of lore, that is actually meaningful to the storyline. Solo or small-group friendly in all content (scaling up for bigger groups is OK, but make it so us solo/small-group people can experience it too).

    Re: EQ Next...

    Originally Posted by Schezar
    As a monk I disagree entirely, Delores. The class got a ton of lore from doing various quests.
    The class was also a huge outlier. As a cleric, I got one class-specific quest aside from my epic - my holy symbol. It was dropped into the game late in the game, and didn't really give much in the way of lore. It's also worth noting that what you remember most about those lore-drenched quests is something related to a game mechanic - the spawn rate on Raster and Targinn. Guess what I remember about my epic quest? You got it - spawn time. Whoopee, epic.

    Originally Posted by Yaxa
    And the lore was there. You just had to look for it.
    No, it wasn't. But I'll get into that in the next response.

    Originally Posted by Silke
    Everquest Lore was actually VERY good up until the very end of Planes of Power and the whole Quarm fiasco. But it wasn't spoon fed to you. You had to go out into the world, talk to NPCs and see how things relate to each other. Freeport was a great example of this. As a Paladin you go around and find out just how corrupt the city is and if you're not hated in two-thirds of the city, you're not a respectable paladin.

    Najena? I can't remember. It wasn't one of my favorite zones. Lower Guk, however, was a civil war between the Frogloks. The Froglok King represented the living Frogloks in their war against the undead Frogloks (I want to say that their leader was a Lord, it was the guy who dropped the shortsword of Ykeshwsomething). Dwarves actually had quite a bit of lore built upon their god Brell. Which is something I liked about EQ, you had a pantheon of gods who mattered. Sure, worshiping one or another didn't give you bonuses but each god had a very distinct sphere of influence and flavor. Religion in EQ felt like it mattered. Remember, Sol Ro was the only "accepted" god of Wizards here on Graffe's and all respectable wizards worshipped him.
    That's an interesting theory about Lower Guk. Which NPC told you that? Oh, right - there wasn't one. Not a SINGLE NPC told you why there were undead frogs in Lower Guk. Not one. They were just there. You could tell they were opposed to the live frogs by faction hits when killing stuff and because they'd fight if in proximity. But what gave rise to undead frogloks? Corruption, experiment gone wrong, why were they there? More importantly, why were you there? Trolls and ogres were fighting the frogs for living space. But why would anyone else go kill live frogloks, especially when many races started off non-KOS to them? I remember going through Upper and Lower Guk on D, trying to talk to every frog in the place. I repeated this on my enchanter, even going so far as to Charm some frogs and see if that changed what they said. Nothing. Ironically, it wasn't until LDoN that players started getting lore on Guk, and it was from a source that players hated - Morden Rasp.

    Everquest had a more in-depth faction system than WoW. It had all sorts of nifty rep tricks - illusions, sneaking, charm tricks, you name it, you could raise pretty much any faction in the game. And yet it did very, very little with any of it. The vast majority of factions did nothing for a character. No items missed out on, no quests opened up, just another place where stuff would chase you. It was just as I described it - undeveloped. Some people like that, where there's a hint of a story and they let their imaginations fill in the rest. I expect more. It's worth noting that, when the new devs went through the old game files in response to player questions of "was there more to this quest, did these statements lead anywhere, was this in game", the answer was uniformly, "No."

    (And to continue with the "no story" thing from EQ - story of Unrest? Why were Vox and Nagafen where they were? Efreeti who dropped the GEBs, why was he there? What about the efreeti who dropped your faction if you hailed him in the Temple of Solusek Ro - was there more to him? Many of those questions had answers, released in expansions years later or in outside-the-game fiction - can you remember any?)

    Originally Posted by Silke
    World of Warcraft had decent lore (use of the past tense is intentional).
    WoW does an excellent job of telling me why I'm someplace and who these people are around me. It has lore you remember. Speaking as someone who plays Alliance: Van Cleef. Whitemane. Pamela Redpath. The buildup to Pathaleon in TBC. The Lich King in WotLK, and even supporting characters like Thassarian. It says something about how much players expect from the lore that, when Halion was dropped in game, one of the primary complaints from players was, "Why are we killing this guy? Who is he?" I agree that recent lore has been pained, though. I can't stand the whole "we're fighting one another except when something big comes, then you get quests from someone whom you once got an achievement for killing" thing. Either eliminate the barriers between Horde and Alliance and make the heads of PvP players explode, or keep it intact and do the work of having two storylines for every event - the Horde one and the Alliance one.

    Originally Posted by Silke
    It sucked in EQ when we played water-boy to Morden Rasp....
    Honestly, I never had this problem. I can understand why some people would, but I never felt "OMG I'm a superhero, why doesn't everyone I meet bow down to me". This likely had something to do with how many people I had around me when I accomplished anything. I can just imagine Morden Rasp talking about it to players: "Oh, you ended the threat in Vex Thal, did you? Which one of the sixty or seventy people I heard were in on that were you? Oh, you were player #1 in the CH chain? Pretty good at counting to '10', are you?" And in terms of the game mechanics, as much as people may hate hearing it, LDoN was a high point for Everquest.

    Originally Posted by Khieran
    I was talking more about the vastness and open feeling of the world, rather than the backstory.
    I can see that. Make people move slowly through vast, mostly-empty zones and the world does feel open. I guess I just want more. One of the things that amazed me most about WoW when I first started playing was how the quests would send you to pretty much every part of a zone. There weren't vast areas which could be summed up by the level of what wandered there and whether it could be single pulled. Everything had purpose, everything was part of a story. Like I said, the word "sandbox" really turns me off a game. I want a reason to be killing beyond "it's worth XP lol".

    Originally Posted by Dmitry
    Things I like that will never come back:

    1. Real Death Penalties
    2. First Person MMOs
    3. Obscure difficult quests
    4. Camps
    5. Real group content
    6. Slow levelling
    Point one encourages the most conservative gameplay out there. I could see death penalties being stronger than they are for PvE, but the old EQ "Evergrind" was weak and needed to end. (Side point: should these death penalties kick in during PvP, especially world PvP? Why or why not?) I was a first-person player throughout EQ, but it's not something I feel strongly about now. It's much better immersion, but you lose out on the environmental challenges you can reasonably expect a player to handle. Point three, agreed. I think WoW went overboard with the quest overhaul in Cataclysm. There was something nifty about killing something in EQ and getting some oddball item off the mob, even if the "magic and mystery" ended twenty seconds later with a trip to Allakhazam's. Point four can die in a fire, although it's interesting to note that MoP has added some camps to the game (although not the old "kill placeholder, hope for named" camps). Point five is out there, but you usually give up and declare a game "beat" before you get to it. And I don't agree with point six, at all. I don't want a game where new players are stranded in level Neverland, with nobody experienced near them to play with, and with days of slog ahead to get to those other players. It's worth noting that Everquest didn't have this, either. People who had friends already in game could get powerlevelled. People who didn't, oh well.
