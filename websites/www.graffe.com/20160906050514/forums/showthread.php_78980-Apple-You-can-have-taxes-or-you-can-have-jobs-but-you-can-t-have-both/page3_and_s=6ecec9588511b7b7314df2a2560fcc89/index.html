    Re: Apple: You can have taxes or you can have jobs, but you can't have both

    Originally Posted by Etelka
    ...all of those companies and employees have to pony up in order to be able to bribe companies like Apple, just to hire a few k extra employees.
    Those companies are paying the same rate of tax they would pay if Apple didn't exist. The same year Apple got its sweetheart deal, corporate tax was lowered to its current rate for all companies. That low rate was phased in as part of legislation from five years previous, prior to discussions with Apple. It's also worth noting, again, that even with the low tax rate, Apple is the top individual contributor to Ireland's tax coffers. The other companies aren't subsidizing Apple. Apple is subsidizing them.

    Originally Posted by Etelka
    Something that I've been strongly opposed to.
    Ok, let's go to something smaller. The Netherlands taxes capital gains on farmland only if those gains are a result of speculation (flipping land). Denmark, Germany, and France tax capital gains on farmland in all circumstances. The Dutch system was put in place because of the generally high value of property in the country, to prevent farmers from losing money on fluctuations that were out of their control and which could not be converted to cash flow without selling the farm and going out of business. The Netherlands is also the world's second largest agricultural exporter, and favourable tax schemes like this one help Dutch farmers maintain that lead over other nations.

    Should the Dutch be allowed to protect their agricultural sector in this manner, or should the market dictate land use in the country, leading (most likely) to a slow but complete collapse of domestic food production as farms are converted to general commercial and/or residential use? My position: the Dutch have a vested interest in production of food for local consumption. It's an industry of strategic value, and should be protected to the greatest degree possible under EU regulations. If other industries in the country don't get the same level of benefits, it is because those industries are not as important to the security and well-being of the nation.

    My position on Ireland and Apple is similar. Ireland should do whatever it believes is in its long-term interest with its tax system. If they've violated EU regulations, they should bear the penalty for that, and the situation should be fixed. But going out and luring businesses to Ireland, businesses that would otherwise not have come to Ireland, with favourable tax deals is not morally wrong. It is the imperative of the state to provide the best possible economic benefits for its citizens.
