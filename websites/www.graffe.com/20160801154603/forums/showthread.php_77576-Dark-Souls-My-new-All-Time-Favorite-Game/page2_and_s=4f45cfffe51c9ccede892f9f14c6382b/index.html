    Re: Dark Souls: My (new) All-Time Favorite Game.

    Originally Posted by Wool
    Huh, that's strange. I could have sworn you brought up one of these games before in a conversation about how poor modern games are as one of the few you approved of. I get this stuff mixed up a lot, I guess. It was probably a suggestion someone made to you, or something.

    That death penalty sounds much like the one from Demon's Souls by the way. The games are good at teaching you to be careful and conditioning the player to actually care about their surroundings, while not making death so crushing that you just give up.
    I think the main difference is that in Demon's Souls, your max health is reduced when you die and you have to buy something to restore it (I don't know the exact details).

    I think it says interesting things about player psychology and the way games condition you to play. There aren't many games that make as good an illustration of the learning process that takes place when you play any game. A lot of games don't expect much from the player, as they are meant to be leisurely entertainment. The player actually learns to be complacent while playing them, but even then the player is still learning things. It's almost like learning a bad habit. You learn that blank is weak to fire, but it doesn't really matter so you just mash buttons. You have actually learned two things in this scenario. A Souls game will test your ability to learn and adapt as much as they test your actual gameplay skill, and does it in such a blatantly obvious way that what is going on cannot be ignored. You will learn that what you do actually does matter, and when you do something that gets you killed you will learn not to do it again. This is part of what makes them fascinating, even for people who don't really like that kind of game much.

    I was actually surprised at how "fair" the first game was, as it didn't go the route of punishing you just for the sake of pretending to be difficult. I was nervous going in for the first time, but there was almost nothing that could happen to you that you couldn't recover from. In any other game trying to be hard they would have slapped a Game Over screen in your face and forced you to start over. It was all a gradual learning process until you achieved enough mastery to get to the end. In a Souls game, you are not expected to beat a level on the first try. I think dying to a trap is probably less frustrating once you realize this. It's almost like Rogue Legacy, in that it has roguelike traits (harsh deaths), but you get to carry over things that makes the next run easier.
    Dark Souls is mostly this way, but there are scenarios where its minimalism really does bite players in the ass and they miss things. I've heard various stories of players getting stuck in dungeons with broken weapons because they didn't realize there was a durability stat, players deciding to throw in the towel or start over because they ventured too deep into the catacombs or down to blighttown right at the start of the game, somehow managed to make it to a bonfire checkpoint and then couldn't get back out alive.

    The beginning is famously cryptic. Once you leave the tutorial there is a small, safe area to explore with 3 different ways to leave, none of which are obvious. There's the path to undead burg (which is basically level 1-1), the skeleton graveyard and entrance to the catacombs, and the elevator to New Londo Ruins (which is a mid/late game area with a safe area near the entrance, which also has a shortcut to Blighttown, which is basically the last "early-game" area). The graveyard is the easiest to find, and many players figure that's the way to go. It's tricky because the skeletons are very difficult, but not completely unbeatable, skeletons are common for "easy" enemies and the soul rewards for killing them are mediocre. But if they keep going that direction they are in for a lot of frustration. On the one hand, being able to access later game areas right from the beginning is very cool and adds a whole lot of replayability to the game. The NPC at the first bonfire describes where you need to go in enough detail that you should be able to rule out "catacombs" and flooded ruins from the list of places you should be going. It also adds to the "open-world" feeling of freedom and exploration. On the other hand, left to chance, odds are against you finding the path to Undead Burg on your first try. And while most players will figure out pretty quickly they are going the wrong way, those that don't are likely to get quite frustrated.

    Also, frankly, the item listing screen is almost completely useless and in fact massively misleading at first. Most players will figure things out as they poke around the menus and explore the mechnanics, checking up on wikis and reddit and such to fill in gaps. But when you look at your default item listing, few if any of the prominent numbers are numbers you'll ever have to care about. You have to toggle the description screens and status screens to actually see anything useful about your equipment. The list view gives you base damage (useless because weapon damage potential is calculated from base+scaling damage, and each weapon has unique scaling damage. If you toggle the screen you'll see this breakdown, if you don't, you'll have no idea which weapon actually has the highest attack rating. It also gives durability (but not max durability) which is not entirely useless but until you toggle and look at the detailed description there's still absolutely no clues as to what that value is. This is just a simple usability issue that could have greatly improved the game's accessibility, without coming close to "hand-holding."

    Finally, it's also true that some players wind up stuck on one of the early-game bosses and really haven't picked up the skills or knowledge needed to beat them, and will have to suffer getting their head bashed in repeatedly until they figure it out or quit. Most players seem manage OK, and the series has a reputation to maintain after all, but there's certainly an argument to be made that a game like this could be just as good with a more gradual difficulty curve.

    While I am on the subject of learning and complacency, by the way, the Persona games are kind of similar. I saw a thread on neogaf the other day, where a guy was trying Persona 4 for the first time due to all the hype, but he couldn't quite grasp it. He couldn't figure out why he couldn't beat a boss. I never saw confirmation from the original poster, but the conversation seemed to imply that he was making a plethora of major mistakes all likely originating from his own assumptions about what a jrpg is.

    Final Fantasy, and games like it, breed complacency in the jrpg genre. The player learns without realizing it that what they do doesn't really matter, and they turn off their brain as they go through the motions. For some people this is actually part of the appeal. Older jrpgs taught players that grinding will help them surpass any obstacle, and the lesson has stuck years after it stopped being true. The "defend" command in particular is a stickler for me. It's always present, largely only because it always has been, but in 90% of jrpgs there is almost no reason to ever use it. It's always a little sad when a game does, and people don't realize that the reason why they are dying is because they have been taught to completely ignore one of their five possible commands.

    To be clear, I am not trying to denigrate Final Fantasy, or any game that doesn't demand as much from the player for that matter. These games still have things of value depending on personal preference. It's just an interesting thing to observe and understand. People are always learning things that influence their behavior, whether they realize it or not.
    True. I alluded to an issue earlier with players reading spoilers online, and I think many games train players to go read walkthroughs and spoilers, and Final Fantasy games are often the WORST culprits. The game will be a long linear path with a few side branches and secrets hidden along the way, which means you have basically one chance to get every secret and if you miss one: tough shit. Sometimes you can backtrack all the way from where you came, which is usually mind-numbingly tedious, or you push onward until you get your airship, by which time that secret is probably obsolete anyway. Games like that are basically punishing you for missing secrets. Dark Souls just punishes you anyway, and usually gives you plenty of opportunities to find secrets-- and in most cases those secrets only increase your versatility anyway, not your raw power.

    Re: Dark Souls: My (new) All-Time Favorite Game.

    Yeah, and frankly I am quite happy Dark Souls didn't complicate matters in that way. On Dark Souls, if you are killed by a Basilisk or the breath attack of a particular dragon you'll be cursed and suffer a max health penalty until you undo the curse, with either a purging stone which costs a non-trivial number of souls (6000) or access a particular NPC who will purge you for free.

    PvP is one aspect of Dark Souls that is a huge mixed bag for me. Some people love it, but my observation is that the PvP game that the skilled PvPers enjoy is not much like the regular game at all. And it's not just that the invaders are all going to have better equipment, faster reflexes, and be more comfortable with latency and such; they're going to be relying on obscure/glitchy backstab/critical hit mechanics that you almost certainly won't know how to counter about if you haven't specifically gone online to learn about them. Half of my PvP experiences ended with my opponent appearing to teleport behind me into a backstab animation, which typically did 70%-100% of my health.

    I actually consider the backstab critical mechanic in Dark Souls to be a broken/misguided mechanic. It reduces all the subtle differences between weapons to a simple question of positioning. Weapons all have different windup speeds, different hit arcs, different recovery times, different stamina costs, different payloads with varying knockbacks, poise drain, stamina drain, etc.. It's all irrelevant during a backstab. The slowest, heaviest ultra greatsword in the game is exactly the same as the smallest, lightest dagger, except for the actual damage output.

    Re: Dark Souls: My (new) All-Time Favorite Game.

    If you want a gamepad on the PC I would say to get a 360 wired gamepad. All you have to do is plug it in and Windows does the rest. While I like dualshock controllers myself, a lot of people find the 360 gamepad's shape to be more comfortable, which is probably a must if you have pain in your hands of any kind. I would compare the difference in the two controllers to the difference between claw grip and palm grip mice (with the dual shock being a claw grip).

    Aside from which controller you use, I'm not sure if it's doable or not. The game is demanding, but not in the same way other action games are. It's more about being methodical and patient than about having lightning quick reflexes. I think with perseverance you could make progress, but you might not have the stamina to finish it. Luckily your left hand should be more concerned with movement than with attacking or aiming.

    I'll also point out that it's historical low price is $5, if you want to save some money before picking it up.



    As for pvp, the problem is that it's meant to add another layer of complexity, flavor, and danger to the game, but its existence attracts the kinds of people that ruin the intent. People who have no power in their own lives and want to feel power over someone else in a virtual one.

    It would be so much more effective if people didn't know it was happening. Demon's Souls, for example, has a boss that enemy players can control. But because it's moveset and fighting arena is strictly controlled, you are less likely to fall victim to obvious abuse. The player fighting is likely to never even know if they were fighting a person or a bot.

    On the bright side, all the baby pvp griefers have spawned some of the most hilarious anti-griefer griefing I have ever seen.

    Re: Dark Souls: My (new) All-Time Favorite Game.

    I think [thief] was designed as one of the end classes. I requires high evasion skills (B) and good timing with parry which I haven't figured out yet. Maybe I'll restart as a knight.     So basically, starting class determines your starting stats and starting gear. You can level up any stat you want and equip any gear you want. Most basic items early in the game have fairly low stat requirements. You might have to put a point or two into strength first, but if you're having trouble parrying it shouldn't be too long before you find some more standard shields (the wooden shield and caduceus round shield are both easy to get). With the Master Key you have as a thief, there's actually a pretty cool shield you can get right from the very beginning of the game.

    Knight is a good choice for new players. The advantages of Knight for new players are that you have points in VIT and END right off the bat, and basically every new player is going to want to level up their VIT and END eventually. Knights also start with armor that has "poise." Poise is how many hits you can take before you get staggered. If you are in the middle of swinging a giant two-handed sword, for example, but aren't wearing any armor, the slightest tap will stagger you and break you out of the attack animation-- unless you're wearing armor with Poise. And if you don't like how slowly the knight moves and rolls, you can always just strip off armor. Early in the game I spent a lot of time bare-chested wearing only leggings to get the fast rolls.

    Sorcerer is a funny class and honestly can feel kind of cheap and easy at times (though there are trickier parts). If you just want to get a little feel for what a sorcerer can do, thief actually starts with enough INT and attunement to use sorcery so long as you find some. There's a sorcery vendor right near firelink actually... though I won't spoil any more than that.

    The thief class starts with a parry shield. Small shield with 4 spikes for deflecting blows. I don't think it offers much protection but the game says thieves specialize in parrying then swiftly countering due to their lightweight armor. I read that you can equip heavier armor, but you need to wait until your stats are up. I assume that means STR. I'm not sure.
    Weapons have stat requirements. If you try to equip a weapon without stat requirements you'll get a warning.

    Armor has no stat requirements. The only downside to wearing armor is that it increases your equip load (also the very heaviest armor reduces stamina regen a bit but no need to worry about that now). Maximum equip load is dependent on your Endurance. The higher your endurance, the higher your maximum equip load. Your movement and roll speed are affected by your equipment load. Equipping lighter weapons and armor means you'll move and roll faster. Equipping heavier weapons and armor means you'll roll more slowly-- unless your endurance is really high in which case you can sometimes fast-roll with heavy weapons and armor (to a point). Note that only *equipped* weapons and armor count towards equip load, your inventory is effectively infinite and weightless.

    More about Shields:

    In addition to the weight and damage reduction, the key stat on a shield is the stability. Higher stability means less stamina drained on a blocked attack. You'll note that when you try blocking things with your thiefs parrying shield, you'll lose a huge amount of stamina because that shield has really low stability. A shield with higher stability will allow you to block without losing so much stamina.

    The parrying shield has extra "parry frames" and a fast recovery time from a parry. This is a pretty nice advantage for parrying. The key advice I see for people trying to learn parrying is to "watch the enemy's hand, not the weapon." Parry/riposte is a really popular way to play the game because it's challenging and effective, but it's also a pretty high risk/reward proposition because most of the time if you fuck up a parry you get hit in the face. Personally, I hardly ever parry and in fact like greatshields a lot which means I can't parry at all. Again, you aren't stuck parrying as a thief.

    What I would like the most of all is an ability to pause the game mid fight so you could compose yourself. With so many different scenarios being thrown at you it seems fair. If you hit start it takes you into your menus but the beasts keep pounding on you.
    Yeah, no pause. I was a little disappointed too. But on the upside, there are very few roaming enemies in the game so assuming you aren't in human form and vulnerable to invasions, outside of combat you're usually free to take as much time as you need.
