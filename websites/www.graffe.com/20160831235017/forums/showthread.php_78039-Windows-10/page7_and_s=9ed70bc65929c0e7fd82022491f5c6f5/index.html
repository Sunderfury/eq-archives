    I don't think people suddenly have a problem with it. There have been outcries before this. I think what you are noticing is perhaps a congruence between ignorance and impact. Ignorance being how many users understand and care about the subject. Impact being how important the service is to the users, i.e. availability of alternative choice.

    With Apple, their largest market displays the largest ignorance and apathy for the subject. Though there is a decent amount of choice present here, I wonder if some of the apathy might come from users feeling like there is none. They feel attached to their particular devices, the same way someone might keep playing EQ even when they hate it because of their guild/character.

    With Google, you have both medium ignorance and medium impact. The number of people who understand and care increases, and while Google has done it's best to become inescapable, there is still a fair amount of choice. I've certainly seen a lot more people protesting Google than Apple, either through vocal opposition, internet articles, or using other services.

    Now with Microsoft you are getting a userbase who both potentially understands and cares about (or portends to) this stuff more, while also being more locked into this particular product. Linux is still fringe enough that only the most dedicated will use it as an alternative. So you get more people complaining because more know about it, and less feel like they have a choice.


    I think I, personally, may be more put off by how they are doing it than by the act itself. Though I still find the backdoor shit kind of creepy. I already didn't upgrade to 8 because I felt the redesigned interface was taking away control from my experience, and here you have a system designed to enable the penultimate level of control. Ick.

    Re: Windows 10

    Microsoft no longer allows administrators to block Windows Store access in Windows 10 Pro

    Up until a month ago, admins could use Group Policy to shut off employees' access to Windows Store if they were running Windows 10 Pro. Controlling this access is a requirement for some businesses.

    But last month, Microsoft changed that option, claiming that Store access was required for all versions of Windows 10 except Enterprise and Education "by design." (Thanks to @SwitftonSecurity for the heads up.)

    Admins still can use AppLocker or Group Policy to block access to the Windows Store if their employees (or students) are running Enterprise or Education.

    A Microsoft spokesperson confirmed the change, noting that businesses who need to shut off Store access have no recourse other than Windows 10 Enterprise. The official statement:

    "Microsoft is focused on helping enterprises manage their environment while giving people choice in the apps and devices they use to be productive across work and life. Windows 10 Enterprise is our offering that provides IT pros with the most granular control over company devices. Windows 10 Pro offers a subset of those capabilities and is recommended for small and mid-size businesses looking for some management controls, but not the full suite necessary for IT pros at larger enterprises.The ability to block access to the Windows Store is typically for organizations who want more control over corporate-owned devices. This fits into the value of Windows 10 Enterprise.
    The clamp-down on administrator control over the Store was timed with Microsoft's declaration that Windows 10 1511 (a k a, the November update) had been designated the new Current Branch for Business (CBB) release.

    Driving visibility and use of Windows Store has been one of Microsoft's goals with Windows 10.
