    That's beside the point however, as I have read nothing from any member of Sad Puppies, be they moderate or extreme, save for the two founders being discussed in these articles.

    Between the two of them, Correia has made arguments which I can imagine having a shade of truth to them, and Torgersen is responsible for saying the things that ticked me off and sparked your objection. He is the one quoted as saying "a book with a spaceship on the cover used to be about adventure, but is now about racism and exploitation, etc".

    It's pretty clear from his own blogpost that he believes ideas themselves are ruining his simple space adventures. It's this line of thinking that dismisses a book because it has an idea that makes the reader uncomfortable.

    Now as I mentioned earlier, liking poppy and mindless adventure is perfectly fine. But I object to the idea that anything that deviates from escapist fun is bad science fiction, and I doubly object to the idea that this is some kind of battle that needs to be won.

    Where Correia seems to be battling the politics of the Hugos themselves, Torgersen seems to be more concerned with changing the medium as a whole. Where Correia seems to be tired for being dismissed for being on the wrong team, Torgersen seems to want his team to come out on top where it belongs. I may be overthinking this, but that is the impression their two separate arguments have given me.

    Having said all that, let me also point out that the purpose of the statement was to show equivalence between two opposing ways of thinking. The idea that an author's politics affects your enjoyment of a story will have the same consequence whether you are conservative or liberal. If you choose to follow this path anyway, you have to be prepared to accept people of opposing viewpoints doing the same, as well as the conflict that inevitably results.

    Re: Hijacking the Hugos?

    Originally Posted by Wool
    It's pretty clear from his own blogpost that he believes ideas themselves are ruining his simple space adventures. It's this line of thinking that dismisses a book because it has an idea that makes the reader uncomfortable.
    He's basing his theory on the claim that his "nutty nuts" cereal has always been the same cereal and that it's never changed in the past. The problem with that is that there probably isn't a literary genre that has seen so much change over the years as SF (or SF&F if you prefer that name). There's pre-modern stuff like Frankenstein and Jules Verne, there's the pulp era, there's the more scientific approach by Asimov and Arthur Clarke, the fantasy SF by Jack Vance & Co, the days of cyberpunk, the list just goes on. Makes sense, too, every time that the present changes our predictions for the future are going to change as well, so SF is inherently a child of it's times.

    His complaint that SF has lost it's "original" taste and that that's why people are disappointed now assumes that there was one particular taste to begin with, and that somehow the genre has strayed from it. In reality it's more that he doesn't really like the current taste much so the taste right before this must have been the "correct" one.
