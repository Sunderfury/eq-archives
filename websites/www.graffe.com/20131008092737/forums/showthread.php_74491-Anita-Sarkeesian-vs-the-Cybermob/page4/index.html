    Re: Anita Sarkeesian vs the Cybermob

    Yup, just like this issue (the portrayal of women in media) is a symptom as much as a cause.
    It's more of a symptom. Question is: what's the cause? For a game like Double Dragon, the cause is that there are lots of young boys who like the idea of fighting and are interested in playing a very thought-free, button-mashing game about fighting. The highly masculine male characters are meant to maximize appeal to the exact demographic most likely to purchase the game.

    Would it have better to have Luigi be a hard-nosed female plumber named Luigina? That would have been awesome, IMO, but it's too late now. The franchise was created decades ago and addressing the issue now will require some care. Adding a Token Woman to the Mario cast isn't necessarily going to improve the situation.

    It's self reinforcing. It's easy then to look at the new video games and respond with "but it's always been this way".
    Well there's a difference between "derr its always been dis way we canna change <drool>" and "This game setting carries with it certain expectations with regards to characters, plots, and tropes and we need a really good reason to thwart those expectations and risk inadvertently killing these geese laying golden eggs." Such is the case with Mario and Zelda. Even in the AS video, you see that the makers of Zelda have been toying with the expectations and standard tropes. Other settings, such as World of Warcraft, were quite successful at including a mix of sexualized, modest-but-pretty, cute-only, plain/homely, beastly, and downright grotesque female characters for players to choose from. Damsel in Distress plots are probably there (I don't remember) but also insignificant.

    Many games that include DiD plots use them quite effectively, such as Marle in Chrono Trigger. Other DiD plots, even those that superficially seem quite sexist, really aren't bad in context, like Rosa in FFIV. TMNT1 uses a DiD plot for one stage only (later on uses a Rat in Distress plot and I think culminates with a Save the World plot?) Mega Man was save the world, not DiD. Lots of NES games didn't even have recognizably human characters. There were monsters (Bubble Bobble), vehicles (RC Pro Am), puzzle blocks (Tetris), and then there were the cyborgs (Mega Man) and armored-warriors (Metroid) who were, like Link in Zelda, not exactly choice specimens of stereotypical masculinity (Well, maybe Metroid was until she took the helmet off).

    So it's worth criticizing, but not problematic...

    So, how would you criticize their portrayal of Krystal?
    She could be wearing more clothes, though that enhances her appearance of vulnerability which is a good trait for a Damsel in Distress to have. She appears to be a passive one-dimensional humanoid character with nothing notable to do. Anyone looking for an interesting, dynamic portrayal of a realistic female character should look elsewhere. Teenage boys aren't going to care unless they've been shamed into feeling guilty about it and may even choose this over Zelda due to the sexier damsel; although most people probably aren't going to care much either way. But then-- I haven't played the game and am only going off the clips AS showed.

    Re: Anita Sarkeesian vs the Cybermob

    So what would your reply be to a white supremacist for using this statement as a justification for racial inequality?
    That people are idiots who failed to understand the point, which is not meant to offer justification for anything but to be used as a basis for objective analysis. Complete morons can take the quote "there are always winners and losers" and frame it as a fatalist mantra and I can't stop them. Luckily, though, more intelligent, rational people can use the exact same quote to expose the flaws in that reasoning.

    My point is this: For any cultural change, there will be winners and losers. There will be winners and losers that are easy to identify, and winners and losers that are hard to predict because the cultural changes wind up having consequences that were not anticipated. Additionally, there are varying degrees of winning and losing. In many cases, it's possible that there are very few losers, and those lose very little. In other cases, the losers may lose a lot and the winners may win very little. Agitators for a particular change will play up desirable winners while denying, ignoring, marginalizing, or downplaying view of the losers.

    To use your race example, let's look at the issue of slavery abolition. If abolition succeeds you can immediately identify some winners and losers. Winners: slaves who will be free. Losers: Plantation owners who rely on the slave labor to maintain their privileged lifestyle. That's just one example, you could come up with plenty of others, like politicians who will get elected with black votes vs. politicians who will lose because blacks can vote, and so on down the line. For an objective, impartial judge to make the best decision possible, it's important to evaluate those winners and losers. However, it is generally in the interests of the abolitionists to avoid giving any credit at all to any of the potential losers and to deny the validity of their interests at every possible opportunity. If you watch the movie Lincoln, you will see an excellent, excellent example of this dynamic playing out. Thaddeus Stevens sticks by his radical viewpoint, denying all others at every opportunity except where he absolutely MUST make a verbal compromise for the sake of winning enough votes to pass the amendment.
