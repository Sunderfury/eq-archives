    Re: The Big Bang Theory

    If a situation provides laughter for its viewers and/or participants, how is the situation not humorous? What makes the situation being laughed at not funny?

    The pejorative "nerd blackface" is pretty silly, really. I can't wait for either South Park or Big Bang Theory to use it, in the same way that Seinfeld used the Bizzarro Jerry storyline, the Coffee Table Coffee Table Book storyline, and the "show about nothing" storyline used their own television show that they were a part of, hitting the 4th wall, in the same manner that the spectating public has created the 4th wall.

    Raj and Howard demonstrate an over the internet kissing machine. They are disconnected from the experience because neither one actually understands the merit of having a girlfriend, or for which Leonard will be using it. Thats funny. It is innocence and ingenuity completely missing intent.

    Raj ninja loots the kill in an Age of Conan raid and Ebays it all in the same amount of time it takes for the raid to figure out that the ninja was Raj, all in the same room as them - C'mon. Nerd Blackface? Yeah, it takes being an MMOer to undersand it, but in no way does an outsider even understand the sheer hilarity of all that involves.

    Howard lives with his mother (spoiler alert, not anymore), thats not a nerd quality. That's Costanza. That's Ray Barone/Robert Barone. He's a momma's boy. He is a nerd, but the joke is that he's still stuck at the comfort level of living with his mom, without realizing that all his problems are related to living with his mom.

    Sheldon is socially awkward. He is possibly brilliant. His character is definitely well versed in most lexicons of general science, chemistry, biology, physics, and the upper eschelons of quantum mechanics and related theoretical materials of science. He is socially awkward. It doesn't matter that he has a scientific explanation for everything. The humor is not that - most viewers don't even grasp the things coming out of his mouth. They laugh because his lines and dialogue are just as predictable as Friends' Joey engaging the world coming to a halt because an innocent niece doesn't know how to tell him that she might be pregnant and his only concern being, "IS THAT A SANDWICH?"

    I'll give you that Penny is a 2 dimensional character. Sometimes I think she's a bigger nerd than the rest of them. If there is anything about social culture that the "guy gang" has no understand of whatsoever, Penny has a cornhusking cornerstone of council for the group in order to let them in on what they've been missing all these years. If not for the thrill of her patience, she's blasted left and right for being a ditzy blonde, and thats okay, because you don't have to be a blonde to be a ditz, but its somehow more acceptable for the viewer public when you've got tits, an ass, a decent set of legs and a plain face and - oh yeah, she's blonde.

    Leonard, however, is the only character I truly have any complaint with. He's really the only character who is feigning his voice so badly, that he truly is emulating himself to be characterized specifically as a nerd. He doesn't need to act as though he is acting. He could just be himself, but he's already pigeon-holed himself into that person with the fake voice he uses that is over the top for himself, he occasionally steps outside of his character to use his own voice, and builds himself a character that is more than comfortable with being Leonard, but is forced to shrink back down into his scared to death of being confident self. His story is constantly beaten back to the same ashes from whence he was borne. That's a little boring, so no wonder they never seem to focus on him for very long - and instead, so often, have him out and about, or off on another mission in order to isolate the other characters from eachother in order to create the true situation of the episode.

    You mgiht say that Raj' character is guilty of the same faults, but when it really gets boiled down, Raj continues to progress episode by episode, and though his schtick sticks around, the progress doesn't go into remission.

    Be opinionated. Like it. Don't Like it. Don't try and convince people that something they are laughing at isn't funny. if it wasn't funny, they wouldn't be laughing. That's how humour works. Go look up "Theory of Comedy."

    Originally Posted by Backster
    Which have you enjoyed? And maybe the better way is caricatures that aren't stereotypes like Seinfeld or Friends, but I've never enjoyed them as much. Modern Family is hilarious but full of stereotypes on the other hand.
    RE: sitcoms & stereotypes, that is true to an extent, however compare how BBT utilizes its nerd stereotypes to Abed on Community.

    If you are unfamiliar with the show, Abed is somewhere on the autistic spectrum, and is really, really, really into television shows and pop culture, and he is a walking tv tropes database.

    In BBT for better or worse the characters are fairly static and to the extent the characters have grown any it has been marginal. Off the top of my head the most significant character development is the relationship pairings, Raj can talk after 7 seasons of befuddlement, and professonially all the male nerds have taken on pretty significant projects with all but Sheldon's being successful (Sheldon did not find evidence to that validated the string theory related experiment he was doing).

    Amie has recently taken on what appears to be a large project but none of the nerds on the show have had significant career growth or any other facet of their lives has changed much.

    Penny's most significant development is after 7 seasons taking courses at a community college again, the cause of which could have been a really great episode but was the result of an offhand comment by Leonard earlier in season 6 rather than some overarching journey.

    The show's humor relies entirely on playing off of someone's stereotypes, and the "jokes" about Amy's and Sheldon's relationship is really uncomfortable and incredibly rude, something which is pointed out in at least one episode.

    Anyways, back to how this relates to Abed: while Abed is incredibly nerdy (realistically so IMO) and quite a few plots unfold based on his habits, none of them are making fun of Abed, ever. They all basically celebrate how fantastic Abed is with his eccentricities and how that has resulted in so many great experiences for them. And in the episodes where Abed's eccentricities have lead to mayhem for his or the rest of the casts' personal lives it is used as an opportunity to flesh out the characters and even then Abed's friends use it as a teaching moment for him to make him a better person without being cruel about it.

    Were Abed on the BBT the plot would have had the entire episode revolve around the snafu, the cast somehow resolving the situation, Penny making fun of Abed for causing that and none of the characters learning anything in the process or having any lasting effects.

    Which is fine, BBT has its thing that it does, and it is sometimes funny (season 7 has been thus far for the most part IMO), but the way that BBT portrays a sitcom isn't the only way to do it, even if the humor is reliant on stereotypes.

    Anyone can not find something funny. Humor is always a matter of taste, and while one of the most important aspects of a sitcom is not the only relevant one. Certainly, Seinfeld does not have the most likable characters.

    Originality. Creativity. Insight. Thematic content. Culture commentary. Acting. Writing. I never found Cheers very funny but still found it entertaining.

    PS The Modern Seinfield twitter captures the spirit of the show, for anyone who misses it.

    I think that I don't like BBT (for what I've seen of it) largely because of the hopelessness of the characters. As MI mentioned, they seem entirely static. Not unusual for a sitcom, but given the nature of the character flaws, social awkwardness is the kind of flaw I'd prefer to see in dynamic characters who find ways to to cope, compensate, or self-improve. Instead, it's just throwing them into situations where their awkwardness causes them problems and laughing at it. The message I've gotten seems to be that social awkwardness is a trait that cannot be changed: so don't even try.

    This tends to mean that the exaggerated flaws are annoying and the plots with the subtle, more realistic flaws are highly disappointing. Though again, I've only seen a few clips and episodes.
