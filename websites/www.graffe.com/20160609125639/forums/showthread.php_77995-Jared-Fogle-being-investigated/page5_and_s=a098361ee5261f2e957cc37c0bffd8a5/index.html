    Re: Jared Fogle being investigated

    Originally Posted by PPatty
    So who gets jurisdiction if it's cross-state fapping?
    The FBI can get involved in anything like this if it wants because the pron usually travels from one state to another. But where the case is just a matter of a guy who was caught downloading from a sketchy website, it shouldn't be in federal court. Going after a child porn distribution network definitely is the work of the FBI and I.C.E. and perhaps even interpol, because the distribution networks travel all over America and even abroad. The federal system is more prestigious and better paying, so the FBI attracts lots of high quality candidates, and the judges sitting on the federal bench tend to be the best in their profession. So going after small potatoes is a waste of these resources. Another reason is that if you have too many federal crimes, the President has an avenue to wield dictatorial power, because it will be too easy to just investigate someone and pin a crime on them. That's a serious problem.

    I don't believe they should even have the statutory authority to prosecute child pornography consumption, in that case I believe they should be required to bring in local law enforcement and have them prosecute the crime. The justification for making this a national crime is very small - it's the mere fact that pictures can cross state lines that gives jurisdiction. The FBI should be more of an elite force and go after big distribution networks, or go after terrorists, espionage rings, and the like. It should also be involved in corruption and civil rights. The FBI describes itself as a "national security organization", explicitly not a police force. I think right now counter-terrorism and counter-intelligence are the FBI's two most important functions. If a child porn wonk gets investigative priority over a terrorist or spy, that would be a huge problem.

    Re: Jared Fogle being investigated

    Originally Posted by PPatty
    You've described the drug war model, whose effectiveness has been questioned. Why not attack child porn by bringing down the heaviest hammer (i.e., the feds) on its consumers? No buyers = no financial incentive for the producers.
    I believe that the state and local forces can do just as good of a job with this. If the FBI gets information about child pornography they should share it with local law enforcement, but in general should take the lead on the investigation. Local law enforcement is also a pretty heavy hammer - they have SWAT teams and tanks and everything. But from what I understand, child porn cases mainly revolve around getting hands on people's hard drive and inspecting what they have been consuming. This is not something we need the immense power of the FBI to deal with. If local law enforcement really needs help from the FBI, then the FBI does have jurisdiction to become involved in cases at local government request.

    But my main reasoning is still that, the less power the President has to go after particular individuals, the better. In the 50s and 60s, the FBI and CIA went after a lot of good-hearted civil rights activists - Mandela, King, and even killed some of the SNCC guys. I think the history has shown if you put too much power in the hands of the President to do domestic policing, you wind up with an abuse of power. There's nothing particular about child porn that should be addressed directly, but rather it's the vast plethora of federally criminalized local crimes that are a problem. The number of crimes is so vast that very few people go through life without breaking these laws, and if the President wants to selectively enforce laws against certain individuals, he would have the power to persecute them. Moreover, the federal level serves a supervisory role with regards to civil rights and corruption, but there isn't a higher level of government controlling what the federal government does.

    Sheriff Arpaio was a fairly horrifying case for how he would abuse police investigation power to persecute certain people. But those people could move to a nearby state to escape Arpaio, or the federal government could be involved in investigating civil rights abuses. Federalism prevents any single person from becoming too powerful - whether Arpaio or Trump, or someone corrupt like Nixon, so I believe Congress needs to start scaling back the President's prosecutorial power by returning criminal law to the state of federalism it was in closer to the founding of the republic. The main issue is that the President should have no capability to "go after" any particular American individuals, instead where possible, we should maintain a system of checks and balances and federalism, distributing domestic policy power evenly, while at the same time giving the President total authority and power in the foreign policy arena.
