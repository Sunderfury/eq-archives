    Re: New fall series: Revolution

    What would really happen: neighborhoods would immediately form governing bodies of the most primitive form, connected people would last longest due to better nourishment and medicine, deals would be struck between neighborhoods in order to secure safe transit for workers and produce to move from farms to home neighborhoods, we would spend about two years in this near-stateless state, then the older forms of travel and communication would begin to reassert themselves, mail would be delivered, government would take and transport prisoners in wagons, and the main sufferers would be people with no marketable skills during the transition period living in poorer neighborhoods. They'd get bottled into what would become ghettos and spend a couple years living on next to nothing, based on black market capacity per capita. But they would prove to be the source of much farm labor once old-fashioned organization and transportation was restored.

    After all, the farms would still be there, as would the crops, and all that would be needed to feed everyone would be the organization of labor and transportation. And we gots legs and can pull wagons ourselves while we wait for horses and donkeys to repopulate. And we could hammer out some steam locomotives and steam boat motor refits on tugs and whatnot to keep the federal system functioning. After all, it wasn't that long ago that we didn't have most of this stuff anyway, and steam used to rule industry.

    Re: New fall series: Revolution

    I find it a bit perplexing how easy people think the shift from industrial food to steam powered food would be.

    Let's assume that people can get from the collapse of cities to somewhere they can grow without anyone starving. Hell, let's even assume that they can form new social groups easily, without opportunists and everyone gives up for the meanwhile everyone they rely on the phone to keep in touch with. Some kind of gentlemen's agreement that the two weeks of food that cities hold is enough for everyone, a spirit of sharing, and the confidence that they can walk to good land in those two week.

    The first winter kills a lot of people. More than if people would just lie down and accept they're the ones who're going to starve. There's just not enough food in most places. Some places will have an excess, but there's no way they can get it out in time. They don't know where to send it even if they could. This winter, if you live, mostly you've either stolen food or put a gun in someone's face and told them to look elsewhere. Plus, obviously, hoarders within your idealised, freshly-minted society.

    The next winter kills fewer, but it does get everyone who can't get the sweet spot of defensible, fertile land, with people who have the knowledge to work it, and the tools and skill to make the tools to do so. Failing at food production due to a lack of every prerequisite would have happened in the first winter as well, assuming that there was enough lead time.

    After that, yes, things get easier for those in the sweet spots, but by then you're either a pack of predators or have been fighting predators off for two years internal and external.
