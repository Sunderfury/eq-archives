    Design is what distinguishes a product -- without design patents, anyone would be able to make knockoffs of anything with impunity. Should anyone be able to make handbags exactly like Gucci products, or watches that replicate the look of a Rolex? I have a hard time accepting that.
    Assuming they aren't pretending to be a Gucci, or a Rolex - but they are their own distinct brand and logo, with their own bespoke technology, with their own reputation for workmanship and price (whether that's high or low) - I really don't see why not.

    I mean, I look around my house and I think: What if everything had to follow this rule? What if every company selling something in a plastic bottle had to come up with their own unique shape and noone could copy anyone else? What if every company publishing a book had to have it a different shape or pay licencing fees? What if only one company could create round shower heads? It's all ridiculous.

    So long as companies don't infringe on each others logos and trademarks - so Apple have 'iPhone' written on theirs and Samsung have 'S3' on theirs - who cares if they are the same shape and size? And who cares if they are all 'swipe to unlock', just as who cares if two companies making faucets both use a 'rotate to open' function? Trivial elements of design should not be protected, just stuff related to execution (eg actual source code). If you're the first company to think of 'swipe to unlock' - great for you! You'll be the first to have it and that'd be worth something in terms of USP and market share. But there should be no protection from others playing catchup - we should all stand on each others' shoulders here. Any other way is a huge backwards step for mankind.

    Re: Jury buys Apple arguments.

    Originally Posted by Nadiar
    I believe a trademark already covers something looking so identical to something else that you can't tell the difference. Putting a patent on it opens it up to ambiguity.
    Trademarks cover things symbols, slogans and symbols -- basically brand identifiers. Design patents cover the look and feel of specific products that might be created under that brand, if there is one. Thus, the Apple logo is trademarked; the iPhone designs are patented.
