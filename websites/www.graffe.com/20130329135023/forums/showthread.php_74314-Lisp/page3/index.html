    Re: Lisp

    This is how I feel while writing algorithms in Racket (a scheme implementation):


    And this is how I feel while using VIM/EVIL:


    So far I feel like functional programming (Scheme in this case) is similar to my experience with differential equations in that they both added a way to go from a concept's description to what the implementation will be. What I mean by this is that when I first was introduce to ODEs I was impressed (and a "woah, holy shit this is amazing" moment) by how its notation mapped so well to how we would talk about the model in English much more so than how I felt when just having access to calculus for solving problems, which made how the models we had used before knowing about vectors and things of that nature arise intuitive; they were just the solved form of what we had already been using the whole time (you do use calculus in solving these equations, but writing them down uses differential notation). Note: this kind of broke down when it came to partial differential equations where we were dealing with time and space and while representing what was going on was harder but not all that dissimilar, solving them analytically may be impossible.

    Functional programming is similar for me in that going from the mathematical concept to the functional implementation maps more closely than an imperative way for doing so. So, as someone who has spent much more time doing math problems than computer science one how to go about solving problems gels with my neurons better. I would not be surprised to find out that most math people who end up programming feel this way, and that the converse is true for people who start out with a stronger CS background than math background first. In conclusion, it is pretty cool and interesting and reusing code and breaking problems apart has been faster to implement thus far. I'm curious to see how I fee about the situation in a year from now.

    Originally Posted by MI Redeux

    For many proponents of OOP (notably Booch), "solving the problem" is the secondary concern and naturally arises out of modeling the system. And while that can be taken to unnecessary extremes (again, looking at you, Grady Booch), part of the power of OOP is developing a good system (aka object) model and working from there for solutions to specific problems. Most engineers don't take it that far, though, and well-designed OOP systems tend to be easier to maintain and extend than other types of software (granted "well-designed" is one hell of a gotcha).
