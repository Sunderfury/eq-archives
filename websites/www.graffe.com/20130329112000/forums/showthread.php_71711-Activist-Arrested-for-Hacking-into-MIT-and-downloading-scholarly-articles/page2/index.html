    Re: Activist Arrested for Hacking into MIT and downloading scholarly articles

    Originally Posted by Elasto
    Well, if it costs them nothing for the articles to be reviewed, and it costs them nothing for typesetting (they simply return the article if it's not suitably laid out for online viewing), and it costs them nothing for printing (since I propose it being online-only) - where are the remaining costs? Someone needs to manage the articles going back and forth between authors and reviewers - and what else?
    Firstly, it doesn't really cost "nothing" for the articles to be reviewed really. The reviewers aren't paid, but the editors and infrastructure to support and assist the reviewers has a cost. From my advisor who was editor-in-chief for a journal for a bit, it's actually a gigantic pain-in-the-ass to keep things organized and on track, particularly if you don't have an actual "staff" to do so.

    Typesetting actually does cost quite a bit, but that cost is in personal. When I submit an article, all the figures are submitted in high-resolution, which is then sized/formatted to fit within printing/journal guidelines, which are based on the format of the typeset/publication. (I.e. 1 column, 2 column, 3 column, etc). This is time consuming and requires paying someone a salary, and given the number of articles in a journal, usually several someones. Oh, and if these someone's have an understanding of the science involved (not experts, just educated, and thus paid more) then they are even better equipped to make sure important points are conveyed clearly via layout/etc. Believe it or not, I've read the same paper that was pre and post "typesetting" and the post-typesetting was a LOT easier to follow, even though the pre-typeset/layout contained a lot higher resolution images/figures. And this was a paper I co-authored! So if anyone should have been able to follow it initially, it was me!

    Sadly, online-only isn't here yet. The current generation of Grad/Post-Doc/Early Scientists is a lot more accepting of online-only, but it's not there yet. In geology, there are 4 "highly regarded" online only/dominant, open-access journals (Geosphere, G3, Paleo-3, EGU Climates of the Past). 3 of the 4 have publication costs if your paper is accepted (ranging from $500 - $2000 depending). The highest "impact factor" is still less then 1/3rd that of Nature Geoscience (which is the highest in geology), and less then 1/2 of Geology or GSA Bulletin (which is 2nd and 3rd highest)

    Additionally, there is something to be said for "sitting down and flipping through a journal". When it's online only, you tend to only go looking for what you're looking for, while with a journal in front of you, you at least scan other topics, and frequently keep in-the-loop of other subjects that you're not actually working on. E.g. I don't study diatoms at all, but I'm familiar (in general) with the changes to the diatom contributions to the geological time scale in the Neogene.

    As for color, believe it or not, it is STILL more expensive to print in color then in black and white. With black and white (or greyscale) you are only using 1 ink/toner, with color you are using 4, and frequently you are using a mixture of those 4 to create intermediate colors. The online only journals do not have an extra charge for color figures, but if it's going to end up in a printed journal somewhere, it's got an additional cost.

    On top of that is, to quote:
    Originally Posted by Melcar
    Bandwidth; Storage Costs, Backup costs, IT infrastructure, Facility costs, Electrical bills, data center cooling.
    And additionally, there is a decent amount of organization and maintaining of the database that needs to be done.
    Especially as modern databases can track who's citing what, in what journal, and why, and allow you to 'backtrack' lines of evidence to original sources.

    Personally, using a program called "Papers" I have over 20,000 pdf's of scholarly articles from all walks of geology. I end up spending, just on my little pokey collection, a few hours a week maintaining and organizing my papers collection.

    My collection just has to be organized to make sense to me. Which is admittedly a non-standard organizational system at best. (Though if anyone want's them, let me know and I'll pass them on!, it's a couple of DVD's worth of journal articles going back 75+ years! )


    Personally, I would LOVE there to be a single, worldwide, open-access database of all scholarly articles. Google is actually doing a somewhat decent job of building this database, but until someone foots the "not-insignificant-bill", Google Scholar is about the best around, and it's lacking sorely.

    Building and maintaining such an online database would cost probably more then NSF's budget in a year. NSF's budget isn't large, but it would be have to be doubled do undertake that project.

    In my mind, that project is worth FAR more then building another Gerald R. Ford class Supercarrier at 9 Billion a pop. (These are the replacement for the Nimtz class, and the first two are under construction right now). But I'm a bit biased

    Originally Posted by Pyron
    That's pretty trivial to get around. All you need to do is make some adjustments to the standards for citation, such as counting by non-whitespace characters from the beginning or something. And as long as everything is electronic, that's pretty easy to do.
    It's not about making new citations. It's about using old ones. If I have a 1924 paper citing a 1919 paper, and I need to find where the author of 1924 thought a certain claim was made by the author of 1919, I need to have the same page numbers 1924 had.

    And before you say it, I can't just search the text, most citations are not connected with direct quotes, but are more of the following form:

    It is a consequence of 1919's view that the golden mountain exists but doesn't subsist (1919, p 24), but this is obviously hinky, ergo, my view is superior.

    The relevant text may include no mention of the golden mountain (since 1924 is drawing out a consequence of 1919), and the entire text may be riddled with discussions of existing vs subsisting. If I want to assess the claim I need to know what text is on p24 in the physical copy.

    Graffes seems to be mainly hard science and applied science types, so it's totally understandable that folks wouldn't realize how important preserving the meaningfulness of old citations are to historians and other humanist types. Journal articles in our fields don't have expiry dates in the same way
