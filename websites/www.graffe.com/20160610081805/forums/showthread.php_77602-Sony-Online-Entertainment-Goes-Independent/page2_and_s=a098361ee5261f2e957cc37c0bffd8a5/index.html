    Re: Sony Online Entertainment Goes Independent

    Originally Posted by Goladus
    UO probably influenced EQ in some ways, but I wouldn't say it "strongly informed the design."
    I didn't say "strongly," although I don't think that's far from the truth. But I believe EQ's team learned what to avoid by observing UO. Origin Systems' team did many things wrong, even by the rather lax standards of game design -- UO basically showed MMOs how NOT to handle PvP, how NOT to handle load balancing on servers, how NOT to handle economies, how NOT to handle environmental balance, how NOT to handle combat balancing, etc.

    That's not to say that UO was a terrible game, by the way. It wasn't. To this day, the immersiveness of its world has not been surpassed or arguably even equalled in fantasy MMOs. It had a lot of cool touches.

    But its flaws did much to improve subsequent games. If UO didn't make the mistakes, another game would have had to make them.

    Re: Sony Online Entertainment Goes Independent

    On the other hand, TOR and WoW are just too quest-focused. I thought total quest-focus was a good thing years ago, but now I'm seeing it as bad.
    Quests have to balance a very fine line between giving the player a goal to help guide and direct them through the game's content without subverting the core motivation that a player has to just explore and play(ie fight).

    Not to mention that simply being a "quest" doesn't mean it's a good quest, so "more questing" or "less questing" is a bit hard to evaluate. The way I read it most of the time (feel free to correct me...), this type discussion about quests seems to present questing as sort of an alternative way to get rewards as opposed to straight-up fight and pillage, which is partially true but a bit misleading. In the context of MMOs, it's also often a way to trigger scripted events that wouldn't make sense without appropriate context (though all too often it simply functions as a series of roadblocks preventing progress).

    For example, in Baldur's Gate 2, there's a quest to "Investigate and Destroy the Cult of the Unseeing Eye, which is a textbook example of a great way to implement a quest. It starts out with a priest asking you to investigate this rival cult that is likely dangerous-- in fact you probably saw a zealot in the street preaching. After infiltrating the cult's secret lair in the sewers, the cult's leader-apparent, Gaal, (various ominous clues suggest the being worship is real and far more dangerous) sends you on a mission deeper to obtain a powerful item, along the way you encounter a guy who says "yeah you definitely should retrieve the item but don't give it to Gaal" where you discover ancient ruins of a cursed and forsaken village that guards the item; where in fact some members of that civilization still alive. Turns out you've only got half of the item, and the other half is being held by that ominous, dangerous creature worshiped by the cult, so you have to go back and sneak into the creature's lair, grab the other half of the rod, then use the rod to kill the creature. THEN, after all that awesomeness, you go back to the surface and collect your final rewards (bonus experience, money, loyalty of a paladin character you met along the way, and affiliation with a temple).

    In other words: the point was the quest itself, not the final rewards. It was designed like a good story, with a mix of surprises, twists, memorable (however briefly involved) characters, and satisfying resolutions along the way, and the PC plays a central role in all of it. Indeed, by the time the quest is over, satisfying the desires of the quest-giver seem like an afterthought. But in the end, the game is indeed rewarding you for all the theoretical "non-combat" stuff you did along the way while completing the quest. But for the most part, the reward of the quest was the actual experience of it.

    So in that sense, questing as a way to offer a sort of alternative reward for just killing stuff makes a lot of sense. But if you're talking about a system where all the "quests" really are, are excuses to get you to kill stuff in Zone/Instance A rather than Zone/Instance B, and you get some bonus rewards and dialog for doing everything in the right order. Eventually it just starts to feel like a pointless layer.

    Re: Sony Online Entertainment Goes Independent

    MMOs face a very challenging problem, though in the scope and structure of the game as well as volume of content that their playerbase consumes. Most non-massively multiplayer games don't have an expectation to keep fresh new content for individual characters with hundreds of days played on them. Or, when they do, they either rely on a "New Game +" or procedural system for recycling and generating new content.

    I just spent the last few months playing Dark Souls, for example, I have about 20 days played, 11 of those on my initial character(1 regular playthrough and one New Game+ playthrough), and 7 more playthroughs on other characters which range from 15 to 53 hours. It's entirely self-contained, while there is on "DLC" pack, there won't be any more expansions or anything. The team that made Dark Souls went on to make a new game instead (Bloodborne). The only real "level cap" in dark souls is if you reach 99 in every stat, which is not really practical and would take enormous time to achieve for increasingly diminished returns. The "meta level cap" for optimim PvP matching, where most players stop, is 120-- while the theoretical max is 700ish.

    While alt addicts aren't rare in MMOs, it seems to me there is much more focus on content at the current level cap and infinite progression. Unless you're able to have multiple characters at the level cap, odds are that you'll have a "main" that you are heavily invested in. That's the character you'll want to consume new content with, whether it's raids or PvP or dailies or whatevef is popular in WoW now. So that means quests have to be designed with that in mind.
