    Filan gets it. While mine and this are classical utilitarian arguments [1], keep in mind that the math changes if there exists nonfinite resources. It is because of the existence of a finite amount of resources as a constraint that choosing less effective usage of those resources ends up being greater overall harm for some given time and population size values. Also keep in mind that the outcome may change if the specific time frame or population size changes.

    So while no one should want to kill granny the reality is that her remaining potential production to society is almost always going to be less than the kid.

    [1] There are some interesting things about this philosophy: the way it is originally laid out is described in terms that include math that did not exist until the 20th century, it is inclusive of both rational and irrational factors so it is not intrinsically hostile towards theology as its detractors claimed.

    All outcomes can be precomputed and described in a way that is programmable and it is unambiguous, it is dynamic, only relies on two variables for any calculation, and is entirely evidence based. So as new information is known the outcomes can account for it.

    Compared to most theologies which are static and end up being the source of additional harm because of that, it is the most compassionate way to make decisions.

    The accounting for both mysticism and science is tremendously advantageous because human beings are always irrational as a whole and mysticism or beliefs are important for keeping an organization effective.

    It's only when beliefs stand in the way of reducing our ignorance that they become harmful.

    Originally Posted by Schezar
    Driving is a privilege, not a right like guns are. Technology changes, and that has been forced in the past.
    There won't be a "human drivers off the road after XX date". That law would be horrendously skewed towards people with money.

    There WILL, however, be reduced premiums on car insurance if you have a self-driving car. This won't happen right off the bat, but once the insurance companies have a look at the raw data (and believe you me, they process data like madmen), they will do just that.
    I even see incentive programs.

    Originally Posted by FilanFyretracker
    I still think the first roads to go "auto pilot" only would be freeways.
    Won't happen. No politician who wants to be re-elected is going to suggest that. "Think of the children" is going to clash with "I can't get to work!".

    The self-driving cars are going to be in self-driving mode on freeways first, obviously, since freeways have the least amount of variables.
    Volvo has a project starting in the Swedish city of Gothenburg and in parts of China in 2017.
    The article doesn't say so, but the experiment is on "select roads" in the cities - meaning freeways equipped with extra sensors/markers for the autonomous cars.

    Upgrading infrastructure like that is probably not free, and considering the state of [un]repair that the NORMAL US highway infrastructure is in, I wouldn't get a car which needs that kind of extra infrastructure.

    Once we have better, road-tested sensors on cars - then the shift will happen. But right now we need several Petabytes worth of real-life data to find the problem areas.
    The labs have done just about all they can.
