    Re: The Big Bang Theory

    Originally Posted by Yaxa
    As far as BBT's (lack of) character development goes, isn't that par for the course for sitcoms? I can't say that I've ever watched a lot of them, but I don't recall any of them ever standing out in that department. BBT has actually been pretty good at getting in new regular cast members and rotating guests. If I had to change anything I'd put Leonard into a relationship with someone other than Penny.
    Just to make a semantic distinction, when I say "character development" I mean narrative intended to convey details about characters. This is different from static vs. dynamic characters.

    Sitcoms tend to have plenty of character development (sometimes even for mundane or shallow characters). But sitcom characters also tend to be very static. If they change, they either change very slowly or in isolated ways that aren't of long-term importance. A dynamic character, you put into a conflict situation to watch them learn from it. A static character, you put them into a conflict situation to watch them react.

    Tim Taylor on Home Improvement was a dynamic character for a sitcom, typically learning a fairly simple lesson in each episode. Although many of his flaws were recurring and he had to learn similar lessons multiple times, to say nothing about a number of flat-out static traits that never changed, Tim's self-improvement was a regular, major component of the show.

    Costanza on Seinfeld was a very static character. Although he evolved over time, character evolution (ie CharacterizationMarchesOn) isn't the same as dynamic narrative growth. One of Costanza's features is that his anger is comical. Watching him get all upset is funny. So the show would throw unfair situations at him so he could sputter humorously. If you don't find Costanza's anger funny, you probably won't appreciate those kind of episodes. Or, his laziness is funny: they'd put him in a situation where he'd apply that laziness in unique and unexpected ways. If you don't find bizarre laziness funny, you probably won't like those episodes either. Etc.

    So the question is, when you have a character like Leonard whose core flaws are low self-confidence and obsession with Penny, is the show going to be about him gaining confidence, even if it's in baby steps, or is it going to be about throwing him in a variety of situations where his trait of low self-confidence leads him to engage in cringingly awkward behavior that people will find funny. My impression is that it's the latter: Leonard is a mostly static character we're supposed to laugh at because he's pathetic.

    I don't mind if people find it funny. I just have no interest in watching it.
