Saturday, August 21st

"Uh, Officer? I don't mean to objectify you, but would you mind posing for a photo with my sister?"

Yes, yes. I was finally pulled over for speeding, but as anyone who drives with me can tell you, once in 3400 miles ain't too shabby.The cop was extremely polite and far more sociable than anyone we had met in the last two thousand miles, unlike the scary-as-fuck Connecticut state troopers. I can almost begin to understand fantasies about being pulled over by police officers. Not fully, though, since men in uniform give me the creeps. Actually, anyone in uniform gives me the creeps. Especially girl scouts.

And yes, I really did say those exact words. What the hell is LIDAR anyway?

This really was a weird fucking day. We tried to see the Mormon temple, just because I thought it would be a hoot. The brochure about Salt Lake city provided by AAA was strangely tongue in cheek about the father of the Mormons and his angel, Moroni. Suffice it to say, sometimes religious persecution is a good thing.

We had 253 exits before Zion National park, all through flat, flat-as-fuck land covered in scrub.As our cop said, "The biggest accident on this highway is that the land is so boring drivers fall asleep at the wheel." Sometimes we'd see mountains with big white letters on them for the town nearbye. More often we would see thunderstorms, hundreds of miles out, which we wound up driving through.

We stopped at a rest stop where a toothless not so old woman begged for food or money for her and four teenage sons, who were working on their old white car, trying to get it to run. I gave Sabrina five dollars to give to her, and the old woman asked if I was her mother.

We drove into Kolob Canyon, which was spectacular, but not Zion, as we found out belatedly. It was perfectly silent with no people and huge-ass red mountains and rock formations.

Knowing Lexi (that's her Vegas name) would kick my ass, we still decided to take the time and do a quick drive into Zion, which was definitely worth it. Of all the National Parks we've been to this trip, this was the most spectacular. It wasn't just the mile long tunnel carved into the rock. It wasn't just the world's largest freestanding arch carved naturally into orange rock. It wasn't just the bright red buttes and weird stone shapes.

It was a small bowl, on the other side of the tunnel, filled with pink sand soft as powder. It was the dead silence and ancient sounding wind. It was silver desert driftwood and twisted old trees. It was half of the sky blue with white puffy clouds, the other half black with thunderheads and lightening that didn't so much strike as drop down like quicksilver from the sky.

We were very sad to leave.

Now, I have to admit, I have really not been looking forward to going to Vegas. I don't like gambling or talking to the type of men that go to Vegas (no offense, Ken; you know what I mean). My mind was full of silence and nature, as cheesey as that sounds, and I had no desire to return to civilization, especially one that involves neon, slot machines, and really ugly white people.

But it's my friend Lexi's (that's her Vegas name) birthday.

HAPPY BIRTHDAY ALEXIS!

So we ate dinner in the strangely San Franciscan town of Zion, a civilized meal of pasta and espresso. The sunset on the way out was spectacular, and we saw what I think was a moonbow: a pale milky light arch that stretched across the sky between the halfmoon and the sunset.

Driving to Vegas at night was trippy: there were large black mountains and canyons all around us, barely visible against the black sky, a clear moon and stars, thunderstorms on left and right racing against us to Sin City. It was like something out of the Dream Quest of Unknown Kaddath, a thousand apologies to the Old Gent and Tyler if I have spelled it wrong.

Although we were exhausted we went out with Lexi and Jennifer, part of which involved a birthday toast in the Voodoo Lounge at the top of the Rio, on the roof watching more thunderstorms.

So that wasn't too bad.

Maybe - just maybe - me backhanding the guy who grabbed me was, however.

Friday, August 20th - - - - - - - - - - - -- - - - - - - - Yellowstone to Salt Lake City

"Hey, Sabrina, look: Idaho!"

"Yes, yes you are."

Note: We have been getting a lot of enquiries on exactly what type of ethnicity the people were on the bus at Yellowstone that Sabrina and I appreciated. We'll tell you this much: not Canadian. That includes all Canadian sub-ethnicities.

Travis was not our waiter again at the Three Bears. We saw Old Faithful first thing, waiting around in the white hot sun on white hot pavement behind benches covered with all sorts of ugly tourists, of all ethnicities. I guess it was spectacular, but like Episode 1, greatly overhyped. As it sputtered down and out one little girl cried in dismay, "Dad! Old Faithful is losing its faithfulness!"

In the same tradition of the lack of any info at Rushmore about why he did it, the Yellowstone brochure describing its lakes as tranquilizing, and not a single ranger knowing what the little beeping rodent I saw was, there was nary a sign saying how often Old Faithful blew.

We drove south through Yellowstone and then through the Grand Tetons, which like Shoshone quite frankly kicked Yellowstone's ass. We saw a badger crossing the road, and were once again honked at by an idiot local who couldn't figure out how to pull around us on an empty highway when we stopped to look at the animal.

We stopped for lunch in Jackson Hole after countless stops on the mountains for construction. It is a pity we didn't get a shot of the cowboy hat wearing old truckdriver who was nibbling huckleberries off the branch like a ground squirrel, but Sabrina did manage to capture a moment out of time, an old dude in a pink shirt stretching.

Jackson Hole was yuppie to the max and hard to navigate through. Also, there were antlers every where. Goth and interesting, but a little dissonant next to the Polo Ralph Lauren outlet.

People drive the speed limit exactly in Idaho.

Idaho was lumpy little green potato hills and then flat stretches of 65 mph highway interrupted often by 35 mph noxious little poor towns. In almost every town their cop car would see our NY plates and follow us carefully, at a distance, until we were gone. I can only imagine what it must be like driving cross country when you're African American.

My mind was wiped once again by a strenuous drive through Cache National forest, which was beautiful and smelled strongly of pine, but again hard to navigate and a little too damn slow. My biggest problem on the trip has been not being able to look around, forced to keep my eyes on the road.

We drove around Bear Lake, an incredibly blue/green thing with rich developments on the shore and frighteningly poor houses just across the street in the fields. There was even an old shuttered up victorian, looking like it was going blow down in the wind.

We stopped for dinner at a restaurant called Country Grill where everyone was blond and people actually turned their heads to stare at us. As you may have been able to guess by reading, we grow more and more irritable at this treatment.

The best was after we checked into the Salt Lake City Ramada, however. It had a central area with bar, pool, pool tables, lounge, ping pong and a hot tub. It was in this hot tub we actually talked to other people, who told us flat out they hated New York, and that they were 'just country people.'

My friend Matt emailed me saying that when he lived out midwest if a shot of New York was shown during a movie everyone booed.

Summary of the midwest: Beautiful land. Really annoying people.

Thursday, August 19th - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - Yellowstone!

"Uh, you uh, girls, uh, finished with yer, uh, coffee? Have you had enough? What? You want more? Uh, yer, uh, gonna have to make a big ol', uh, pit stop later,"

-Travis

(Our server at The Three Bears Restaurant and Lounge)

A word or two here about our observations on Midwesterners. Our entire life Sabrina and I have been making fun of them because that's just what you do, it's taken for granted on the east coast. We thought that would change when we actually visited them and spoke to them and realized what prejudiced assholes we had been. Here is what we learned:

  • They are all very blond.
  • They all really hate New York.
  • They can't drive for shit.

These are just the basics of course. We could add how odd it is that every restaurant we've been to out here puts a carafe of icewater on the table first thing. Or how when you ask for the check to be brought with the meal 'cause you're in a hurry they look at you with confused, big eyes. Or how they can only drive in the straight lines that God and their D.O.T. gave them; without fail whenever I pulled over to the side of the road, signal or blinkers on, the car behind me would just sit there, even if there was no traffic coming the other way and they had twenty feet of road on the other side of me. They begin to honk and then swear as I sit, confused with big eyes, unable to understand why they don't just pull around. Also, the church-to-coffeeshop/restaurant ratio out here is higher than in the Vatican.

And I'm not kidding about the New York thing. If a waiter or local asks us smilingly where we are from and we tell them the truth, we get scowls and the silent treatment. It got so bad by the end of this day that we resorted to telling people we were from Rhode Island and speaking in a made-up clicking language to each other whenever it looked like someone was going to talk to us.

But back to nature's real beauty, which has nothing to do with the human inhabitants of the area.

We blew our minds out with light and heat by going to Mammoth Springs first, at the top of the park, near the entrance. It became as weird as a Flannery O'Conner short; desolate with funny looking tourists and a sun that ate our brains out. Basically, they were pretty hot springs out of white travertine with bacteria-stained blue, red, and green crusts. We ate some ice cream to try to cool off and reawaken our heads but nothing occurred until we stopped at a river and dipped our feet in far from the maddening crowd.

Then we found a rock bowl which Sabrina climbed around on while I sat in the shade, and a golden marmot actually crawled out on the rock next to me to see what was going on. Also, one of those little beeping things that no ranger could identify came out and, uh, beeped at me. And then an old biker drove up, clad in chaps with spurs chinkling in the dust, and he laid out on his hawg with a cigarette in one hand a map of the park in the other. It was a pretty magical moment, all things concerned.

(Sabrina theorizes that this is where all the cowboys have gone; they became Hell's Angels)

Feeling much better we proceeded to the Norris Geysers, which were beautiful but smelled bad. Some of the pools were an intense acquamarine that defy description. No wait, I just did describe it. They definitely defied my light meter, though, which is why we have no pictures.

Finally we made our way to Fountain Paint Pot, a sort of a 'good' version of the Bog of Eternal Stench, all white and bubbling and smelling foul. We also met the Bacteria Mat(t), whom I got a picture with since he/they seemed to be the nicest of the locals we met. There were sprays of steam and strange bleached bones strewn around the think white thermal crust. A thunderstorm was coming in, too.

Creepy, no?

On our way out of the park my freakish eyes spotted a pair of trumpeter swans, which I have wanted to see since I read the book by E.B. White. I was rapturous, staring through my binoculars for a very long time. White with black beaks and feet! Just the way I imagined them, too.

"No, Sabrina. Taking my clothes off, snorting Mr. Clean and running back and forth like a trapped animal in my tiny apartment is not the same thing as running naky through the woods."

Ok, we've heard a couple complaints that this log is two days behind. Excuse us! Phonelessness at the Bates Motel took us out for one night and we've been too, I don't know, exhausted to fully catch up. From travelling and all. A thousand apologies. We'll buy you ice cream when we get back. Actually, we're just glad someone cares.

Two big rules we should have obeyed when visiting the mother of all National Parks:

1. Don't go to Bear Country USA first to see in captivity up close all the animals you would die to see wild in Yellowstone.

2. Don't take route 212/the Beartooth Loop or you'll wear out your capacity for wonderment thirty miles before you ever hit Yellowstone.

As for Shoshone and Custer Parks... Mighty Fuck! I didn't know mountains got that big. I mean Christ on a rubber crutch the land was unbelievable. Sure you can call me lowbrow for resorting to cheap four letter words, but if I said I was speechless with the inherent majesty of the land it would sound boring and lame.

From down in the canyon we couldn't see the top, but we saw a road above us and were envious. Suddenly we realized that was what our road became, and we could see the mountains became flat, and we were puzzled. Finally we were at the top, driving on top of these huge alpine fields, plateaus, like land that was replaced half a mile above where it should be. Fields of flowers and snow and these strange little chinchilla-like creatures that beeped. No, I did not take any out with my bootknife. I said they were like chinchillas.

At one point we just got out and sat and stared for a long time. At another point we went down a flower-covered prairie to get a closer look at a beautiful mere. Sabrina made fun of my random hand clapping and shouting Bear Bear at first, claiming that bears like forest, not fields. I was however justified when we got back up to the road again and saw a sign we had missed warning that it was grizzly country.

Once again, a hell of a lot of Harleys going up and down the mountain.

By the time we descended the other side into a glorious golden valley replete with sparkling pebble-strewn rivers, we were, uh, done with the day. We were tired and blinded by the snow and beauty, and found ourselves pushing past some of Yellowstone's attractions at high speed. We didn't even get out for one waterfall, and the only reason we paused at the famous Tower Junction Waterfall was because of a busload of, um, really cute guys of a certain ethnicity that the Braswell sisters both appreciate.

Yeah, we saw bison and geysers and elk and huge canyons with weirdly polygonal calcite formations and suddenly too goddamn many people. It was hard after Wyoming and Shoshone.

And I've never experienced road rage in a national park before... Late sunset, black mountains, purple sky, halfmoon, one planet above, and this idiot is going under the speed limit. I fumed helplessly until I remembered my three weapons: chutzpah, rage, and an almost fanatical devotion to the Pope. No, wait...

Anyway, I tailgated him until I got to passing zone.

Don't get me wrong. Yellowstone is freakily fantastic. But for sheer, people-less this-land-fucking-rocks incredibleosity, Shoshone outshone it.

Monday, August 16th - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - Sioux Falls to Rapid City    

"I didn't think less of you when you didn't climb to the top. Please don't think less of me for not using that port-o-john."

APOLOGIES TO OUR FAITHFUL READERS FOR LACK OF UPDATE YESTERDAY. We managed to find the only AAA certified hotel in Rapid City without a phone. Hence, no upload.

Yeah, and more on that. The only reason it wasn't the Bates Motel was because (as Sabrina pointed out) we were in South Dakota, not California. Scary as shit psycho central. Anyone remember the Cereal Convention issue of Sandman?

We crammed in a little bit too much today for any sane mind to handle comfortably. The yellow sign was probably in a corn crop circle and Yog Sothoth has nothing on midwest tourist traps, for instance.

(hey there, natives, we'll take Manhattan for 24 bucks, and move a bit westward...a friendly word or two and suddenly we've fenced in all the fucking prairies and stuck signs up everywhere that say things like WallDrug and Reptile Garden and Cosmos Mysteries: It's Crazy!)

We started with prairie, which was so unbelievably alien to us eastcoasters that we stopped multiple times to take photos. I have to say, I used to think the midwest would come to me at night only in the form of nightmares, but this land was like something out of half-remembered dreams, the kind you wake up from with a sad but eerily exalted feeling. White light, infinite grass.

Then we went to the Corn Palace. All that can really be said about it is that corn architectural technology seems to have declined since the original Corn Palace in 1892, which had spires and onion domes. The one today just has a weird Logan's Run-ish gift shop in the middle of an auditorium, with tired fat white people sitting in front of a giant tv/vcr gone all blue from rewind, waiting to learn about the wonders of corn.

We skipped the enchanted doll world and the balloon museum, pausing just long enough to snap a photo of a sign which said Jabba The Hutt Buys His Garage Doors Here.

We renewed our souls next to a field of sunflowers stretching on to the ends of the earth. We also got horribly tan and freckled, the prairie sun burnng the east coast out, I guess.

Our main stop that day, the one I had been looking forward to since New Jersey, was the Badlands. Holy bric-a-brac of Christ was it beautiful and unusual. If you took all the weird rock formations in a Roadrunner cartoon and squished them together in one small area, there you would have it. As sunset approached they even took on that dreamy orange pink color.

Being adults we exercised our perogative in expensive capricious whimsy, in this case a helicopter ride over the Badlands in a tiny white dragonfly version of an army copter. It kicked ass. We then proceeded to rejoice in our guardianless state by climbing all over the formations, teetering on ledges and going higher than the biker guys did.

We drove out of the Badlands at sunset, just beginning to grasp the concept of the Big Sky, and dined in Walldrug that evening. Thank you Ken and Scott for the recommendation. For people who have been, you know what we're talking about. For those who haven't, you just have to go and see it for yourself. Imagine Disneyland without the rides or the franchise.

We drove like mofos to Rapid City, catching a glimpse of a fantastic sputtering meteor which arced across the night.

The pancakes were most excellent.

Paul and John showed us around their house and yard - it was generally a kickass time. We returned the favor by presenting them with 720 Swedish fish.

The land outside Chicago is hot, flat, and ugly - for an hour there it looked like my agoraphobia was about to screamingly kick in. Then clouds began to pass, huge clouds, sky covering clouds. And the sky out here really is big. 270 degrees of clouds, different formations everywhere you looked, like gazing up from the bottom of a snowglobe.

And the land began to change. For several hours we passed through Homer Winslow light in a more rolling landscape: fields and picture perfect barns, silos twenty miles apart or more. The land began to unflatten itself more, and the trees were dark and square grey rectangles of stone would occasionally stand up against the sky. Whereas yesterday was all Bowie all the time, 13 hours of cds, tapes, and singalongs, this afternoon we were silent. A sort of serenity settled over the car and we ate up the miles like Grays hot dogs.

After hundreds of miles of signs for Wisconsin cheeses and Amish stuff, we pulled over in LaCrosse to see what local life was like, for dinner. Local life was dead, Lutheran, or both, as evidenced by the empty streets and multitude of cemeteries. LOTS of beer halls. A lawn of white crosses which whimsically reminded me of the cruciforms in Hyperion turned out to be an 'abortion' faux burial ground. We did not tarry in LaCrosse, but did stay long enough to eat at one of those diners where a waitress rollerskates out and puts your order on a tray, on your car door. It pretty much ruled. The sky was grey and rain fell now and again. It felt a lot like in New England at Thanksgiving except for all of the antiabortion signs.

At night we picked up even more speed - the limit is 75 in Minnesota. We drove to the moon, which hung in the sky like a cartoon of itself, a crescent unbelievably bright and alone. The stars in the big sky were as they say too numerous to count and heat lightening followed us on the south until we hit Sioux Falls. For hundreds of miles we were the only car. The gas stations are well equipped, clean, and provide every amenity imagined by man.

Saturday, August 14th

"Fuck the road. I'm concerned about our immortal souls now."

Oooookay... Maybe we were pushing the hour a bit when Liz thought the car behind us had turned into a gecko and was concerned that the lovely architecture of Chicago incorporated a gigantic stone Satan and his little fat buddy. There were horns on those buildings. Sure.

AAA said it would take us 12 hours to Chicago. Of course, AAA didn't factor in constant construction, rain, and one or two accidental scenic routes in Ohio. Nor did it precog the fact that when Paul said he lived in Chicago, he really meant in the 'burbs an hour north.

Note to all future xcountrymen: there are no scenic routes in Ohio.
