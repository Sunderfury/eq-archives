Kingdom of Ak'Anon Parliament
 
The Parliament is made up of a number of permanent bodies:
  • Synod
  • Prime Regent
  • House of Lords
  • Speaker of the House
  • Watchman Committee
  • Watchman of the House

In addition to the permanent bodies of Parliament, there will be a number of other bodies/committees as the current Parliamentary bodies see fit. It is also not uncommon for committees to be set up to examine various circumstances.
 
Synod
The Synod is made up of members elected from various regions of the Ak'Anon Kingdom. A member of the Synod has a title of Regent.

The Synod have several responsibilities. It plays a major part in the process that makes new laws, debating and looking in detail at proposals for new legislation. The Synod also has a responsibility to scrutinize the work of the Government and is able to question all Government representatives (including the Prime Regent) about the work they do. It is rare but not unheard of for the Synod to question members of the House of Lords.

Unlike the House of Lords, the Synod looks at financial work, and must approve spending and taxation.

The Synod is an elected body, with a number of members elected from each region:

  • City State of Ak'Anon: 4
  • Upper Steamfont Province: 3
  • Lower Steamfont Province: 2
  • Lesser Faydark Territory: 1
  • Solusek's Eye Colony: 1

Following election, the Synod will appoint an elected member to the position of Prime Regent (see below for responsibilities).

The elected term for each Regent and the Prime Regent is to be determined by the first elected Synod. Every member holds the position for the elected term, or until retirement, resignation, or has been removed by impeachment. If a position becomes vacant, it remains vacant until filled by a regional election.
Prime Regent
The Prime Regent is a member of the Synod and has been appointed by the Synod. The Prime Regent's duties include:

  • Representing the Kingdom at the Union of Faydwer, and selecting a member from each of the Synod and the House of Lords to also attend as advisors.
  • Representing the Kingdom during a gathering of the Collective of Old Fennin. Depending on the rules for the meeting of the Collective, the Prime Regent may have to select a member from the Synod and the House of Lords as advisors.
  • To act as an ambassadorial representative or to appoint such for all other circumstances. (It is common for the Prime Regent to appoint an Emissary).


House of Lords
The House of Lords is considered the elders of the community. This does not mean that to be a member of the House of Lords one should have obtained a great level through the use of combat. It is more that a member is respected by those that they represent, and is considered an expert in their discipline.

In general, the functions of the House of Lords are similar to those of the Synod in legislating, debating topics of the day and questioning the Executive. The main exception is that they are not involved in matters of taxation and finance. The role of the Lords is generally recognized to be complementary to that of the Synod and it acts as a revising chamber for many of the more important and controversial bills.

The House of Lords has members representing each of the following groups:

  • Eldrich Collective: Enchanters
  • Eldrich Collective: Magicians
  • Dark Reflection: Necromancers
  • Eldrich Collective: Wizards
  • Abbey of Deep Musing: Clerics
  • Abbey of Deep Musing: Rogues
  • Gemchoppers: Warriors
  • Ak'Anon Order of Engineers: Tinkers
  • Possibly other trades (Mining, Jewelcraft, Blacksmithing, etc).
  • Each Guild that calls Ak'Anon home

A member of the House of Lords has a privilege of title, such as: Lord Fulcrum of the Ak'Anon Order of Engineers [used purely as an example :) ]

Each member of the House of Lords represents the group of citizens that consider themselves under the Lord's title. If a non-guild Lord belongs to a Guild, the Lord must act in the best interest of the Lord's position and not the Guild. For example, the Lord of the Gemchoppers may belong to another guild, but while attending to the duties of Parliament, the Lord must shed all allegiances to the guild and focus on the Lord’s designated duties.

If it is deemed that the Lord has not been able to focus on their position, then the Lord may be removed from the House. All Guild titles must be hidden unless the member is directly representing a Guild.

Each member is either appointed (in the case of Guilds) or elected by members of the group from which the member will represent. If only a single citizen is interested in the position, then an election need not be held. Every member holds the position for life, or until retirement, resignation, or has been removed by impeachment. If a position becomes vacant, the members of the House determine which member will fill the vacancy until either appointment or election fills it.
Speaker of the House
The Speaker acts as a Chairman during debates, and sees that the rules laid down by the House for the carrying on of its business are observed. It is the Speaker who selects (or calls) Members to speak. He or she acts as the House’s representative in its relations with outside bodies and the other elements of Parliament and the Crown. It is also the Speaker who reprimands on behalf of the House an offender brought to the Bar. The Speaker is also responsible for protecting the interests of minorities in the House.

It is obviously essential that debates should be conducted in an orderly way, and unlikely that the course of debate would flow smoothly if there were not some way of regulating who was to speak. The selection of those who are to speak is therefore a very important one for the Chair, and one that has to be handled with tact and discretion. In a debate, official spokesmen for both sides take part, as well as those Members with constituency interests, those who specialize in the subject under discussion and those simply with a general interest. The Speaker, therefore, has to balance all these requirements when working out who should be called. Members may press their claims for being called in advance of particular debates, but the decision rests solely with the Speaker.

The Speaker must preserve order in the House, and ensure that its rules of debate are observed. For instance, a Member who makes an allegation against another, or expresses himself in language that the rules do not permit, may be directed to withdraw the remark concerned. In the case of grave general disorder, the sitting may have to be suspended. In the case of willful disobedience by one or more particular Members the Speaker can name him or them, which will result in their suspension - for a period - from the House.

The Speaker has to protect the rights of minorities in the House. He or she must ensure that the holders of an opinion, however unpopular, are allowed to put across their point of view without undue obstruction. This is especially important when deciding whether or not to allow the closure to be moved - that is, whether a debate can be brought to a close. If minorities have not been able to make a contribution, this will weigh with the Speaker in assessing whether the closure may be moved.

The Speaker must, of course, be above political controversy and must be seen to be completely impartial in all public matters. All sides in the House rely on the Speaker's disinterest and respect that he or she must stand aside from controversy.

The Speaker is also responsible for recording the events that occur while in the House is in session, either personally or by appointing a clerk.

The King is by right the Speaker of both the Synod and the House of Lords. However, it is rare that the King exercises this right. Therefore, both the Synod and the House of Lords may appoint their Speaker. The Speaker of the Synod and the Speaker of the House of Lords may be the same individual, or may be distinct. In either case, the Speaker then chooses two deputies (upon approval of the House), who may stand-in on occasion for the Speaker, and at least one should be present with the Speaker while the House is in session as council.
Common Responsibilities and Privileges for the Synod and the House of Lords
All bills go through both the Synod and the House of Lords before becoming Acts, and may start in either House. Normally, the consent of the Lords is required before Acts of Parliament can be passed, and the Lords can amend all legislation, with the exception of bills to raise taxation, long seen as the responsibility of the Synod. Amendments have to be agreed to by both Houses (throughout this section, House refers both to the Synod and the House of Lords). The Lords are as active as the Synod in amending bills.

Parliament has rights and immunities to protect it from obstruction in carrying out its duties. These include:

  • freedom of speech
  • first call on the attendance of members of Parliament, who are therefore free from arrest in civil actions and exempt from serving on juries, or being compelled to attend court as witnesses
  • Access to the Crown, which is a collective privilege of the House.

The most important privilege is that of freedom of speech. As members of Parliament cannot be prosecuted for sedition or charged with libel or slander over anything said during proceedings in the House, this enables them to raise in the House questions affecting the public good which might be difficult to raise outside owing to the possibility of being charged.

Parliament has the right to punish anybody, inside or outside of the House, who commits a breach of privilege - that is, offends against the rights of Parliament. A Member of Parliament complaining of an alleged breach of privilege must submit the case privately to the Speaker, who then decides whether the matter can be raised in the Chamber itself. If the Speaker rules in Parliament that a case appears to have been established, the matter takes precedence over all other business. If, when summoned an offender admits the offence but offers a full apology, the Speaker often recommends that it be accepted. The House then considers the Speaker's report and decides whether or not the offender should be punished.

Parliament also claims the right to punish 'contempt' of the House - that is, any offence or libel against its dignity or authority. An offending MP may be reprimanded, suspended, or in extreme cases, expelled from the House. Offenders who are not MPs are brought to the House by the Watchman of the House - one of the officers of the House - where they are reprimanded by the Speaker.
Ceremonies and Conventions in the Houses
When either house is in session, the Speaker of the House is at the front (with deputies on either side), and the members file in to fill the perimeter on either side in front of the Speaker. The Watchman of the House (see below) stands opposite the Speaker at the other end of the hall, just past the last member, and faces the Speaker. Observers may fill in the Common Gallery, which is located behind the Watchman of the House.

Upon the opening of the session, the Speaker will ask the Watchman to step forward. The Watchman will then present the Crown Torch to the Speaker and return to position. The Speaker will light the Torch and hold it for the remainder of the session.

During the session, if a Member wishes to get the attention of the Speaker, the Member will bow to the Speaker once. The Speaker will then choose the appropriate time to query the Member for the requested action. When the Speaker has given a Member permission to speak, the Member (and only the permitted Member) will hold a lit Torch. When the Member has finished his or her speech, or the Speaker has asked the Member to stop, the Member will put the Torch away.

At the end of the session, the Watchman of the House will obtain the Crown Torch from the Speaker, and return it to the Crown.

There are two possible places for the Houses to meet: a small room at the Royal Palace, and in the open hall at the Abbey of Deep Musing.
Watchman Committee
The Watchman Committee is charged with the policing of Ak'Anon's provinces, territories and colonies. Any citizen of the Kingdom may belong to the Watchman Committee, and the Senior Watchman is appointed by the Synod, with approval by the House of Lords. It is expected that all Guilds will contribute members to the Watchman Committee.

The Watchman Committee may also be called upon to aid foreign nations upon occasion.
Watchman of the House
The Watchman of the House is present during sessions of both the Synod and the House of Lords. Very often the Watchman of the House is the Senior Watchman, or one of his deputies. The Watchman of the House reports directly to the Speaker of the House during the session, and is responsible for ensuring that the Speaker’s requests are followed. Duties may include the removal of a member of the House for the session, clearing the Common Gallery of spectators, and ensuring that safe transport of the Torch to and from the Speaker. The Watchman of the House always acts under the direction of the Speaker of the House.
Official Dress
When in session, all members of Parliament will dress according to their station. No headdress may be worn (i.e.: helms or caps) and no weapons or shields may be held. The hands must be free of all items at all times, except as stipulated by Parliamentary convention.

The Watchman of the House is the only member permitted to bare arms during a session. The Watchman must always be helmed, if appropriate, and have a spear, pike, or staff in hand. A shield should not be presented.
