Playing for fun -OR- The absurdity of long camps

[ Follow Ups ] [ Post Followup ] [ Message Board - EQ Cleric ]

Posted by Greggery Peccary on October 15, 1999 at 12:35:49:

To me, the purpose of playing any game, including EQ, is to
have fun. This appears to be self-evident; yet somehow there is no
sign that this is recognized by the staff at Verant.

To have fun, one general requirement is that something should
be happenning. Waiting, almost by definition, is not fun.

However, it seems that the long-term goal of a long list of
updates and patches that I have seen, since I started to
play in April, is to increase the time that people are waiting;
or alternatively, travelling, which is essentially waiting,
in motion, to arrive at a destination (often one's corpse).
I think that in general this can be described as not fun.


Things that I would consider fun, in no particular order,
are:

[
1) Fighting nasties, especially when the odds are against you,
or at least in a situation where it's not a guarantee that
you will win. (The thrill of battle)

2) Developing your character, with equipment, experience, and
role-playing. (levelling)

3) Discovering new and interesting things, and exploring new
and interesting areas. (learning and puzzle-solving)

4) Interacting with other players in ways that help all
parties involved.

5) Engaging in a semi-realistic fantasy world and being a
hero.
]

All of these things were the apparent strengths of EQ
when I first started to play. Then I learned that to
do or get anything that was not mundane and common (and
worthless), you really need to look to the WWW to get
any information at all on how to survive. Learning the
normal way people learn, by making mistakes and figuring
out what you did wrong, is just not practical in EQ.
Exploration and discovery are best done through a web
browser, not through the eyes of your character, even if
you are a druid with SoW. In short, the information-starvation
strategy embarked on by Verant forced the creation of
web sites to spoil the in-game discovery process.

Then I learned about camping. At first, I thought camping
was simply sitting down at a spot that gave good loot or
xp (preferably both), and working with a team to best the
creatures in the area, to everyone's advantage. Further
experience showed this to be the rarest of camping strategies;
the most common strategy was to sit and wait for a particular
creature which had something you wanted - a result of the
proliferation of web sites which now told you where you
could get the stuff you wanted. Hence, the result of the
information-starvation campaign was to destroy Reason Playing is
Fun #3 above.

I guess the final blow to really having fun is the rare
spawn with the essential item concept (especially cruel
when combined with no-drop). Usually by the time you are
able to attempt these things the rare spawn is too green
to even require any rest after killing it.

The archetypical example of this is the Drelzna/jboots
spawn. IMHO, the jboots should not even be in the game,
they have such a dramatic effect on game balance. But
they were there, creating one of the strongest incentives
in the game to camp. The first time I attempted this camp
really drove home to me how absurd and not-fun camping is.
Maybe it was having to bribe someone to open the door. More
likely it was the utterly desperate warrior who had been
camping the spawn for 13 STRAIGHT HOURS and not gotten his
prize. I'm not sure if I can accurately describe the shock
that this poor guy had been through 13 hours of: 15 min wait,
20 secs of heart-pounding action, disappointment at not getting
prize, repeat - all the while surrounded by people who would
try to steal the kill every time, and the generally
unhelpful attitude of people waiting on line hoping for
people ahead of the to get frustrated and leave. Perhaps
this situation is worse than most camps, but I would not
want to spend 2 hours in this state, forget about six or more.

I could further present my analysis, but I have other things
to do at the moment (maybe I continue another time). It is
clear to me that camping violates every one of the rules
above on what is fun. Broken quests, empty or incomplete
zones, long spawn times, and rare drops all make the problem
worse not better. And the trend of updates, it seems to
me, have universally been dedicated to increasing the time
wasted camping. If they are trying to make the spawns so rare that
camping is not worth it, that is a failed strategy, as it only
makes the items more valuable.

I look forward to your impressions.
