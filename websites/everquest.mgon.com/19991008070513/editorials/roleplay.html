A Treatise On Roleplaying And You
This editorial was written by me, Mykil of the EVQCenter Staff. Any comments should be sent to me at mhykl@evqcenter.com. This piece is written primarily for use with EverQuest, but most of it is general enough to be applied to other games.

Roleplaying - What is it?
Roleplaying is a strange thing. It is not as obscure and vague in its meaning as some people would have (and prefer for their own advantages) you believe, but it is still a tough concept to describe. It is one of those things where everyone has his or her own view on it, like time management, and only a few people out of a big crowd share the same opinion on how it should be done.

The basic ideal behind roleplaying is a relatively simple thing to understand. The basic operating concept of roleplay is a person takes on the persona of another person who could be totally fictional, or the fictional rendition of a real life character. That is the core idea that makes roleplay function. Yet it is this idea that gives rise to the questions and obscurities that make roleplaying seem so vague.

That said, the main concept that is the action of actually roleplaying is a very tricky subject. What exactly is roleplaying? The core concept of roleplay is easily understood, but that does little to describe the actual act of roleplay and roleplaying. This is where views on the entire subject diverge, and where the most debate occurs.

Some people define roleplaying by its core concept, which is simply to take on the persona of a character and play that character based on its views and environment. The simplest version of roleplaying and definitely the most direct, this approach is the easiest to understand and its broadness lends a wide range of options for any roleplayer.

Other broad though slightly constricted views of roleplaying tend to impose certain limitations on the concept that people may or may not agree with. Some people think that the only way to truly roleplay is to truly act the part of the character, and that not doing so makes you a "bad roleplayer," a "wannabe roleplayer," or simply "a newbie," despite your level in the game. These people are among the many groups who attempt to impose their views of roleplaying upon you and everyone else in the game. Through "acting," they claim that they are "real players" and, therefore, to be a "real player," you have to be like them.

There are views of roleplaying that people use to attempt to define how certain games are played. One of the biggest and most controversial issues in Ultima Online and Diablo was whether or not player killing (and in Diablo, cheating as well) was roleplaying. Some people accused mainstream player killing of being either hate based attacks or random bullying of obviously weak players for enjoyment. In my personal opinion, it was random bullying. I have never seen a "true" player killer cite a racial, ethical, or political reason for attacking. The hate based attack reason is the result of paranoia on the part of people who are repeatedly player killed and people who want to incite a lynch mob against the player killing community as a whole. These people feel roleplaying is roleplaying as long as it conforms to their views of playing the game. Although similar to the group mentioned above, these groups attempt to tell people how the game is played, while the one in the previous paragraph attempts to frame what the act of roleplaying actually is.

However, just because those accusations are wrong doesn't make player killing "roleplaying". Both player killers of great repute and obscurity alike maintain, despite simple and obvious facts, that player killing is roleplaying when given "a reason." This weak explanation merely generated a new step to player killing: the excuse for doing it. The excuses that "I am roleplaying my character by killing you," "this is an ambush, prepare to die," or "we are murderers here to kill you and take your money," are not examples of roleplaying. The first is an excuse, plain and simple. Claiming that part of roleplaying is killing "you," especially when the odds are this person doesn't know "you," is just an excuse to kill you while attempting to make you think that their roleplaying is the way the game is played. The second excuse about the ambush is just about as superfluous as the first, making the victims think that the killing was specifically planned against them, hence creating the illusion of roleplaying to them. The third reason just doesn't make sense. First, murderers don't kill just to take your money, brigands and cutthroats and rogues do. Roleplaying a "true" murderer is much more complex than that, necessitating the planning out of the killing, a motive, and a method. Secondly, this killing is random, and even rogues and cutthroats and brigands aren't that random, only attacking those who appear to have a lot in the way of wealth and physical vulnerability to make the whole thing worth the effort. This type of random killing is manslaughter, nothing more and nothing less, and there is no roleplay value in this. I know I will get e-mails about this paragraph, mostly accusing me of hypocrisy by saying that player killing is not roleplaying and that what has been written is biased and opinionated. Everything in this paragraph is factual. Roleplayed killing does exist, but player killing is certainly not it. Pirates are roleplayers. Raiders are roleplayers. Murderers are roleplayers. Rogues are roleplayers. Player killers are not roleplayers. They are random killers who use roleplaying as an illusion in order to hide their fun at other peoples' expense. Don't confuse it with the real thing.

There are several other definitions of roleplaying, too numerous to mention here, but they all generally fall into the types previously mentioned. The closest you can get to defining the actual meaning of roleplaying is to use the basic idea, that roleplaying is taking on the role of a character. Beyond that, with all the varied opinions, it is impossible to pinpoint what the exact meaning of roleplaying is, because beyond the core concept, it is basically a matter of opinion.

You - What do you do in roleplaying?
The part you play in roleplaying is comparatively small in contrast to the big picture. I mean, you only provide the background, the views, the beliefs, the personality, the appearance (to a limited degree), the mannerisms, the vocal accent, and everything else that is your character(s). Now, that is an awful lot of work. Thank god Verant didn't allow us to customize the exact shade of hair color we have, the location of every crease and wrinkle on our bodies, and the size and shape of our fingernails, along with the entire myriad of things I didn't mention! Another new game would be out before we finished with our first character. But seriously now, besides maintaining your character's being (i.e. not lapsing into k00l d00d mode or real life mode) within the game, what more to roleplaying is there?

Plenty. Roleplaying isn't just you. Roleplaying is a group effort. Some people will argue with me here, saying, "well, in Final Fantasy 7 I roleplay, and all by my very lonesome without any other people." That type of roleplaying is roleplaying, but not the type I am speaking of here. The roleplaying I am talking about is the type of roleplaying that is needed to make a game of such a scale as EverQuest work. This is the type of roleplaying that the lack thereof is currently in the process of killing Ultima Online. If roleplaying had taken root in that game, and stuck, I probably would still be playing it as I write this. I am one of the many Ultima addicts out there who are sorry to see Origin's work plagued by both their faults (the constant bugs) and ours (those of us who are too immature to play a game of that scale). Ultima Online was destined for greatness when it was conceived, but that destiny ended the moment it was rushed to the assembly lines and the first social degenerate logged in. From there, it went downhill. A have long since bid adieu to the idea of staying in Ultima Online until its last days. She had her problems, but I knew her well.

But I learned long ago not to dwell on the past. What's done is done. The future, EverQuest, is what must be the focus of concentration now. EverQuest has been done better than its predecessors, and some of its contemporaries. It can be called a large MUD (Multi User Dungeon), but it is really much more. A living breathing world, like Brittania should have been, and even more than that. Its incomplete. More land is going to be added in the form of the missing continents, new worlds, and new dimensions that have only been told of in legends. New classes and races are in the process of being planned (or being held back for expansions). MUD tricks, such as the name filter which ensures people name themselves names that conform with "high fantasy" (to prevent Norrath from ever seeing "Duke Butthead" and "Sir Beavis" taint its soil with their foul beings) and which guarantee that no two people share the same name (to make sure nobody can impersonate others and keep identity confusion minimum), greatly reduce some of the problems that other ORPGs have faced, and help to clarify what roleplaying is. The random name generator, for example, can show people what names in Norrath should be, as opposed to the alternative.

Roleplaying is more than tricks to ensure what people name themselves. It is what gives a game such as EverQuest is continuity. As I said before, the roleplaying I am advocating here is a group effort. For it to work completely, everyone must chip in their bit. The part you play is small, but when in conjunction with others, the sum is much more than the part. Imagine a town in Norrath with everyone asking about the Clinton-Lewinsky scandal. Any attempt at roleplaying is ignored in favor of the issue debates and discussions. The political views you find might be interesting, but unless you are a politician, a critic, or an MSNBC (or other network) press member, the experience is not entirely fulfilling. In fact, the experience is just plain not fun. On the other hand, imagine a town where everywhere, you see roleplaying happening in all its ORPG forms. People conversing about their adventures into the mountains or their journey into the gnoll lair. Consumers and merchants haggling over the prices of character made goods. Bards plying their stock and trade singing on street corners. Comrades having drinks in a local tavern, and then stumbling into the street drunk and dumb as a donkies, muttering obscenities and getting berated by both players and NPCs alike. What happens if someone mentions the Clinton-Lewinsky scandal? Someone might say "Sorry milord, but methinks ye be mad. Who be this Clinton and Lewinsky sir?" or it might be ignored entirely. THAT is what roleplaying culminate in. It is up to you to do your part and make that vision a reality, simply because it will make the experience so much richer not only for yourself but your fellow player as well. And you stand a chance of touching those who would ruin it for everyone else, and getting them to realize it is much more fun to play along than ask about the Rangers game or the president's trial. That is what roleplaying is all about: playing along and having fun.

Unfortunately, not everyone will play along. These are among the aforementioned "Duke Butthead" and "Sir Beavis" players who go around acting like their real life television namesakes and annoying people. Some people would classify power gamers among these two, but in my opinion, power gamers are roleplayers. They want money, power, and like fighting a lot. Well, wanting money, power, and liking to fight a lot is a form of roleplaying. Its simple - I mean those things are not very depth intensive - but it is roleplaying as long as they keep it in context with the game and leave real life out of it. Back to "Beavis" and "Butthead," these two will ruin the game for anyone. Inadvertantly or intentionally, these two will not roleplay - beyond acting like imbeciles - much if at all. They will go through town using modern ebonics as opposed to the regular or old english roleplayers will opt towards, mentioning things that, as far as Norrath is concerned, do not exist, and generally inspiring players to abandon their roleplaying long enough to verbally attack them, which further damages continuity and ruins the game for more people. This is how fragile roleplaying is. It only takes one (in this case, two are the cause, but not necessary) to ruin it. That is why you must do your part to maintain the flow of things on your end. You can't control, and therefore make up for, the acts of others, but you can keep yours in control, and save some of the roleplaying flow. But rest assured that, somewhere, there is someone who is ruining it for people. You WILL meet people like this, and you have to be prepared for it, or else you might find your own experience ruined.

Another example of the fragility of roleplaying is how human error, which is unintentional, can have the same effect. All the races in Norrath are relatively the same, having similar levels of sophistication and civilization, right? WRONG! Two races, the troll and the ogre, (not to mention anything similar that Verant might have ready for expansions) are dumber than stones (one moreso than the other, but that makes no difference here). Now, lets say we have a troll whose name is "Burp." Burp goes through his troll town, smashing things, dropping pieces of his latest meal from his relatively unkempt body, grunting at passing trolls as he walks by. His days consist of smashing things, eating them, and hiding under bridges waiting to capture some billy goats gruff. :) That is a character with a good roleplaying scheme. Trolls are simplistic creatures. Rude and obnoxious, they are hardly civilized (they are a civilization, however), and they are smarter than ogres! Imagine what an ogre's routine might be! Now, lets say we have a troll whose name is "Johnas". Johnas goes through his troll town, being careful not to hurt anything, his personal cleanliness in stark contrast with his surroundings, bidding fond "how do you do's" to other trolls as he walks by. His days consist of helping the poor, giving people food and drink and healing concoctions, and having detailed debates about the politics of Norrathian nobles. Roleplayers will look at this character and get either extremely frustrated or extremely angry. Trolls are stupid. They are supposed to act that way. A troll, or worse, an ogre, asking for some grey poupon, is definitely against the image of those races. This type of "affront" as some will regard it is, in my personal opinion, not intentional (generally). It has to be hard acting "dumb" for some people. Others will do this because they are like Beavis and Butthead: they have no clue. And still others will do this to ruin the experience. But in general, it will be human error. Whatever the cause, however, I expect to see at least one ogre with unusually high communicative skills in Norrath. Do that player a favor: don't bust his/her chops. Ignore it, and if you feel like helping people out, try sending a private message to it saying that what it is doing is not in context (don't use the word "wrong," as it is harsh and direct and makes people think you are trying to tell them what to do) and explaining how one of its race usually acts. You have done your good deed for the day, preserved the flow of roleplaying, and hopefully done someone a favor by enlightening him/her as to a way to make the experience fuller for themselves and others.

As long as you maintain your role, the experience will at least remain somewhat saved. But your role, although small, is still very significant. You help make the whole thing work. Remember the comparison between the roleplay town and the Clinton-Lewinsky town. If you play your part, you might inspire others to roleplay and forget about what has no place in Norrath, making roleplay succeed and the only responses to the Clinton-Lewinsky scandal something to the affect of, "I know not what ye speak of sir," or, "Thou'rt mad! Keep thy distance and do not hurt me, I beg ye!"
