Club Passing - Patterns

This section contains descriptions of passing patterns for 3 or more people. This page only describes patterns in which there are three clubs for each passer (Sometimes less). For patterns with more clubs look here. It has not had much work though as I'm not good enough to do any of the patterns :-)

I have attempted to list the inventer of the pattern (or who I learnt it from if I do not know who invented it). If you know who invented a pattern please let me know.

The timing of the patterns is not shown unless it is an important aspect of the pattern. Most patterns can be done with many different timings (4-count, 3-count etc).

3-count Triangles

Several 3-count versions of the triangle pattern can be done. They include:

  • Outside: Right hand passes go to the person to the right, left hand passes go to the person on your left.
  • Inside: Right hand passes to to the person on the left, left hand passes go to the person to your right.
  • Outside/Inside: See the following table and the above diagram:

    ARP-BlsrsLP-Brsls RP-ClsrsLP-Crsls

  • Jims 3-count: Just start chucking throws at the correct person but to whatever hand you want. Something should work :-)

Bruno's Nightmare

In this pattern three jugglers move around as if they are being juggled in a reverse cascade pattern. Imagine the two hands are located at A and B and C is "falling" towards the right hand. B is the next person to move across the top, followed by A and so on. While two people exchange positions (eg C and B in the first case) the other person feeds to them (A). Next the person who just landed (C) becomes the feeder while the old feeder, A, moves across the top and B "falls" into the left hand.

The version I do has the feeder feeding in 4-count but I have heard it can be done in 2-count if all passers sprint around each other :-)

Karamazov Shuffle

I beleive this is the correct name for this pattern, stolen from the passing team of the same name.

In this pattern the passers go through the same seven positions, once by crossing vertically (as shown) and a second time horizontally.

The base timing is 4-count except for occasionally.

The following positions correspond to each pass.

   1       2     3     4     5     6     7
          A            C
   A C    C     C A    A    A C   A C   A C
          B            D
   B D    D     D B    B    B D   B D   B D
A A-BsssA-BsA-D sA-BsA-DsA-Cs
B B-AsB-CsB-Ass sB-AsB-CsB-Ds
C C-DsC-BsC-Dss sC-DsC-BsC-As
D D-CsssD-CsD-A sD-CsD-AsD-Bs

Now repeat 1 to 7 horizontally and finish back at start.

NOTE: At position 6 the diagonal passes are collision-prone. It can be done with one pair doing left handed passes one beat before or after the other pair, or with some kind of doubles/triples.

Feeds

Line Feed

A line feed is a pattern in which there is a line (wow) of feedes each waiting their turn to pass with the feeder. The feeder is usually doing 2-count while the feedes are doing something slower depending on the number of feedes and the type of feed being done. There are two ways the feeder can pass to the feedes.

  • Sweep Feed - The feeder moves along the line, then reverses the direction back along the line and so on. In the example below this involves passing to D, C, B, C, D etc.
  • Typewriter - The feeder moves along the line, then jumps back to the starting position. In the example below this involves passing to D, C, B, D, C, B etc.

The following are examples of a 4 person line feed: 

B C D
  A
Sweep Feed
A DCBCD
B ssAss
C sAsAs
D AsssA
Typewriter Feed
A DCBDCB
B ssAssA
C sAssAs
D AssAss

Zig Zag

In a three person feed extra people can be added to the pattern by forming two lines of passers. All passers are feeding to the two passers opposite, except for two people who are just normal feedes. The following table shows a six person line feed:

A  C  E
 B  D  F
A Bs
B AC
C DB
D CE
E FD
F Es

It is possible for one of the passers at the end of the line to move to the other end of his line. For example A can move next to E and rejoin the pattern. When A leaves B becomes a normal feedee. When A rejoins the line F becomes a feeder.

Conveyor Belt

This pattern is a variation on the Zig Zag pattern. It involves 5 passers and 9 clubs. As people run out of clubs they dash to the end of the line.

A  C  E
  B  D
A, B and C start with clubs. When A runs out of clubs he dashes to fill the spot next to D. E now passes to A and B runs next to fill the spot next to E and so on.

For extra difficulty start with more clubs (this speeds up the pattern).

Fools Feed

This is esentially two 2-person patterns combined together. The feeder passes 5 clubs with B by using his left hand, while at the same time passing 4 clubs C by using his right hand.

To start the pattern A has two clubs in each hand. B has two clubs in the right hand and one club in the left. C has one club in each hand.

A does a five club shower with his left hand with B, throwing to B's left hand and receiving from B's right hand to his left. At the beginning B starts slightly after A. B must juggle faster than in a normal 2-count. So A and B are doing the following:

A LPLP--LP--LP--LP
B --RPlsRPlsRPlsRP

A does the same thing with his right hand to C but as there are only 4 clubs it is much slower. When you put it all together A has a hard job as the timing on both sides is different so moves in and out of sync. A should also be able to manage if C doesn't pass everytime.

The pattern can be varied by having A do four clubs or five clubs on each side. If you do this you can also vary by throwing synchronously or asynchronously.

Another variation is to have A and B do six clubs. In this case B starts with 4 and A throws doubles.

This pattern was described by Allen K on rec.juggling.

2/3-count Feed

There are a few variations of this pattern. The essential pattern is the feeder performing 2/3-count with two feedes.

For the first two variations the feedes perform PSSSPS which is essentially one iteration of 4-count followed by one iteration of 2-count. One feeder passes right handed while the other passes left handed.

The last variation the feeder alternates between inside and outside passes. The feedes both perform 3-count.

Rotating Line Feed

In any of the "one feeder" line feeds one of the feedes from the end of the line can take over the feeders job, who then moves into the line. The following example shows how this is done for a 4 person feed (B becomes the new feeder while A moves to the end of the line.

     A     B A   B A     B
   B C D   C D   C D   C D A
A A-BA-CA-A A-BA-AA-A
B B-AB-BB-D B-AB-DB-C
C C-CC-AC-C C-CC-CC-B
D D-DD-DD-B D-DD-BD-D

This can be done continually.

Martin's Mildness

The next few patterns were invented by Martin Frost and are based on Jim's 3-count.

The first is a three person feed in which the feedes perform Jim's 3-count with crossing passes, while the feeder performs a kind of 2/3 count. It is shown in the following table:

C   B
  A
B RxP-AlsrsLxP-Alsrs LxP-ArslsRxP-Arsls
A RP-BRP-ClsRP-BLP-C,RHrs LP-BLP-CrsLP-BRP-C,LHrs
C rsLxP-AlsrsLxP-Ars lsRxP-ArslsRxP-Als

Note, on the 5'th and 11'th beat the feeder does one beat of a shower. This means you do a pass and a hand off at the same time.

Feast

A feast is a pattern in which each passer feeds to each other, including themselves. Each passer can take it in turns to feed or all feed at once. Here is an example of a three person feast:

C B
 A
Feeding in turns
A CBssCsBss
B sAsCssACs
C AssBAssBs
Feeding at once
A CBsCBs
B sACsAC
C AsBAsB

2/3-count Feast

B A
 C
Three people, each doing PPS at the same time, all feeding to everyone else, themselves included.

The tricky bit comes from the fact that you only get two thirds of the way around the feed on each pair of passes; then you do a self, then you start feeding again from the next person around from where you left off.

  PPSPPSPPS PPSPPSPPS
A L-BR-ClsR-AL-BrsL-CR-Als R-BL-CrsL-AR-BlsR-CL-Ars
B L-CR-AlsR-BL-CrsL-AR-Bls R-CL-ArsL-BR-ClsR-AL-Brs
C L-AR-BlsR-CL-ArsL-BR-Cls R-AL-BrsL-CR-AlsR-BL-Crs

Another way of thinking of things, in case the fact that there's three people and a three-long pattern confuses things:

Imagine you were doing a PPS feast with twenty-five other people, of whom you're A. Then you'd do:

Pass to B, pass to C, self.

Pass to D, pass to E, self.

etc, etc.

Pass to X, pass to Y, self.

Pass to Z, pass to A, self. Youre A, so this is PSS.

Pass to A, pass to B, self. Youre A, so this is SPS.

Pass to B, pass to C, self.

etc, etc.

2/3 count Star

The star can be done with a 2/3-count timing. In this version all right hand passes go to the second person to the right, while all left hand passes go to the second person on the left. The following table shows this:

A R-C L-D rs L-D R-C ls
B R-D L-E rs L-E R-D ls
C R-E L-A rs L-A R-E ls
D R-A L-B rs L-B R-A ls
E R-B L-C rs L-C R-B ls

This can also be done with passes to the inside. So right hand passes go to the second person to your left and left hand passes go to the second person to the right.

Shooting Star

Four people, three with clubs doing the star pattern described above. D is initially empty, A,E,C have clubs B has none. Do the following:

A CCC
C EEE
E BBB

Now A has no clubs so must move to position D to catch B's first club. To do this: As A passes last club E will be passing to B. A goes after this club to the center. On the next beat B passes to D so A must follow this club from the center to D while dodging the pass from C to E. Then C moves, then E and so on. If someone runs to fill in the gap immediately to your right then you are the next person to run.
