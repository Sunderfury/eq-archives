Club Passing - Timings

People seem to refer to both the physical location of passers and the timing of the passes as the Passing Pattern. For example 3-count is a passing pattern and a 4 person line feed is a passing pattern. This is confusing so I refer to the timing or count of the pattern as the Passing Timing and the physical location of passers and who they pass to in what order as the Passing Pattern. Only passing with two people is described in this section.

N-Count Patterns

There are many names for the different passing timings. I prefer the n-count notation and use it throughout these pages. I have also included some of the more common names for these timings.

Probably the most common timing for two person passing, and one that most people learn before any other, is 4-count. It is also referred to as "every others".

4-count

In general an n-count timing means that during a regular 3 club cascade every n'th throw is a pass to the other person. Thus for a right handed 4-count every 4th throw, or every 2nd right hand throw is a pass straight across to the other passer. 6-count, 8-count etc, are just slower versions of the 4-count pattern and good variations to try when first learning to pass.

The standard 4-count (both passers do the same thing) can be represented in the following table:

4-count RPlsrsls RPlsrsls

Obviously you can do a left handed 4-count by passing with the left hand on every 4'th beat.

2-count

2-count (Also called "solids" or "everies") is the fast version of this timing. It involves passing every time with the right hand.

3-count

If n is an odd number it means that you must pass with both hands. For example 3-count (Also called "left-right", "alternates","waltz" or "rejects") involves performing a right handed pass followed by two self throws followed by a left handed pass followed by two more self throws. It then repeats. Again 5-count, 7-count etc are slower versions of 3-count.

3-count (both passers do the same thing) is represented in the following table:

3-count RPlsrsLPrsls RP

When learning 3-count first try a left handed 6-count or 4-count. Then you can try 5-count and finally 3-count. It is useful to note that in 3-count the same clubs should continually be passed back and forth. Thus you can concentrate on just throwing back the club your partner has just thrown to you.

1-count

The last n-count timing is 1-count (Also called "ultimates", "solids" or "thundershower"). For this timing all throws are passes. Thus you continually perform a right handed pass followed by a left handed pass. This is represented in the following table:

1-count RPLPRPLPRP LPRP

M/N-count

An n-count timing can be thought of as doing 1 pass for every n throws. This can be extended to timings in which you perform m passes for every n throws. This leads to some interesting patterns such as 2/3-count (Or "pass pass self") which is represented in the following table:

RPLPrsLPRP ls

Other examples include 3/4-count (pass pass pass self), 2/4-count (pass pass self self) etc.

I find these patterns (2/3-count and 3/4-count especially) good practice for 1-count.

The m/n-count notation is not really sufficient because it assumes you perform m passes followed by n-m selfs. It does not describe the order of the passes and selfs. Another type of notation that can be used (I may ditch the m/n-count notation) is the "PS" notation which simply lists the order in which you perform passes (P) and selfs (S). Thus 2/3-count is PPS, 4-count is PSSS and 3-count is PSS. With this notation you can describe timings such as PSPSP (2-count which changes directions), PSSPS, etc.

The rest of this section describes variations of these passing timings.

Jims 3-count

In this 3-count variation one passer throws crossing passes with both hands while the other throws straight passes. The rhythm is right right left left.

A RxPlsrsLxPlshrs LxPrslsRxPrshls...
B RPrshlsRPlsrs LPlshrsLPrsls...
This pattern is named after Jim Brennan.

Random 3-count

Similiar to Jims 3-count. Just throw your passes to a random hand of your partner. You will soon discover that there are various combinations of passes that are collision prone. This leads to:

All Collisions

Perform a Jims 3-count type timing where all of the passes are collision prone. With practice you can work out to (just) avoind the collisions.

This is a one count version of Jim's 3-count. One person passes straight passes, while the other person throws crossing passes.

2/3 count

You can do the siteswap 441 in a 2/3 count timing by making each pass a double and each self a hand off. this is shown in the following table:

PPS (441) R2L2rh L2R2lh

Seven

2-count Doubles

Both passers do doubles, passing out of phase. The person with four clubs starts the pattern (A in the following example) and the other person starts half a beat later.

A R2lsR2ls
B R2lsR2ls

2-count Singles

Both passers do singles passes instead of doubles.

4-count Triples (4-count)

This pattern was invented by Martin Frost.

Pass every second right hand club as a triple. To start, the person with four clubs (two in each hand) throws a triple pass. At the exact same starting moment, the other person throws a self. After that simultaneous start, each person simply alternates between triple passes and right-hand selves.

A R3lsrslsR3lsrsls
B rslsR3lsrslsR3ls

Triples/singles
This pattern was invented by Martin Frost.

One passer does triples and the other does singles. Each passer throws in phase (passes at the same time,starting on the second pass).

A R3lsR3ls
B --RPlsRP

The two passers can change roles (ie one goes from singles to triples and the other goes from triples to singles:

Method 1 R3rsRPR3
RPR3R3rs
Method 2 R3R3hRPR3R3
RPR3R3R3R3h

Odd-Godz
Person B passes every throw straight across. Person A passes every throw diagonally across. Passer A starts with 3 clubs in the right hand and 1 in the left and passes with the rhythm right, right, left, left.

Passer A Rx2rsLx2lsRx2rsLx2ls
Passer B --R2--L2--R2--L2

Popcorn
A does triple/singles with four clubs for a beat then passes to B who does triple singles for a beat.

A rsx3lsRPls
B RPlsrsx3ls

variation 1: Instead of triple/singles each person does 4 club fountain

A rs|2ls|2RPls
B RPlsrs|2ls|2

variation 2: All single self except RP3 which alternates back and forth.

3-count
Yet another Martin Frost pattern.

A throws diagonal doubles, B throws straight doubles.

A lsRx2lsrs Lx2rsls
B R2lsrsL2 rslsR2

When starting the person with four clubs passes to partners hand that has 2 clubs, and the second person (three clubs) does a self 1/2 count later followed by a pass to the first hand that passed.

Combinations

Various timings can be strung together into new patterns for extra fun. The most common of these patterns is known as 3-3-10. In this pattern you perform 3 iterations of 6-count followed by 3 iterations of 4-count followed by 10 iterations of 2-count.

There are many other (infinite) combinations that can be created.

Another nice combination pattern involves performing the following sequence of timings: 2-count/3-count/4-count/3-count.
Only one iteration of each timing is performed. Because this involves passing to only one of your partners hands (because the 4-count starts with a crossing throw) it is then repeated with passes going to the partners other hand. The final result is shown in the following table:

RPlsRPlsrsLxPlshrsls RPlsrs LPrsLPrslsRxPrshlsrs LPrsls...

This pattern is interesting because of the repeated increase and decrease in passing frequency.

It was created and posted to rec.juggling by Jim ???.

Patterns From Hell

These patterns involve throwing doubles throws (whether they are passes or selfs) on a certain beat of a n-count pattern. The following table shows an example of a 4-count timing with doubles every fifth beat.

R2lsrsls RPls2rsls RPlsrs2ls RPlsrsls2 RPlsrsls

These patterns can be extended to n-count with doubles every m beats. For example 3-count, doubles every 5 or 2-count, doubles every 3.

These patterns were created by Sean Gandini and Kati Yla-Hokkala.

Fast-Slow or Slow-Fast

These patterns involve one person doing an m-count timing (where m is odd) and the other person doing an (m-1)-count timing. The person doing the even count pattern must juggle slower than the other passer, and must alternate between throwing to the partners left and right hand. The person doing the odd count passes faster and all passes go to the same hand.

Typical examples include 1-count/2-count, 2-count/3-count and 4-count/5-count.

RRLL (Right Right Left Left)

These are variations on the Fast-Slow pattern. One person performs a 2-count with passes going to alternating hands (I'll assume each person has three clubs, two in the right hand and one in the left. The 2-count person starts with w fast start to the other persons right hand). The other person performs RP rs LP ls. the following table shows this:

2-count person RxPlsRPls...
RRLL person RPrsLxPls...

This pattern has been credited to Dave Morten, Rick Koshi and Erin Hellner. I am not sure about the other variations.

You can also do a 3-count variation of this. Now one person passes 3-count as normal while the other person performs RxP rs LxP ls.

3-count/1-count can also be done. From the above pattern the RRLL person starts performing normal 1-count, as slow as possible. The 3-count person should now be doing a VERY fast 3-count.
