The Shard of Akalabeth

The shadowy figure approached the fire, and Sarangoulis prepared to defend himself. If this was to be his last moment – even if this was Death itself come to claim him – Sarangoulis did not intend to die quietly. He would fight to the last breath, and perish with dagger in hand.

But things are not always as they seem. The stranger drew back the hood of the cloak to reveal a sun-weathered face with kind eyes and a pleasant smile.

"Do not be alarmed, good sir," said the stranger. "I harbor no sinister intent. I was simply passing by, and noticed your campfire. May I rest here a moment?"

Sarangoulis lowered his dagger with a smile of relief and waved his hand in a gesture of invitation. "You are welcome to rest here as long as you like. The fire is not mine; I merely built it. All are welcome to share in its warmth."

"Very kind of you," said the stranger. "But would you say the same to a villain? For all you know, I might be a murderer. Mine might be a dark heart, and this smiling face could disguise a cruel purpose."

Sarangoulis nodded. "But your words are true and wise, and thus lead me to believe that yours is not a dark heart. And if you had a cruel purpose, it would most likely have been carried out by now. Villains are typically devoid of art; their skill lies in weapons, not words."

"Unless it should chance that I am an uncommon villain," said the stranger. "Are the words of the Devil himself not silvered and sweet?"

"It is rumored to be so," said Sarangoulis. "But your appearance is somewhat less than demonic. I have heard that the Devil is twelve feet tall, and that his skin is orange like fire."

"And that he can assume any form he wishes," said the stranger. "What else have you heard?"

Sarangoulis paused nervously. "He offers to grant people what they most desire…at the price of their souls."

The stranger smiled. "Well then, I am not the Devil, for my price is not nearly so steep. Tell me, Sarangoulis…what is it that you desire, more than anything else in the world?"

"You…you know me?"

"I know of you. Until yesterday, you were one of the most powerful wizards in the world. Now stripped of your title and power, you walk the earth as a mere man once again."

"I was not aware that the affairs of the Circle were common knowledge," growled Sarangoulis.

"They are not," said the stranger. "But there is very little that passes in this land without my knowledge."

"Who are you?"

"I am called Brimstone, if there is anything to be gained from a name."

Sarangoulis blinked in surprise. Brimstone was the name of the shadowy leader of the Lost Order of Akalabeth, an ancient order of knights and wizards. For ages, their members had been charged with the defense of the High Forest surrounding Empath Abbey.

"I am something of a mage, myself," continued Brimstone. "When I learned of your plight, I decided to seek you out in order to present you with an offer. What would you say if I told you that I knew the way to Norrath?"

Sarangoulis raised his eyebrows. "You know the way?"

"I do," said Brimstone. "And I can show it to you, if that is what you truly want."

"It is," said Sarangoulis.

"That was a hasty reply," said Brimstone. "Think, for a moment, upon what you are doing. All the people you know, all the friends and allies you have garnered over the ages – all of them will be lost to you. You will be alone, in a strange new land."

"I understand," said Sarangoulis. "I have weighed these thoughts in my mind a hundred times during the past few months. My decision is unchanged."

Brimstone shook his head and smiled. "You are young, Sarangoulis. Not too long ago, I stood where you now stand. I, too, felt my blood burn with the desire for new discoveries. Since then, I have traveled all over the world. I have seen kingdoms rise to glorious heights and crumble into oblivion. I have walked the world, from the icy lands of the north to the southern isles where the rivers are made of fire. In all my travels, there is one simple lesson that I have learned, perhaps more important than all the others combined. I have learned that the world is a dark, lonely place if you do not have friends. Power, wealth – these are meaningless without the simple comfort of companionship. Think upon that before you cast your eyes toward a distant shore."

Sarangoulis simply nodded, his eyes still resolute. Brimstone smiled sadly – perhaps where were some lessons that could only be learned from experience.

"The gate lies just over that hill," said Brimstone, pointing to the south.

Sarangoulis frowned. "That is the Moongate, is it not?"

Brimstone nodded. "It is. But if you walk through at the stroke of midnight, when the twin moons lie in perfect alignment – as they do tonight – the Moongate will take you to Norrath. And Britannia, along with all of its people, will become nothing more than a memory to you."

Sarangoulis looked up at the night sky.

"Yes, it is nearly time," said Brimstone. "Before you go, I offer you a gift." He opened his hand to reveal a glowing crystal.

"A lightstone?" asked Sarangoulis.

Brimstone smiled. "In a manner of speaking. To some, it offers illumination, and serves as a shield against many different kinds of darkness. For others, it burns to the touch, and they are unable to even gaze upon its light for very long."

Sarangoulis took the crystal, and looked at the swirling iridescent light from within. "Magical?"

Brimstone nodded. "This is one of the Shards of Akalabeth. Consider it a talisman of sorts – a ward against the various forms of evil that seek to injure or dominate."

"Where did you find it?"

"Deep within the forests of the north, there lies an ancient, sacred vale. Within that vale grows an enchanted tree, made of glass which never breaks, and ice which never melts. At certain times of the year, if one knows the proper spells and incantations, a crystal may be drawn from its frozen boughs."

"And I take it that you know the proper spells," said Sarangoulis.

"I should hope so," said Brimstone. "After all, it was I who first planted the tree, ages ago. And just as I gave it life, so too may you plant this Shard wherever you choose. By doing so, you bind your spirit to that place. Thereafter, you shall be shielded from the lure of the Void. Even if death itself stretches forth its talons to claim you, your spirit shall return to this Shard, and you will be reborn in a new form."

"A valuable gift, indeed. But why?"

"While you were a member of the Circle, you participated in the defense of this realm. By sending you to Norrath, I am sending a weapon against darkness. And there is darkness to be fought, make no mistake. There are powerful, malevolent forces at work in that realm already, and you must be constantly on your guard. If you find that you cannot persevere on your own, you may use the Shard to find allies throughout the land. But you must make haste now, for midnight is nearly upon us. Farewell, Sarangoulis, and remember what I have told you."

"Farewell, Brimstone. And thank you."

There was no more time for questions; fate was beckoning once again. With a racing heart, Sarangoulis turned and walked into the dark forest.

Brimstone was an Earthmage; in his time, he had planted all kinds of seeds and watched as they grew from ideas to reality. To him, the entire world could be seen as a garden, where some things grow proud and tall, while others grow dark and twisted. Through his careful, patient ministrations, he did what he could to make the garden a pleasant place to live in.

Now, he watched in silence as the shadows seemed to swallow the departing wizard. Once in a while, he thought he could see a faint glimmer made by the Shard, and he wondered what would take shape – sturdy oak or stinging briar, it was sometimes difficult to tell which would emerge from a simple seed. Only time would tell.
