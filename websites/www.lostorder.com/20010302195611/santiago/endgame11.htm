Dwarven Ale

Greyhawk eyed the sign on the tavern with apprehension. "I do not think this is a good idea," he said.

"We’re just stoppin’ in for a drink or two," said Bierfreund.

"What about the goblins?"

"What about ‘em?"

"Are we not supposed to be fighting them?"

"Aye, but not before we put down a few ales."

"It is not wise to drink before a battle."

Bierfreund shuddered and crossed himself religiously. "’Cause you’re an outsider, I’ll let that one pass. It ain’t your fault you were born a human. But you got to understand, you’re in the Dwarf Lands, now. And to dwarves, there ain’t nothin’ more important than ale, save for gems and Brell Serilis himself. We drink ale before going out to battle. We drink ale after we come back from battle. Heck, we’d probably drink ale during battle, only the ale would spill. A dwarf ain’t a dwarf unless he’s got ale pumpin’ through his veins."

"I am not a dwarf," Greyhawk protested.

"Aye, that’s the sad truth," said Bierfreund. "But trust me…one flagon of dragon breath ale, and you’ll fit right in with the rest of us."

Pub Kal was always busy in the evenings. When the dwarves were not mining or killing goblins, they were drinking. And they drank a lot. Ale was Kaladim’s chief import commodity; rumor has it that the main reason for establishing a ship route between Freeport and Kaladim was to keep enough ale in stock.

The dwarven bartender raised his eyebrows as the eleven adventurers filed into the tavern. He didn’t even have to say anything; he simply started filling up mugs of ale.

Bierfreund grabbed two of the mugs and handed one to Greyhawk. "This here is the finest ale that Kaladim has to offer. Ale like this ought to be savored." Bierfreund drank the ale in three gulps and slammed the flagon down, wiping his beard. "Alas, I ain’t no good at savoring."

Greyhawk looked down at the dark brown liquid in his mug, and frowned hesitantly.

"Go on," said Bierfreund. "It won’t kill you."

The shaman shrugged. "When in Kaladim…" He raised the flagon, and took a great gulp. Almost immediately, he realized why it was called dragon breath ale. It felt as though he had just swallowed fire. He sputtered and coughed, but nothing seemed to help.

"Here, drink this," said Bierfreund, handing him another mug of ale.

 

ONE HOUR LATER…

 

Greyhawk crawled around on the floor and glared at the dwarves. "Look!" he shouted. "I’m a polar bear! Raaaarrrr!"

"He’s blasted," said Kherghon.

"How many ales did you give him, Bier?" asked Walterus.

"Dunno. Lost count after six."

"You think HE’S drunk…you should have seen Corwin last Saturday!" The dwarves laughed.

"Will you people shut up?" shouted Jarlaxle drunkenly. "I’m trying to conshentrate!" He aimed his dagger at the dart board on the wall, and launched it through the air. There was a crash and a tinkling noise as one of the windows broke. The dwarves roared with laughter.

"Shut up!" he yelled. "I was jusht warming up." He readied another dagger.

"Guys, I propose a toast," said Dargo, standing up. Just then, Jarlaxle threw his dagger, and this time it landed in Dargo’s shoulder. Dargo scowled down at the blade, then plucked it out and tossed it aside.

"A toast," he continued. "To dwarven ale!"

"To dwarven ale!" roared the crowd.

"That reminds me of a song!" cried Penaden, who propped his legs up on the bar and plucked at the strings on his lute.


"The very first ale is like a punch to the head
Your brain is on fire, and you wish you were dead

"You keep going after the second ale, though you should have stopped there
You’ll feel like hell tomorrow morning, but you don’t bloody care

"You feel very brave as you finish the third flagon
You start to get ideas – ‘Hey guys, let’s fight a dragon!’

"Things start to go wrong after ale number four
The room starts to spin, and you wind up on the floor

"You don’t stop with the fifth ale, though you know that you should
‘Cause even dwarven women are starting to look good!"


Jarlaxle laughed. "Now that was a good one!" He looked around, but none of the dwarves were smiling.

"Let’s hang the elf by his own lute strings," growled Walterus.

"Sounds good to me," growled Kherghon. "Let’s get him!"

"Death to the elves!" cried Dargo, eager to join the battle, though he didn’t really know why there were fighting.

In seconds, the tavern was in complete chaos as chairs, empty flagons and dwarves flew through the air. There was a loud clatter as Bierfreund crashed into a table at the shadowy end of the room where Rothan and Eshay were kissing. They did not even seem to notice the dwarf – in fact, they seemed oblivious everything in the room, save for each other. Bierfreund stood up, shook his head, grunted and then charged back into the battle.

Meanwhile, Kherghon had jumped on top of Penaden and was in the process of hitting him with what looked like a large trout. Jarlaxle had picked up Dargo by the legs and was spinning him around in a circle. Walterus was frantically beating Greyhawk with his club as the large barbarian bit him in the leg.

"Kileyan, what are you doing behind the bar?" shouted Jarlaxle. "Do something!"

"I am!" said Kileyan, taking another gulp from his tankard of ale. He looked up and saw a tall, shadowy figure standing in the doorway.

"Uh oh," he said.

The battle came to an abrupt end as Ralineth quietly entered the tavern. The archdruid said nothing, but his eyes seemed to smolder with anger as he looked around the room at the piles of bodies and broken furniture.

"We have one chance," said Ralineth, "to save the world. And where do I find the champions of Faydwer? I find them brawling in a filthy alehouse."

The heroes looked down at the floor, guiltily.

"Well, what do you have to say for yourselves?"

Kileyan raised his flagon. "To the end of the world!"

"To the end of the world!" roared the crowd.

Ralineth felt like beating his head against the wall. How could he make these people understand that they were Norrath’s last hope? What could he say to motivate them?

"You know," said the archdruid, "if the world comes to an end tonight, there will be no more ale."

"No more ale?" whispered Bierfreund, turning pale.

"And if there is no more ale, you will have to be sober, forever."

The dwarves cried out in anguish.

Kherghon rose to his feet, unsteadily. "Fellow dwarves," he said loudly, "and pig-stinkin’ elves," he muttered. "Ralineth has told us of a great danger to this land, and I say we should forth at once on this, the most important quest of all time. We must…we MUST save the ale!"
