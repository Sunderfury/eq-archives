Twilight

A gentle breeze wafted through the mountains, its warm breath hinting at the arrival of spring. Bats circled overhead in the darkening sky, their tiny cries carrying faintly through the air as they went in search of nocturnal prey.

On a hillside near the city of Kaladim sat a lone dwarf. A casual passer-by might have thought at first that the dwarf was a statue, perhaps placed here by a whimsical sculptor. The dwarf did not move, nor did he make a sound. He simply sat there, watching the sun as it fell past the edge of the world, perhaps for the last time.

Bierfreund closed his eyes and listened to the sounds of twilight. The crickets had already begun to play their music, as if on cue from the dying sunlight. The sound was everywhere – the timeless, tireless symphony of a thousand invisible miniature musicians.

"There you are!" came a voice from below. Bierfreund opened his eyes and saw Walterus, Kherghon and Dargo climbing the hill. "We’ve been lookin’ all over for you," said Walterus. "What are you doin’ up here?"

"Thinking," said Bierfreund.

"Uh oh," said Dargo. "That’s me least favorite activity. Always makes me head hurt."

"Sounds like you might be in need of ale," said Kherghon. "Lucky for you we brought some!" He set down the barrel he had been carrying.

Bierfreund smiled. "Bless yer hearts, fellas! ‘Twould be a terrible fate to die sober."

Mugs of ale were passed around.

"Here’s to the end o’ the world," said Walterus.

"To the end o’ the world!" the dwarves echoed.

"I guess that’s it, then," said Bierfreund. "We really screwed up, didn’t we?"

Kherghon shrugged. "I dunno. I’ve always thought that if somethin’ is meant to happen, thar ain’t no way o’ stoppin’ it. Like death, or night, or becoming sober. Ye can try to put it off, but it’s gonna happen sometime."

Bierfreund gathered a fistful of dirt and poured it back onto the ground. "Kherghon, yer a cleric, right? Do ye think thar’s life after death?"

"Darn’d if I know," grinned the priest. "Brell Serilis forges us mortals out o’ dirt, and we go back to dirt when we kick the bucket. But the dirt never dies, ye see. Who’s to say he won’t see fit to forge us into somethin’ new some day?"

"I’d hate to be forged into a gnome," said Dargo with a shudder. "I’d have to run out an’ get meself killed again as soon as I could manage it."

The dwarves chuckled in agreement.

Walterus raised his flagon. "Here’s to life after death," he said.

"To life after death!" said the dwarves.

"Well, that’s it," said Kherghon. "The ale is gone. What do we do now?"

"Let’s go fight somethin’," said Walterus.

"Aye, one last battle," said Dargo. "Let’s go out with blades held high."

"You comin’, Bier?" asked Kherghon.

"Aye," said Bierfreund. "I’ll be right down."

The other dwarves left, and the dwarf once again gazed upward to the darkening heavens. The night sky was now the color of burning embers, slowly fading into gloom. He knew that this was the last twilight he would ever see, and his eyes took in every minute detail. If there was nothing after death, just an eternal sleep, then he wanted to remember this moment. For here, at the edge of night, in the hills of his homeland, he was at peace.

He could hear the voices of the dwarves in the valley below. They were laughing – apparently Dargo had tricked the elves into fighting some lowland basilisks, and the elves were now statues somewhere in the forest. They were planning to go and have a laugh at the expense of their silly pointy-eared brethren.

Bierfreund got up and went to join his friends. The four of them made a good team, and there was nothing better than fighting side-by-side with fellow clan members who would gladly lay down their lives for one another. With blades held high, singing a bawdy dwarven drinking song, they marched off into the night.
