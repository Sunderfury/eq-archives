In the Hall of the Dwarven King

Greyhawk was miserable.

From the moment he entered the dwarven city, and saw that most of the buildings were shorter than he was, he knew he was in for trouble. When he followed Bierfreund into an armor shop, he hit his head on the top of the doorway and nearly passed out. Everywhere he went, shopkeepers eyed him with suspicion, children pointed and laughed at him, and the guards followed him with drawn weapons. He really felt like an outsider.

Then, as if he wasn’t uncomfortable enough already, he had to follow Bierfreund as he swam through an underground river. The dwarf explained that this was the secret passage to the hall of the dwarven king. Greyhawk thought it odd that anyone who wished to visit the king had to swim through a river – but then again, the dwarves were an odd bunch, all in all.

So now he paced back and forth in a marble antechamber, careful not to bump his head against the ceiling. Bierfreund sat calmly on top of the oak table, with the patience of a statue.

"Why can’t we see the king now?" asked Greyhawk.

"He will send for us when he is ready," replied the dwarf.

"When will that be?"

"As soon as he is ready," said the dwarf.

"You said that already. Will it be minutes or hours?"

Bierfreund shrugged.

"You dwarves don’t talk very much, do you?"

"We don’t flap our gums all the time, if that’s what ye mean. When there’s something that needs to be said, we say it."

Greyhawk scowled at his wet clothing. He was dripping onto the carpet. "I suppose I won’t be able to find any dry clothes around here that will fit me."

Bierfreund shook his head.

"Does everyone have to swim through that river to get here?"

Bierfreund shook his head.

Greyhawk stopped and stared at the dwarf. "You mean, there’s another way in?"

Bierfreund nodded.

"Then why in the name of Innoruuk did you make me swim here?"

"You smelled bad," said the dwarf.

Before Greyhawk could even think about swinging his hammer at the little dwarf, the door opened, and the king’s herald nodded toward them.

"The king will see us now," said Bierfreund.
