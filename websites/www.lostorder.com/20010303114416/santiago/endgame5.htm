The Legend of Night

Dozens of tiny yellow motes of light surrounded Rothan as he murmured the words of his spell. A series of simple gestures followed, and suddenly the lights coalesced into a yellowish fire on the floor of the old, abandoned temple. The shivering travelers hastily drew near the fire, warming their hands and faces.

"Magical fire," said Rothan, "but warm nevertheless."

"Warm enough for me," said Greyhawk. "It would have taken me much longer to build a campfire."

"The art of Conjuring does have its advantages," said the mage. "On the other hand, one often has to endure the whining of irritable elementals." He glanced toward the entrance, where his familiar stood watch, no doubt grumbling to himself even now about the cruelty of his master.

"What brings you to Everfrost?"

Rothan glanced at Eshay. "Well, it’s a long story."

"It’s going to be a long night."

"Funny how you put that," said Eshay. "Greyhawk, have you ever heard the Legend of Night?"

Greyhawk nodded. "It’s an oft-told tale among my people."

"Could you tell it to us?"

"If you wish," said Greyhawk. He cleared his throat.


"Long ago, when the world was young, there was no such thing as night. The sun was always shining, and the skies were never dark.

"That was before the arrival of Veeshan, the father of all dragons. So great was his size that his wings covered the sky, and our world was plunged into darkness.

"The spirits of the world cowered in fear, for they did not know what had happened, nor did they know what to do. Without the gift of sunlight, the world would surely die.

"Among their number was a tiny hawk, a creature of light and shadow. ‘I will fly up and see what has happened’, he said.

"So he soared up into the air until he touched the very wing of Veeshan himself. With a mighty effort, he pierced the wings of the great dragon with his claws until he became weary, and fell from the sky.

"Time and time again, he flew up and attacked. Time and time again, he fell to the earth, only to fly up again. Finally, after days and days, he managed to sever the dragon’s wing.

"Veeshan roared in pain, a roar that was heard all over the land. The dragon flew away from our world, leaving its broken wing behind.

"That wing still covers part of our world. As Norrath spins, the wing sometimes comes between us and the sun, and that is why we have night and day.

"But at night, you can see stars in the sky. Those stars are the holes that were made by the claws of a tiny hawk whose bravery saved this world from eternal darkness."


"A good tale," said Eshay.

"But there is more to it," said Rothan. "Lately, there have been dark rumors. All across the land, in every city and town, the people are frightened. Priests are foretelling the end of the world. Scholars and mages predict that some great catastrophe is drawing near. Some say that Veeshan will soon return to this world, seeking vengeance."

"It is also said that, from among the people, one will be chosen," said Eshay. "In the final battle, there will be a chance, a single chance, for that person to save us."

"And you think that person is me?" asked Greyhawk.

"Perhaps," said Rothan. "What little we know, we know only from legends. What do you say, Greyhawk? Hawk of light and shadow? Will you help us to save the world?"

Greyhawk frowned. "The hawk in the legend is tiny. I am not tiny."

"That depends," said Eshay. "We’ll see how you compare when Veeshan arrives."

(Author's Note:  Part of this story is based upon what I believe to be the Native American legend of the first night, wherein the blanket of night was first cast over the world and the spirit of an eagle flew up to pierce it and create the stars.)
