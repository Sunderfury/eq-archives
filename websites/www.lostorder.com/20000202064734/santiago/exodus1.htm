Judgment

Sarangoulis sat alone in the antechamber, listening to the ticking of the clock. Twilight had come and gone, and the only light to be had now was from a small candle on the table. Try as it might, the tiny flame could not dispel the shadows. It could hold them back for a while, but in the end the darkness would win. It was inevitable.

In front of the candle sat a closed spellbook. Scrawled upon the pages of that tome were all of the spells that Sarangoulis had ever learned – the sum of his magical knowledge. He had spent years traveling the world, searching for bits of lore to fill those pages. He placed his hand gently upon the red leather cover, his fingers tracing the contours of the ornate brass corners. But he did not open the book. He would never again open it.

Outside, he heard the gallop of hooves as a knight rode through the street below. Somewhere nearby, a bard was playing a song on his harp. The lilting melody carried faintly through the air, like a ghostly echo. Sarangoulis closed his eyes and listened to the sounds of Britain, the sounds of what had been, until now, his home.

Then there came the sound of footsteps. The door opened, and a young apprentice entered.

"They’re ready for you," said Castaldi, his eyes downcast.

"I can tell from your expression what they have decided," said Sarangoulis.

"I…I’m sorry, sir," said Castaldi.

"Why should you be?" asked the wizard. "This is not your fault, nor theirs, nor mine. It is simply the way things are. Do you believe in fate, Castaldi?"

The youth shrugged. "I don’t know. I guess so. But I don’t always like what fate decides."

Sarangoulis smiled. "Fate can often be cruel, it’s true" he said. "But not always. Sometimes, what seems to be an end can turn out to be a new beginning."

Castaldi said nothing.

"Come," said Sarangoulis. "Fate beckons…we mustn’t keep her waiting."

The two of them left the room, leaving behind a closed book and a tiny, flickering candle.

In truth, Sarangoulis had known what the council would decide before he even set foot in the Tower of High Sorcery. He had been expecting this for some time now – it had only been a question of when.

His suspicions were confirmed as he entered the council chambers and looked upon the faces of the other wizards. Sarangoulis could read the thoughts written upon those faces, as clear as any words on a parchment. Anger, mostly – for he had dared to trespass upon forbidden ground. But hidden just beneath the surface of that anger, he could sense fear. They considered him dangerous, now. He had become a threat – not to them, but to their Circle. Many of the younger members were impressionable and curious, and the elders feared that they might be enticed by talk of other worlds. The result would be a division in their ranks that might signal the end of their carefully constructed hierarchy. This could not be allowed to happen. Therefore, Sarangoulis had to be eliminated.

"Sarangoulis," said the grand wizard Ravindranath. "It has been brought to the attention of this Council that you have recently sought to unlock the secret of travel to Norrath. Is this true?"

"It is true."

"The pursuit of such knowledge is strictly forbidden," said Ravindranath. "Are you aware of the penalties for such transgressions?"

"I am."

"Is there anything you wish to say to this Council in your defense?"

Sarangoulis stepped forward. "Honored council members, I appear to you as a criminal for having violated one of your laws. I do not deny my own actions. I knew the path that I was walking, and I knew where it would lead. But I want to explain to you why I chose to follow it.

"It is said that in ancient times, magic was feared. Most people did not understand it, nor did they want to. Those who pursued such knowledge were persecuted and put to death.

"Today, magic is commonplace. It is used to heal the wounded and feed the hungry. It is used to travel great distances in the blink of an eye. It is also used to wound and damage, and to summon beings of terrible destructive power.

"Were the ancients right? Would our world be a better place without magic? We will never know. As beings of intellect and curiosity, we are irresistibly compelled to learn more, to know more. If the pursuit of knowledge is a crime, then I am certainly guilty of it."

"It is not the pursuit of knowledge that we condemn," said Ravindranath, "but rather the study of certain subjects. These subjects are forbidden for a reason."

"Just as necromancy has been forbidden," said Sarangoulis," yet I know for a fact that many of you practice it." Several council members looked away. "And why do you practice it? Because you hunger, as I do, for something new. The old ways have grown stale. We mindlessly cast spells over and over, day after day, in order to improve our skill. We toil endlessly in order to make enough money to afford our precious reagents. And to what end? I am sick of magic, as we know it.

"When I first heard the legends of Norrath, I was ecstatic. The very thought that there might be worlds beyond this one was enough to rekindle my hope. So I embarked upon a dark journey. I perused forbidden tomes and oracles to learn everything I could. Yes, I knew that I was violating edicts of this Council. But what was the alternative? To stay forever in this forsaken land, which has become a prison to me, barren and desolate.

"Honored council members, you know me. You know that my heart is devoid of iniquity. I do not seek to become a prophet, leading thousands of people to some ‘paradise’ that may not even exist. I simply search for something new, something to revive the wonder that this world used to hold for me."

"That is all I wanted to say," he said. "I humbly await the decision of this Council."

The council chamber was silent for a moment as the other mages contemplated his words.

"Sarangoulis," said Ravindranath, "this Circle was founded more than a century ago. Since its inception, our purpose has been not only the study of arcane lore, but also the defense of this realm against all manner of threats. Whether it truly exists, or whether it is simply a myth, we consider the realm of Norrath to be one of those threats. Already there are hundreds, if not thousands of people who believe that the legends are true. Farmers, nobles, healers, merchants…all of them have quit their jobs and gone out in search of this mythical realm. As a result, our society has begun to collapse. Unharvested crops wither in the fields, abandoned buildings have begun to decay, and foul creatures now roam the wilds, unhindered by the warriors who used to oppose them. This must not continue.

"In these trouble times, the people look to people such as us for guidance. We set an example for them. If our members were to set out, as you have, in search of some ‘holy grail’, then matters would be even worse.

"We understand your reasons for your actions. Indeed, we sympathize. You are young. You seek to explore new lands and unravel new mysteries. But you must understand that we cannot allow members of our Circle to study forbidden lore. Therefore, it is the judgment of this council that you shall no longer bear the title of Wizard, nor shall you be permitted to practice magic. While this may appear to be a harsh judgment, the law would have demanded a more terrible price to be paid."

Sarangoulis bowed. "I accept the judgment of this Council." Sarangoulis turned and left the chamber without another word. For what more was there to say?

He descended the stone steps and walked through the main hall, where several lesser mages were purchasing precious reagents from vendors. Sarangoulis quietly walked past all of them until he stood upon the ornate stone bridge that connected the Guild with the rest of the city. Here he paused for a moment to take in his surroundings. It had been a long time since he had crossed this bridge; in the past, he had simply used his magic to teleport him from place to place. Now he would have to rely upon his own two feet.

The night air was warm, foreshadowing the death of winter and the return of spring. The lampposts had been lit, and Britain was a city of flickering shadows and unfriendly faces. Sarangoulis took a deep breath and crossed the bridge into the dark city, a wizard no more.
