SCENE 3: NORTH FREEPORT, THREE DAYS AGO (Continued)

Sir Breel stood up and turned stonily towards a sweating, gibbering young Jasud.

"You are under arrest for the vile crimes of oathbreaking and conspiracy against the Knights of Truth.  You will be removed to the Temple of Marr for confinement and shriving, pending execution in the morning.  May Marr have mercy upon your wretched soul."

With an inhuman screech, young Jasud broke free of the paladins restraining him.  A leap took him to his sword, which he brandished threateningly.

"IT'S A LIE!" He howled piteously, "A LIE, A LIE, A LIE!"

He actually wounded two Knights before they came at him as one in righteous fury.  Self-preservation won out, and he tore out of the tavern in a mad run, a squadron of paladins at his heels.  He was a dead man now, here and in all of Freeport, for the militia would kill him just as vigorously.  If he managed to escape his pursuers, he would run to the Commonlands for safety.  That is where I would seek him. For this was not over.

I gathered up my weapons and stopped before Sir Breel, who had removed his helmet and had his face in his hands, weeping softly.  Poor, noble man.  He deserved better than this.  I touched him gently on the shoulder.

"I am sorry," I said, meaning it.

He exhaled slowly and stood, wiping the tears from his weary eyes and running a hand through his graying hair.  All at once he was himself again.  Proud and straight he stood before me, once more an exemplary bastion of true honor.  If they were all like, him, I thought, I would be out of business.  Not for the obvious reasons, but because I probably would not have the heart to confusticate people like him.

"You are best without such as he, Noble Sir," I confessed gently.  "He would poison your souls and blacken your names.  You would lose your Way, with such ilk to guide you."

He nodded slowly.

"Of that I have little doubt.  He is damned to us now and he will be brought to justice.  Know that I am not entirely convinced of your innocence in all of this, Szardos, but I have no evidence to set against you.  I think it wise that you leave North Freeport before the dawn, and that you refrain from returning for at least a month.  It will take that long for my Order to recover and return to normal operations."

I bowed low and respectfully.

"You wish is my will, Sir Breel."

SCENE 4: THE EAST COMMONLANDS, TWO DAYS AGO

N'Mar had better pay me a bloody big bonus for this miserable assignment.

Young Jasud had indeed escaped to the Commonlands, as I thought he would. Fanatics always have the strength to overcome terrible odds.  Eight warriors, paladins and militiamen alike, had felt the sting of his blade in his crazed dash to freedom.  It was said that young Jasud had not emerged unscathed himself.  Hope springs eternal.

If you are curious as to how such damning evidence came to be in young Jasud's private quarters at the Temple of Marr, permit me to enlighten you: that morning before our rendezvous, I myself forged the letter from N'Mar (his rather childish scrawl is particularly easy to duplicate), with information I had heard some time ago from the Ebon Mask assassin would had actually killed the Knight Vikspin.  This I placed into the slim packet, along with the 10 gold pieces (a loan from Enoyna; I was to pay her back as soon as it was convenient ... or else she was willing to 'take it in trade.' Dear Enoyna.) in order to complete the illusion of young Jasud's treachery.  As for secreting it within his quarters, well.  If you have never executed a breaking and entering into the Temple of Marr, I certainly will not make it easy for you so to do by telling you how I did it.  I will merely say that Bristlebane himself must have blessed my stealth that extremely tense afternoon, as I snuck past the guards and priests on duty and reached young Jasud's private room.  Did you know that the fools actually have their names emblazoned on the doors there? The lock, I need not tell you, was a joke.  Very trusting fellows, these paladins.  Once in, I chose a tapestry that looked as if it had not been touched in years.  The rest you know.

Once I left Freeport, I set out to track this double agent paladin bastard in the Commonlands.  Why?  Two reasons.  One, the papers he still carried for the rendezvous might have been genuine.  I had to be sure of this.  As for the second reason, I felt I owed it to the honor of the Knights of Truth to rid them of this blackhead on their pristine surface.  Be silent, all of you.  I am still as treacherous as ever.  I just have certain standards to maintain, that is all.

I found young Jasud a day and three-quarters later, shortly after dusk.  He was shivering at a makeshift fire, his bloody sword on the ground beside him.  The infection of madness was evident in his excited mumblings and his twitching movements.  It would be a mercy to send him to the conspirator's reward.  Silent as I was, he heard me approach.  The lunacy had provided him with preternatural senses, a survival mechanism which has been well documented by the scholars.  His hatred of me cut as deeply as a razor as he shuddered with anger.

"You," he acknowledged in a wavering, ill-controlled voice. "Do you know what you have cost me?"

I shrugged.

"Nothing whatsoever," I countered calmly.  "You threw it all away yourself."

"Filthy liar."

"Nonsense," I insisted.  "You strove by your own actions to ruin the Knights of Truth, young Jasud.  They serve the Good as valiant and true warriors of Light.  Treachery and espionage are my business, not theirs.  I could simply tell you that crass amateurs such as you insult the dignity of professionals such as myself.  It would even be the truth.  But the greater truth is that people such as you would set your Order on a path that would destroy them from within.  Where honor is concerned, the end does not justify the means."

"Besides," I said wryly, "As much trouble as they are to me and mine, I am rather fond of your Order as it is.  I have enough competiton in the shadows from other rogues.  I do not need any more."

He stood, trembling with venom, and lifted his gore-spattered sword at me.

"Butter wouldn't melt in your mouth, Dark Elf scum," he growled at me.  "All you say only proves that my cause is just.  Ridding all of Norrath of blue-skinned trash such as you.  That was worth bending a precious rule or two."

"I am curious," I said honestly.  "Why do you hate us so?"

"You are Dark Elves," he spat.  "Our enemy.  Secretive, impure threats to human rule.  You must die."

"So it all comes down to bigotry," I mused, shaking my head.  "I suppose the Trolls would be next."

"YES! YES!" he shouted, scattering frightened birds from the area.  "Then the lumbering Ogres.  All who threaten humanity must be eliminated.  You understand this.  That is why you must die."

"And the other races?" I inquired politely.  "What, for example, of the halflings and the half-elves?"

Young Jasud's lips curled into an ugly sneer.

"The halflings are too small and weak to bother us.  As for the mongrels, they are not against us."

"If they were to be against you?"

"Then they die as well."

I drew my weapons and did not bother to salute him.

"You are insane.  I do Norrath a favor by removing you from it."

The blast of a warcry drew our attention as we crossed swords.  An orc legionnaire and two centurions burst from the thickness of the trees.  They were running full at us with all the speed they could muster, destruction on their tiny, vicious minds.  I shot a glance at young Jasud.  Surprisingly, he was lucid enough to understand and silently agree. 

This threat comes first.  We will finish this later.

A single sweep of young Jasud's heavy blade disarmed the legionnaire, sending his rusted scimitar into the bushes. Taking advantage of the beast's surprise, we both attacked the centurion with which it arrived.  I struck at it from behind, burying the razored points of my rapier and dragoon dirk into its back, as young Jasud decapitated it with a frenzied swing of his sword.  Crazed or not, young Jasud was a Knight, and a fell warrior.  The second centurion was clubbed aside by the angered legionnaire, who had regained the scimitar.  The hapless centurion was unconscious ere it hit the ground.  The legionnaire then turned its attention to young Jasud, slamming a muscled fist into his side.  I heard the ugly sound of ribs splintering and snapping and something far more ominous, as the orc drove young Jasud to his knees.  It lifted its awful weapon to finish him off, but I was faster.  Again I plunged the deadly tips of my weapons into an unprotected back.  The legionnaire howled in fury and pain, turning to me with tusks bared and dripping pinkish foam.

Several times was I struck, and several times did I strike back.  The legionnaire was winning this fight, however, as I was rapidly running out of breath and forcing bruised and battered limbs to parry and thrust and praying quite dearly that the blood soaking my armor was not all mine.  To his credit and undoing, young Jasud struck the legionnaire once more, cutting his hand off at the wrist.  Roaring in rage, the legionnaire took him by the throat with its remaining hand and broke his neck with single squeeze of its powerful fingers.  It tuned to me, a hideous grin on its animal features.  But the sound of that awful crack had galvanized me, and before it could move, I threw myself at it with all my might, the full force of my charge behind the dragoon dirk which I drove unerringly up into its jaw and beyond to pierce what the orc laughingly called its brain.  The lights dimmed in its tiny eyes and it fell alongside the man it had killed.  Rest, young Jasud.  You were a disgrace in life, but a worthy Knight in death.  There are worse epitaphs.

The grunts behind me told me that the danger was not quite over.  The second (and last) centurion had awakened and was even now advancing on me to avenge its brothers.  I took some solace in the fact that, despite my weakened condition, it would be a much easier foe to dispatch than its leader.  Moreover, once it was killed, I would suffer no further trouble and could depart in peace and safety.

SCENE 5: THE EAST COMMONLANDS, RIGHT NOW

Perhaps they'll put that wretched sentence upon my wretched tombstone.

An orc legionnaire and pair of centurions are one thing.  But an orc legionnaire and pair of centurions followed immediately by a bloody young kodiak bear is another matter entirely.  I know not whether you have ever encountered kodiak bears.  The best way to describe them is to take muscle, fur, fangs and claws and wrap them into a large and bad tempered mass.  Fit and rested, I could take this one facing me with but moderate effort.  My instant condition, however, precluded any thought of success whatsoever.  Perhaps because I knew my cause to be hopeless, I felt my lips curl into the mocking smile that has ever graced my features in the face of the longest odds and most insurmountable dangers.  The bracing flow of adrenalin within my sinews assured me that I would go down fighting, my wounds in front.

As a gentleman and rogue of Neriak, I could do no less.

I dodged four blows from the roaring beast, plinking at it here and there, as a mosquito would fight against a shadow wolf.  My much-vaunted luck held for two more swipes of its razored claws.  The third caught me in the face.  Two more savaged my body, sending me tumbling headlong into the trunk of a tree.  Amazingly, I was still nimble enough to twist in mid air.  When I crashed into the tree, I landed on my feet, weapons in hand.  It took the last of my reserves so to do.  The mad rush of adrenalin had left me, lacing my tortured muscled with lead.  My right arm would not respond to my mental commands.  Indeed, I marvelled that it was still attached to the bloodsoaked shreds of my shoulder.  The great beast approached me, great gobbets of drool dripping from its snarling fangs.  Braced against the tree, rapier wavering unsteadily in my trembling left hand, I awaited it.  The kodiak lifted a mighty paw, its terrible claws stained proudly with the precious scarlet of my blood. Gazing into its murderous amber eyes, I saw my death.  My smile widened.

Bristlebane, I come to you now.  Have a drink ready for me, you tricky bastard.

Fire suddenly filled the air, enveloping the kodiak, who bellowed with rage and agony.  A club the size of an adult wood elf lifted skyward behind it, arcing downwards to crunch meaningfully into its back.  Crippled and angry, the beast turned to its new opponent, who continued to flail and pound the bear into pulp.  I never heard an aria so sweet as the piteous whine the kodiak let fly before its skull was shattered under the weight of that club.

My vision was already fading as my rescuer let fly a deafening roar of triumph, painful to the ear, but as delicious to me as the death cry of the kodiak.  I discerned that he came near to me, but I could only see a huge outline, dark and blurred with the effects of my delirium.  A moist, shattering spasm deep within my chest told me I would have no breath with which to force my dried lips to thank my club-wielding friend for my murder's revenge.  A pity.  I do so like to give credit where credit is due.  I perceived that he was gesturing at me.

Then everything went black.

A force akin to lightning coursed into the ruins of my body, and I felt it jerk and jump with the waves of pleasure-pain that is indicative of magickal healing.  I felt my very cells regenerate, felt the muscles growing back, felt the tendons and ligaments reknitting  themselves, as if thousands of tiny spiders within me spun webs of flesh and sinew.  Several times did that huge figure gesture at me, and several times did I feel that blessed energy flow into my very atoms.  It preserved me.  It restored me.  When it was over, I felt as if I had been pounded flat and remolded into my original form by giant hands.  Aching and light-headed with the pain of the massive healing session, I lifted my head and opened sweat-ringed eyes to view the face of my savior.

Yikes.

The massive, misshapen face I looked up at belonged to a very, very large Ogre.  The tiny, glassy eyes that peered at me underneath the shade of his beetling brow were of different colors: one blue, one green.  His crooked nose looked as if it had itself seen the wrong end of his club more than once.  The gaping maw that was his mouth was filled with huge tusklike teeth, sharp and uneven.  Looming over me like the shadow of a mountain, he grinned hugely at me.

"Hullo, Inkie!"  he bellowed at me.  "You be Oopz' Frend?"

"Er, all right."  I answered warily.

"You gud Inkie!"  he boomed.  "Nice Inkie! Oopz call you 'George'!"

"'George'"  I asked.

The behemoth bobbed his head up and down excitedly.

"Well," I stammered, "That's ...  certainly very nice.  But my name is Szardos."

"'George.'" he insisted.

"'Szardos.'" I replied.

"'George.'"

"'Szardos.'"

"'George.'"

"'George?'"

"George."

I sighed.  One in my position should never argue with a grinning hilltop carrying a large club.

"'George' it is, then."  I told him, with gritted teeth.

Oopz beamed with pleasure.

"HULLO, GEORGE!"  he cried happily.

"Indeed."  I mumbled weakly.

I gave Oopz the honor of stripping the orcs of their weapons and armor, while I relieved the broken body of what had once been young Jasud of his documents.  Glancing at them briefly, I noted that they consisted of correspondence between the Guildmaster of North Freeport and the King of Felwithe.  Genuine or false, they would be of worth to N'Mar.  I put them inside what remained of my tunic for safekeeping, making a mental note to charge N'Mar double my fee for this awful assignment.  I had not entirely lied to Sir Breel in North Freeport.  I constantly avoided any assignments that would jeopardize my position in any part of Freeport.  This was my first and my last venture along those lines.  Never again would I risk losing the ability to enjoy the vibrant, cosmopolitan humors of my adopted city.  Not, I grinned inwardly, when there were numerous other cities to steal blind.

The pair of us made camp later that evening, not far from our battlefield.  The kodiak's pelt was worthless, but his meat was not.  Roasted with a few spices on the portable spit I had in my pack, it turned out to be fairly tasty, and plentiful enough to fill both of our bellies.

"Yum!"  Oopz rumbled contentedly.  "George make-um gud bear steak, George does."

"Thank you, Oopz," I said.  "Oopz.  That is a rather ... unusual name."

Oopz bobbed his massive head up and down in agreement and bent low to warm his hands at the fire.

"Very strange name, yes.  Shamenz name Oopz wen Oopz wuz li'l baby ogre. Shamenz allus name new baby ogres.  Oopz special.  Wen Oopz born, star come down frum sky.  Dis important.  Shamenz, he come to cave to look at li'l baby me.  He tickle me and tell Mumz and Dadz dat me be powurful shamenz one day.  Den he lift me to sky to name me and dere was big thunder and he name me ... right after he drop me."

"You're joking," I blurted out without thinking.

Oopz grinned.

"I wish I was."

I shook my head in disbelief.  Then I narrowed my eyebrows as I suddenly realized something.

"It must be rather tiring pretending to be feeble-minded all the time, eh Oopz?"

Oopz shot a glance at me, his eyes suddenly very sharp and extremely wary.

"What George say?"  he asked innocently.

I rolled my eyes.  "George wonder why Oopz talks like Oopz talk," I parroted, "Especially when Oopz just said 'I wish I was', and not 'me wish me wuz,' or something similarly moronic like that."

I stood up.

"A tiny slip," I admitted, "But one that tells me that Oopz is much more intelligent than Oopz lets on."

A slow nod and a grudging smile followed from Oopz.

"You're quick, Dark Elf," said he finally.  "Quicker than most."

"Most gracious."

"Not at all."

"So why the charade, Shaman?"

Oopz leaned back and spread his arms.

"Look at me, Dark Elf.  Look closely at me from the perspective of almost any race on Norrath."  He put his fist dramatically under his chin.  "Do you see a scholar?" Oopz asked.  He then let his jaw go slack, and his eyes go wide and glassy.  "Or do you see a large, clumsy, but altogether dangerous oaf with magical powers?"

"I stand shamed for my prejudices," I confessed.  "But why indulge in playing the fool at all, then?"

Oopz cocked his head to the side, regarding me thoughtfully.

"You tell me," he said.

I considered.  The constant intrigues that have been my life provided me with the answer to my question almost immediately. 

"As a fool you are tolerated," I told him slowly.  "Perhaps even liked.  People tend to speak more freely if they perceive that you are no threat.  By pretending ignorance, by feigning that you cannot understand their subtleties, you lull the suspicious.  As a fool you are less threatening, and predictable ... from their point of view."

"Exactly," Oopz replied.  "I said you were quick."

"It must be difficult," I mused.

"You have never disguised yourself, Rogue?" He asked mockingly. "You have never pretended to be other than who you are?"

"Of course," I countered, smiling.  "But never all the time.  How draining it must be."

He scratched the back of his broad and hairy neck.

"It is, sometimes.  Damn, but you would never have guessed my secret if it wasn't for that slip.  May the Warlord curse that idiot shaman for naming me that name!  Even today, I could roast him alive for that."

"Can you not change it?"

"No," he said unhappily . "Names have power, especially among my people. I am stuck with it."

"My condolences."

"Phaugh," he muttered gruffly, waving a ham-sized hand away in dismissal.

"Well, I am nonetheless glad to make your acquaintance, Noble Shaman, especially when a certain kodiak was proving somewhat intent on providing his cubs with a Teir'Dal rug for their cave."

Oopz chuckled.  Or, rather, I took it to be a chuckle.  In truth, it sounded remarkably like two large boulders grinding against one another. 

"You fought well,"  he told me, "Even at the end, you still stood against it in defiance.  The bear could have killed you, Dark Elf, but it would never have beaten you.  By the Warlord, I could not let such a fighter die."

"Most kind," I said.

"Phaugh," he replied.

My natural curiosity again welled within me about my large companion.

"What exactly are you doing in the Commonlands, Oopz?  I rarely see your people in this area."

Oopz lifted his chin.  "I travel in service to my people and to the Warlord."

"I'm afraid I don't understand."

Oopz glowered at me, his mismatched eyes sad and distant.

"Ogres were a power once," he rumbled.  "We ruled through our great strength and cunning.  No race could stand against our might.  The Warlord loved us and spoke to us often, as I speak to you now.  We enjoyed his favor and his counsel.  The other gods became jealous of our place.  They united the other races and set them upon us.  We laughed at their interference, and sent the other races to flight.  Then we challenged the gods themselves.  We lost.  Our beloved Warlord himself came to our side, and together we brought war to the gods once more.  The battle shook the earth and rent the heavens, but again we were defeated.  The gods captured the Warlord and imprisoned him in his Plane of Power, sealing him away from Norrath forever."

"I have read the histories," I said.  "The Teir'Dal chronicled the banishment of Rallos Zek."

Oopz looked impressed.

"Know you what happened afterwards, Dark Elf?"

"Not entirely," I confessed.  "The accounts thereafter are somewhat sketchy."

A sigh like steam hissing from a teapot came from my ponderous comrade.  "My people were beaten, and we were scattered across Norrath," he said grimly, "Strong we remained, but knowledge was taken from us.  We were made weak in mind, to keep us from rising again.  Confused, we began to fight with one another over petty things.  And the Warlord was now gone from us.  So my people wandered, one group searching for a home where we would be safe.  A strong home to protect us from our enemies until we could rebuild ourselves." He grinned me a toothy, lopsided grin.  "This we made in Ogguk.  Guarded on all sides it was.  Rivers to block invasion, a desert to be avoided, jagged mountains to guard our backs, and a thick jungle to slow down armies.  It was the perfect new home.  We carved a city for ourselves from the country's tough bones, and called it Oggok, which in Ogre means Revenge."

"And have you rebuilt yourselves?" I asked.

He slowly shook his massive head.

"That will take time.  Many, many hundred-years have passed.  Most Ogres are still weak in mind.  And the Warlord speaks to us only in our dreams and in battle.  Still, though Smart Ogres are slow to come, come they do.  We teach them our past, and how we must improve our people so we can regain our place on Norrath."

"And some, like you, go forth to learn?"

"Yes.  I go traveling the world to find knowledge that will help my people.  And to fight."  He pointed a meaty finger at our battlefield, at the naked bodies strewn about like stringless marionettes, stiffening now amid the iron tang of Death.

"Do you see this place, Dark Elf?" He asked me.

I nodded.

"Only here am I alive," he said quietly.  "Everything else is shadow.  Or sleep."

Much as I myself am only truly alive in the midst of risk-laden plots and in the most dangerous of assignments, I thought to myself.  There is symmetry there.  I looked up at his misshapen, almost wistful face.

"I believe I understand, Oopz.  I truly do."

EPILOGUE

The next day, we were on our way to Neriak.  I to collect my fee (and perhaps a pound of flesh) from N'Mar, Oopz to visit the city proper and the Ogres in the Foreign Quarter.  The silence of our journey was eventually broken by a brief, yet particularly pungent curse coming from my large companion.

"Problem?" I inquired.

"I could use a drink, Dark Elf."

"It's an hour to the nearest inn," I reminded him.

"Phaugh," said he in reply.

"Half a moment," I told him, "If you're feeling daring, I've a bottle of homemade bog juice with me."

"Didn't know you were a brewer, too," Oopz grunted.

I shrugged.

"Strictly as an amateur, Oopz.  I only started two weeks or so ago."

Unslinging my pack, I located the pale green bottle, wrapped in furs to protect it from breaking.  I placed it in his meaty paw.

"Here you are," I said.

Oopz gazed at the bottle with a baleful eye, then threw his head back and chugged down its contents in two large gulps.  I watched his eyes widen in surprise, and cringed visibly at the soggy bellow of a belch that followed.  We would in all likelihood not be bothered for the remainder of our trek, I thought, for a noise like that would in all probability have sent any animals within range to stampede as far away from the source as possible.

"So," I asked.  "Any comments?"

"This is pretty much the worst stuff I have ever had in all of my days, friend Szardos,"

"Really."

"It tastes like the Feerot River," Oopz said.  He licked his thick lips. "With a dead body in it."

"Ah," I replied.

"Got any more?"

Grinning, I bent down to fish out the other bottle.

"By the way, friend Oopz," I ventured, "May I ask you a question?"

"Of course, friend Szardos."

"Why the 'George' nonsense?"

"Oh, I just like the name."

"Ah."

Sometimes the answers are simple.
Life is funny that way.
