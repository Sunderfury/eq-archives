SCENE 3: NORTH FREEPORT, THREE DAYS AGO

The next day found me refreshed and confidant. It is quite remarkable what an invigorating evening with a Teir'Dal damsel can do for one's creative faculties.  I highly recommend it.  Presently, I had myself a scheme so cunning you could pin whiskers on it and call it a fox.  Most of the morning was spent putting it into practice.  A few aspects were extremely difficult to accomplish.   One task in particular was well-nigh impossible, but I got it done.  Later that afternoon, I had a leisurely lunch of spiced lion steak with a tossed side salad.  The chardonnay was piquant and well-suited to the meal.  I then took a small stroll, enjoying the day and making sure that my preparations were in place.  They were.  When night fell, I would be ready.

The meeting was designated to take place at the Jade Tiger at nightfall.  I was there at least an hour and a half before, drinking at the bar with an old friend.  I had known Bracewell for about three years now, a fine sort for a half-elf.  The bard spun the best stories, and one could easily spend an entire night listening to him.  The poor soul was not in a merry mood this evening however; he was in love yet again, with a lady who in all likelihood was totally unaware of his existence.  I nodded sympathetically at his tale of woe, all the while reminding myself of the dozens of times I had heard this same tale and variations thereof from his lips.  Bracewell was never happy unless he was suffering from some form of unrequited love.  Terrible as it was, it always inspired him to remarkable feats of eloquence.  Ah well, I thought.  Whatever sharpens your blade.

Young Jasud entered just twenty minutes after Bracewell and I sat down to dinner.  He was of medium height for a human, stocky and unlovely in his livery as a Knight of Truth.  His movements were arrogant yet furtive, and he clenched and unclenched his callused fists several times before striding purposely to the bar.  There was a cruelty about him I did not like, a haughty superiority around 'lessers' that is the hallmark trait of the bully.  Above all, there was a burning in his eyes that made me realize that the rumors I had heard about him were accurate: this was a fanatic.  I do not care for fanatics of any stripe.  They are a danger to all, including (if not especially to) their friends.  I would sooner trust a wounded griffin than one such as he. 

Young Jasud ordered no wine, nor any alcohol of any sort.  His angry glare cut short any comment the barman would have made when he ordered a glass of water.  He sipped at it, looking over the residents of the tavern for his contact.  His predatory gaze stopped at me for a long, long time.  It moved on soon after Bracewell and I got into still another heated argument over the merits of Teir'Dal women versus half-elf women.  Young Jasud's eyes then lingered at the only other Dark Elf in the hall, an expatriate named Dravolt, but desisted when it was clear that the poor man was so deep into his cups he could not lift his head from the table at which he slumped, much less conspire.  I imagine young Jasud's impatience and frustration had just about reached their limits when the human female tapped him on the shoulder.  I fought back a grin.  The fool had all but jumped.

"A pleasant evening Sir Jasud," she said casually, lifting a mug of beer to her lips.  "The night has spread its cooling blanket of sable over the sky."

I was gratified to observe young Jasud's mouth drop open as she gave him the code phrase. She was fairly unremarkable and blended easily with the rest of the clientele.  He took in her ordinary clothing and the presence of a dagger at her side, and forced the counterphrase through his now clenched teeth.

"Indeed," he rasped, "Like a gentle mask."

The human smiled and asked quietly, "You have what I seek?"

The paladin reached into his jerkin and pulled out the corner to a leather document case.

"I have it right here."

"May I have it?"

"You'll have it and more," he sneered.  "I was expecting a Dark Elf, you know."

"Many serve Neriak," she told him quietly.

"I suppose," he conceded, "I just expected better from our kind."

With an ugly smile he threw the contents of his waterglass into the human woman's face.  At that obviously prearranged signal, four paladins threw off the cloaks they hid themselves in and leaped from the seats in which I had previously spotted them, surrounding the woman with blades drawn. 

Young Jasud laughed harshly and leveled his sword at her, hissing "You are hereby arrested in the name of the Knights of Truth for espionage and attempted conspiracy against the sovereign states of Felwithe and Kelethin, our allies.  You will be remanded to the Temple of Marr for shriving and confinement, pending execution to be carried out tomorrow morning."

"Your death," he gloated, "Will serve as a lesson for the Dark Elf scum you serve to take their stinking plots elsewhere and beware the day we take our swords to their own throats."

The human woman wiped the water from her eyes and looked at the circle of steel surrounding her.  Drawing back to young Jasud, she grinned and winked at him.  A flash of light followed, blinding all within the tavern temporarily.  When we could focus again, the woman was gone, leaving nothing behind but her musical, mocking giggle.

"Gated," one of the paladins snarled.  "The bitch was a spellcaster."

Indeed she was, I laughed inwardly.  A most wondrous spellcaster, my dear foolish paladins.  Enoyna  (bless her wits and her steaming carnality) had played her part most beautifully.  The moment I had explained my plan to her that morning, she had responded by clapping her delicate hands in delight and agreeing to it most enthusiastically.  After briefing her about her role, I set out to scout the Tavern and establish a little 'insurance' in case my suspicions regarding young Jasud were correct.  As he even now turned my way with his burning, piggy eyes,  I congratulated myself for thinking so far ahead.

"You!" he bellowed. "Dark Elf!  You're in this as well!"

My friend Bracewell instantly got to his feet in outrage.  Ah, these romantics.  May the gods love them.

"Here now," He cried, "How dare you accuse my friend of any wrongdoing?"

But young Jasud had already made up his mind.

"He's a Dark Elf and a Rogue!  He's part of this, no mistake!"

The other paladins slowly began to approach our table, eyes hooded and dark.  It was time.

"Excuse me, good gentlemen," I began, rising slowly, my hands empty and spread benignly.  "If I am to be accused of something, should I not be apprised of what that something is, so I may ascertain whether of not I have in fact done this something?"

"You and your foul people seek to conquer the Faydark Forests, you filth," young Jasud snarled.  "We Knights of Truth set a trap to catch you and catch you we did!"  he cackled bloodthirstily.  "Your accomplice may have escaped, but you won't!  Seize him!"

Bracewell interposed himself between us.  The drama had reached its next peak; my cue had arrived and I had better take it before my well-meaning bard friend found himself subjected to expensive healing spells at the local hospital.

"My dear paladins," I began.  "Before you clap me in irons or worse, will you hear me?  The accusations you level against me are serious and I wish to respond to them.  You are the Knights of Truth, not shadowknights.  Therefore the truth must have some meaning to you."

Sir Breel lowered his sword.  Of all the paladins in North Freeport, I reluctantly admired this one.  His word was his bond, he treated others with kindness and courtesy, and he actually had a working brain in his head.

"I will hear what you have to say," he said in his deep bass voice. 

Young Jasud looked outraged, but he was clearly outranked by the older Knight.

I bowed.

"Thank you, Sir Breel," I intoned.  I cast a long, telling look at the paladins and the patrons who had not left when the rumpus had begun.  "You all know me," I began quietly. "I am a Teir'Dal by blood, and a Rogue by profession.  That much is true.  The rest, I fear, is folly.  To associate guilt with my race is ridiculous.  If that is the case, then every last one of you is damned for every evil the Freeport Militia has done, since they are made up of humans like yourself.  To condemn me for my profession is equally fell.  Does every Paladin of Marr know precisely what his or her guildmaster does or thinks, or at what secret tasks the other paladins are assigned?  I think not.  I have come to North Freeport many times over the years, always under flag of neutrality.  I have never burgled, pickpocketed or engaged in any form of confidence trickery at any time.  Indeed, I have often served you and the citizenry on several occasions."

I paused at looked down at my empty, long-fingered hands.

"You know that by the color of my skin and by my trade I am shunned throughout most of Norrath," I continued gently.  "Welcome only in my own city or that of the Trolls and Ogres to the South.  I have never visited the South.  Freeport, therefore, is my only refuge outside of Neriak from the harsh world in which I must make my living.  I am no hate-worshipper.  I would not jeopardize the trust I have worked so hard to build, nor lose the friends I have here for my Guild.  I am a rogue, gentleman, free to take or reject assignments at will.  Free to live my life as I see fit.  And I chose a long time ago not to bring harm my second home."

"Bravo," Bracewell cried, applauding vigorously.  Young Jasud glared at me with naked murder in his eyes, but the others were nodding slowly.  I had them.  Now to slit the throat.

"If accusations are to be leveled at me, I reject them and ask for actual evidence against me," I continued.  I locked gazes with young Jasud and set my jaw purposely.  "I also respectfully suggest that it would be in the best interest of you and the Order you serve to examine more closely the verity of my accuser."

Young Jasud purpled.  Sir Breel turned to me, face darkening.

"What exactly do you mean by that statement, Szardos?" he inquired dangerously.

Not taking my eyes off young Jasud, I calmly replied, "I have heard rumors regarding this one.  Ugly rumors."

It took two paladins to restrain young Jasud from leaping upon unarmed little me with his nasty sharp sword.  Strong fellows, these paladins.

"You will tell me these rumors, Szardos," Sir Breel demanded.

I proceeded to relate all the rumors I had heard from the townsfolk yesterday regarding their dislike and cautions about young Jasud and gave Sir Breel their names.  Questioning them would provide the Knights with the same information and would only serve to blacken young Jasud and cleanse mine own reputation.  I paused for effect, and then launched the killing blow.

"There is another matter," I whispered, "but one I will not disclose in public."

They leaped upon that statement like fish to a wriggling lure.  The patrons, drunk and sober, were all ordered to leave the Tavern at once.  On his way out, Bracewell gave me the handsign that meant 'you will tell me what this was all about later?'  I gave him a barely visible nod.  He threw me a cheerful wink and left with the others.

"Now, then," Sir Breel declared, "What is this other matter?"

I sighed with remorse.

"This is as close as I shall ever come to betraying my Guild, Sir Knight," I told him carefully, "So respect my courage and my confidence."

Sir Breel nodded gravely.

"Approximately two months or so ago, I overheard Elder N'Mar of the Mask talking with a courier about a new 'advantage' he had gained in North Freeport."

Several of the paladins growled audibly.  They knew of N'Mar, and that he was the guildsman in charge of Ebon Mask operations in Freeport.  Theirs was a frustrated hatred, as N'Mar has never left Neriak since he was two and thirty, preferring to work his schemes through subordinates and informers.

"The 'advantage' he spoke of was a new ally he had gained.  A Knight of Truth he had subverted."

"LIAR!" Jasud screamed.  "You seek to divert suspicion away from yourself by condemning me falsely!"

I regarded him sadly.

"Do you think it easy for me to share information from my Guild with you, Sir Knight?  I do what I do reluctantly, torn between conflicting loyalties.  In truth I have no evidence for these rumors.  That N'Mar has been using you and paying you for the past two months is only heresay."

Sir Breel stared at both of us: me, calm and steady; young Jasud, panting and swearing.  The next few moments were delicate indeed.  His reaction would make or break me.

"There must be proof!" he roared.  "One of you must provide proof against the other, else we are doomed to live in suspicion of you both tor the rest of our days!"

Young Jasud was thunderstruck.  "You would take the word of this Dark Elf trash over me?  I am your brother in arms!"

Sir Breel gave him a hard look and said the words I was waiting for him to voice aloud.

"So was Lucan."

"Sir Breel," I interrupted softly, ever the helpful gallant. "I have nothing to hide.  My room at Tassel's Tavern and my person are at your disposal to search to your satisfaction."

"You have nothing more to add, Szardos?" he inquired.

I drew my brows together in concentration.

"No, Sir Knight, nothing.  Except ... well, I know not if it is important, but I do recall something else N'Mar said that evening.  Something along the lines of his informer having a tapestry more valuable than any would realize.  I don't know what that could mean or whether it is important."

One the paladins restraining young Jasud looked up in alarm.  Turning to his charge he said, "You have a tapestry in your quarters, do you not?  I have seen it."

As young Jasud looked on in horror, Sir Breel turned to another Knight and ordered him to arrange for searches of our rooms and belongings and to question the people I had named.  They left quickly, a grim set to their lips.  I gave my weapons to Sir Breel, and permitted him to search me.  He found nothing, of course (I had left all of my usual surprises back in my room and had destroyed the battered militia helmet early this morning).  After much argument, young Jasud did the same.  Then we all sat down to wait.

An hour later, the paladins returned with barely suppressed rage smoldering in their faces.  One of them came forward and slapped a slim leather packet onto the table before Sir Breel.  "The citizenry confirm Szardos' statements," he barked.  "And I found this," he went on, his voice replete with disgust, "behind the tapestry in Jasud's room." Sir Breel's hands were steady as he opened the packet.  Inside he found a pouch with twenty gold pieces padded with cloth to prevent clinking and a letter.  The letter was a note of congratulations and thanks for information leading to the death of Mojax Hikspin, a Knight of Truth stationed in the West Commonlands.  The letter was signed by N'Mar of the Ebon Mask and dated last month, a sevenday after Hikspin's death.

We now had our 'bloody dagger.'
