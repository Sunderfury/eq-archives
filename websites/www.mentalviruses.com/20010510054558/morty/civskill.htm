The Arts of Civilization
Skills that make life easier

The Necromancer does not spend every moment engaged in life-or-death combat and manipulating dark powers. There are many other worthwhile pursuits - hobbies, if you wish - which may be found appealing. Some of these pursuits, of course, are more appealing than others.

In the "more appealing" category are those skills which offer the Necromancer alternatives to standing around bored while waiting for that shadow knight to show up. Some of these are entertaining diversions in and of themselves, and some are more supportive by nature. All of them are available to the starting Necromancer, and all are limited by level to a maximum of 5x(level+1).

Alcohol Tolerance
Level 1

Most Necromancers tend to avoid drinking as a regular practice, since it leads to a temporary intelligence penalty. However, occasions do arise, and increases in this skill will help reduce the negative effects of imbibing. This skill is, not surprisingly, increased by drinking.

Begging
Level 1

Just in case the Necromancer is feeling too popular, the practice of begging is available to help stem the tide of love and adoration. Repeated begging attempts are an invitation to attack the beggar. Successful begging results in the gain of a few coins, although rumors persist of skilled beggars gaining powerful items from the right marks. While no self-respecting Necromancer needs this skill, some may find it amusing to pose as a beggar, and then blast uncharitable souls into oblivion when they refuse to cough up some change.

Bind Wound
Level 1

By using this skill and a bandage, the Necromancer can heal small wounds. At lower levels this is quite expensive, as bandages cost around 5 silver a piece. When it becomes affordable, though, it is worth getting into practice, as the timely application of healing can be life-saving (and there won't always be a convenient rat to drain the soul out of). Having a friendly Magician on hand can be a great help in this, as they can conjure up bandages at a low level.

Fishing
Level 1

Ahhh, fishing. The natural extension of necromancy. With a pole and some bait, you can acquire fish for food, daggers for your pets, sandals for your feet, and fish scales for spell reagents. It also gives you something to do while you're waiting for a boat, or meditating near water. All in all, a good thing.

Sense Heading
Level 1

The ability to know which way you're facing is amazingly useful. Practice this skill until you've reached the maximum for your level. Since this skill is particularly slow to advance at lower abilities, you'll be practicing it pretty much constantly until about level 5 or 6. It's tedious, but you'll be glad you did.

Swimming
Level 1

Your level of ability in swimming determines how fast you swim, how quickly you lose stamina, and how long you can hold your breath. There are a lot of places that can only be reached by swimming, and quite a few places where swimming is, if not necessary, very convenient. Always keep your swimming skill reasonably high.

Trade skills are used to create new items of various types. This typically involves buying the appropriate pieces from a merchant, putting them in some container, and combining the pieces into a new, useful item. There are many things that can only be obtained through the application of a trade skill.

As it turns out, the Necromancer is poorly suited to a life of trade. Merchants will overcharge us for the items we need (if they don't try to kill us outright), and they won't give us a fair price when we sell back to them. It's a losing proposition. As if that weren't enough, most of the things that come out of the various trade skills are worthless to us. There are a couple of exceptions, but for the most part, the Necromancer should stick to killing things and raising the dead, and let the timid spin their pots.

Baking
Level 1

Baking allows the creation of tasty food from otherwise inedible hunks of dead monsters. Aside from the obvious advantages of finding food in the wild and fewer trips to those pesky food merchants, it also gives you the chance to share some food with your companions. Baked goods are also noticeably lighter than rations for the weight-conscious. This is one trade skill the Necromancer is advised to pursue - there's nothing like devouring your enemies. Baking doesn't advance very quickly, but it's not painfully slow to do so either. Just don't try to get your ingredients from the baker in the West Plains of Karana.

Brewing
Level 1

By taking a few odd ingredients and mixing them properly, you can create an alcoholic beverage. Whoopee. Alcohol decreases your intelligence (unless you've got a great deal of alcohol tolerance), which isn't exactly what we're looking for. The skill does go up fast and the ingredients are cheap, but Necromancers are some of the soberest folks I've met.

Fletching
Level 1

Every Necromancer I know just can't wait to walk into Kelethin to pick up the fletching supplies to start a trade in creating bows and arrows for their Warrior and Ranger friends. Right. This skill is for those pitiable fools who can't call down necromantic doom to consume an enemy on the other side of a river.

Jewel Craft
Level 1

Skilled jewelers are able to combine metals and gems to create both magical and nonmagical rings, necklaces, and so on. Top of the line jewelry items are in reasonable demand and command a respectable price. There's a long tradition of gnomish jewelry making; sadly, Necromancers make lousy jewelers. Only Enchanters can enchant metals, and enchanted metal is required to make magical jewelry. If you can get enchanted metal, the sheer cost of the ingredients is daunting even if you're not hated by merchants. As if that weren't enough, the skill tends to advance slowly and failures destroy your expensive ingredients. Leave this to our Enchanter brethren.

Pottery
Level 1

There are a lot of things that a potter can make, including bowls, statues, and vials. Some of these items are used in other trade skills, and a few are useful on their own, so potters are occasionally in demand. Personally, I can think of nothing more dull. If you could make burial urns, canopic jars, or tombstones, there might be some appeal.

Smithing
Level 1

A smith can sharpen rusty weapons, create useful metal things like skewers, and will eventually aspire to create armor. This is a good and useful skill, worthy of a gnome's time and talents. It's also pretty much worthless to a Necromancer. More expensive than pottery but less expensive than jewel craft, this skill increases at a reasonable rate.

Tailoring
Level 1

By taking dead animal skins and following patterns, it is possible to create fairly decent armor and a handful of other useful items. Most armor is not suitable to a Necromancer, but advanced tailors can make silk garments that are quite serviceable. Patterns aren't very expensive, and certainly weigh less than pelts, so this might be an interesting hobby for some since you get to play with dead bodies. Tailoring increases at a reasonable rate as skills go.

Tinker
Level 16

Every gnome grows up learning how to tinker. Even the youngest gnome Necromancer has a tinkering skill of 50, although this cannot be increased without gaining 16 levels of experience. A gnome with a toolbox and some odds and ends can put together quite a few interesting things, from the standard compass to the unusual stalking probe to the famed aqua lung. There are a few unfortunate facts that keep this skill from being the true joy it deserves to be, however: the unique parts necessary are only available around Ak'Anon and Steamfont; the special parts range from somewhat to extremely expensive; failure is common and destroys all of those expensive parts; and, just to make things worse, the skill increases at a very, very slow rate. The true gnome will give it a try in spite of these difficulties.
