In the Beginning...

I began my crusade with Ultima Online.

Ultima Online laid before us an experience that was unlike any other. It was full of promise. "This is what I have been waiting for!" we all said to ourselves in unison. But the promise was never fulfilled. We all got a glimpse of what could be, and that's all we ever got.

Origin Systems promised us a dynamic economy. They promised us in-game ethics. They promised us life cycles and dragons that would attack the town when the players had been too voracious and they promised 24/7 connectivity with full customer support.

None of it happened. We had players stab other players in the back. We had players allowed back into town - absolved of their murderous ways by simply peeking into each other's bags and resetting their "karma". We had players bankrupting the economy by creating skullcap after skullcap and looting all of the available currency. We had mighty players fall by the humblest of spells and others getting poisoned by the act of shooting arrows into the venomous snakes that were clear across the screen.

Nothing was done about it. Yet OSI still continued to accept our money. We paid to beta test their buggy game and a year into the experience, things were still laughably broken. You can read my original manifesto here.

Months and years later, we were promised new expansions and editions. In an incredibly cynical maneuver, we were promised necromancy when UO was faced with the threat of EQ hitting the market. Does UO3D have necromancy now?

If our social contract affirms that we live in a meritocracy - whereby the best and the brightest attain the highest levels of success, fame, and respect - then those who are both incompetent and unethical deserve to be at the lowest steps of the social ladder. Why EA/OSI is getting their act together at this late date is beyond me, but it does demonstrate that while the millstone of justice might grind slowly, it grinds with inevitability.

As you well know, I am a different breed. There are others, like me, who walk this line, but we are few. We know your pain because we have lived it. We know your dissatisfaction because we have experienced it. We want your justice, so we do something about it.

Most feel unable to do anything against these big game companies. Some don't care. Some don't know. Dissatisfaction with your gaming experience is not something to be taken lightly. Not only do you dedicate your $10.00 a month to your leisurely pursuit, but also you dedicate your time. Hours upon hours each week, this is time that you will never get back.

We could be going to the movies, going bowling, going to nightclubs, hanging out with friends, swimming, riding skateboards, mountain biking, driving around or working out. Face it! In today's society there are hundreds of options available for our time, our money and us. But instead, we play games. If you're a game company, don't you dare cheat me out of my time. Don't you dare cheat me out of my life. Why? Because when you cheat me out of my time, you have just demonstrated that you don't give a damn about my life.

The moment your company stops caring about my time, is the moment I stop caring about your company. If you are going to maximize your extraction of my money - then I am going to maximize my extraction of value. If maximizing my value means that I post every problem with your game, then so be it. It may be YOUR game, but I paid for MY piece of it, and I'm going to get as much satisfaction out of that piece as I can.

We've paid good money for our time with you. You'd better hold to your promises. If you make us promises, you damned well better believe we're going to hold you to your word. We hold our family members to their word. We hold our friends to their word. We hold our spouses, bosses, teachers and employees to their word. We hold those who sell us products and services to their word. You make games? You're no different than the fellow who owns the hardware store down the street. You want a relationship with us? Expect to be held to your word. Quality businesses and people keep their word. If you don't want to be held to your word, don't try to sell us your product.

Don't lie to us. Lying to us is like OSI telling us Necromancy will be in soon and two years later it still has not been delivered. It's that simple.


The Road Less Traveled

The spectrum that is the industry feels I am every MMOG's redheaded bastard stepchild. I've been referred to as the "Anti-Christ" of online gaming and even less savory titles. I suppose the title is appropriate, after all, if you make an MMOG, I tell people how to run amok in it. That kind of power scares game developers. Why am I the scourge of the online gaming universe? It's because I care about the players. I care about promises made and promises kept. Most importantly, I care about promises broken.

I really do believe that most game companies want to do the right thing for their fans and consumers. They want to deliver value to their customers. They got into the business of making games because they want to share their worlds with the rest of us. This is certainly a noble goal. In every conversation I have with a developer, when I point to EA/OSI's shoddy treatment of its customer base, everyone said, "We're going to be different, we're going to care about our players."

Great. I'm all for that.

If you're a gaming company that listens to your players, that keeps an open line of communication with your players, that is willing to see adjustments need to be made or policies changed, then you will get the deserved recognition. Ironically enough, those companies with those open lines of communication are invariably the most capable. These companies usually have the shortest list of bugs.

Then there are game companies that deploy new features while old ones remain broken. They do not keep open lines of communication, they do not respond to bug reports. In the most extreme examples, they prevent any access, and along with it, any mechanism of conflict resolution. These companies usually fail to appraise their players of changes that are *going* to be implemented, and fail to enforce their rules to a single, authoritative, standard.

Strangely enough, most companies are a combination of the two sets of behaviors listed above. What we at the Dr.Twister Network are trying to do is look at how the game companies treat their player base, and reciprocate in kind.

Turbine, for example, is great with communications regarding their game engine, their network, updates, etc. They accept responsibility for their bugs and do not penalize players for what are developer failings. However at the same time, Turbine doesn't fix critical bugs that receive a lot of attention and complaints from the players. (Turbine Example)

The people at Turbine and Microsoft appear to be concerned that the player base of Asheron's Call is dropping by about 10% every month. Listen to what your players want. Implement it. You might be surprised when they stick around. Don't be surprised, however, when you don't listen and people leave. If you don't care about them, there's little reason they should care about the world you've built.

Ironically, by posting bugs and telling people how those bugs work, we may actually be keeping players around in these games longer than if they just saw people cheating around them, with nothing for them to do about it. It certainly strikes me as more unfair to let a bug reside with only a select few, than making the bug well-known and available to everyone, so that not only the "elite" will benefit.

Additionally, I don't think it's fair that only the "hardcore" gamers get to exploit the system. While we don't like anyone exploiting the system, the casual gamer who pays just as much money as the "hardcore" gamer should deserve the same access to information that the hardcore player enjoys. This belief means while we only intend to help the casual player protect themselves from exploits, the unintended outcome is that we make exploiting possible for the casual player. This is a regrettable, but obviously a necessary feature, and evil of getting bugs fixed by developers.

Asking nicely the first three times didn't work. By placing the power of information in everyone's hands, including developers, we play on an even level.


For What It's Worth

John Smedley, president of Verant, developers of EverQuest, told me "Until you become more legit you're not going to be successful as other game site."

The Dr.Twister Network isn't a game site. It is a bug site and a gaming advocate site. Just like there are advocates who make sure that the car you buy is safe, that the water you drink is clean, and the food you eat is pure, I do the same thing for games.

When food is bad or cars unsafe, it means that the maker of the car or the person who prepared the food didn't keep their part of the agreement. Cars get recalled only after someone exposes it to the public. Restaurants and other food vendors get shut down when someone reports food poisoning.

There is not a similar mechanism for gaming. So I make bugs public.

If this website remains 100% true to its mission, then I will continue to get bugs from the player community. I will continue to post them. Entities such as Verant, and Turbine, and others will always be uncomfortable and complain that I'm "ruining their game" but their games will get fixed. If they do not get fixed, then players will be open to exploit and be exploited by them.

If having a goal and meeting it determines success, then Smedley's advice is wrong. Sure, he may not like to have any contact with me. He may not like that I post the game-damaging exploits. But ultimately, I will be "more" successful by staying the course. If success is "notoriety" or "fame" or "appreciation by game developers" than Smedley's advice is correct. I do not share John Smedley's perspective on "success". "Electronic Arts, Verant, Turbine, etc, don't pay my bills. I have no responsibility to them, and they have none to me. However, the volunteers of the Dr.Twister Network and I have made a commitment to you, the members of the gaming public.

The games get fixed because people complain or enough people start cancelling their accounts, or sadly, enough people become disillusioned and start making everyone else's gameplay miserable. It has been demonstrated on a number of occasions that game companies do not fix these games because they like you or even because they "like" me. It all comes down the almighty dollar. We've been repeatedly shown that the only way to get any responsiveness from these game companies is to punch them in the moneybags.

I made an effort in trying to play nice. I put away the teeth and we're living with the result: bugs not getting fixed; games being released in broken states.

This site was no longer meeting its goals. I think it could be successfully argued that it still isn't. This site wasn't, and isn't the successful "catalyst of bug repair" which was its original conception. For evidence of such it seems only elementary to point you to the examples of the recently released WW2OL, and Anarchy Online.

Had I stayed the course, would things be different? Probably not. However, there is something to be said for fighting the good fight; for raging against the dying of the light. Perhaps with this example others would have more quickly picked up the same torch and made the fight more visible, and more uncomfortable for those who would desire to sell us broken, shoddy, and incomplete software. For that, I apologize to you, the gamer. By compromising, I may well have set back this fight for a good period of time.

The current state of affairs is broken and completely unacceptable. Something must change. At the very least, I hope that after reading this, you can agree that the parties which require change are neither you nor me.

So with all due respect to John Smedley, I'll remain "alternative" instead of going "legit".


In the Eye of the Beholder

There are gamers that have a problem with me. They think that I shouldn't be posting the bugs that these games possess. I somehow contribute to the ruining of these games. I would respectfully posit that these players don't care about the long-term health of this industry, but instead get angry with me because the information I provided allowed a n0oB to exploit their character or house, or somehow otherwise impacted their gameplay.

They're entitled to their opinion. They pay the same $10.00 a month.

However, a closer examination, I think will reveal they don't care so much about the game. They care more about their in-game character. They care that somehow "they" are going to get "screwed" because I posted these bugs and exploits. Yes, their chance of being individually targeted goes up, but they also now know that they can be targeted and they in turn raise a cry with the game makers.

Some people would rather never know it was possible to target them. They would prefer to remain blissfully unaware. I despise these sorts of people, and so their opinion of me is, as far as I am concerned, really of little worth.

Those who only care about "their" character have a loser motivation. If we are just as committed to the notion of community as we are to meritocracy, then airing the dirty laundry is done, ultimately, for the health of the community. It is not done to further its failings. After all, only through the recognition of our problems are we able to correct them. Only through understanding why your online experience got messed up are you, the player, able to make an informed decision as to whether you will stay, whether you will exploit the game yourself or whether you will give up the game entirely.

If you didn't know that people were being victimized similarly, or that there are means of protecting yourself, you might just continue along your merry way. This would mean that the game companies would not have to fix the problems, compensate you for your loss, even the playing field, or face losing you as a customer. This is exactly the kind of situation they want, and exactly the kind of thing I want to expose. Those game companies which refuse to change this mode of operation are really operating in bad faith with you, as they obviously intended not to fix the bug or exploit, but did still intend to continue to receive your money. Just like every consumer, you should be able to make informed decisions. You may hate me, but I make that informed decision possible.

Now, in light of this perspective, I would argue that it follows that I am providing a public service. I am making game companies accountable where they usually refuse such accountability. No, I don't make the rules. As a consumer advocate - looking out for you and me - I enforce the rules. When you think about it, there's really not much more available to me than to post bugs. Game companies have shown repeatedly that they don't listen to bug reports. They don't respond to inquiries. They don't release information or fix things unless they have to.

All the intermediary steps have already been tried. They have shown to be ineffective. When what you're doing doesn't work, you adopt a strategy that does. Given the passion with which people love and hate my work, obviously I'm doing something right. However I'll tell you again, if you don't "get it" or don't like it, please don't come back to the site.


Only Going Will Get You There

I've now stated my position. I'm willing to meet the game companies halfway. There are ways that we can work together to post bugs, bring things to public light, and make better games. There are ways to allow you, the gaming public to make informed decisions about the games you play. Making better games takes commitment, and when a game company shows that I have a higher commitment to making their game better than they do, they will suffer.

Again, I reiterate - to the game companies of the world: You fear my kind and me. This is because my team and I represent a group of angry game players. I do not want this to be the case. I too want to be a team player. But I can only play so far as you will allow me. If being a team player means that I must give up my principles and join you in agreeing that it is acceptable to release sloppy games, then I must refuse. I am not alone however. Everyone who cares about a quality gaming experience eventually comes to my perspective.

If someone like me does not exist, you might find that computer games in general, and online games in particular, will slide down a slope into a morass of disgusted, disaffected players, and bankrupt and demoralized game companies.

This is why we have a society which values freedom of speech. This is why we have competitive markets. We have been shown time and time again that the combination of competitive pressures and critical (even vicious) feedback forces companies to make products that satisfy.

Let's raise the standards; let's bring quality back to the games we play.
