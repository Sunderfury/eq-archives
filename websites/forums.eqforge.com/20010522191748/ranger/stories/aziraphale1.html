Stories from Norrath


The lone figure walked through the dense mass of trees, humming quietly to himself a tune from his childhood. His feet made little sound on the soft earth beneath and the heat of the day caused a light sweat to break out along his brow. A rivulet of his body’s moisture ran down the centre of his back - he wished it would soon rain and break this terrible warmth.

Almost as if Tunare had read his thoughts (and he believed in all sincerity that she could easily do so), a drop from the heavens found it’s way through the canopy above and landed on his left cheek. Many more soon followed and Aziraphale found a large oak under which to sit for a spell. The huge branches tried their best to keep him dry but the rain found it’s way through in sufficient quantity to keep him well refreshed. He leaned back against the mighty hardwood and listened to the lulling sound of the water falling all around him.

He stared intently at a drop of rain slowly running down the length of his arm. It followed a convoluted path, zigzagging back and forth through the wrinkles in his leather tunic, and it brought an analogy to him regarding his own life. After years of travelling the globe, he had happened upon the lands of Norrath; finally settling here for good. Had he a birds-eye-view of his life as the God’s did, he was sure his meandering would have appeared much like that of the raindrop.

Aziraphale leaned his head back and closed his eyes for a moment. The smell of pines wafted through the air and the sharp odour tore through his memory like a knife. Suddenly his mind’s eye was back at his birthplace, on the Isle of Barra. He could see his mother’s beautiful Elven face, bringing him his supper as he sat by the end of the path to their house. He would wait there most days for his father to return from the mines, and on those occasions when he ran late, she allowed Aziraphale to eat outside.

He thought back to his father lying in bed, dying from the horrible black dust that was decaying his lungs. He remembered the news of his mother’s slaughter at the hands of a band of Ogres shortly after Aziraphale left home; recalled the teachings he’d received from them both on tolerance and fairness. He’d done, he thought, his best to do them proud and hoped that Tunare would keep them safe and warm for all eternity.

The loud snap of a twig jarred him from his daydreams and in one swift motion Aziraphale was up on his feet, his large sword clutched lightly in his right hand. The rain had dulled the sounds in the woods and his normally acute hearing had allowed something to get quite close without his noticing. He scanned the forest with his sharp eyes and spotted a dark shape passing between two trees perhaps fifty yards in front of him. He quietly closed the distance to get a better look.

As he approached, the dark shape began to take clearer form and the ranger quickly recognized it as that of a large dog-like beast. It walked on two legs, slightly crouched, and was sniffing the air as it went. Aziraphale knew it was a Gnoll, but he’d not seen one quite this large before. His heart raced a bit faster and he quickened his pace.

Whether it caught his scent or it’s hearing was that much better than Aziraphale had thought, the beast suddenly turned and looked right at him. They were now merely twenty yards apart and the Half-elf could clearly make out the markings on the tunic worn by the dark creature - identifying it as one of the Sabretooths from Blackburrow. But that den was several miles from here and to see a fully matured Gnoll out this far was a rarity indeed.

With a loud howl the brute ran towards Aziraphale, waving its arms in the air and baring its sharp teeth. The ranger finally noticed the long sword the beast carried and brought his own two-handed version up to the ready. Setting his feet apart to steady himself, he awaited the imminent collision.

The Gnoll brought its weapon down hard and Aziraphale quickly sidestepped to avoid it, bringing his own up in a high arc and catching the gruff creature across the ribcage. The blade sliced cleanly and he was rewarded with a satisfying grunt of pain from the Gnoll. He followed with a firm kick and connected well with the Gnoll’s left knee, but it was like kicking a tree and did little to harm the creature. Spinning around to face it, Aziraphale brought his sword up high once again…or tried to.

Still somewhat new to the large weapon, he forgot how heavy and slow it was. Intending to get a quick attack on the beast’s backside, he instead found himself well open to the return blow. The Gnoll’s sword cut deeply into Aziraphale’s chest, and he could clearly see his own blood flinging off as the steel passed by his face on the continuing arc. He reeled back and brought the mighty two-handed blade down again, catching the Gnoll well on the left shoulder, burying the sharp steel easily down to the large blades of bone in the hulking dog’s back.

Although the Gnoll’s long sword was rusted and in poor condition, it was still quite lethal and continued to cut through Aziraphale’s leather armour again and again. Worse, the mangy dog had teeth and claws, and they were adding quickly to the wounds the young ranger was suffering. Blood flowed freely from both adversaries, staining the undergrowth beneath their feet.

The battle continued back and forth in this manner for what seemed like an eternity. Damage to both parties was heavy and anyone witnessing it would have been hard pressed to choose who was landing the most hits. While the Gnoll had sharp claws and a fast sword, Aziraphale had the huge blade of his two-handed weapon, and his kicks were starting to take their toll. Exhaustion was closing in on them, along with the dark of night.

The Gnoll took a hard swing and Aziraphale quickly ducked. His reactions were slowing as the battle wore on and the blade barely passed over his head. The beast had lost the use of its left arm, and with that last pass of its sword, had left itself open. The Half-elf quickly seized the opportunity and brought his own sword to bear as hard as he could. The long blade arced swiftly across the Gnoll’s midriff, and kept swinging so effortlessly Aziraphale was sure he had missed! He could almost feel the strike he knew would come next, and said a silent prayer to Tunare for forgiveness of his failure.

But the creature howled louder than ever; a painful sharp sound that he’d not heard before. It was then that he saw the gash he’d opened up with that last swing. The Gnoll dropped its sword and looked down at itself, desperately trying to push back with its one good hand the intestines that were now spilling out of the large rent. Weariness swarmed Aziraphale and he dropped to his knees, letting his sword fall to the ground. As if in a mirror the Gnoll also dropped to its knees, and for a moment they stayed that way, appearing in mutual prayer. Then the dark beast fell over on its side and lay still, its tongue hanging limply out of its mouth.

Aziraphale gulped down air as quickly as he could, answering his lungs begging calls for oxygen. Sounds from the woods started to come back to him as the ringing in his ears from the din of battle subsided, and he noticed for the first time that it had stopped raining. He sat down to recover himself and watched a lone drop of blood wend its way down the arm of his tunic. Whose blood, he did not know.

When he was able to concentrate once more, he began working the limited magic he’d been taught at his guild. Weaving a complex pattern in the air with his hands, he called upon the invisible powers that surrounded everything in Norrath. Some of his wounds closed a bit, and the bleeding diminished enough that he knew he would not die from its loss. Aziraphale said a quick thank you to Tunare for granting him this small power; for while not even close to being in the same class as those who used spells for a living, he was grateful none the less.

Shakily he rose and walked over to the inert form of the Gnoll. He bent down and searched the pockets and pouches, finding only a scrap of paper. Opening the note, he read through it quickly. Then once again to be sure. The implications of the words within came to him right away and at once he knew who this beast was. His mind raced back to the previous week, when he had spoken to Larsk Juton of Surefall Glade. Larsk had told Aziraphale to be on the lookout for a Gnoll by the name of Furgutt, who occasionally roamed the region of Qeynos Hills. And to bring him proof should he ever encounter and slay the beast.

The weary ranger bent over, picked up the large two-handed sword from where he had dropped it, and held it across his shoulder with his right hand. He then stooped over the Gnoll and, with his other hand, grasped a handful of thick fur on the beast’s head and began to drag the body. A few yards away lay a fallen tree and Aziraphale laid the creature’s limp head over the trunk. With no small effort, he raised his mighty sword high above his head and brought it down in one swift stroke upon the neck of the slain Gnoll. The head severed cleanly, rolled off the trunk and bounced to a stop on the ground. Aziraphale bent and tore the mangled jersey off the remains of the Gnoll and wrapped the head carefully, so as not to ruin his other goods. He then stored the package neatly in his backpack, along with the rusty long sword, and headed back in the direction of the Glade…humming a song to himself from his childhood.
