Stories from Norrath

My ears hurt.

It seemed to be a strange feeling, given the warm summer-like weather, but nevertheless, they did. Looking over my shoulder, I realized that in all likelihood, any pains that I might be feeling were more of a psychological nature than actual physical distress. They were still at it – shouting and stomping, chasing hither and yon after any small creature that caught their fancy. One fell backwards onto the muddy path, with an amusingly shocked look upon his face – I had told them to leave the basilisks alone. I shook my head, beads of water dripping from my chin, and thought about how I had gotten into this silly situation.

I am a hunter. It is what I do, both by choice and vocation. My days are spent running across the land, crouched behind trees, or sighting down the shaft of an arrow. To me, there is nothing more exciting than the thrill of the chase…the pounding heart…the adrenaline rush when the prey is finally cornered…. And if I can make a few coins in the process, it is all the better.

After hunting in my home forest of Faydwer until the constant chatter running through the trees drove me to distraction (I can easily understand why the orcs attack everyone in sight at times.), I thought I might travel to the docks of Butcherblock and see what opportunities I might find across the seas. I stocked up on my gear and picked up a few of my recently crafted bows as well, hoping to make some coin in the greater market away from my homelands. With the rising sun behind me, I began my journey. The forest was peaceful in the early light, and it was this peace that put me into a very pleasant mood. Soon, I traveled through the pass into Butcherblock, thinking to soon reach my departure point.

Not long after entering into the dwarven lands, I was approached by a group of locals. Apparently my name had proceeded me for they knew that I was a ranger-trained hunter who sometimes hired out my skills. It seems that a pair of ogres roams the mountains, preying upon any wayfarers they come upon. These adventurous dwarves had gathered for the goal of destroying these creatures (and collecting the reward offered, I later learned). As I had helped one of their brothers in the Steamfont mountains a week or so earlier, they immediately offered me 20 pieces of gold to help them find one of these creatures – a surly beast known as Corflunk.

For 20 pieces of gold, my friends, I’d lead them anywhere…or so I thought.

Hastily sealing our bargain, I asked them where this foul creature usually hunted. Knowing the nature of a beast allows a hunter to anticipate the prey’s every move. With that knowledge in mind, I began my work. The mountains are a beautiful place, easy on the feet and eyes, and I felt sure that my skills would soon lead them to their goal…and me to my gold.

Being a solitary traveler, I am used to refining my choices, focusing in upon one goal, and pursuing it until completion. As such, that is what I expected this hunt to be. I could not have been more wrong. My employers seemed to have the stealth of a mammoth calf and the attention span of a pixie. Every creature that crossed our path fell victim to their swords and axes. Sometimes, to my amusement, they bit off more than they could handle. Quickly hiding myself among the trees, I would watch them as they ran, short legs pumping, to the nearest guard station, yelling for assistance. A simple hunt…the thrill of the chase…the valiant slaying of a foul beast…. This was merely the playing of children, and I began to feel that the 20 pieces of gold were nothing more than a collar about my neck. And to top it off, my fine summer day clouded over, and rain began to fall.

My reverie was broken by a scent brought upon the wind. Even the downpour soaking us to the bone could not cover the smell of the carrion-beast. With a slashing motion of my axe, I told my employers to cease their prattle, eliciting looks of surprise as they lifted their stunned friend from the muddy path in which he’d fallen. Scanning the trees…. Listening for the movement…. Breathing in deeply the scents of the forest….

There! With a wave to the dwarves, I ran off into the woods. No natural beast of this area would cause the hush that had fallen among the birds or the smell that drifted on the air. Corflunk was here, and I would find him!

His tracks proved what I had already guessed. The beast was easily twice my size and probably more like three times my weight. Whatever foul anger he held, he expressed violently to all the trees and animals that happened to cross his path. I followed his trail over a hill and through a winding valley, finally reaching…

The Beast.

Corflunk, for it could be no other, stood some 20 yards away with his back turned to me. His scarred face was the sweetest thing I had seen in hours. Not only had I again brought the prey to bear, but I also had fulfilled the terms of my bondage and would soon be free. Turning to the dwarves, I caught the eyes of their paladin leader, signing to him in the language of hunters around the world (which I only hoped these underground dwellers would understand) that their prey was just ahead and unaware of us. I quickly began to think of an ambush plan…and was shocked into stunned silence as 5 dwarves began yelling at the tops of their lungs, crashing through the trees like miniature bulls, and dove headfirst at the creature easily as big as all of them together! Like a pack of hounds upon a bear, they threw themselves at the ogre, blades swinging and battle cries shattering the silence. Amazing! Even as I regarded their lack of sense with shocked amazement, I felt grudging respect for their bravery…or their madness!

The Beast, however, obviously felt no such respect. As the first cry sounded out, he turned with a look of confusion upon his face. This look turned to feral glee as the dwarves careened down the hillside, and he lifted his treelike paws to welcome them. The battle joined, and I rapidly began to wonder who the victor would be. The blades of the dwarves bit deeply into the Beast’s hide, but his massive blows drove one after another of the small ones to the ground. Slaying the beast had not been part of my hire – it had even been mentioned that I was not to engage the creature as this was "work fer the stout blades of dwarves, not the fair nature of elves." However, my employers were falling fast, with only the healing ability of their cleric and the paladin’s god-given powers keeping them in the fray. Corflunk was too much. The hounds had cornered the wolf…and found him to be a kodiak instead!

Shaking my head, I limbered my bow, its fine elm arms glistening wetly in the rain. Sighting down the shaft of the first arrow, I let fly. Before the shaft had thudded into the creature’s fur covered chest, I had strung another. Shaft after shaft I sent, pounding into the Beast until I though surely my arm would weaken to the point of dropping my bow. Two of the dwarves remained – the paladin and another warrior who fought bravely with a spear twice his own size. Neither of them looked as if they could last another minute, but luckily, neither did Corflunk. With the realization of his doom finally penetrating the thick haze of his anger, and the Beast turned tail and began to flee. Calling upon the gifts of my training, I muttered the words of magic that would bind the plants of the mountains to my will, and they began wrapping themselves about the giant, immobilizing him where he stood. The dwarves, knowing themselves to be at the end of their abilities, did not follow, but looked to me and my bow instead. Nodding to the paladin, I raised the bow one last time, aiming the shaft at the Beast’s one remaining eye. Corflunk glared at me, spouting thick unpronounceable syllables in some dark tongue…and then fell to the ground as the shaft pierced his brain. The Beast lay dead.

Silence. No words spoken. The surviving dwarves fell to their knees, doing what they could to staunch the wounds of their fellows. I went with them, offering up what healing magic and binding of wounds I could do. This accomplished, the paladin walked to the ogre’s corpse and performed the grisly duty of removing Corflunk’s head, which he dropped into a large bag. Limping back to me, he nodded and tossed me a small bag that *clinked* as I caught it.

He winked at me, holding out a hand. "Yer not much of a fighter with that axe, do ye know that?"

I nodded back as we shook. "And you, my short and bold friend, are not much of a hunter."

Andruil
