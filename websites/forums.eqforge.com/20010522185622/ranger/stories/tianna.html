Stories from Norrath

Ugh. I woke up one morning behind a tree in Surefall Glade. I looked around me dazedly to confirm that yes, indeed, I was behind a tree. I stood up slowly and it hit me -- I had spent all of the previous evening carousing with my beloved Findail and a couple of friends that we hadn't seen in a very long time. We had a blast, consuming great quantities of fishwine and scaring all the people on the Qeynos dock with our levitating and staggering hither and thither over the water, dock and boat. And I had awakened still drunk. Not a good way to start the day, especially when my goal for this day was to become a master fletcher, like my father before me.

Shaking off the tendrils of hazy sleepiness, I decide on making a run to town. I needed to go to the bank anyways. I stepped around the still sleeping form of my betrothed and headed off through the caves to the hills surrounding Qeynos. Not many were yet awake and the dawn was dewy and sweet smelling. The run was pure exhilaration and I was able to think clearly again by the time I reached Qeynos. I ran straight to the bank and then headed back to Surefall. No time to dawdle today.

Back in Surefall I bought up all of the arrow parts that I could carry from Jarse Kedison and quickly made my way into the bear caves, so that I could be clear of any distractions. Fletching has been difficult for me, and it requires the utmost concentration on my part. Still, I continue with it, in memory of my father. I settled down, balancing the book of fletching on one leg and bringing my backpack full of arrow parts closer to me. I pulled out the pieces I would need and started toiling away -- 'point, shaft, nock, fletch', over and over again, sometimes badly mangling the shaft or fletch, but most times coming up with right serviceable arrows. In no time at all, I had depleted my stock of parts, and a bundle of arrows lay on the ground next to me. I got up, stretched, and started to run back to Jarse. I figured he'd want to buy my arrows to sell to other rangers, and I could get better parts and learn to put those together. This is what I was thinking as I stepped lightly around the sleeping bears, patting the little ones on their heads. Imagine my surprise at suddenly coming face to face with a poacher! He didn't even have a chance to really see me before I leapt to attack him, my scimitar flashing. Although he put up a good battle, my rage and fighting skill helped me get the better of him. I took his head to Gillarian, who had told me of the poachers that would have long ago made the bears of Surefall extinct, without the help of the rangers and druids sworn to protect them.

Jarse took my arrows in exchange for more arrow parts, and I set to fletching again. These arrows were more difficult, but much nicer, and I took pride in seeing how well they looked when I had done with them. Again I trundled my load back to Jarse and again bought more parts, always upgrading and learning to make better arrows. He commented on my growing skill and I began to grow excited at the prospect of holding the title of master. At long last I exhausted my reserves of money and yet had not quite attained my goal. It was close though. I began to think about the treant wood bow staff I had set aside for the day that I could try making a Trueshot bow. I remembered that I needed to get some Dwarven wire from a recalcitrant dwarf in the city of Kaladim. It was so close. I decided to go back home, even though it was a long journey, and attempt to make my Trueshot bow in the land where it was born, the Greater Faydark.

I had made the trip between Surefall and Kelethin many times, and as such, knew the best route to take like the back of my hand. I nudged Findail to tell him of my plan, but he was still fast asleep. He knew the tricks of how to camoflauge himself and others, and could call down the spirit of a wolf to quicken your feet and speed your journey.

The trip would be much longer without him, but he looked as if he could sleep forever. I could not wait. I left him a note and started my journey. My path was clear and the journey uneventful, until I crossed the old timber bridge and found myself in the Eastern Plains of Karana. I shivered and wondered how I could get across, without knowing how to camoflauge myself. I knew how terrible the claws of the griffons could be, especially when a lion or two joined in. I cursed myself for my foolishness and was just about to turn back, when I saw that the way was open before me. I scrunched up my determination and I ran for my life, aiming for a certain gorge that could be easily traversed, if one knew the way. Throughout this heart-pounding run I had to quell the urge to look behind me to see if I was being followed, and I gave a wide berth to an evil eye that I spotted in the distance. Soon the walls of the gorge loomed over me, and half of the most harrowing part of the journey was behind me. I rested for a short time, in the lee of the mountain. Gathering my fortitude again, I prepared to climb halfway up the wall of the gorge and run along the tiny path that I knew was up there. First though, was a short sprint to the place where one could find a way up. I sprinted in and rounded a curve and stopped dead in my tracks -- for there were two evil eyes and a vertitable carnival of minotaurs ahead. I must admit I was shaking with fear at this point. I had lately learned how to sneak up behind monsters and escape their notice, but I hadn't been practicing this skill at all. Even while mentally kicking myself for that, I decided to try and sneak along the wall. Much to my surprise, it actually worked, and I was able to come to the place where one can begin to climb without any of them noticing me. I was terrified to turn my back and start to climb, but I had to. So I climbed up. Once at the tiny ledge, I began to quietly walk towards the far end of the Gorge, heart beating very fast. I jumped for joy when I saw the entrance to the goblin clan of Runnyeye's caves. I had made it, and the rest of the run ahead of me was nowhere near as dangerous.

The rest of the journey was uneventful. Trantor Everhot was as chancy as always, but he gave me a spool of his dwarven wire once he saw that I had a couple of his favourite drinks to give to him. I arrived in the sweet city in the trees of my birth, and I was nearly ready to attempt to create a Trueshot bow. I gathered up all of the supplies that I would need (still more arrows to make though, before I could feel comfortable enough to be called a master, as well as all of the components for the bow) and headed into the less populated area of the forest (the Lesser Faydark as we call it) to do my fletching and my first bow attempt in peace and quiet.

When the arrows I was making began to seem trivial to make, I decided it was time. Even though I didn't expect to be able to make the bow right away, my heart skipped a beat as I gathered the materials together in my lap. I opened the book on bowyering and laid it tenderly on the grass next to me. I gathered the treant wood staff into my lap and began to move the plane in a gentle sweeping motion over the staff. When the curve was just right I began to attach the dwarven wire and the micro-servo and I botched up the whole job. The staff cracked and now I'll need to get another.

Still I feel I learned from the experience. And there's lots of arrows left that I don't know how to make. I will learn them and try again, and again, until that bow is mine. In the meantime, I got together the materials for an ashwood bow, and was quite happy when I made it just fine.

Not bad for my first bow attempt!

Tianna, master fletcher on her way to becoming grand master.
