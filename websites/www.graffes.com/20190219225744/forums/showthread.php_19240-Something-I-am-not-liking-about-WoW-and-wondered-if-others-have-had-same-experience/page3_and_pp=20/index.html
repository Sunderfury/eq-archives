    There's nothing about EQ that requires the EQ-communities interaction to stay there, as opposed to, for example, moving it to WoW. There's nothing about WoW that stops you from socializing with other players if you want to. Sure, you'd be less effective at advancing in WoW if you spend a couple minutes just chatting between fights. But if you're really playing for the community, and not the game -- then why would you care if you're less effective?

    Either the game matters, in which case some aspect of the game itself keeps players coming back, not the community alone; Or the game doesn't matter, and having to negatively impact your performance to socialize isn't a barrier to community formation.

    In EQ, the only players you see around are those who are active community participants because it's nearly impossible to play the game without being active in the community after a short time. You made friends grouping because you were predisposed to making friends. You kept playing the game because you had the contacts that were all but required for later content. You knew people, so when you needed a group, you could get one consistantly. Those who didn't participate in the community, who weren't predisposed to making friends and joining an uberguild, they couldn't consistantly have fun, so they left. EQ doesn't encourage community, it just prunes away anyone who isn't in the community.

    The average player, the guy who plays a bit of Quake but doesn't immediately see the tight-knit community; he doesn't see it because people can enjoy the game without the community. You can play a pickup game of quake, or enjoy The Sims without ever seeing or joining the mod community. But this ability to enjoy the game without the community doesn't preclude the formation of the community. It just makes it more likely that you'll run into players who only care about the game itself, and not so much about making friends online.

    You might see the ratio of players-to-active community participants in WoW and think that it's low -- compared to something like EQ it certainly is. But I think interpreting that as some sort of failure of the game design is a mistake.

    Originally Posted by Whizbang Dustyboots
    Taking 5 minutes to chat while running to the Mogrosh ogre camp isn't something most people will begrudge. (Of course, as in all MMORPGs, if you fart around for 45 minutes, people will certainly get irritated with you.)
    That point was directed up thread to people who said the game doesn't matter, that people only play because of the community. If the game didn't matter, then these community-people wouldn't care that they weren't advancing. Sure, most people in WoW would be bothered by that, because they do care about the game.

    It's a game first, and a community second. Community happens or doesn't happen based on the people that play, not the way the activity is structured. Community ties are portable. They're not tied to the activity they formed around. There's little stopping a community of leisurely fishermen from joining a bowling league.

    To clarify my point, let me extend an upthread analogy of EQ to fishing.
    See, EQ is actually more like deep-sea fishing. It can be fun, and it leaves your hands free for a bit of time which surely lends itself to socialization. But it requires a large time commitment up-front, and almost requires a group of people to get it done - and certain skills are required (you need a boat, someone who can navigate and drive it, someone who knows where the fish are, etc). Short of paying for the privilege, you won't just find a 'pick up' group of deep sea fisherman sitting at the wharf whenever is convenient for you. All the administrative downtime in organizing a group means that only those who have a hook into the community have the resources to enjoy deep sea fishing.

    WoW is more like basketball. Many more people enjoy basketball than deep sea fishing because it can be fun, right now, for 30 minutes or 4 hours. You can have fun 2 on 2 or 5 on 5, and pick-up games are generally the norm. It doesn't require a 3 hour time commitment like fishing does. It doesn't require a perfectly balanced group of skills. The much lower organization required to get an bit of basketball together means that you don't need community hooks to enjoy it.

    So most people you'd play basketball against would certainly begrudge you yacking it up during a game. Odds are they don't care about the community, they just want to play ball. Some might not mind, but the vast majority would. Yet that doesn't prevent basketball community formation. Peope still chat between 'games' (analogous to, for instance, time between quests or restocking in town, in a game like WoW), and communities certainly form.

    As EQ is to deep-sea fishing, WoW is to basketball. Most people who play WoW won't like you stopping to chat -- but that doesn't mean the activity can't or won't result in a community.
