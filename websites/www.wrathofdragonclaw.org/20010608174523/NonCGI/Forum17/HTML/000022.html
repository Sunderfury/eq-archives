(OOC note: I hope you don't mind strange vistors posting on your boards..I just read the story of the young pallie leaving and wanted to share an alternative with others that might be considering a fairie hunt. Please feel free to delete if this is old info to y'all.)

The growing chorus of insects and nightbirds roused Kohl from her reverie and signaled the start of her "day". Pausing for a moment on her perch in one of the great pines, she listened. Any voices that might have been present earlier had long since faded into the gathering shadows.

Lesser Faydark, though never as dark as Kithicor, was not what most races would consider well-lit even during the day. Nightfall brought a gloom that made most of those not blessed with the faesight uneasy enough to seek comfort at elven outpost if not Felewithe itself. To Kohl, a "scout" of Tunare, it was home.

She moved deftly down to the lower branches of the evergreen before dropping quietly to the needled-carpeted forest floor then set out quickly toward the Fairie village.

Her plan had been to arrive well before sunrise, take care of her business, and be back in Felewithe in time to meet her client after his morning devotionals. Unfortunately, and despite her inherent misgiving about most of their race, she was beginning to think the human philosopher Murphy might be right. Ducking hither and yon to avoid those damnable brownie scouts, as well as the cursed Tier’Dal that were unexpectedly away from their camp chasing some foolhardy adventurer, had pushed her arrival well past sunrise...not the best time for her games.

Kohl had decided to make the best of it by using the daylight to catch up on some much needed rest and hope that her client really was the forgiving sort. He was not going to be happy when she didn’t show but hopefully she could still catch him after evening devotionals. Paladins were such a stuffy bunch anyway, especially the high elven ones and it was a wonder she had ever convinced them to give her the job in the first place. Most were so blinded by their black-and-white view of the world that any shade of gray was lost to them. Few wanted anything to do with one in her line of work and it had taken a great deal of persuasion to get one to hear her out. His grudging acceptance of the lesser of two, in his words, "evils" still rankled. (The nerve...calling her a thief.)

Oh well, her game with the Fairie Folk would lift her spirits…it always did. A smile tugged at the corners of her mouth as she considered her approach. The Folk had long since gotten wise to her ways but most enjoyed the sport and so played along. This time she’d brought along some berries gathered near home. Her goal was to slip a few of the sweet treats into a hopefully unsuspecting young courtier’s pouch while relieving her of some of Fairie dust. The berries weren't necessary, but she did like to leave something behind. It usually went very well, though Kohl sometimes chose a rare Fairie in a bad temper and ended up scurrying toward home with Fairie curses (a most odd sound) ringing in her ears.

Kohl’s smile faded as she neared the village. The lilting sound of harps, so soothing when one usually approached, was almost non-existent. As the Fairie village came in sight, Kohl whispered a silent prayer to the Mother. Fairie folk lay dead or dying all about…corpses bloodied, wings broken or missing. Guards were returning from the forest, the tips of their weapons clean. The raiders had gotten away.

Her heart aching, Kohl could only pray that it wasn’t her fault…that her client had not decided to take matters into his own hands when she failed to show up at the appointed time. The temptation to own the renowned Armor of Ro sometimes overcame even the strongest of morals. Even elven rangers (who in Kohl’s personal opinion, really should know better) were sometimes driven to such slaughter in their quest for Ivy boots.

As it stood, there was little she could do for the Folk; her talents did not lie within the healing arts and playing was currently out of the question. Best that she slip back into the forest, giving the Folk time to heal their wounds and repair the damage left by the raiders. The carefree disposition of the Folk would not let them grieve for long. Soon she would be able to play...and hopefully others would one day learn the "rules" of her game as well.


Kohl Nightthorn
Rogu--er, Scout and game player extraordinaire ;-)

I wasn’t stealing...I was just relieving them of things they didn’t know they wanted to sell.
