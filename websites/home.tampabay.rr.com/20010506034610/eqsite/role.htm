Roleplaying vs. Internet norms

With a game like EverQuest, it is very important to remember that it is a High Fantasy Roleplaying game. This is not an online graphical chat; the normal internet decorum does not apply. The words you type are the words you say, and how many High Elves in Middle Earth, for example, would say "brb" or "kewl?"

The game has several methods of portraying action, including the emote ability. Instead of :), use one of two options: preface your emoted text with a colon (:) or with /em, e.g., /em smiles devilishly.  And, the "social" buttons can be customized by right-clicking them and assigning emotes; these commonly used, buttonized emotes can be hotkeyed by YOU! *hehe, inside joke :P*

Other great tools for expressing yourself through your character are the various actions that the game designers have incorporated, including:
/cry
/cheer
/wave
/rude or /gesture

Hopefully they will create actions for these also:
/panic
/rofl
/smile

Playing a Role

When you create a character, you are doing more than choosing a face and calculating your stats. Your character is alive in the world of Norrath; make him or her live and breathe! If you are only going to be a powerhungry Wizard, then play him or her that way, such as with a desperate look or an insane laugh.

Ideally, your character should start with a concept (although it can be fun to pick one on a whim during character creation). As you make your new character concept, imagine what events in the character's life caused him to reach the goal of being trained in his profession. Was her mother killed by Orcs, thus causing the young Wood Elf to train as a Ranger to better protect her homeland? Did your family's social niche put pressure on you to become a particular profession, such as a noble house in the Dark Elven society being more geared toward Wizardry?  As you get to know Norrath better, you will come to learn of the various factions and power struggles that lie at the heart of the game; use these existing paradigms to your advantage while creating a new character concept.

Once the basic history, in a general sense, develops, you should have a more clear understanding of what kind of goals your character seeks. Is she a wanderer, without a home, content to have no lasting place? Is the recovery of an important artifact his consuming passion, since his grandfather died while trying to locate the same object? Is he content with debauchery and ill-gotten wealth, and if so, why? Does he follow a code of conduct or is he amoral?  Is your Troll seeking revenge because the Greenbloods ate his mother?

It might be fun to decide what kind of quirks your character displays, making him or her more unique. Does he cough often, due to a low Stamina and thus a more difficult ability to fight disease? Does she smile and wink at strangers flirtatiously? Does your halfling feel the need to stand on barstools at taverns to have that "eye to eye" feeling with other patrons?  Or does your halfling feel the need to "jump cutely?"

Some gamers, myself included, like to draw a sketch of the character, bringing more into focus the physical extension of themselves in another world. Of course, items of clothing you add to your inventory will fluctuate, but a commonly worn set of clothes or a typically sought look can be conveyed if you have enough patience.

If you find it helpful, you could write a background story, which would help you nail down the most important and life-changing events in your character's life. Is your Dwarf afraid of boats because of a racial stereotype, or did his brave Uncle Rowan Strongaxe drown after being capsized by cowardly assassins? It is very enriching for both yourself and the gamers around you if you can refer to the causes of your phobia against dark places, for example, or if you have "real" reasons to distrust anyone of the Rogue profession.  Did the Orcs of Crushbone slay your half-elven mistress while on her way to the Lesser Faydark?

Remember to stay in character. Don't just start acting completely differently unless you have legitimate reason, such as losing some of your morals due to a disillusioned feeling after witnessing many violent crimes. This is especially important to the classes which must abide by a code of conduct (presumably), such as the Paladin and the Cleric, who can lose favor and (possibly) lose powers if their god is displeased.  Remember that people around you will be easily inspired to roleplay and create an in-game persona if they overhear your conversations in character.

 

Name thyself

One of the most important parts of bringing your character to life is the name, one of the first impressions anyone who wants to get to know you will deal with. A suitable fantasy name has already been insisted upon by the game designers, as reflected by the Name Filter during character creation and the ability of Guides to change any inappropriate names. Naming is not difficult, as many sources of fantasy literature and mythology can provide word roots and examples to emulate.

Another great web-based tool is a fantasy name generator, which can come in handy if you are in a pinch or having a mental block. Some good resources:

  • A Dictionay of Names (onomastikon)
  • Fantasy Name Generator, varied and helpful.
  • Flat Earth Game's Name Generator.  Another excellent name generator for fantasy gaming.

Pleae don't plagiarize, though. I have seen dozens of Dark Elves whose names were a derivative of  Drizzt... ugh! YOU can be as creative as any author, just give it a try!
