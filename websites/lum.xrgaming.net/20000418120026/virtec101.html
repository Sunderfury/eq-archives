VIRTUAL ECONOMICS 101
Rowanne

Rowanne is Lum the Mad's better half. She has no tolerance for fools or PKs, and doesn't want to know what you're wearing.

Bah - who cares! I just wanna kill stuff.....

Well, this is often the case with players in online worlds, and it also seems to be the case with some of the designers of these worlds. The economy is an afterthought, and so far - no one seems to have come up with a system that really works. Why SHOULD they care? Well, very much as in the real world - money makes the world go round - or at least provides a counter whereby players can 'keep score'. This is not necessarily a bad thing, it provides a sense of accomplishment, and a REAL working economy even more so. Even monster slayers need an outlet for their loot. Everyone needs goods and services every once in awhile - and a monetary system provides a much neater way to do this than barter. Luckily - a virtual world economy is much easier to control than the one in our real world. Unfortunately, so far - no one seems to have the first virtual clue how to construct one of these so that they don't melt down in short order.

Here are some truths about a healthy virtual economy:

1. Counterfeit currency (i.e., duping) has the same effects that it does in RL - it waters down the value of currency and therefore should be on the high crimes list. That said - any effects of this will EVENTUALLY balance itself back out.

2. A healthy economy has a steady stream of money coming in from many sources.

3. A healthy economy has a steady stream of money going OUT of circulation between players.

4. As a world matures some players WILL become wealthy - those that spend long periods of time pursuing this goal will achieve it.

5. Money on its own - is worthless. People perceive its value, but by itself it cannot be eaten, worn, or lived in.

6. In an RPG, non-player characters are theoretically as legitimate as player characters for interaction - including monetary interaction.

7. An economy is the core of a virtual world. Included in economy is anything of value, such as rare or special items.

Let's look at this, point by point - and see how the two major online worlds at present - Ultima Online and Everquest - meet the criteria.

1. Counterfeit funds.

UO had a major problem with duplicating exploits early in its history. There were few controls in place to track this sort of thing, and many castles sprung up overnight.

Everquest just recently had an instance of many people making 8000 Platinum and up overnight through speculation on an item that the price had changed on. This was NOT making money from thin air - this was some savvy folks taking a look at the programmers over at Verant - and their track record on such matters and speculating. In essence, this has the same effect - but is at the same time the essence of good merchants everywhere, buy low, sell high - and do your homework. This change was primarily because they were hell bent to ignore point #6 - more on that later.

2. Many sources of income.

UO does this very well - it has many trade skills - as well as income from adventuring. Players to some degree need the majority of craftable items.

Everquest has taken the stance that income should ONLY come through adventuring. They have limited the cash in - to monster loot - and cash off of monsters. Trade skills in Everquest are also very limited in items that a player can make to sell to fellow players. This is not only very frustrating to the segment of the population that don't care to primarily monster bash - but also has taken away a very fundamental framework that makes the world seem 'real'. Without many types of players with many different interests - you have a very shallow albeit pretty hack n slash - a la Quake.

3. Many drains of items and money - OUT of the world.

Neither of these worlds does this well - and that is the number one reason an economy breaks down.

UO does have item decay through use. This helps a bit, but is a strain on server resources to track wear and tear on items, and is also very irritating to players. While realistic - and perhaps even necessary - UO also has problems with incredible amounts of storage potential. Hoarding is also a problem - both in terms of economics, and server resources.

EQ does not have item decay. It also has limited storage. However, since EQ has decided to focus on monster bashing as the only viable source of income for players - and X item is introduced (and necessarily camped for funds) every X minutes - divide by number of players on a server - eventually EVERYONE will have this item. When an item becomes too common the price for it drops dramatically, the bar is raised on what people perceive their 'starting equipment' should be - it spirals not in cost, but in items. While some extremely expensive items are available for players to purchase from NPCs that WOULD take money out of circulation, these are so insanely expensive - and players can find better on monsters - that very few people ever even consider the purchase of these goods (steel plate and potions are good instances of these).

4. Elder players are going to become wealthy

UO has been going for 2 years or so. Many MANY players have incredible wealth. Does this effect the world at large - well, it can. Some things are very expensive to train in - for instance - magery. Does the cash itself make a difference, no. The fact that UO is a more full-bodied world in terms of economy - player interaction - other things to do BESIDES fight monsters - and that MANY players still subscribe are telling. Money in and of itself does nothing but sit there and look shiny. UO has a land problem. People want houses, but the physical landscape prevents placing anymore except on rare occasions. This is about the only thing to spend vast amounts of wealth on in UO - so funds sit in banks.

EQ is a much younger system. Already - the money problems have hit EQ. EQ doesn't have anything like houses to spend vast amounts of wealth on, although it too has just a few skills that are incredibly expensive - jewelry and smithing for two. Now THESE two skills are in demand by players - and the return is very good - but at the same time Verant says trades shouldn't make money (from NPCs) - they have given many players the capital to now train in the two that DO make money - saturating the market with these two tradeskills - undercutting those that had a much harder time developing these trades - and eventually saturating the market with those non-decaying things that CAN be made that players desire.

E-bay syndrome:

There is also a phenomenon of players trading virtual money and gold for real world money on sites such as E-bay. Some folks do not have time or patience to develop their own characters, earn their own gold - or in the case of UO - there is simply no room left to place a house. Both OSI and Verant take a dim view of this - when they really should be welcoming it. What this does is redistributes the wealth in-game. There is a danger of temptation to those with administrative powers using this for real world financial gain - but that is due to poor supervision of employees - not from this being a bad thing. If Verant or OSI really wanted to defuse this activity - they need to take a proactive, instead of reactive approach.

1. Let players have an in-game way of trading excessive items and/or gold for game time. This translates to real world dollars bottom line - and ALSO takes money and items out of the world.

2. OSI and Verant should do this themselves. WHAT! - yes you heard me. This activity cannot be stopped, by doing it themselves, not only does it provide additional revenues - it also defuses the market by players, since the companies themselves can basically dictate the market.

3. A combination of 1 and 2 - where the companies can resell what is bartered for game time, and also siphon off goods and items as they think is necessary out of the world.

THAT'S NOT FAIR! Ummm, nothing in any virtual world is ever going to be 'fair'. No one is put at a disadvantage by this - money is a timesaver - it does not make up for skills or experience. The perception of players is the only negative - and it is a false perception. Longtime gamers know the irritation factor involved in 'newbieness' - after making your Xth newbie character - the fascination with exploring the world and building a character simply fades - you've matured past that point, you want to get on with the program. Your other options to this same end are - twinking - the process of having a higher level character giving a lower level items and money - rampant at the moment in EQ. Buying - which is exactly what is ALREADY happening over on E-bay. Time is money - to some folks, it's worth it obviously.

OSI and Verant want people to play and pay as long as possible. Given the fact that there is insane amounts of wealth in UO - the theory that people will think they 'won' when hitting a certain level, and they want to extend that as long as possible - doesn't jive. In UO's case - it's because there are other things to do, and socialization to fall back on. EQ may well suffer this problem once everyone hits 50 - dumps their money and items on a new class and rinse repeats.

5. Money - you can't eat it

After a certain point, money just sits there and looks shiny. If there is nothing to buy - it just starts to accumulate.

6. NPCs are people too.

UO takes the stance that NPCs are as integral to the world at PCs. Merchants buy and sell. On the newest shard - Siege Perilous, they got away from this, trying to take merchants out of the equation. This is a mistake.

NPCs provide the safety net for indispensable items - they also provide an item and money sink out of the world. Granted - neither is the best solution - and a vendor system that hits a middle mark would be preferable, more on that later.

EQ has just finished ripping the trade skills to shreds. Visit any EQ message base, tradesmen are up in arms. EQ has taken the stance that tradesmen should ONLY make money from other players. NPCs are not in the equation. This puts the focus squarely on adventuring for an income - and also disenfranchises any player that does not care to be an adventurer. IMHO this makes for a much more shallow world. Even hard core adventurers occasionally want a change of pace. To make matters worse, there are precious few craftable items needed by other players - AND - most of them can be purchased from NPCs more cheaply than from players. It's pretty basic economics - If you can buy from an NPC for less than it costs a craftsman to manufacture an item - they will buy from NPCs. In their quest to try to force craftsmen into PC to PC interaction, they have totally ignored PCs buying from NPCs - I guess THAT is all right.

7. It's the economy stupid!

Game designers are going to have to make a decision. Are they going to construct virtual worlds - or are they making a hack n slash 'game', and trying to wrap it up in pretty bows, and CALL it a virtual world.

A virtual world mirrors our own. There should be as many reasons and agendas for being there, as there are players. Granted, most fall into some broad groups - but you have to look at what builds a society and make sure that those needs are addressed.

The two things that help promote trade, and provide the glue that a society can start forming around are need - and greed.

If there is no NEED for items - a craft system will never work. If there is no PROFIT in crafting items, very few folks will waste their time with them. If everything can be found 'in the field' adventuring, there is also no need for someone to take the time to create it. All right - why CAN'T everyone be an adventurer? Well, for starters, not everyone wants to be - and more importantly, if everyone is an adventurer, the world is very shallow - with only NPCs populating the towns. To roleplayers, trades are often times easier to use as a roleplaying vehicle than combat. This encourages getting to know people, dealing with others, establishing repoire with customers - hopefully for repeat business. Fictionally - it makes no sense for EVERYONE to be the Avatar (oops) - Hero.

UO comes much closer to addressing this issue than does EQ. People have played UO for over 2 years, people are starting to leave 'levelquest' in increasing numbers. Just as soon as the new wears off - and they see they have no future as Norrath being a place 'to live'. One common comment is "I can play Quake for free".

The only part that EQ got 'right' about economy was their monetary system. By having 4 units of currency, instead of just one, this allows for much tighter pricing, and negotiations. It saddens me that Verant DID learn many valuable lessons from UO - and that they totally ignored the parts that DID work - at least enough to provide a base to improve upon.

OK smartie - how ya gonna fix it?

By going back to square one - and looking at where money comes in, what is needed, where money goes - and ways to suck it back out of circulation preventing TOO much pile up (remember, some folks ARE gonna be stinkin rich).

NPC merchants are your safety net.

They provide a needed service - they are there when no one else is - for very, very basic - can't do without items - they are the factor that spreads things out over time.

What NPCs SHOULD do: 1

. Carry the most basic of items, in goodly numbers.

2. Buy craftable items at a SMALL markup over cost to manufacture with modifiers for difficulty, quality, need, etc.

3. Retain a percentage of goods purchased for resale to players at a substantial markup - thereby providing an outlet to craftsmen for a SMALL profit - retaining the desireablity of dealing directly with a craftsman vs. the merchant and also functioning as a siphon to take goods and money back out of the world.

4. Stop buying once their quota of an item is filled - and/or have the item set by player until the NPC resets. An item they have none of should be more profitable to sell - and item they have few of should be more expensive to buy.

Here is an example.

Joe is a tailor, he buys hides at 3g each - it takes 5 hides to make a tunic, but tunics are very difficult to make, he may have 6 failures before he makes one. As his skill increases - this number goes down substantially.

So, here we have 15g per attempt X 6 = 90g.

Sarah is a merchant NPC - she pays out 20g for leather tunics, and resells them to players for 250g.

Now, as Joe's skill increases, and he makes less and less mistakes - he eventually gets to the point that he can actually turn a profit selling to Sarah - not much, but at the same time - SHE is selling them for 250g. Any player would be well advised to seek Joe out and negotiate a price with him, somewhere UNDER 250g.

Now, how would a newbie ever finance his education taking a 70g loss on every item?

Well, for starters, there is still a large margin for negotiation between 90g and 250g - with plenty of room for quality to be taken into account.

Another option to decrease cost is gathering the raw materials yourself. In this case time = money.

Joe gathers his own hides, therefore - any profit he makes does not necessarily take a cash investment.

Once Joe gets to the point he HAS made a little profit - he can then pay others to hunt for him, bringing him his raw materials instead of doing his own gathering - OR he can maximize his profits by doing it himself still.

Now, although all these numbers come out of a hat and are very flexible - the premise is simple cost accounting. If everything starts with raw materials - gathering them yourself is always an option, or paying others to gather them. NPCs should buy raw materials always - at a very low rate of return, but it does make it an option for new players to get their foot in the door, and also supplies craftsmen when the gatherers are not around - and they don't want to do it themselves.

In UO - although tedious and time consuming - some folks made LOTS of money selling to NPCs because it was simply TOO profitable. On SP merchants buy nothing, this is already creating a barter system, which is not necessarily a bad thing, but does nothing to shortcut the money problem, it just forestalls it. It is also creating a LOT of goods made for skill gain, and just thrown down on the ground. Allowing income from NPCs is not a bad thing - as long as care is taken to restrict the profits.

Another aspect that skewed the UO economy in regards to crafts - they are easily macroable. Restricting how much a merchant will buy from any given player, or at any given time goes a lot farther to address this problem, than having them not buy at all.

Recycling of items back into raw materials - or at least a partial return is a far superior way of letting craftsmen that want to gain skill do so, help with cost in training, and not flood the world with unnecessary items (and as a side effect, gold).

Decay of items is how UO addresses some item sinks - but it's irritating. Yes, there needs to be consumables, but they should not wear out at such a rate that inspires someone to take up the craft themselves. There is a happy balance between needing to buy hatchets to lumberjack - and having to be a tinker to do it yourself because it breaks every 5 min (see SP).

Item and gold sinks:

Now that you have craftsmen making SOME money from NPCs, adventurers bringing in cash and selling loot from monsters - you now have established a way for cash to enter the economy.

Trading among players is grand, wonderful, and the desired end - but if you don't have some sinks OUT of the economy, the levels keep getting higher, and higher and all you are doing is recirculating the ever increasing funds.

One way to do this, is have a critical item that can ONLY be gotten from NPCs. One example of this is reagents for Mages in UO. Yes, you CAN gather them occasionally yourself in the field, but their numbers are scarce. No mage or alchemist is going to be able to do anything meaningful with just that found in the wild. While this is a good example - it's about the only one in UO. Magery is powerful - it's expensive, it SHOULD be. Reagents should perhaps be even MORE expensive than they currently are.

Recycling items into component parts is in development in UO - this will help GREATLY with unneeded, unwanted, hoarded items.

One thing that should be looked at in UO - recycling magical weapons or armor - should yield magical components. If a magic halberd melts down to 3 ingots of that type of magic - this would allow smiths of high skill to refashion them into something else. This would cut massive inroads into chests and chests of hoarded - low level magical items. Even if it was 'generic magic' - and smiths of high level were allowed to use these somehow in a random manner for a CHANCE of producing a random magical effect, this would have people melting down items left and right, especially if these affects could be applied to colored armor.

Supplying things that have no tangible benefits whatsoever, but are ways for extremely rich elder players to show status. One such proposal in UO was a "title". Providing absolutely no benefit other than to show off wealth, players could purchase a "title" from Lord British - wonderful money sink.

Another option is in game gambling. Anyone that has ever been to Los Wages, errrr. Los Vegas - knows that great sums of wealth can be sucked from your pocket in a short amount of time - but it's voluntary, supposedly fun (at least for those that like that sort of thing) and would provide a wonderful way for money to exit back out of the economy.

Well, that's all well and good - but how do you retrofit an economy.

That is the question that undoubtedly has developers scratching their heads. They painted themselves into a virtual economic corner, how do they get out - with their player base intact, and into a system that is both more viable - and fair to both the newcomer and elder player.

Since EQ obviously has decided to become a very pretty Quake - let's take a look at UO, and how this economy can be 'fixed'. Note, all these have to be done and implemented together. Piecemeal is worse than no change at all.

1. Every item has to be evaluated - cost to manufacture/difficulty determined, merchant buy and sell prices adjusted accordingly.

2. I would implement a varied monetary unit system a la EQ. Convert gold already in existence to the new funds. Note: This will not be popular, but it will be fair since the scale is merely a conversion. Set a base under which the truly poor have a safety net, i.e., you can't start with less than X $. In some cases, this will be BENEFICIAL, but will even itself out. No one 'loses' wealth - it's just on a different scale, and is relative.

3. All monster cash loot must be adjusted to this system at the same time.

4. Implement recycling and make sure wear and tear on consumables is 'reasonable'.

5. Implement some kind of money sinks a la the title or gambling proposals - or anything else that is voluntary, visible or enjoyable and has the perception of status. Let in game funds (a lot) purchase some playtime to drain the excesses. Got 4 million gold? OK, your next UO month is free.

We are likely not to ever see a true functioning economy in either EQ or UO - simply because the retrofit is so exhaustive. I sincerely hope that upcoming worlds take a hard look at what makes a virtual world really function - and spend as much time considering if their economy really works, as they do tweaking combat.
