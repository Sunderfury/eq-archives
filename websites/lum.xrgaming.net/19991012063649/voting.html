Voting With Your Wallets 
Lum's Better Half

Lum's Better Half plays UO and EQ and can be persuaded of anything given enough chocolate.

The recent updates from Origin got me to thinking about how Origin and other companies relate to their customers.

My, my - customer relations - a nightmare at any company. Much more so when it's a service - not a product. Throw in evolution of a project as a work in progress - a healthy dose of customers that desperately want to be part of a bigger constructive process, limitations on technology, and you have the scenarios we see currently playing at OSI, Verant - and soon - Microsoft.

I don't envy these folks - I really don't - they have forces tugging them in every direction and it's up to them to keep the boat afloat. To consider all angles - and decide what is best for the game/world as a whole and do so within what is physically possible with the technology available.

First - let's examine what a MMRPG -- as a persistent world -- is.

  • To the company: It generates revenue from initial sales - and from renewing subscribers. It is a product and it is a service.
  • To the user: It provides a framework to create a life in a virtual world and society. It's entertainment. It's a way to share with other humans in a way no other genre to date has provided.

Now, while all 3 of the current and upcoming games of this genre claim to be worlds - so far, UO has by far the most impact and potential of 'creating and living a virtual life'. EQ has pretty eye candy - but not very much structure for a community to spring up around, and what little I've seen on AC - it has the same problem.

This genre cannot successfully be administrated in the fashion that all other games before it can - for the sole reason that they are TRYING to create persistent societies of human users. Once a community starts to form - it is human nature to want SOME input into the world around one. It is also a very basic human need to want to make a mark of SOME kind on the world.

One reason the item decay proposal strikes at the heart of so many users - item accumulation has been one way many people feel they have 'made their mark'.

There are other ways people make their mark - some are good, some are bad, some are personal, some are more visible.

Building organizations or places that become well known is probably one of the hardest - yet most visible - and community oriented activities for making ones mark. Ask Kazola - or consider what caused Origin to add the "Priests of Mondain" to Siege Perilous. Those that contributed to building both these establishments - and all the others scattered across the shards - will be remembered. If not for themselves - for what they built and inspired.

The flip side of this are people that go out of their way to become infamous. Well known perhaps, but very few of these are long remembered once they are gone.

Placing a house - much more modest, but visible and useful. This is a very important goal for a lot of folks, it invests them in the world. Some like to decorate and highly personalize, there really should be ways they can do this without compromising server performance.

Along with houses - 'stuff' is some folks counter - they need other ways to demonstrate visible wealth, it's obviously important to them.

Another subset of players are basically armchair designers - they make suggestion websites, post on message boards, subscribe to mailing lists. They try to make their mark by more directly influencing the designers with ideas - and trying to dialogue in a reasonable manner for the betterment of the worlds design.

Now, what has all this to do with this genre?

One of the key differences between UO and EQ besides the eye candy - besides community - is lack of feedback from Verant. There are just as many mailing lists - just as many websites - just as many people interested in EQ developing into a world that can be lived in - but dialogue is all very one sided. People talk, but it's a VERY rare occasion when Verant talks back. This leaves the player community with the feeling that Verant is the enemy - that they are not listening - this is deadly to community building.

Microsoft - well, AC isn't out of beta - we don't know what they are going to do.

UO has historically had much more an open dialogue with it's user base. Sometimes in dream sessions - players have misconstrued ideas for promises - this can cause problems.

There is a difference between kicking around ideas - and tackling a problem.

Being a company - most companies don't particularly like giving competitors any more information than they have to - although looking at what the two up and comers learned - and more importantly what they IGNORED from lessons in UO - it appears that information readily available by playing, by reading user feedback - is and has largely been ignored. It boggles the mind that such user feedback would be ignored - but it has been - it's obvious that the 'designers viewpoint' is still paramount - user be dammed.

There is a new dev team - there is new management - and there is MUCH less dialogue going on now than ever before in UO's development history.

Technically - they make a product - it's theirs - we just pay for playing. They can make horses pink, the ground blue and the sky orange (umm, UO doesn't have a sky - never mind) if they want.

What this does not take into account is that the REASON people play - and the reason they KEEP playing - is because this is an online society. When humans form communities - they tend to want some say - some representation into how the rules around them work. Does every player have a burning desire to change things - or take the time to make posts or essays or websites? No - lots just want a world to play in - but they ALL have opinions - they all want to make their mark one way or another.

In the traditional single player game - or even in session based online games - you walk into a set of rules - you play - you win or lose (either a game - or a session). The mechanics of the world aren't ever shifting after the fact - you either like it, or you don't - no persistence involved. In an evolving product - with long-term persistent ramifications - a different set of dynamics come into play.

Not every person in our world picks up a picket sign and marches - but that doesn't mean they aren't concerned about issues - sometimes they just watch as others more motivated do the dirty work. Does every person run for office - no - but they usually vote for folks that seem to have the issues they are concerned to represent them. Also - polls are a politicians bread and butter - they not only (appear) to listen to their constituency - they spend LOTS of bucks polling to 'get the pulse' of the people. They are accountable at voting time - ignore the people you work for too much - you aren't around next time. Even when unpopular things are done - it is political suicide to not TRY to build a consensus of the public. The mark of a great leader is listening - building consensus - then going forward with the best answer for the most people without crippling others.

In any case - information and dialogue and input is critically important.

Now, looking at this as they created this world - it's their property - they are god. Being human - they are very imperfect ones - with a lot of room for improvements.

Looking at it from the perspective that without users, and without people forming communities you have nothing but a program running on a box - you have something more akin to a country.

Countries without representation are called dictatorships. Not necessarily a bad thing - but people tend to resent living under dictators - even benevolent ones. People try to escape dictatorships.

Countries with representation - well, we might not be any better off, but at least we FEEL like we might be able to make a difference. People bitch and moan - but usually don't try to flee.

Why have UO players stuck around thru so much? Why have they held on? Why have they not given up?

Mainly - because we have gotten feedback on issues - we knew we were listened to and considered - it might take forever, but it's 'on the list' (tm) - and by golly - sometimes they do get done.

As madding as some things have been - as disgusted as we get at things that just don't make sense - as long as it's taken for some things to be implemented or fixed - we had one thing - HOPE.

When people feel heard - they become allies - people working towards a common goal, even if the details may be opposing viewpoints. When people feel shutout - they become enemies - they feel alienated and will not put up with nearly as much.

I have no idea how many people constitute the 'dev team' - I would venture a guess that there are many thousands of players for every one of them however. There are as many play styles and agendas as there are players. There is simply no way that even the brightest and well motivated designers can possibly anticipate what so many different people consider important. This does not mean every idea has to be implemented, it does not mean every idea is even worth serious consideration - it does mean that taken in aggregate - users tend to fall into definable groups - and that most of their ideas and feedback IS of invaluable use as to how it affects THEM. Taken as a whole - this is stuff that adds depth and life to a world.

All this said - we do have a vote of sorts - we vote with our dollars. These worlds aren't just games that you can win and sit on a shelf - they weren't designed to be that way - once you invest hundreds of hours 'building a life', it's damn hard to let go and experience a virtual death. These companies are well aware of this - this is the hook. Still - people will only put up with so much, when hope is gone, there is no reason to continue. It's a heck of a lot easier to flee a virtual dictatorship - than a real one - all you need is a better place to go.
