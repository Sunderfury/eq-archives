Everything Wrong with Game Developers Today
Monday, June 12, 2000 - 9:00 AM [Filed by arcadian del sol]

Well okay. I can't possibly go into everything, but I can certainly point out the biggest sin in game development today: selling incomplete products. So far, I'd have to slap the wrists of every damned game developer in the industry with the single exception being Blizzard, who has always stuck by the rule that they will not release a game until they feel that product is completely finished and ready for retail shelves. Black Isle (Interplay) has always claimed to have such a policy but I don't think anyone really believes that anymore. They get half-credit however, for trying to fool us.

Nihilistic is the latest prostitute to whore out it's incomplete services, much to the cheers from the UO Live team, still fumbling with the features of UO:R. Anything to divert the spotlight, right guys? But remember folks, it's not their fault. God delayed the UO:R release. That's right. God Almighty. There, with my habitual and customary dig at OSI out of the way, on to the point.

Only hours after the retail release of Vampire, Rob Heubner put the following in to his .plan (courtesy of gamespot.com):
There's one patch already that's just about finished with Activision's QA process that is just to address the bandwidth used by games hosted on modem servers. Everyone should get the patch (small) when its released because it obviously makes the network protocol more efficient for all users, but its specifically designed to help people hosting on 28.8k or 56k modems.

Kudos to Nihilstic for spotting this bug within only a FEW SHORT HOURS, and then having a patch nearly complete and ready to release - NOT! Clearly the product went off to press, and out the door - while this patch was being coded. Meaning, the game was still in development at the very moment that anxious customers were purchasing it and taking it home. Perhaps Nihilistic has taken a page from the OSI manual (dig 2 for those scoring at home).

It's one thing when a product hits the market, and an unforseen problem arises. It is an entirely different story when a problem is detected by a myriad of beta testers, and is released on schedule anyway. At least Vampire didn't ship with any virus infected .txt files. But I still think they deserve to rot in "retail returns hell" for running a business like this.
That's just my opinion. Results may vary.
