Mixed Nuts, part I.
Monday, January 17, 2000 - 8:13 AM [Filed by myschyf]

Is reading all this just too hard? We can help. Click here to hear Myschyf read her latest update to you. --Lum

Good morning class. Today's topic of discussion is bugs. Not those creepy, crawling things you find hopefully outside and not inside but the kind you find in UO and don't report to the development team but do report to your favorite bugsite so that you, along with your fellow abusers, can be banned. Because you know OSI has to get their information from some place and if the players are only reporting bugs to bugsites guess where OSI is going to go for their information?

Let us think about this for a moment. Why aren't the players reporting bugs to OSI? Well, why should they? To protect the game and themselves. Ok I'll bite. That's a good reason and it works very well as long as you are talking about a dupe bug or maybe a house break-in bug or even a bug that will raise stats and/or skills above the cap. But what if it is something more innocuous -- say a bug that will allow you to raise strength.

Since we don't want to talk about current bugs let us talk about a bug from days gone by. Once upon a time in UO no one had strength above 70. Well a few did but it was a very few. You could mine all day and all night and never gain a point of strength. You could cut every tree in the Yew forest and still, no strength. Then one day one enterprising young player discovered that if you repaired a non-breakable weapon over and over and over and over and over again you would gain strength. Overnight every man, jack and woman in the game became a blacksmith. If you didn't have a forge (they weren't add-ons in those days -- they came with the house) you went and bought one (small forges cost like 10k back then). All of a sudden everyone had 100 strength and life was good. Now what possible reason would any of us have for reporting this bug to OSI? Strength gain was broken (still is IMHO) and all of a sudden here was this marvelous new way to gain strength. It didn't hurt the game or a players gameplay -- on the contrary it made it infinitely more playable. YOU try fighting a drake with 66 hits and staying alive all at the same time. So why would any of us report it? What possible incentive would we have?

Ahh but that is a special case. Thats only one bug and it is understandable why you wouldn't report THAT bug. OK lets talk about another bug from days gone past. Once upon a time in UO magic didn't affect you if you were wearing leather armor. Oh it did some marginal damage -- maybe 4 or 5 points. So of course all of a sudden everybody, everywhere ditched bone in favor of leather because mages, then as today, ruled the earth, the sky, the dungeons, PVP and PVM. So why would a player report this bug? Oh I don't know -- maybe because plate is sexy and fashionable and s/he really really really wanted to wear it?

It was Runesabre's letter of a week or so ago that started me thinking along these lines. Even though in that letter he wrote specifically about people who ran bug sites, it seemed to me he made an assumption that the players SHOULD be reporting bugs to OSI and I thought, well, why? Why should we? We really have very little incentive to do so.

Lets think about your average player here for a moment. Not the readers of this site and certainly no one from Lum Inc. (because as you know we here at Lum Inc. are safely snuggled up against Tyrant's ass and ALWAYS report bugs). But your average player probably doesn't read this site or any of the fan sites -- including bug sites. The average player has probably never called a GM. The average player probably never noticed that it was the leather armor he was wearing that protected him. He just saw everyone else wearing leather and figured it was the fashionable thing to do. If he did manage to put two and two together he probably thought it was neat. I guarantee you his first thought would NOT have been to go running to OSI to tell them about the bug. If I was not an employee of Lum, Inc. it wouldn't be my first thought either.

However, this isn't actually a 'bug' problem, per se. It is something larger -- lack of a relationship with the player base. The failure of OSI (or any game company) to provide incentives for reporting bugs is possibly something that could be solved or wouldn't even be an issue if we, the player base, had an actual relationship with OSI. One that was encouraged by OSI and even built by OSI. Right now, most of the interaction between game company and player is customer-driven. OSI has made great strides in this area but they aren't there yet. However, since I've already made you do enough reading and thinking for one update and since I'm already late for work I'll continue this train of thought in my next update.

And since I loved Ktalk SO much, you may post your thoughts here. Class dismissed.
