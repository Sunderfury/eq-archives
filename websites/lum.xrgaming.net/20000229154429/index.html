THE PLAY'S THE THING, TO CATCH THE CONSCIENCE OF THE KING
Monday, February 28, 2000 - 10:51 AM [Filed by lum]

Why do we play online RPGs?

I suspect if you ask 10 active players you’d get 10 different reasons, ranging from meeting up with a group of friends for some co-operative monster bashing, to spending a few hours twiddling resources maintaining your economic empire, to the thrill of besting another human being somewhere in virtual mano a virtual mano combat.

Now, no matter how many people you ask, I suspect “being killed by the people who run the game, with no hope of survival” will never appear on the list. Yet both in Everquest and Ultima Online, the folks who run the storylines seem to think that players really enjoy that sort of thing.

Everquest has always been the worst offender here. EQ has already mastered Skinnerian psychology (“hit the Ignite icon 50 times and you get a tasty treat!”) – now they are working on establishing the learned response “Roleplaying = Death”. Every single time a GM event has been run in Everquest, it has ended with a field of corpses.

Some will say it’s not the fault of the people running the “quests” – that in any quest, the K3wld3wds run amuck trying to kill anything that moves and most of the stuff standing still. Well, sorry, that is the fault of the game admins. Remember – EQ is the master of learned response gaming. The absolute best items in Everquest – “artifacts” – are obtainable only through these sort of events. Everquest is a game where he with the best toys wins. Thus the powergamers will always, always seek out and destroy anyone involved in a GM event. You can look at them and go “Tsk! Tsk! Shame on you for not staying in character and bowing before the Goddess Tunare!” but those powergamers have pretty much made a career out of Everquest (a pretty lucrative one, judging from eBay listings) and they really don’t give a fuck what you think about their style of play. Don’t want powergamers to nuke everything that moves everytime a God shows up? Don’t drop k3wl toys when they die. Simple learned response. Probably too late to change now, though, and Everquest will retain its status as a game with an excellent backstory that absolutely no one outside of Verant cares about.

Now, you’d think Ultima Online would know better. UO has had an Interest section for years now. Granted, not the most active part of the support team, but one that Origin has committed to plowing time and resources in. The BNN reports are pretty funny even if they do keep getting my name wrong. Everyone loves the new town invasions. The very concept of Lord British being 0wned by a tribe of mongbats is too cool for words.

There’s just one problem – you can’t win. The story has been pre-ordained. Set in stone. Well, maybe not stone, but in nice neat silvery “Ultima Online: Renaissance” boxes. There is nothing you can do about it. No amount of personal bravery, no amount of sacrifice, no amount of slaughtered liches in the streets will change the script that has already been written in Austin.

Now, granted it’s a good start that the world of UO is actually changing once in a while, but the whole point of Ultima Online is its dynamic nature. That strength is subverted by the work of a bunch of presumably well-meaning folks who have decided they know what will entertain you. I mean, it works for television, right? You get to appear in their story. You don’t get to do anything that has any meaning, mind you, but you can die.

And you can die frequently. We’ve been getting reports of GM kills that rival the worst of EQ. IGM/Seer run characters that spawn 50 hell hounds at once, causing everyone on screen to lag out and be slaughtered. Effectively invulnerable “main plot” characters that wordlessly cut down anyone who tries to fight them, and teleport away if by some insane reach of combat they actually lose 10% or so of their 36,737 hit points.

This is not fun, folks. Well, maybe it is for the GMs who get to whoop up on the players. But they don’t pay for the privilege. We do.

Of all the ORPGs out, UO has the potential for the most, for lack of a better word, adult plot realizations. It’s a good sign when Origin signals that they are willing to inconvienence some players by letting cities fall. The next step is to let the players drive the direction of the plot. It’s a necessary step. Otherwise, Origin is the immature dungeon master we all remember who gets an ego trip up and running and decides the game has become “how best can I kill off the players”.

That is not what it’s about. For god’s sake, if Origin won’t, maybe Turbine or Wolfpack or god forbid even Verant should hire some goddamned professional writers and game designers. The writers to create a plot that can be compelling and on a scale that makes sense both for the community leaders and the third spearman from the left in the Cove defense perimeter. And the game designers to teach the writers how to write decision trees, and to implement them so that the story doesn’t totally collapse if some bright player figures out “Hey! What if we do this…”

It’s basic Game Mastering 101, folks. You allow for the players to drive the story. You roll with the punches. You change the plot in response to their actions. And you don’t kill all the players.

The first thing we do, let's kill all the lawyers

KNOWLEDGE IS POWER, ESPECIALLY WHEN YOU HAVE TO CLICK "OK" TO GET PAST IT
Sunday, February 27, 2000 - 4:37 AM [Filed by lietgardis]

Do you remember way back when the UO Auto-Patch message would tell you what was in development and the contents of the most recent patches? It’s a very dim recollection for me, and I don’t remember exactly when it ended and what the explanation for its discontinuation was. Would “our staff are too busy to continually update the message” be more important than keeping the players updated? And what’s the connection between this and the people that put “OSI fukin’ SuX” in their bank macros? Let’s see.

I watched someone perform an elaborate scam with a “tower scroll” at the Britain bank this evening. He swore the “new patch” enabled people to carry around a scroll representing placed buildings, and told people that they just weren’t up-to-date enough if they hadn’t heard about it. Ignoring my “don’t do it! you’ll be sorry! don’t say I didn’t tell you so!” warnings, the victim put in his 100k or whatever in the trade window with the "tower scroll," and found himself with nothing after clicking his diamond. But how was he to know that that this “tower scroll” wasn’t the way “secure house trading” works, as of last week’s update? The victim didn’t feel so betrayed by the scammer, but instead by Origin -- “fucking osi and their stupid patches.” (We could also argue about how stupid somebody is for believing anybody would sell a tower for so little when he or she could get $800 for it on Ebay, but that’s not the point.)

What with the recent New Idea a Day phase the dev team is going through and the ensuing outrages, it’s more important than ever that the entire playerbase keep updated on changes. Any amount of time spent at a high-traffic area in-game will prove to you that the majority of players are ignorant about what’s going on (the house scamming above is indicative of the phenomenon). Visiting a web site for the news is too much work; most people now would rather wait for a patch and then ICQ their friends to ask about it. Were players more educated back when the Auto-Patch message told them about it? I’d assume they were.

Most people probably just click through the Auto-Patch message as quickly as possible, and probably wouldn’t even notice if it was changed. But if a patch went in, and they knew the message would tell them what was in it, I’d imagine they’d read it the next time they loaded up the client. It would be even better if there was some way to access the information from within the game without loading a web browser, some kind of extention of the “Help” window that itemized everything included in the last update, but that’s probably not something we can hope for. Updating the Auto-Patch message with a summary of the changes is realistic, though -- it’s easy and accessible for everyone to see, and it doesn’t require visiting an external resource.

When the playerbase is forcefully kept up-to-date on changes in the game, they’ll not only be protected in whatever small way from scams taking advantage of their ignorance, but they’ll feel closer to Origin. This is the point -- the players will feel like they’re active, rather than sitting back and letting things happen to them by an unknown force; they’ll feel like Origin is going out of its way to reach out for them, freely giving them information, rather than tweaking things behind their backs. It might help stop some of the blind “I hate OSI” sentiments so many people adhere to. Alienation between the company and the playerbase is one of the worst things for the developer of a continually-changing “persistant world” to have, and alienation is what most players get when information isn’t forced down their throats.

I know you’re all up-to-date, but this site’s daily readers only make up .05% of all “UO subscribers.”
