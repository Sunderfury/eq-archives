BARNEYLAND: ONE WEEK LATER
Saturday, April 29, 2000 - 10:54 AM [Filed by great bob]
For those of us on the Sonoma shard, it's been about a week of living with the divided lands. While waiting to place our towers, let's sit back and look over what we've learned from this social experiment.

If players won't go to Everquest, Everquest will come to the players. Do you like camping? If so, the "happy fun lands" might just be for you. Numerous spawn sites have become home for some players. The bandit camp, for example, is manned 24/7 by players, each jumping in glee and attacking any red name they see. Why leave untill you have more gold then your warrior can carry? Clothes make great bandages, after all. Don't think you can chase off the bastards either. Just bring more people to killsteal targets until the other group grows tired of the chase. Oh, and those people talking to each other in the wilderness? It won't last long. Might as well make a macro saying "he's my kill" since that's the extent of conversation nowadays. Is this a temporary aberation, or a permenant change in social structure? We'll have to see. For those who wanting to play EQ, but hate those 3-d graphics, you might want UO. We just don't have half naked elf chicks or anything.

Sell moonstones early. Thought your stocks took a tumble this month on Wall Street? Wait untill the moonstone market crashes on your shard. Day one, moonstones were selling for 1-5 thousand. One week later, the price drops to 150 gold. If the current market holds, by the year 2001 players should be able to recieve a placed castle in exchange for taking a moonstone off someone elses hands. I think. I slept through my economic classes when I went to college.

Free gold for everyone! Risk vs. Reward, folks. For those of you who don't play UO, dungeons are a great place to get lots of gold, however the night will normally end with the gold being stolen from you and someone named "PiMpDaddY" emoting "*assrape*" over your corpse. OSI has solved the later with the barneyland. Now you can make free money with no risk involved. Case in point? I took a half devloped bank thief with no combat skills. With casual play (less than an hour every other day) I'm now a master warrior with 40k in the bank. Great for casual players, but say "bye-bye" to the economy. Newbies begging at banks should soon recieve a few hundred thousand gold everytime they pester somebody at the Brit bank. The biggest impact will be on the real world value of virtual gold in UO. Instead of being worth more than the Italian lira, expect future press releases to brag about the relative value to Etheopian currency instead.

I'm crawlin' with bugs! Given the complexity of creating a mirror of the old lands with new play rules (Did I say mirror? Ooops. As we all know, this is not a mirror!) some bugs were to b expected. As long as you don't need the healing skill, or objects staying in your backpack accross server lines, this patch wasn't too bad. (It's all relative, you know)

Despite all this, all one has to do is look at the empty cities in "Nuclear Winter Land" to see that, for the most part, the player base is pretty happy with it. Of course, they might just be in the new lands for the free loot or the housing space, but the players have flocked there. It's only us grumpy malcontents who seem to have an issue here. For those you waiting for the patch to hit, that's what you have to look forward to. For those of you who stayed away from UO because of all the terrible horror stories you heard about "PiMpDaddY"....well, c'mon back. This patch is for you.

Us surly reds, and other grumpy bastards, will be sitting in the Brittania retirement homes telling the damn kids who visit us "how good it was in the old days".

Knight - Knight is the Executive Producer of Ultima Online 2. Knight is responsible for providing an unlimited supply of soda and pizza to the team, and making sure that everyone gets paid on time. Blackthorn - Blackthorn is the Producer of UO2. Blackthorn's goal is complete domination of the world by technology. Ubiq - Ubiq is the Lead Designer of UO2. Ubiq's job description entails giving his lead programmer an aneurysm at least once a day, keeping Mobius and Windfeather from killing each other, explaining to the fans how and why things will work, and trying to imagine all of the ways that the less scrupulous elements of the UO2 player base will attempt to break the game. In his rare moments of spare time, Ubiq has been known to sleep and eat. Ubiq has a pathological fondness for flaming kittens, which no one fully understands.

Gaffer - Gaffer is the Tech Lead for UO2. He is responsible for overseeing the consumption of soda and pizza, and making sure that milestone and ship celebrations happen in a timely fashion. Additionally, Gaffer manages what looks like the programming team, but is actually a crack ninja fighting force of extraordinary magnitude.

Cynthe - Cynthe is the UO2 Community Coordinator. She oversees cool stuff like interviews, IRC chats, message board postings, website updates, and the prequel fiction release schedule, as well as making sure that no gerbils go homeless within the Origin building.

Tungsten - Tungsten fancies himself the Art Director for UO2. A normal day consists of pointing any one of the 13 fabulously talented UO2 Artists in the right direction, administrating Leads meetings, reviewing gobs of amazing art, plunging fearlessly into the schedule to find our way to ship and finally closing the day with a quiet session of Unreal.

Hoyboy - AKA Puddin' - AKA Heliogabalus. As Creative Director he massages the visual coolness factor to a high degree of polish. Amongst other things he keeps the artists fat and happy. How does he do this? With plenty of love, olive oil and, what else? Puddin'.

Windfeather - Also called a 'Lamer Roleplayer' by the bloodthirsty components of the design team, Windfeather's job is to design social and community systems for UO2, a task he frequently likens to designing systems for herding cats. Windfeather's goal is to survive until beta without getting PK'd by the programmers for creating too much extra work.

Mobius - Mobius is a game systems designer, number cruncher, and UO2 Live lead designer. Mobius eagerly awaits being the scapegoat for any and all potential game balance problems during beta. He is also a proud, card-carrying member of the bloodthirsty designers that call Windfeather a "L4m3R RPer".

Ghosthacker - As Lead World Builder, GhostHacker tirelessly pounds out the beat beneath the decks of the UO2 world building barge. Occasionally, late at night you can hear his drums from the depths of Origin with his foul cry, "Generate terrain faster, ye scurvy polygonal monkeys..."

Gunner10 - Gunner10 leads the game programming (scripting) side of the team, which means working on stuff like AI, Combat, Inventory and other game mechanics. He is also the unofficial standard bearer for formal software engineering practices, which everybody agrees is A REAL GOOD THING (TM). More or less :-)

Ahriman - Ahriman is the Minister of Fiction for Ultima Online 2. He is responsible for all things fictional, including crafting clever stories to justify more soda and pizza. Ahriman's goal is to weave the various threads of Ultima Lore into a seamless tapestry, or to marry Tyra Banks, whichever he can pull off first.

Fiction in gaming. What a concept. One that apparently no one knows how to do well, that everyone wants to do, and one that everyone (read gamers) wants. How do you drive a consistent story line in an MMORPG (massively multiplayer online role-playing game) with two-hundred-thousand players? By having two hundred volunteer seers/elders/whathaveyou that play ubercharacters and pk the paying customers in the name of evil? This, so far, has been EQ's answer to fiction in gaming and, to a large extent, UO's as well. Who among us hasn't heard the horror stories of going to an 'event' in EQ or UO only to see the dead bodies of player characters spread 3 screens out and some seer-played invulnerable evil character doing an MMORPG maniacal laugh and promising to take over the world.

This, of course, brings me to another question. Is good vs. evil all we can do? Is this the best our imaginations can bring forth? Have we really explored the extent of our story-telling capabilities? Have the numbers proved to be so large and unruly that all we can do is throw up our hands exasperatedly, swallow half a dozen Advil and send out more ubercharacters? I know there's only 6 or 7 plots in the world, that Shakespeare wrote extensively on all of them and that all we can do now is rehash the same old same old but you know... authors do manage to write new and interesting books with new and interesting stories. Movies are still made that people enjoy seeing. Why does online gaming, which should be the marriage of fiction, cinematography and interaction, which seems like the perfect venue for a blending of all these things, which everyone jumped into thinking that NOW they could finally be the hero or the villian or the damsel in distress or whatever archetype floated their particular boat, fall so short of the mark? Can we really only come up with killing each other or any odd assortment of monsters as an answer? Surely there must be more.

So what would it take to introduce a good fiction to an MMORPG? Or an MMOAG (adventure game)? What is the difference between the two anyway and does the second one really exist? Well.. when was the last time you played a game and felt you were going on an adventure, that you were going to explore new places and see new things and maybe even accomplish something more than killing monsters and getting phat lewt. I'm not talking about an in-game marriage, or something that your guild did, but something that was actually provided by the game mechanics and was part of the initial and intended game design. Your silence is deafening.

Games, online games, and damn few single-player games as a matter of fact, provide some sort of fiction you can play with, work through, or be a part of. To my knowledge its NEVER been done in an online game... ANY online game. But everyone wants it and everyone thinks there's a market for it and everyone would love to design it. And, as far as the publishers are concerned, it should be designed for a pittance and net millions. And they think WE are weird? Bah!

So where is this great full-of-plot-and-story-line-where-we-can-all-totally-immerse-ourself-in-the-fiction-game? Why hasn't it been done yet? Is it even worth doing? Why should a developer even try to make a content-heavy adventure game when the online (and offline) gaming community has proven that it will settle for a 3d first person shooter or a game whose only goal is the 'kill monsters, get stuff, go up levels, kill bigger monsters, get better stuff, go up more levels, kill even bigger monsters... etc etc' type or some sort of monty haul where you kill, buy and sell things and store your ever-growing piles of phat lewt until you finally grow bored and sell your account for thousands on ebay.

For that matter, why can't it be done effectively within UO and EQ with game staff? Is it because these games were never designed to carry a story in the first place? Every time I look at a message board these days I hear players screaming about fiction in the game -- or, more truthfully, the lack thereof.

According to some, MMORPGs are, by their very non-linear nature, not made to carry a story line. However paper D&D games are non-linear too -- and they do story very well. Even large LARPs (Live Action Role Play) do story very well, albiet oftentimes the ending that the GameMaster envisioned and the ending that actually happens are two different things -- making for some very tired GMs on the last day of the LARP as they stayed up all night rewriting the story. And LARPS and paper games, even very large LARPs and paper games, don't have two hundred thousand people in them. But you know what -- neither does any one event ever held in an online game to date. The most I've seen is about a hundred. I've done LARPS -- fun LARPS, with great stories -- with a hundred people.

I think sometimes we tend to get stymied by the knowledge that there are so many of us in a particular game. When writing a story we say "well I can't do that because too many people will show up for me to do that" rather than just going with the flow and dealing with the situation as it arises. To go back to the example of the large LARP, the GMs there often don't know exactly how many will show, whether they will stay to the end, or even if they will turn the story in an unexpected direction. But they deal, adjust, adapt. No one does that like LARP GMs do. Is it really so wrong to expect that MMORPG GMs and Seers do the same? Can't this sort of interaction be incorporated into a content-heavy adventure game to make that rich rewarding roleplaying and fiction experience everyone seems to be looking for? I think it can. I also think its worth it. I think ANYTHING that moves us out of the old time worn 'lets go shoot and kill things' rut that we've stuck ourselves in is worth it.

"You may not sell or auction any EverQuest characters, items, coin or copyrighted material."

E-bay no more? Is this a wave of the future? Can it work? Will other companies follow suit? Let's take a peak into the good, the bad and the ugly of this situation.

The Good

There was once a time when one could actually buy a house deed, and have a chance of placing it. I know, this sounds like fiction, but it was true. When players left the game they actually allowed their homes to decay. Then came E-bay. OSI nodded and winked at the auctions, and the population became homeless overnight...unless, of course, they were wiling to shell out some benjamins. Suddenly, the dynamic changed, and the good intentions of the Dev team with regard to finite rescources crumbled. If you're leaving the game, why sell your house for in game gold when you could get some beer money? This folks, is a no brainer. In EQ, camping became more than a playstyle. It became a job.

The Bad

So should we pop open the champaign corks just yet? Well, let's think about this. You see, people who have lives don't have time to sit in front of a computer screen playing a damn game 6 hours a day. I know this can be tough to relate to. After all, when we get home from work we play these games. At work we load up Lum's and other web sites. We are not normal people. Should games be playable only by those with obsesive compulsive traits? Let's say you make 20 dollars an hour at your job. Now imagine an ingame object that costs 36,000 gold. The Ugly

The medium of online games is evolving. Every decision made by a company should be looked at closely. This one bears a special level of scrutiny. Do we flock to the banner of "mad" Richard and embrace the notion of real world money for in game value, or do we take Verant's lead and try to put a stop to it once and for all? This story, I am sure, is going to be developing for a good long time.

THE MAN IN THE HIGH CASTLE
Monday, April 3, 2000 - 10:49 AM [Filed by lum]
So, what happens when you build an online game and its main featured character leaves?

Ultima Online was unique in that it actually had a star - Lord British, the longtime alter ego of Richard Garriott - who planned on appearing in the world on a regular basis. In Garriott's words:

I actually play the main character, one of the main characters Lord British, and the director of the game is a guy named Starr Long will play a character named Blackthorn, and we're the two primary antagonists in the game that kind of set the stage for the big "save the world" questing activity that takes place within the game.

You have to participate in this game to keep it going?

Absolutely. In fact, to a large degree-- to as much as possible, the game is automated as to how it kind of keeps track of progress and doles out activity to players, but we wanted it to really be as living and breathing and believable a world as possible. And so in this case, both Starr and I will be present as often as possible to kind of keep up the kind of real world believability that we want to be there.

Richard Garriott and my alter ego, Lord British are so much a part of this game that my work is never done.

Thankfully for Origin's sake, the fate of Ultima Online didn't rest on Garriott and Long appearing ingame on a regular basis. If nothing else, this original vision was doomed by sheer numbers - with 150,000 citizens, personally interacting with even 1% on a regular basis would leave Garriott with little time to do anything else. The ingame fiction proceeded on, and although built around the struggles within British's errant kingdom didn't actually require his presence. (Or Long's, who to our knowledge appeared in character a grand total of twice after UO's beta.)

Now that Garriott is gone, presumably off to work on worlds non-Ultima in nature, the fate of Britannia's namesake hasn't been really touched on yet. It's time that it was.

One of the most promising aspects of virtual worlds such as UO is the ability for the world itself to evolve, based on its playerbase. However, to this point, the government of Britannia itself has been locked. Although several attempts had been made to overthrow British's rule in-game, they usually ran square up against the fact that Lord British's continued rule was hard-coded into the game engine. His castle wasn't going anywhere, not even decaying from disuse, and his army consisted of utterly unstoppable Uberguards. Any rebellion was somewhat doomed to futility.

Until UO:Renaissance, that is. The faction system is derided by those it was supposedly designed for as a bug-ridden exploit-happy poor brother of Order/Chaos townfighting, but the political aspect of factions to this point hasn't been really explored. Here's a perfect opportunity.

The King is dead. Or gone. Or whatever. The point is that for the first time in UO's history, the supposed government of the shard - the collector of taxes, the commander of guards, the supposed repository of every citizen's loyalty - is up for grabs. This is about as perfect an introduction for a system of political power into UO as it gets.

For this to work, of course, factions would have to be more meaningful then the latest sop to get PKs to mend their ways. They would have to have actual, meaningful power on their shard. They would have to have the ability to affect those who may not want to be affected (that being what governments do).

Picture the resolution of the endless stat loss debate being handled at swordpoint. Picture one shard being ruled by iron-fisted tyrants who penalize their citizens for appearing improperly dressed. Another shard where rules are set at a whim, ruled by robber-barons out to exploit their victims - er we mean citizens - for as much as possible. And still another shard where those who stand for justice and honor still try to hold the line, in the memory of their departed soveriegn.

Picture a world where players matter. It can happen.

Assuming that Origin has the courage to give the shards back to their players. And, even more frightening, the courage to challenge their own icons.

We shall speak of this again, you and I.

Anyway. Cynthe, Calandryll for the New Generation, put her foot down about a recent Origin employee's speculating about interesting uses that motion captuie equipment has been used for:

Richard may have insinuated something that isn't quite accurate... according to Ubiq, our lead designer, sex won't be in UO2 any more than it is in UO.

Not to say that people won't find creative ways to do the things they want to do, but no, we're not motion capturing steamy love scenes. :)

Ubiq, UO2's Designer Dragon for the New Generation, has also been exceptionally busy of late. Here's some postings for you.

A nice big one on Stratics including a discussion about UO2's non-use-based skill system:

Having implemented two use-based systems in the past, and then observed Ultima Online’s experiences, I have come to the point where I do not like use-based systems. I realize that this is a matter of philosophy, and you are welcome to disagree, but then there you go.

He goes into even more detail on XRGaming's UO2 devboard:

Skill trees start from a small number (currently about a dozen) boxes, and expand and unfold quickly as you advance. Fully expanded, our skill trees are large enough and numerous enough to wallpaper a room - I know this because Mobius has done so. Each skill tree has numerous branches, which offer different sub abilities. Some skill trees are quite large (the combat one currently has 150+ skills in it), whereas others are quite small (there are two skill trees that only have two boxes in them). We're talking a lot of freakin' skills here.

It is difficult enough to simply implement all of the ways that these skills can function, advance, and interact. Ensuring that each skill can only advance on use of that skill quickly becomes a technical infeasibility, a data storage monstrosity and a design nightmare. It also becomes difficult to add new things, and hard to balance. Given the options, we had a couple of choices:

* Reduce the number of cool skills that players can learn and perform, but allow them to advance only by doing them.

* Put in a simple-to-use, hard-to-exploit advancement system, and then put in a ton of freakin' skills.

Now then, if you ask me what makes for a better game, a more immersive world, and a more complete community, it is one that offers more skills and ways to grow and expand those skills, and by extension, greater character customization

The only good thing I see out of such a system is that it encourages combat.

Incorrect. If you are an accomplished blacksmith, and you attempt to advance by killing things, you will find it inefficient, since you're not very good at killing things. You're far better off doing what you're good at, which is makin' swords.

And, when someone started speculating about abusing the system by making multiple GM combat skills (gee, who'd wanna do that?), Ubiq said that, yes, related skills could be brought to mastery, but

My goal is no dual GM tankmages. If a way to develop one pops up, I'll whack it down like I'm playin' whackamole.


Some day, we can all play Whackamole Online.
