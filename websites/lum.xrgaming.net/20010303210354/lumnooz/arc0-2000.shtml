    RAPH'S LAWS: WHAT SHOULD HAVE BEEN LEARNED PT. 2
    Thursday, January 27, 2000 - 2:07 AM [Filed by great bob]
    Now it's time to seperate the men from the boys, the wheat from the chaff, the puppies from the...well, whatever one seperates puppies from. Strap yourselves in as we dig deeper into the "Big Book of Raph" and look at some more things he brought to the table.

    Before we begin though, Raph was kind enough to point out a mistake in the first part of this little essay. The law "Looking at what parts of your game players tend to automate is a good way to determine which parts of the game are tedious and/or not fun." was indeed added *after* his experience with UO. As he stated "The Laws are a living document that has been evolving since before UO shipped to well after." How would he do things today? Well, I guess we'll have to wait untill we find out his mysterious next project...which I can only assume is an Massivly Multiplayer Online version of Frogger unless someone on the inside is willing to tell me different.

    Anyways, on with Raphs rules...nd how the big three stack up to 'em.

    If you write a static story (or indeed include any static element) in your game, everyone in the world will know how it ends in a matter of days. Mathematically, it is not possible for a design team to create stories fast enough to supply everyone playing. This is the traditional approach to this sort of game nonetheless. You can try a sim-style game which doesn't supply stories but instead supplies freedom to make them. This is a lot harder and arguably has never been done successfully.

    UO and EQ: Let us accept for the minute this is true. Does this mean that it's not even worth trying? Well, EQ and UO would have you believe this is true. We've seen some good steps recently by UO to buck the trend, but no matter how many uncorruptable seers you have, we're still on our own when it comes to enterntaining ourselves. Is this law true though? Well, AC doesn't think so....

    AC: They think different. They think they can do it. Of course there are people who believe you can levitate. Those people wind up hopping up and down on their ass. AC seems bent on giving it the old college try. Let's wait untill after they hurt their ass before we pass judgement.

    It's possible that technology and really good bots will be able to provide players, someday, with stories. Not today though. The much touted quest engine in EQ turned out to be nothing more than a good way for folks to camp for l33t goodies. If a quest engine ever went into UO, expect more of the same.

    The expectations are higher than of similar actions in the real world. For example: players will expect all labor to result in profit; they will expect life to be fair; they will expect to be protected from aggression before the fact, and not just to seek redress after the fact; they will expect problems to be resolved quickly; they will expect that their integrity will be assumed to be beyond reproach; in other words, they will expect too much, and you will not be able to supply it all. The trick is to manage the expectations

    UO: Just get players used to the response of "I'm sorry, there's nothing I can do to help you." and you're problem is solved. Actually, that's just harsh and more than a bit unfair...although for a monent I caught Myschef nodding her head. Raph was talking about more than player grievences. He was talkiung about the whole big picture. A carpenter expects that his hour of work will wield the same profit as the hours work of a blacksmith...and in a virtual world, why not? In the arena of "fairness" in skills UO has had it's sucess and it's faliures. UO has also paid attention to the grivences of all groups. They haven't done too badly. We can tell because everyone in UO thinks the dev team is out to get them. A fair compromise is where both sides walk away from the table unhappy. In regards to resolving complaints...well, it's a long road ahead to repair the damage. Will faith be restored in the GM program? We'll see...we'll see.

    EQ: If EQ has a greater flaw than security, it would be in the intrest of "fairness." From a combat system that is unfair to all except Rangers to a blatant disregard to the complaints of the community, EQ has it all. In EQ you are not rewarded for any labor except camping. The trade skills are little more than ways to spend money wihtout reward. Even in upholding the rules, the one thing that can be said about EQ GMs is that they make UO look good.

    AC: The new kid on the block allways has it the easiest. EQ was once reknowned for listening to the players and for good support. Will life in AC be "fair" for the players? Ask me in a few months.

    Online game economies are hard. A faucet->drain economy is one where you spawn new stuff, let it pool in the "sink" that is the game, and then have a concomitant drain. Players will hate having this drain, but if you do not enforce ongoing expenditures, you will have Monty Haul syndrome, infinite accumulation of wealth, overall rise in the "standard of living" an capabilities of the average player, and thus unbalance in the game design and poor game longevity.

    UO: An online economy might be hard, but you can't fault UO for trying. Of all the three, only UO has come close. From rare items like paintings, to housing, to replacing lost or decayed items, "too much money" is a problem a player won't face in UO for a good long time. In UO there is a reason for blacksmiths to continue making items, there is a reason for an alchemist to grind away, there's even a role for the tailor and the fisherman. There are sites on the internet and in front of banks that see as much acticity as any middle east bazzar. Perfect? Of course not. The final drain needs to be established. Player titles? In city housing? Items rare and desirable sold only by NPCs? The ideas are there.

    EQ: For those who doubt Raph on this subject, boot up EQ. Your items never decay, your body is allways recovered, and what do you do after you've camped every spawn point? Well, give it to some newbie to twink 'em up. What the hell, it's only stuff, right? The minimal trade skills EQ has are seldom used. Why? Well, a blacksmith has no need for money and where will he sell his things once his customers all have magic swords of whoop ass?

    AC: They can't even plead ignorance on this one. They had years to figure it out. Still, like EQ, they overlooked this one important facet of what makes a virtual world as opposed to a first person whooter with swords. In any world, no matter how simple, there is an economy. In a world where there's no place to put your things, nothing to spend money on, and an allegience system that encourages twinking, you are left with a world devoid of one of the most important aspects of human society. For a group that got so many things right, they got this one wrong.....horribly, horribly wrong.

    Ownership is key. You have to give players a sense of ownership in the game. This is what will make them stay--it is a "barrier to departure."     UO is the anvil. All the anvil does is sit there and take the blows. UO originally expected community to arise, phoenix-like, from the ashes of the corpses lining the streets of Trinsic, with no attempt at in-game programming support. Support for guildstones was quickly added, however, and the most rewarding moments in UO often occur in the context of guild interactions, whether between guild members cooperating to meet an objective or conflict between two or more guilds.

    EQ by contrast is the sledgehammer. Everquest was designed to be played by groups of 3 to 6 players at a time. You can try to play EQ by yourself, but the experience is painful and tedious at best. The game really is built around this one concept - a team of six, with interlocking dependencies and strengths. A cleric can heal exceptionally well but has little offensive capability. A warrior can take brutal punishment but is totally helpless if the target decides to, say, leave. A wizard can kill creatures merely by looking at them sternly, but woe betide her if that creature survives long enough to hit back. If you work with the sledgehammer - the intent of the designers - you can find a fairly decent gaming experience. If you work against the sledgehammer, well, it feels about as if a sledgehammer hit you. Larger-scale player interactions don't really work in Everquest. Not only is the 3D engine not well suited to 30 people talking to one another, but there really is no function for a large-scale guild in EQ save the coordination of forming those smaller, 6-person groups. You will play the game the way it is decreed or you will suffer.

    AC is too young to really make honest judgements in its community building. Call it the air between the sledgehammer and the anvil? Naw, let's just walk slowly away from that metaphor and not look back. The allegiance/fealty system is interesting in theory, but so far in practice it mainly consists of a total stranger walking up to you and offering you phat l3wt if you promise to give him your XP. It will be interesting, however, to see if a larger guild can really make the allegiance system work, especially in a roleplayed environment. I expect this will take place on Darktide (the PK server) first as the artificial constraints of PvP switches tend to hinter the ability to deal with idiots without outside intervention. Azile is weaving a fairly interesting tale of shifting allegiances on her site, although I'm kind of lost as to how she can make ruthless fun of Alice-te Katrina one day and ally with her the next. Then again, I've been nice to Durga lately, too.

    With the possible exception of AC, community building is really an afterthought for all of these games. The expectation with UO was that the sheer critical mass of thousands of people online at once would just spontaneously generate community. Maybe it got PKed. EQ is a good example of the other side of the equation. Community building is almost impossible in EQ simply because you rarely play with the same people in any given gaming session due to the heavily forced nature of grouping with those of identical needed skills. I'm told that once lvl 50 (the top tier of advancement) is reached this changes, and uberguilds form at that point. Maybe in three years or so I can tell you about that first hand.

    But what if a game was actually built on the thought that the fun of the game is in interacting with a given group of friends on a somewhat long-term, persistent basis -- I know, it's crazy, but it just might work.

    Let's discuss three of the new games hoping to prove it - Shadowbane, Neverwinter Nights, and "X".

    SHADOWBANE - "THE WEAK SHALL PERISH!"

    Shadowbane is probably the closest of these to release, and we may well see it around the same time as UO2 (late 2000), with a limited beta due to begin soon. It's made a few interesting assumptions.

    Shadowbane has positioned itself as a Player vs Player game. While a risky assumption when looking at Everquest and Asheron's Call's successes with a PvP switch, bear in mind that games such as Quake and Unreal outsell both of those by a wide margin. Lots of people do want to kill each other (virtually, we hope). The problem here is that those same PvPers tend to attract a fairly large subset of ...assholes. You see, unlike Quake or Unreal where equality lays just past the next respawn, it is possible to be a bully in ORPG PvP, and to spend your entire online career preying on those far weaker than yourself. In fact, that's really the most efficient method of advancement, especially in UO. Like Willie Sutton said, you go where the money is, and human beings do a better job of collecting it then "a troll" or "a_froglok_shin_lord". Thanks to developers buffing up monsters to be a challenge, humans, tend to be quite a bit easier to kill, as well. Bullies, in case you missed the experience in grade school, tend to be assholes. Generally, you do not want to spend money to be around assholes. In fact, a lot of us spent a great deal of money to avoid assholes. Especially in grade school.

    Shadowbane's developers seem to be going after said assholes with great gusto, based on their marketing thus far - "I don't play games to bake bread, I play them to crush!" "The strong will do well, and the weak will perish!" Think what would happen if you put Conan in charge of your advertising.

    Luckily, there seems to be a bit more to Shadowbane than testosterone poisoning. Judging from what information has been released, the game is built around a meta-guild system. Players join a guild, which can be run by their friends or by the game masters themselves, and those guilds themselves band together in nation-states, which, as nations tend to do, invade each other for the purpose of rape, pillage, and proving the superiority of their economic system.

    Ultima Online's developers have made noises of implementing a similar meta-guild system called "factions", but Shadowbane's system, if it can be successfully implemented, will provide a unique experience - think of an ORPG less as a monster-harvesting festival but as a wargame - a contest both of strategy and tactics, with each part from the Overlord to Spearchucker #12 filled by a human being.

    If it works, it'll be great fun. If it doesn't work, it will make Ultima Online's darkest days look like a paradise of sharing and caring. Place your bets.

    Dr. TwisTer's Shadowbane site has done a stellar job on keeping an eye on Shadowbane's development. Check the FAQ sections in particular for a large compendium of what the Shadowbane dev team has had to say on the game's design so far (which has been quite a bit).

    NEVERWINTER NIGHTS - "I ROLL A D20. DO I MAKE MY SAVE?"

    Neverwinter Nights, due in 2001, will change the ORPG landscape. Other games will suddenly wonder where all their players went. It looks that promising.

    NWN (not the first game to bear that title, interestingly enough - Neverwinter Nights on AOL can make a good stab at claiming the title of first mass market ORPG) is an AD&D simulator. As described, it consists of two parts - the Player's Handbook and the Dungeon Master's Guide.

    The Player's Handbook - the game client - is similar in form if not function to the Bioware engine used to power Baldur's Gate and Planescape: Torment. While using 3D rendering instead of the 2D sprites of the earlier titles, it promises to be as faithful to the AD&D rules set.

    The Dungeon Master's Guide is where things get interesting. Bioware is promising to include a complete authoring system for NWN. You can design your own "modules", and act as game master to a group of adventurers. A 56k modem might host 6, a cable modem might host 64.

    This model presents all sorts of interesting possibilites, and Bioware looks set to act on them. Among the promised features - portals between servers, which opens up the potential for linked worlds hosted by a co-operative of game masters. A "Character Vault" similar to what is promised for Diablo 2, to prevent character hacking by keeping character data server-side.

    Wanted to be a seer in UO? This goes further. Imagine not only designing your world and your storyline, but controlling key monsters within it - when the players talk to them, they talk to you. Similar to some events we've seen in UO already, but given the lower player-GM ratio in NWN you stand a far better chance of affecting the story. In fact, in NWN you are the story; it restores the "single player" paradigm of the players being the center of their own particular universe.

    NWN is still quite far from completion, of course, and it's always risky basing evaluations on such early specifications. Still, this is one game to keep an eye on.

    "X" - RICHARD GARRIOTT'S APOLOGY

    Lord British has been batting about .002 for pretty much the past ten years now, so you can bet that when he talks about his new project, it's with a certain air of "god damn it, this time we're doing it right". The funny thing is, he might well be.

    We can thank Ultima 9 for what information we know about X - in the wake of that disaster, Garriott hit the interview subject really, really anxious to change the subject, so he was positively verbose about what he's currently working on.

    "X" - purposely not Ultima X, and one can't blame him for wanting to get away from Sosaria after spending pretty much his entire adult life there - is, as described in purposefully vague detail, a massively-multiplayer game with components of the single-player experience. In other words, like Neverwinter Nights. In Garriott's words:

    So with X, I've put these two types of games [single player and massively multiplayer] together. For example, you start the game in what I call a homestead—a place to build your house, hang the head of the dragon you just killed, or meet up with friends and trade. When you want an adventure, instead of doing it like Ultima Online, where a thousand people are ahead of you and a thousand people are behind you waiting to do the same adventure, imagine that the adventures are individual Ultima games with multiple people. You go on these adventures that are custom-crafted from anywhere from solo player to massively multiplayer and everything between. The adventures could be like Quake Arena, where it's every man for himself. Or they could literally be solo-player Ultima IV, where you go on that adventure alone.

    Sounds good so far. Then you get quotes that make you think Lord British's crown is on a bit too tightly. From another interview:

    Garriott says he hopes to break new ground with X, taking gaming to an area that, at first blush, sounds nearly impossible. "I want to create a game that does not reward devotion," he says. In other words, time invested in X won't necessarily equal advancement. Right now, in order to bring virtually any gaming character to a level where you want to be (high level, advanced spells, BFGs, etc.) you have to invest dozens and dozens of hours of gameplay. It's something hard core gamers have come to expect, but it tends to alienate casual gamers, a much bigger audience. Garriott says he hopes to replace that required time investment with something else, though exactly how he's planning to do it is something he's keeping close to his vest right now. "Think of your best friend," he explains. "Your best friend is not your best friend because he spends the most time with you."

    And later:

    "I want to put the ability to put real money in a virtual world," he says, with a proud smile on his face.

    Put those two together and it's a pretty ugly picture. At any rate, it's doubtful we'll see anything concrete about X in the next couple of years. Still, it's interesting that apparently Origin is also aiming for the ad-hoc group adventure model.

    At any rate, UO2 should be the last of the "throw enough players at the screen and see what sticks" model of game design. (And hell, considering that we know absolutely nothing about UO2 save that it will probably look a whole lot like EQ, it's hard to say what design decisions they're working under anyway.) Which is as it should be - as the ORPG industry matures and more options become available, one might actually be able to comparison shop gaming environments and choose the one that suits their fancy, instead of choosing at random from two or three that try to be all things to all people.

    SOME CALM, RATIONAL THOUGHTS ON PRECASTING
    Monday, January 10, 2000 - 9:12 AM [Filed by lum]
    I believe the removal of precasting will utterly destroy PvP. As of right now, the tank mage class is equal, if not worse in a duel, than a well-equipped melee fighter. Precasting adds flavor to dueling; it makes PvP what it's supposed to be--fun. The addition of the effect of dexterity on healing gives the melee fighter an even greater advantage. Isn't it the dev team's goal to preserve the balance of the game, rather than destroy it? Personally, I have made a 7xgm melee fighter (maces, tactics, anatomy, resist, healing, magery, and parrying with 100 strength, 95 dex, and 30 int) and a 7xgm tank mage (swords, tactics, magery, resist, eval int, meditation, and wrestling, with 100 str, 100 int, and 25 dex). However, you will be forcing the PvP community to play these *boring* melee fighters if you actually do take out precasting. Why do I say *boring* you ask? Well, playing a melee fighter involves almost no skill, it's basically just connection & equipment vs. connection & equipment. This makes a very boring duel, one that makes me consider playing other ORPG's. Please take these facts to heart. The dev team will create irrepairable damage by putting this patch into effect.

    Shadow of Sonoma (from CoB)


    The above was the most coherent defense of precasting that I could find in a short search. So let's take a look at this.

    First off - duels vs free-for-all combat. I enjoy PvP in UO, in fact that and the virtual economy are the only reasons I play. The monster AI in UO is a joke and bashing on helpless terathans or whatever from above a cliff is just utterly boring to me.

    That being said, I have never "dueled" in UO. The whole thing just seems artificial to me - and, dare I say it, a refuge of those who are frightened of being outnumbered. So I'm writing this from the position of someone who doesn't duel. I realize this probably disqualifies my opinion for the more rabid among you.

    So. You have an artificial situation already. Now, thanks to UO's current skill system, you have two universally agreed upon archetypes - tank mage and dex monkey. And, if you believe the proponents of precasting, tank mages are only competitive if they are allowed to essentially get in a free hit - a precasted e-bolt/flamestrike/what-have-you.

    Now, let's call this what it is - a free hit. It's not balancing, it's not "honorable", it's not "justified", it's an artifact of bad client programming. In popular parlance, a "bug". (BTW, I've heard from Origin that NO ONE is or will ever be banned for precasting, so those particular wild rumors can stop now.)

    If the tank mage archetype requires a free hit to be competitive, that is a problem with the tank mage archetype. What you should not be demanding is leaving in precasting - what you should be doing is looking at the combat system that makes that archetype uncompetitive.

    Now, let's look at PvP from another angle, the well-balanced group. In a good PvP group, each member of your team has a role. You have mages assigned to healing. You have mages assigned to paralyze duty. You have nukers (ebolt/flamestrike). And you have the melee team members who keep everyone else busy.

    Using this team-based model, pre-casting is pretty irrelevant. I mean, in a good team, you have 2 or 3 nukers already blanketing someone with mana dumps, so they're not going to try to be wading in with free hally hits anyway. They're too busy. If they're trying to engage in melee, they're not doing their jobs - plus chances are they're only wearing leather armor or less anyway for the meditation benefit.

    You can call this gang-banging if you want, but I've fought in battles outnumbered using this system and almost always came out victorious - and the times we've lost, it's usually because one of the mages thinks he's a tank mage and decides to waste valuable time wading in with a hally or something and gets killed. It's called tactics, and it's more than just a skill on your character, but a skill USING your character.

    Again, all of the above is useless for those of you who duel one-on-one. Maybe you can just let someone get a free ebolt before starting or something. It seems about as realistic as precasting.

    Now, this isn't to say that the UO combat system is not in need of a complete overhaul. It is. Archery is worthless in PvP now, for example. Specialization skills could easily be added. Say, Targeted Archery for archers - allows them triple damage or triple speed with bows if maxed. Of course that's 100 less points against the skill cap, too. The more of these the better - and maybe someday we'll see more than Tank Mages or Dex Monkeys on the field.

    Problem:
    Feign death allows you to become an invalid target in all circumstances, providing offensive tactics that are abusive. For this ability to remain balanced, the pet should follow the same rules as invisibility. Offensive tactics used by other classes involve the creature getting a saving throw verses the effect. The Necro can get into the room quite easily with Invis To Undead. To clear the room he uses Screaming Terror on one Bok and Charms the other. When the Ghoul Lord rushes him, he feigns. When the necromancer gets back up, he is in no danger of being attacked due to the way Feign Death works now. He then charms the 2nd Bok and Feigns again. The fight goes on and the 2nd Bok is killed. When the Necromancer gets back up he has a VERY wounded Ghoul Lord AND nearly full mana. The Lord is now an easy kill, and the necro collects his special items.

    Ideally, Feign Death should work like Invisibility, where summoned pets suicide and the charmed ones break Charm. We do not want, however, to adversely affect lower level Necromancers to such a large degree. What we do recognize is the need to prevent higher level Necros from exploiting key spawn points. Hence, when you Feign Death, any charmed NPC will immediately break Charm. However, summoned NPCs (pets) will NOT be affected. While this still allows the use of the Feign Death tactic with your summoned pet, it does not imbalance the game and many dungeon spawns to the degree that charming an NPC does.

    We would appreciate diligent testing of this change, to insure that no unforeseen problems have been caused by it.

    ZAMBONI DRIVER OF TRADESPOT RESPONDS
    Tuesday, January 4, 2000 - 7:25 AM [Filed by wirehead]
    Tradespot (formerly the UO Market) continues to be a lightning rod for criticism and controversy. Here's some words from one of its founders, Zamboni Driver.

    Howdy all, Zamboni Driver here (no, not one of the 146 fake Zamboni Drivers who are trying to steal your gold, the real one).

    I'd like to take this opportunity to reply to some of the allegations against Tradespot that have appeared in the fora on this site. Here are the allegations, as I understand them:

    (a) Tradespot is full of greedy bastards out to make a buck any way we can;

    (b) Certain members of TS staff are low-life scammer doodz who should be drawn, quartered and fired from TS (not necessarily in that order); and

    (c) TS doesn't always respond well to the needs of its customers.

    Here are my responses:

    (a) When I started in the merchant website business (waaay back with The Trading Post, the precursor to UO Market and Tradespot), the website was pretty much all buy/sell ads for UO items. There was no housing resale market (no house ownership yet), there was no ebay market for UO (aside from duping, there wasn't enough of a gold supply in those days to support gold sales for cash) and life was simple.

    Well, The Trading Post merged with UO Market (when our host site, the-egg.net got hacked and we lost all our files) at just about the same time that the big housing patches went in and all of a sudden there was a housing sales boom. Some of us at UOM identified a big problem with the market - there was no way to do secure trades. So, those of us who had already established a reputation in the community started to do some third party brokering - we'd act as the middleman on deals for a fee (5% of the value of the trade). At the beginning, most trades were pretty small - even towers were selling for around 2M gp or so (now that's the average price for a legally placed large brick and it's almost impossible to buy a tower except for $ sales).

    Over time, as the brokering service became more heavily used, we added a Broker FAQ to TS to explain to people how to safely conduct trades with a TS broker. To my knowledge, NO ONE who has ever used a TS broker and who has followed the correct procedures has been scammed.

    I have brokered well over 1500 trades. Yes, I've made a lot of gold doing so - there's no denying that fact and, quite frankly, no reason to do so. Without exception, all of my clients were happy to pay the fee (which is, by the way, less than 5% on big deals) for the security of a safe trade.

    Keeping that in mind, I have been one of the biggest and most vocal proponents of a secure house trading system (check out my latest rant at Zamboni's Wish List.) Our goal when we started to broker wasn't primarily monetary (although the gold was a nice side effect) - it was to help players. I'm not claiming to be a tree-hugging-love-thy-neighbour-altruist, and I'm sure the hard boiled among those who read this spiel won't believe me, but that's the simple truth.

    (b) I've always maintained an open in-box policy. If anyone has concerns with a TS Broker or staff member, that person is encouraged to email those concerns to me at zamboni@tradespot.net. I always investigate the complaint fully and report my findings to the
    complainant.

    That said, people should understand that the benefit of the doubt goes to TS staff - people who have been chosen based on their reputations and their friendships with other TS staff; however, benefit of the doubt means just that - it does not extend to an assumption of innocence.

    To date, no one has made damning accusations against Pucky (the TS Broker who's at the centre of most of the controversy) that have been backed up by evidence. Until someone presents us with evidence that Pucky has scammed other players or duped in UO (an accusation that he's been cleared of by OSI), he will continue to be welcome as a TS Broker. TS will not serve up Pucky's head just to placate a screaming lynch mob that can present no evidence. If anyone has evidence (it doesn't have to be written proof - verbal evidence is welcome) that he or she would like to submit, please do so.

    (c) On this last point, I'm forced to agree with some of our accusers. TS hasn't always been fast enough in responding to the needs of our users. As TS has grown (far beyond our original expectations), we've made some questionable calls and expanded too quickly to keep up with the additional work. The area of TS which has suffered the most has been the Banner System - we didn't have a clear chain of responsibility for the Banners and consequently, when problems arose, everyone assumed that someone else was looking after them.

    As we continue to expand, I'm sure there will be other problems - we never claimed to be (even close to) perfect. We'll tackle those obstacles as we come to them, making mistakes along the way, but doing our best to serve the UO community.

    I hope that answers a lot of your questions about TS and recent events. As I mentioned above, I'm always happy to chat about events at TS, in UO or in the world at large - feel free to drop me a line at zamboni@tradespot.net.

    Happy Trading!

    RUNESABRE EXPLAINS IT ALL FOR YOU
    Sunday, January 2, 2000 - 12:58 AM [Filed by wirehead]
    I suspect the CoB Dev Board isn't Y2K compliant, because an honest-to-god informative post appeared, where Runesabre, UO's lead programmer, took the time to answer some interesting questions. In case it scrolls off the board before you get there, it's reproduced below.

    1) From practical game experience, my vanquishing collection went from 4 in approx 18 months of playing, to over 60 in 3 additional months.Would you agree or disagree that the SOS has become a blight on the magic weapon market of UO?
    1a) If you agree, what are you going to do about it?
    1b) If you disagree, what function does dumping large amounts of gold and items into UO serve?

    1) I have personally loathed the day the fishing changes were done. If you recall, I was the one that "nerfed" the original implementation within a week after its release. I am in favor and enforcement of reasonable Risk vs Reward. Treasure hunts were a good reward but I made sure that the risks and cooperation was pretty high as well. Fishing up free loot in the form of treasure maps and SoS's do not represent any sort of risk yet they are nearly the same rewards as treasure hunts. That of course is where the players come in. My initial "nerfings" were not met with a very positive reaction and it was decided to relax the changes slightly. I still believe fishing is way too lucrative and does nothing but present a thorn in the economic side of UO.

    2) What incentives do you plan to offer in the future for people not to own a house?
    2) That seems to me like asking what incentives am I going to provide for players to stay as a newbie. Owning a house is one of the big goals players strive for just like they strive for being GM in skills. We have made adjustments to make owning multiple houses not necessary. That is why you can now place multiple vendors in one house ( as opposed to one vendor per house limits ), you can assign friends and co-owners who can have their own personal storage, and internal lockable doors.

    3) What are your thoughts on gold sinks? If you like them, why don't we have more of them in the game? If you dislike them, then why?

    3) Of course I want gold sinks. Their time allocation has not arrived yet in the whole scheme of things.

    4) What are your thoughts on starvation? I'm confident that eating could be done in such a manner that it wouldn't be any more annoying than having to buy regs. Assuming you could, on a 1-10 scale, 10 being the highest, where would you put starvation as a priority.

    4) I think Cooking has the potential to be just as fun and important to UO as Blacksmithing and Magery despite its current lack of luster. I have many ideas for Cooking but again its time has not been prioritized like so many other things we would like to do.

    5) Do I actually have a Guaranteed Tower (tm) waiting for me in these new lands, or is that just a myth? It sure seems like everyone says there's going to be one there for each of them. What are you going to do for the highly disenchanted have-nots of housing after the "new land" patch?

    5) Did someone say that everyone was going to have a tower? I am sure you realize that is a tad ludicrous and I doubt anyone seriously believes such a rumor. There will be significantly more housing, though, so those who have never had a chance to own a house can certainly expect their chances to be much much higher at owning one in the near future.

    6) One of the worst things that can happen in UO, aside from being hacked, is to be scammed out of large sums of gold for a house. When, oh when, is secure house trading coming? Also, what about house swapping, as, with no gold sinks, it's hard to get someone to give up a house when you don't have a house already.

    6) Its one of the top priorities.

    7) You said many moons ago that stat advancement was troubling. What progress have you made on that front? Have you guys considered stat locks until you get a better solution? They can't be anymore out of context than skill

    7) If you work with skill locks enough you pretty much have stat control. You know which skills raise which stats so I don't see stat locks being all that necessary. But, the real reason stat locks do not exist is it would go against the idea of if you want certain skills you get their associated stats. You can't have everything just the way you want it. :) You can't gain snooping and avoid the dex or anatomy and avoid the int gains. If you want the skills you have to have the stats that go along with those skills.

    8) You asked for input about hard-to-raise skills. After the input, what did you learn, and what potential solutions do you have?

    8) Check out raising skills on Test Center right now. All skills that are non-difficulty based have been adjusted so that they raise both at low levels AND high levels. That was something I put in last night ( along with all the other latest developemts ) when Test Center went down a few times.

    9) The basis for murder count decay timers was on the accepted fact that people macroed. Now that macroing is illegal, what are your thoughts on changing the decay concept, for those that were playing by the rules, then the rules changed?

    9) The additional timers were put in because it was realized that 8 hours to wear off kills was not enough of a deterrant and not just for those with multiple accounts or those who macro.

    10) What are your thoughts on purchasing skill training at high levels for certain skills? Not only would it curb macroing, but it would also act as a gold sink.

    10) Its been brought up... as has been the side effect of "instant" characters by those who have more gold at their disposal than they will ever need. To stave off the abuse, the amount of gold it would cost would have to be so extreme that very few players would ever benefit.

    11) Is beeswax a precursor to bow repair?

    11) One of the designers is working on something related to beeswax but I forget what at the moment.

    12) PvP balance is a touchy issue. From my perspective, you guys won't touch PvP because it would be time consuming to balance it, and you guys don't have time. Is this an accurate interpretation?

    12) I would personally love to overhaul PvP in UO. Despite the fact its better than it has been in any time in the past, it pales to what I can envision PvP being. My goals are always for true, viable diversity as opposed to lots of choices but only a couple real true options for PvP. ( For instance, do I be a Tank Mage or a Dex Monkey? ). I want to see a lot more rock/paper/scissors related to PvP, I want to see all the weapons have their unique purposes depending on the situation, more choices and ramifications to using different types of armor ( leather armors could be more arrow repellant while plate tends to snuff magical ability for instance ). We have made a lot of tweaks here and there. One of the things that makes messing with PvP are all the side-effects one change can make. PvP is a web of interdependencies so there is rarely any true "I can fix/change this and bring everything into line" because in changing one thing you end up changing a lof of other things. Its come to where I believe you can't make just a couple magical tweaks and make PvP more balanced like we have in the pasy and most likely it would just be only slightly different and overall more of the same. I would like to see PvP have a complete scale of choices and tactics but such an endeavor would require much more than a couple tweaks and hope things stay balanced. So yes, time is one of the big factors for holding PvP changes back.

    13) What is your view of a balanced PvP system? One in which mages/archers/meleeers/tamers/bards/etc all have an equal chance of winning, or is it a group dynamic?

    13) Pretty goes with #12.

    14) Tamers have a few built-it checks on their targeting so that they don't have to wait for the skill cycle to re-tame. How hard would it be to give this to bards?

    14) No real thoughts.

    15) The whole point to karma is as a role playing tool. It was stated as such back in the days of rep. However, to PvM and to have low karma are counter to each other. Any thoughts on a karma lock? Any thoughts on a "i kill monsters for pleasure" toggle so that we can kill for fame and not karma.

    15) Karma is like stats... its not really an option you choose. If you kill monsters then you gain karma. If you do "bad" things you lose karma. What really needs to happen is more choices of "doing bad things" that will raise your fame but lower your karma that are equal to killing monsters. Perhaps more "good" creatures that still carry loot and such.

    16) A looooooooong time ago, showing one's title was turned off, as a way, if I remember right, to make being a dread lord tougher, as you couldn't tell newbies from experienced players. UO is VASTLY different now, yet we still have this legacy hold over. Any thoughts of simply turning titles back on for all? If no, then why does one's ability to cast EV determine if you show title or not. You -have- to be a mage to show title, wouldn't you agree? If not, instruct me on how to show title without casting a spell.

    16) Yes, just haven't gotten around to it yet

    17) When in the 12 month timeline are you guys going to fix the house placing code?

    17) You can try it out right now on Test Center.

    17b) Is a house with a basement that is placed by clicking the deed, clicking the land, and having a house appear an illegal house or not?

    17b) We don't have any houses with basements do we?

    18) What are your views on the rare item market? Are you sympathtic to them, or do you want to put them out of business by making everything craftable. Where do you stand, on a 1-10 scale, 1 meaning you actually want to take some craftables out, 10 meaning you want every piece of art craftable.

    18) Its not as cleancut as don't touch anything considered rare or make it all available. Some things ( like silverware ) should be craftable and I would say its not fair that they would be left as rare ( hence they became craftable ). We do not go out of our way to purposely screw the rares market and are more attune to it now.

    19) On a "rare" item note, can you PLEASE delete all "a scrolls" from the game when you fix this bug? I don't want to hear this advertised as a rare item like no draw tiles. Heck, can you delete them too?

    19) *chuckles* Personally I like the whole dynamics of things being considered "rare". Which would you prefer time spent creating more gold sinks or time spent deleting "a scroll" items? :) As with all things it boils down to time, priority and who is available to do the development.

    20) As I've stated before, I think monster death will backfire, by putting more vermin in the dungeons than you can throw a stick at (see my attached pic, do you want to fight that?!?!?). How far along do you see a dungeon re-themeing taking place (if it's on the horizon) and if it does come along, will we, the playerbase, get a say in how it goes. For example, despise used to be a pretty good dungeon, now it's almost deserted. Is maximizing the playability of all dungeons a goal for you guys? (easy yes answer there).

    20) Of course we want dungeons playable. Again, it comes down to ... well you heard that before.
