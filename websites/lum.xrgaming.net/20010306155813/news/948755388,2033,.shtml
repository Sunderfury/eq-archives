RAPH'S LAWS: WHAT SHOULD HAVE BEEN LEARNED PT. 1
Monday, January 24, 2000 - 4:09 PM [Filed by great bob]

Be carefull what you wish for kids. Recently a rumor has been circulating that the lummies were fired by Lum, hece the lack of updates from us. Well, prepare to pay for that with a long ass multipart rant that you probally won't care about but realy should. (in truth, nobody at Lumcorp had time this weekend. Myschef was busy at a weight clinic for retarded asian lesbians, Arcadian was busy questioning his sexuality after dinner at KFC, I was busy doing terrible things to sheep and writing a new comic and liteguards...well she was doing something vitally important to the future of the human race that if I told you about I would have to kill you. It's a busy life here at lumcorp.)

Now that the big three of MMORPGFWIWSD (Massivly Multiplayer Online Role Playing Games Filled With Idiots Who Should Die) have been established, let's take a look at Laws of Mud design as compiled by Raph Koster and look at what the big three did right...and where they just screwed the pooch.

The Laws of Mud Design

The secrets to a really long-lived, goal-oriented, online game of wide appeal:

have multiple paths of advancement (individual features are nice, but making them ladders is better).

make it easy to switch between paths of advancementt (ideally, without having to start over)

UO: Well, it should come as no surprise that UO nailed this one on the head. I started playing UO with the dream of being a great fighter, weilding the sword of righteous fury against the scum who terrorized the land...then I realized that a sword did no real damage in the days before the dex monkeys, and the idiots will allways win. I wound up wandering between being an archer, an armsman than finally a mage. If I had to restart my character for every path, would I feel as attached to him? Probally not. I look at my character now, and see remnants of the skills he once had. He's unique.

EQ: Levels and Classes. If you're a mage, here are the skills you can and cannot have. Want to be a mage who wields a weapon different from other mages? Sorry. Want to become something different than what you are? Just start over. Neither of those games ever broke clear of the paradigm established by D+D back in the eighties. Levels may be helpfull to mark advancment, but classes force every character down a very narrow path. In the real world I work with computers...does that mean I can't also write a comic strip or weild a sword? Of course not. In EQ it does.

AC: [correction added after rant was put up] I have been informed that AC is closer to a skill system than a class system. This is quite so, and I apologize for mispeaking. That being said, the transition between advancment paths is moe dificult than UO.

No matter what you do, someone is going to automate the process of playing your world. Corollary: Looking at what parts of your game players tend to automate is a good way to determine which parts of the game are tedious and/or not fun.

UO: It's hard to practice what you preach, even if you're Raph Koster. UO is an environment where you almost have to macro. Fun is not clicking on an object hundreds of thousands of times. Instead of looking at the reasons why people macro, the dev teams (both old and new) decided to try and stop the practice instead.

EQ and AC: They got this one right, that's for sure. In AC you can macro. People don't because the process of gaining skills is actually fun. You do something. It might be killing rats or drudges over and over again, but at least it's an activity.

It is always more rewarding to kill other players than to kill whatever the game sets up as a target.

UO: It led to bad reviews. It led to violent debates. It led to a society of gangbanger wanna-bes. Before it fades off into the sunset, let's look at what Player Killing has brought to the table. It's an issue which devides the community, but would UO still be here without it? Most Likley. Would it still have as many players today without it? Actually, I don't think so. No matter how agressive an AI you establish, it'll never match the challange of another human. No matter how good you are, every combat you engage in will be dangerous. What is a game without a challange? More importantly it's a needed ingredient for building a society. Societies are often formed for mutual protection. If protection comes from code, why bother building one? It's not just a PK/Victim debate. There's another group like me who has fun defending an area from scum.

EQ: Charlie don't surf and apparently Elves don't fight. Even when PvP is allowed it's pathetic. After all the 50th level mobs are eliminated, what will remain except a chat room filled with half naked elf chicks? Damned if I know. The elves are easy on the eyes though.

AC: Will Rodgers once said "the middle of the road is the surest place to get run over". Well, AC walks that path and it works for them. Sure, they have a segmented community, but they actually put some thought into PvP combat. Is it perfect? No. Is it better than EQ? You betcha. Is it better than UO? Well, that's the tough one. You will never have the heated level of debate over PvP that you have in UO. If someone in AC doesn't like it, they can go to another server. Will the society be as dynamic? We'll just have to see.

Never trust the client. The client is in the hands of the enemy.

UO: Before the days of beta, people sat back smugly and said "all information is stored server side. There shall never be a hack". You in the back row, stop laughing. At the time it made sense. Raph was right. ANYTHING on the client will eventually be hacked. Want proof?

EQ: Here's your proof. I want to see a show of hands here on who believes that there isn't a massive cheat program out there? You with the hands raised, you're wrong. ShowEQ was the tip of the iceberg. EQ sat back fat and lazy with it's belief that security was good enough never to allow a cheat. They'll pay for that. Diablo sure did.

AC: I don't know, I've never taken the damn thing apart. I will say this to Turbine....if there is information stored client side, change it fast. You'll thank me for that advice later.

But where do the differences really lie between the big three? Next up, we'll look at the all important laws of ownership, storylines and a bunch of other goodies...and we'll talk about what the next generation needs to learn.
