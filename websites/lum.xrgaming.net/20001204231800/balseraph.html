Interview with Brian "Balseraph" Urbanek
Programmer, Wolfpack Studios
(3/3/00) Mr.Urbanek is currently working on Shadowbane, an ORPG which already has a suprisingly vocal and avid fan base. Myschyf, our intrepid Amazon correspondent, caught up with him for this interview.

How long have you been gaming?

Nearly twenty years. At the age of nine, my neighbor across the street who was four years older than me got me to play in a game of D&D with him.

I was hooked forever.

What games have you played?  You mentioned LARPs, D&D, and other RPGs in your posts.  Who? What? When? Where? Specifics?

Um…. all of them. My favorites are the WhiteWolf system for their theme and world (plus I’ve even done some writing for them), Champions for the brilliance of its design; Fading Suns, Shadowrun, both of the TSR Saga Games (Dragonlance 5th Age and Marvel Super Heroes) and (of course) AD&D.

Not quite making the “favorites” list are uncountable other products, each with their own touch of brilliance – In Nomine, Ars Magica, Gurps, Everway, Earthdawn, 7th Seas, Aria, SenZar, Scraypers, Rifts, Unknown Armies, Armageddon, and a few dozen others.

I’ve run as many as three campaigns, using three different systems, per week. My best campaign was an AD&D Birthright game that lasted for three years, and only ended because I moved to Texas.

I LARP whenever I get the chance, particularly at cons… it’s a great way to meet people and be social while gaming.

I also enjoy Renfaire, the source of the enclosed pic.

Obviously you’re a programmer and it sounds like you’re a game designer too.  Have you developed or designed any other games?  Which ones? How long did you work at the various companies?  How much actual impact did you have on the various games.

Yes, I used to work in my home town of Pittsburgh at Dreamforge. It was a great company, and they gave me the opportunity to pursue design, which I went after with great enthusiasm. The main title that I have to my credit is the WarHammer 40K adaptation, Rites of War. I was lead design on that project, as well as the “vice lead” programmer.

There is a really great underlying object oriented engine in Rites of War that I developed that was my primary selling point to Wolfpack. It was extremely robust and functional, and I think the guys here could see that, and “liked my style.” The rest, as they say, is history.

What brought you to WP? You pursue them?  They pursue you? Had you heard about SB before you came there?  If so, what about the game attracted you?

Yeah, I knew about Wolfpack and Shadowbane beforehand.

As a matter of course, I try keep up to the nanosecond tabs on the industry, and to play every game I can get my hands on, whether it’s my style or not. I firmly believe that a good idea can be found everywhere, and I really like to keep in touch with the industry – and that certainly includes consoles. An unfortunate side effect of that is that I don’t get to spend nearly as much time in individual games as I would like.

That’s where sites like Lum’s come into the picture. I’ve been an avid reader of Lum’s since the GM Darwin scandal quite a ways back. I had even posted infrequently over the year or so I’ve been reading it, under my previous pseudonym of Raziel, which I abandoned when that damned Konami game came out. So, about a year ago or so, Lum talks about an up and coming game called “Shadowbane”. I go to their site, check it out, and file it away for later reference.

Anyway, I was growing dissatisfied with Dreamforge’s direction – to put it simply, there weren’t any RPGs in the scheduled lineup, and I wanted to make one… bad. I sent out a couple feelers to companies with projects that looked promising, and Wolfpack was right near the top of the list. In January, I flew down for a spur of the moment interview.

As to the attraction… well, to be honest, the whole “play to crush” thing had me worried at first… I was afraid it was going to be a ravenous bunch of pkers with no real appreciation for role playing as I understood it. I couldn’t have been more wrong. The scope of the game is extremely broad, concentrating on politics, warfare, intrigue, storyline.. I actually much prefer the original tagline (“Join a Clan, Secure a Throne, Build an Empire”) because, from a game design standpoint at least, that seems to be more what the game is about.  The phrase “Play to Crush” is used in the office with a bit of a tongue-in-cheek tone.. but, unfortunately, that doesn’t really come through over the ‘net.

Except Belthior. He just plays to crush. But hey – it takes all kinds, no?

So anyway, they showed me what they had and gave me the inside scoop, I loved it, and three weeks later, I was at a desk in Austin.

What do you think makes a good MMORPG.  What are the most important factors?  Why?

Having the rules change every week, hard caps for character abilities, and spawn points that can be camped endlessly are definite musts. Also, it is very important that a key feature, like being able to own a house, be impossible for any player who wasn’t involved in the first month of playing, because newbies suck.

Bleh. That left an awful taste in my mouth.

Seriously, the most important thing in a role playing game to me is character, and the importance is increased a hundred fold in a MMORPG (we need a shorter acronym, and PSW ain’t it.).  It is extremely important to me that every player be able to craft a character that matches an internally held vision, and to develop him so that he feels like he is, if not unique, at least special, and if not the best, at least one of the best. At the same time, every character needs to have weaknesses, to have made trade offs, to have sacrificed things in his quest for power.

In addition to making for more interesting characters, this makes for good community. When other characters aren’t easily replaceable commodities (I want to join your guild! I’m a 7x GM Tank Mage! / Great… so are our other twenty members. Get lost.) but distinct entities in themselves, who can both contribute to a team and require the backing of others in certain circumstances, it fosters cooperation and camaraderie.

SB first put an emphasis on 'crushing' an opponent and then, it seemed, backed of a bit and touted the RP aspects of the game.  You (or maybe it was Varios) said in some posts that you and many of the other guys at WP are roleplayers and I can't see a group of roleplayers just making a 'bash the other guy' type of game.  But then, the other day, on Lum's board, you said "gameplay programmer + love of military strategy = game mechanics that support military strategy."  Do you really feel that SB is going to appeal to roleplayers and the tradition non-powergamer type of player?  How so?  How about those folks that don't give a rats ass about military strategy?  Will there be a place in SB for them?  Where?

WARNING!!!!

LONG RANT EXPRESSING MY PERSONAL OPINIONS! THESE ABSOLUTELY DO NOT REFLECT THE OFFICIAL VIEWS OF WOLFPACK, OR ARE MEANT TO BE INDICITIVE OF ANY “OFFICIAL” COMPANY STATEMENT!

Right, that’s the big issue, huh? Well, I guess it comes down to your perspective on the thing. Let me put it this way: Everquest has no real PvP, but how much role playing actually occurs? I mean, sure, you can scream “Have at thee!” all day when your paladin fights a demon thing on the Plane of Hate, but battle cries at AI critters is hardly role playing in my book.

The kind of role playing that Wolfpack is going for in Shadowbane has less to do with Le Morte De Arthur and more to do with Braveheart -- intrigue, betrayal, loyalty, honor, and fighting over something that you believe in. Without a background of tension and the risk of great tragedy, moments of sublime beauty start to lose their impact after a while.

I don’t like the “crush” moniker. Crushing your opposition, in and of itself, is a wonderful thing. Crushing doesn’t interfere with role playing. It’s the perception that “crushing” is the whole point of Shadowbane that I have a problem with, because it’s not. That’s a gross understatement of a system that was designed.

Yes, there is crushing. Crushing your opposition, seeing them driven before you, and hearing the lamentations of their spouses (heh) is an absolutely supported means of gaining territory and resources for your guild, kingdom, and empire. The key is, it is only one of the solutions, and it won’t likely be the best one in most cases. Going to war with other guilds costs. It costs A LOT. It costs time, it costs XP as guild members die, it costs money as weapons and equipment are destroyed or looted, and it leaves you vulnerable to attacks from other fronts. Diplomacy, economic alliances, assassinations, covert warfare, religious conversion…. those are ALSO all methods of strengthening your empire. To me, when all you can do is crush, it isn’t role playing. When you could negotiate, but instead choose to crush, that is role playing.

What we’re after is the tensions and drama of a dynamic, thriving political society.  That means that people will learn to work together, because we, unlike UO or EQ, are going to give players things worth fighting over – cities, guilds, religions, and ideologies.  People have focused so much on the “I can fight other players!” aspect of the game that they’re missing the big picture of what we’re building.

Sure, a lot of players are going to form “bandit kingdoms” like the Knights of Chaos and try to screw the game up. Hopefully, they will be destructive enough to inspire blessed outrage among the guilds and the cities, and be crushed under an onslaught of role playing righteousness.

It’s true that player justice completely failed in UO. However, we are a different beast than UO. See, In UO, the same people were the antis as were the dreads. It was easy, because both roles required the same skill set, and let them do the same thing – indiscriminately kill other players. The tradesmen players, on the other hand, would need to have multiple skill sets, equipment derived from divergent sources… and, in most of the cases, being a hard core PK wasn’t what they wanted to do anyway. The system had no accountability, and no motivation besides basic human morality (oh, if only that were enough…) for “good” players to rise up.

Shadowbane is different. We have accountability, in the form of guilds with fixed bases of operation and crests. If someone kills you, you’ll know where they came from. We’ll have motivation to defend your allies and fellows, because everyone jointly contributes to a guild’s and city’s productivity.

And, of course, if anyone starts to go out of their way to not just play evil (which I’m all in favor of) but get really nasty and attempts to screw with other PLAYERS and ruin the game (like hacked res killing… which, by the way,  is impossible in our engine), the Dark Angel may pay a visit.

As for “powergamers” in general…. see, this is a point of contention for me. What is it about trying to make a character as powerful and competitive as possible is it that rules out role playing? It isn’t the goal that should be hated – it’s the methodology.

Take two UO characters – one power macroed overnight (okay, over a week) a 7x GM Tank Mage. The other played with friends, hunted monsters, and built a 7x GM Tank Mage over the course of a year, all with the goal of being a great hero and striving to rid the land of evil (or bring it to its knees). Both have the same character in the end, and both may very well have “elite skills”. Both can be considered  powergamers. One is also a role player, and one is not.

I like powergaming role players. People who would build up a character through loopholes and exploits, however, I have no use for. Do they have a place in the game? Absolutely. Will they enjoy Shadowbane? Probably. If they pay their money and DON’T RUIN THE GAME FOR OTHERS, then they have the right to play the game however they want, and I have the obligation to make sure that they have fun.

I take that obligation very seriously.

Are you guys gonna get a cute girl with lotsa teeth to be yer PR person for those trying times when you need to divert attention away from problems with your game?

God, I hope so!

Speaking of which, can you get me Lady MOI’s email address? Or is she still married? I lost track….

What do you think it is about an MMORPG that can keep a player interested over many years.  I'm not talking about 1 or 2 years -- that's small change.  I'm talking about 5, 6 or even more years.  I know a lot of people kept playing NWN for almost the entire time it was around without any type of possible character growth or game support (meaning new patches or expansions).  And when Kesmai slapped a GUI on IOK and called it LOK there were players that ported over their 10-year-old characters from Compuserve.  Do you envision this type of player loyalty for SB?  What is it about SB that makes you think you can keep players interested for that long?

Damn. Tough one. I will admit this: I don’t fully understand the marketplace today. Then again, that’s not my job – that’s Warden’s job. What I do understand is the mindset of the hardcore player and hardcore roleplayer… exactly the kind of player that you’re talking about. I understand it because I’m both of them.

To me, gameplay is everything. I recently replayed Ultima III and IV (not to completion, though) just to get back in touch with that kind of experience. Until Age of Wonders came out, I still had Master of Magic on my system and played it regularly, and I played a few missions on the first X-Com a two weeks ago.

I think it comes down to this : 70% of  Shadowbane’s audience will leave us three years from release when the next big thing comes out, just like they’ll leave Ultima Online when Shadowbane, UO2, EQ2, and whatever else is in the pipeline come out. The other thirty percent, the hard core, people like me, people who have tired UO and EQ and AC and rejected them for us, and will be hard core with us for as long as we support it. This is because Shadowbane unquestionably has the breadth, the depth, and the features to keep someone playing it for a decade without ever being able to fully explore every option.

Besides, I firmly intend to make certain that the next big thing after Shadowbane is another Wolf Pack product.

What kind of impact are you, personally, going to have on the design and development of the game?  What changes are you bringing?  Do you just listen to Ashton's and Belthior's ideas and then say yes, well I can code that or no, that would be a coding nightmare, or are you actively involved in the game design? 

I am NOT actively involved in design. Wolfpack had a hundred and fifty page design doc complete and ready when I walked in the door, and it would be insane for them to let some newcomer change it on them. Are there things I would have done different? Of course. The key is this, though: though overall vision of Shadowbane is wonderful, and THAT I wouldn’t change a bit.

I will, however, have tremendous influence over the details. I was hired exclusively to help “fill in the blanks” of the game system implementation.   This includes things like combat and spell casting, so I’m uniquely qualified to suggest to the design team how they can be best tweaked. Plus, since I have to essentially memorize every formula and number, as well as EXACTLY how they interrelate, I’m in a good position to spot imbalances and imperfections or loopholes that may have slipped into the system. That’s actually one of the reasons they hired me, because my experience makes me extremely suited to just that role.

You've said more than once in your posts that SB is going to kick ass in the market place.  Why? What makes you say that? 

Shadowbane is going to kick ass because we have a style of gameplay and feature set that is built around the idea of supporting and encouraging what people already try to do in UO and EQ without support – build powerful, distinct characters and form a great, self maintaining community.

Eventually, the success of any game in the market comes down to this : is the game fun to play?  All the marketing in the world can’t fix a bad game design (well, okay, it could probably help you sell units.  But it certainly can’t help you build a community) and we’re going to build a game that is incredibly fun, even if it kills us.  We're hungrier than the other guys and we’ve got a great vision for the game we want to build.

Now it just comes down to execution.

Thanks Balseraph for a great and informative interview!  And thanks also go out to Warden for making him beef up the RP parts ::wink!:: (you really didn’t expect all this free publicity for nothing, did you?  You have to let me get in at least ONE jibe.)
