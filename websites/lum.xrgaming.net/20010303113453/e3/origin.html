Preview/Interview: Origin (The Game Formerly Known As UO2)
Developer: Origin Systems, Inc.
Publisher: Electronic Arts

Origin has had a bad year. At its beginning, it released Ultima 9, a critical disaster, technical nightmare and commercial disappointment, and was followed by a hemmorhage of employees including its founder and symbol, Richard Garriott, a botched release of an expansion to its one ongoing commercial success (Ultima Online: Renaissance), and most recently a radical change in direction of the marketing of its sole title known to be in development, from a sequel to Ultima Online to something that might have been constructed by a pack of rabid marketing specialists eager to cram as many buzzwords on a box as possible.

A rose by any other name is judged by its engine. I actually got to look at UO2's engine in action. (UO2, by the way, is still the name still used by the development team in casual conversation. This will probably change, but I suspect using "Origin" in this review would be a bit confusing, being also the company that develops Origin, I mean UO2. We wouldn't want to actually be confused by marketing. That would be counterproductive. Marketing would never be counterproductive!) It most definitely is not the Ultima 9 engine, or anything like it. It looks cleaner, it runs faster (even in its current state), and it doesn't as far as I know make you actually swim to anything. We'll get that out of the way right now.

I was shown UO2 in a closed door presentation crammed in with WEWP!, Crossroads, and a few other fansites. It was a very slick, very professional production. Very high production values. Very slick video. Very nice dancing. UO2 wins the Battle Of The Dancing MMORPGs. Jeff "Knight" Anderson memorized his laugh lines well. But what did the actual engine look like?

Well, it doesn't suck, and expectations for UO2 have been lowered to such a point that this is probably what they were aiming for. The engine does a good job of being graphic. It was pretty choppy, especially considering it was probably running on a supercooled Cray or something, but the dev team said that was due to almost no optimization having been performed on the engine yet. Clothing can be dropped on characters and the meshes will deform to suit, similar to what we saw in Anarchy Online. We were told (though not shown) that characters can do multiple animations simultaneously (such as running and shooting a bow) similar, again, to what we saw in Anarchy Online. Characters have a dizzying amount of emotes and social movements available, and I bet you can probably guess where else I saw them. Unlike UO, Origin actually has competition this time around. Sometimes Origin seemed aware of it, and sometimes they didn't, with statements like "You know, we're the only ones who ever actually did this before...". I guess Everquest and Asheron's Call were some other genre. Maybe "Games Without Unrestrained Peekays".

The demonstration, to a frustrating degree, focused to the exclusion of all else on eye candy. A revolutionary new skill system was touted, but not actually shown. A paradigm shift to Windows user-interface-centric right clicking was described, but not actually shown. Basically we saw lots of dancing, pretty scenery, and Blackthorne the Claw. What we did not see was actual combat, actual skill building, player crafting, or really anything else to distinguish the game from a very pretty 3D Diablo.

To be fair, the team was vey forthright when asked about this - "it's not done." UO2 is still a year away from release. What we saw looked good for a game with twelve months of programming work yet to go. It was reassuring to see the engine actually chugging away, especially with a 28.8k connection (E3's internet connection was down intermittently throughout the show). There really is a working UO2 client, Virginia, and it is capable of actually showing cool things. The motion capture technology that Origin is spending kazillions of dollars on (or possibly just going next door to EA Sports and saying "Hey, can we borrow those electrodes? We'll bring them right back") allows for some very cool animations. Everyone is laughing at the dancing meerlings in the demo (including me) but the truth is that no one has ever seen something as simple as two people actually dancing in step in an online game before. Small steps are good steps.

What we saw was really a "technology preview". We saw zero in the way of gameplay, although we did get a chance to discuss some of it with the design team later (see below for the full interview). If what they say holds true, UO2 will be far closer to a blend of the best of EQ and AC than to UO, which I suppose makes the ditching of the UO2 moniker even more of a good thing.

Some things we did find out about the game system are new (well to me anyway). UO2 is a level-based game, although in a fashion similar to Asheron's Call, levels are more guideposts to a person's advancement level than any actual real benchmark of power. Although two zones are labelled "unsafe" (aka PK+), the impression I got from talking to Damion Schubert was that PKs would not be welcome in his game. (It was probably him saying "PKs will not be welcome in my game" which clinched the suspicion.) Expect reputation systems/penalties similar to UO in their punitive impact on random PKing.

The skill system used in UO2 was also discussed, although more around the edges than in detail. It's obvious that it is still deep in development; it's also obvious that this is really the core of the game itself. Players will be able to be generalists (the "Tank Mage", say) or specialists (the best damned two handed axeman money can buy). There is a hard level cap and a hard skill cap, and it was mentioned that it would take "months" to get there. However, newbie levels were described as quick to pass and in any event new characters would be born "competent".

So what's my call? Too early to tell. The rapid dumping of core features (housing, guilds, rideable mounts) could mean a ship-by-this-date-or-else mentality that destroys every other Origin product, or it could mean a new commitment to quality and not promising what you're not sure you can deliver. Depends on who you talk to. At any event, the game is the farthest from release of any of the upcoming MMORPGs I saw at E3 (Shadowbane has a similar release date, but seemed much further along in development). We probably will see another E3 before release, at which time we can deliver a more reasoned judgement. Until then, watch the pretty dancers in the intro movie and read these interview transcripts while you download all 22 megs of it.

Everquest has guilds, and Asheron’s Call has allegiances, which are really quite cool.

Lum: What sort of issues did you come across with player housing that caused you to postpone implementing it?

DS: Friends list, bandwidth, ability to lock down, ability to decorate in a 3D environment, to build them, the ability to transfer them securely, and then there’s just exploit-proofing the hell out of them. Because invading someone’s house is actually as close to invading’s someone’s personal privacy as you can get in a game. It is more horrific to our players than being playerkilled. So when houses go in, they will be as exploit proof as we can make them. I’m sure we’ll miss something. I’m sure you’ll rant at me about it.

Jeremy Gaffney: Did you ever notice that Designer Dragon always had everyone yelling at him about everything, whether it was his fault or not?

DS: That’s true. But quite seriously. We could put in a half-assed guild system, you can name your guild and you can send messages, and the players will say “Cool! Guilds!” and the marketing department will say “Cool! Bullet points on the back of the box!” but you can’t screw around with housing. You can’t do it halfway. I would get destroyed, pilloried on the chat boards, and we would spend twice as much on GMs taking calls about people getting resurrected inside houses and all the other weird crap people do to exploit bugs. So would you rather it not go in, or that it go in and be broken? Essentially we need to build trust with the customers. It has to be done right. All the art for the houses is already done, most of the areas for the houses are already being zoned out, but the big thing is simply the programmer time that it takes to implement it, and actually get all these different systems working, and that is spitting distance, but still outside. It’s close, don’t worry about it, I just can’t promise it, I am not going to promise anything I can’t deliver because that would be wrong. Don’t laugh. No laughing!

Lum: Let’s talk about rogues for a second…

DS: Did Brad put you up to this?

Lum: Huh?

JG: They are group unfriendly, they are impossible to group, everything they take comes out of the party’s loot pool…

Lum: Been done. [laughter] Are they going to be able to steal stuff?

DS: Yes.

Cassandra: Are they going to be able to stuff their heads in your backpack and rummage around and get what they want?

DS: If they are really really really good.

Cassandra: So how do you just stick your hand in someone’s pack and rummage around without their knowing and…

DS: So how do you summon thunderstorms from the heavens or do super kungfu moves and teleport while flying ten feet through the air? This is a fantasy game.

Cassandra: That is fantasy, but things like thieving are basic reality. If you’re going to be a pickpocket and have pickpocket abilities, when you pick someone’s pocket you take what you get.

JG: OK, there was a thread on your site, the most fun you ever had in UO. And the first ten entries were these weird exploits.

Lum: That thread made me want to throw up.

JG: I know.

Lum: Threads like that tell me I need to take the forum down.

JG: So what’s the most fun you ever had in UO?

Lum: Very early in the game, I was just learning how to work a vendor. Vendors were just going into the game and I was building up my little merchant empire.

LoH: Selling shovels.

Lum: Yeah. Wealth through shovel sales. Building up stock, branch offices, underselling my neighbors, simulating being a greedy little capitalist, all the things I should be doing in real life instead and getting rich.

JG: You mean the actually encouraged proper real-world behavior and reinforced it? Unbelievable! And without exploits!

Lum: Oh, and standing on random street corners yelling weird crap at people as they pass. “You must be saved from the angry weasel horde!” People would think I was a quest figure or something. Of course, I do that on the web now, so…

JG: Of all the things Raph has done right and wrong, I think the largest thing he ever failed at was convincing players that the most fun, the most successful, the most innovative coolest, weirdest stuff in the game is the stuff the players come up with themselves. The players live there, they work there, they have weird goofy ideas. You know, we’ve got writers, and the players will either play along with the story or run amuck with it. That’s true for every RPG.

Lum: Back to rogues…

DS: We left the subject?

Lum: Jeremy tried very hard to change the subject…

JG: And I was very successful!

Lum: But I have a memory.

DS: UO2 is a social game. Every time I have to make a decision between social or antisocial, I always choose the social game. I have no problem with you playing alone. Playing alone is fine. You want to go have a world of fun all by yourself, I’m not stopping you.

Lum: And I’m not touching that line.

DS: So my implementation of rogues is, yes, they will be somewhat antisocial, but I’m going to be more concerned with how they will tie into the adventuring rogue, as well as the faction system and other things that we have. That’s what I care about, and as opposed to me just screwing your character around because I can. That’s not good gameplay.

Lum: So you’re not going to have scam artists or…

DS: People want to be that kind of thief, they will do that outside of the game system in general. I’m telling you the philosophy that we’ll launch with, we may screw up, we’ll fix what we can, people will scream nerfing, yah yah yah, I’ll hang myself from the garage.

Lum: Player vendors in UO, I already told you why they are near and dear to my heart. Are they going to make it to UO2?

JG: That would be part of player housing.

DS: That’s connected to player housing. If we can do it well, we’ll do it, if we can’t, then we won’t.

JG: We don’t want them to be an exploit of the system. We want to make sure that these vendors can buy stuff and create quests and whatnot, that’s part of the vendor system that really needs to be there.

DS: Making that exploit-proof – you think that’s a simple problem until you draw it out on the board.

Aureus: OK, so Gordon has said that scamming people is an exploit, and Jeff Anderson has said that scamming people is OK as long as it’s done within the game system.

DS: So, Gordon runs Live, and Jeff runs development, so it will be perfectly allowable to scam people during beta.

[Laughter]

DS: Gordon probably wins there. OK, let me put it this way. You’ve actually read about my first experience in UO, right? It made some list of  “Damion’s quotes” that scared the crap out of me, I thought, oh my God, I can never say anything again. So here I am, drunk and talking to Lum. [laughter]

Lum: With a tape recorder, yet.

DS: Even better. So my first experience in UO, me and my friend got bored of killing chickens within about 30 minutes, and we said, OK, so what can we do, so, we walked up to people and said, “Hey! We’re going to lead you to the unicorn!” And all these people said, “There’s no unicorn in UO!” And they followed us anyway! And we’d just go and lead them out of town – we didn’t even do anything, we’d just lead them offscreen. So, I was born in a realm of screwing with people’s minds.

Aureus: Are you saying this is finally how we ended up at this restaurant tonight?

JG: “There’s a unicorn up in northern LA!”

Lum: “Keep on going! It’s up on the 405!”

DS: So the answer to your question is, it’s up to the dev team to one at a time stamp out all the obvious exploits that are possible, such as “give me your axe and I’ll enchant it”. That being said, no matter how deep we make these systems, the stainless steel rats will still find a way to work their way through them.

Lum: So is preying on the idiocy of others going to be bannable?

DS: You’re going to have to ask Gordon, I wouldn’t risk it if I were you.

Lum: Another question. Let’s get away from idiots and move to a whole new realm of idiocy: politics.

DS: Now that’s a very near and dear one to me.

Lum: Myself as well. Now how much power are you going to give players in the world? In terms of political power? How much influence will they have on the environment, on the interaction between the three races?

DS: Basically what we will do, is we will ship with a relatively UO level of power, except that Aaron is working on a backstory that’s driven, and we’re working on ways to shift the things going on throughout the stuff, so players actually have an effect on the story that’s going on. My goal is sometime after ship to actually implement some sort of faction system, we already have a plan for it, that will give more ownership to players. But truth be told, I’m not really ready to give that at ship to players anyway, because we’ve got to make sure that everything else is unexploitable, and the faction system depends on so much of that stuff.

Lum: The faction system you’re referring to, how similar is that to what we’re seeing come out of UO?

DS: The funny thing is, we didn’t even know they were working on a faction system…

Lum: I find it odd that you’re using that terminology.

DS: We at first called it the political game, but now everyone calls it the faction system so I kind of picked up on it. Derek [Windfeather], the guy at the other end of the table, he’s already designed a faction system. It’s similar in some ways to his, it’s similar in some ways to what you’ve done, what you posted on your web site. In some ways we’ve got some of our own zany ideas about player interactions. And it’ll continue to be tweaked as we get closer and closer to ship. It’s very focused on the guild unit as a whole, because it’s easier to work with guild units as opposed to individuals. But this will happen post-ship, not before.

Lum: Back to PvP. Could someone actually maintain a successful career doing nothing but PvP? No monster bashing, just other players?

DS: Monster bashing will be necessary. I’m not allowing you to advance by playerkilling.

JG: Unless it’s zero-sum. I kill you, you lose as much XP as I gain. Otherwise me and my buddy sit there and kill each other all day.

DS: You will have to gain experience in some other fashion than killing people. Monster bashing is an excellent way to do this but not the only way.

Lum: So what is the thing you’re proudest of in UO2?

DS: The skill trees. Skill trees are far cooler than anyone outside our building really suspects.
