Auction or Sale of Characters, Items and Coin

We have consistently advised all players -- through newsgroup postings and otherwise -- that selling items and/or coin is inappropriate gameplay. BRAD VRS. E-BAY
Thursday, April 6, 2000 - 10:48 AM [Filed by great bob]

In the bad craziness of the great EQ debate over privacy, another big story was overlooked.

"You may not sell or auction any EverQuest characters, items, coin or copyrighted material."

E-bay no more? Is this a wave of the future? Can it work? Will other companies follow suit? Let's take a peak into the good, the bad and the ugly of this situation.

The Good

There was once a time when one could actually buy a house deed, and have a chance of placing it. I know, this sounds like fiction, but it was true. When players left the game they actually allowed their homes to decay. Then came E-bay. OSI nodded and winked at the auctions, and the population became homeless overnight...unless, of course, they were wiling to shell out some benjamins. Suddenly, the dynamic changed, and the good intentions of the Dev team with regard to finite rescources crumbled. If you're leaving the game, why sell your house for in game gold when you could get some beer money? This folks, is a no brainer. In EQ, camping became more than a playstyle. It became a job.

The Bad

So should we pop open the champaign corks just yet? Well, let's think about this. You see, people who have lives don't have time to sit in front of a computer screen playing a damn game 6 hours a day. I know this can be tough to relate to. After all, when we get home from work we play these games. At work we load up Lum's and other web sites. We are not normal people. Should games be playable only by those with obsesive compulsive traits? Let's say you make 20 dollars an hour at your job. Now imagine an ingame object that costs 36,000 gold. If you make 6,000 gold an hour in game, you're looking at 6 hours of labor. That's 120 dollars in real world money, folks. Given that, does it make any sense not to just purchase a 7xGM character with a house for 400 dollars? You see, these games aren't that much fun at early levels. UO is the worst offender. Try making a new character on a new shard. Tell me how long before you just get bored of the character building aspects. People pay thousands of dollars for hobbies like golf or fishing. Why not MMORPGs?

The Ugly

The biggest problem, however, boils down to one of enforcement. Is Verant going to be monitoring E-bay? Web pages? IRC chats? Everytime a character gives an item of value to another player, will they launch an investigation? Perhaps Verant should take a hard look at OSI's attempt to end macroing. As long as an object in game has "value" people are going to try an profit from it. It's in our nature as people to assign value to our posessions.

The medium of online games is evolving. Every decision made by a company should be looked at closely. This one bears a special level of scrutiny. Do we flock to the banner of "mad" Richard and embrace the notion of real world money for in game value, or do we take Verant's lead and try to put a stop to it once and for all? This story, I am sure, is going to be developing for a good long time.

I may just put my Lum account up for sale!

WRENCHING DEFEAT FROM THE JAWS OF VICTORY
Thursday, April 6, 2000 - 7:21 AM [Filed by lum]

Gordon "Abashi" Wrinn shows why he earns the big bucks, as in the aftermath of much good will towards Verant, complains about those stupid, deluded fools who had some problem with Verant poking around their user's systems:

Unfortunately it is a case where paranoia ended up winning out. I think that we could definitely have done a better job explaining what it was we were doing, and that would have lead to a bit more buy-in. Instead, some people decided to make up reports that we were scanning directory trees (false), internet files (false), internet history (false), cookies (false), and email (false), and unfortunately many people believed them.

The general paranoia resulted from the assumption that we (meaning: our servers) were actively collecting information from your system. This simply wasn't the case. The client simply would examine a small subset of information on your system, none of it containing information personally identifiable to a third party, and only send it to our server in the event that you were "running" an illegal program at the same time you ran EQ. We had absolutely no interest in what was installed on your system, only what you were running when you connected to ours.

I think privacy is important as well, but I don't really care about what a piece of client software is doing on my system. I only care when that piece of client software is transmitting information from my system to an outside source. In this case, the only time any data transmission was to take place was when something bad was found by the client. There was to be no server-side analysis of raw data. I'm sure that most people would agree that we do have a right to insure that our software license is being complied with.

In any case, I guess it's water under the bridge now. I'll blame Hollywood for all of the misunderstandings

Gee, a bit bitter, are we? Once again, for the slow readers in the audience, the problem was not with Verant "enforcing compliance of their software license", but in modifying said license so that compliance could be anything they said it was.

Yes, some folks posted alarmist and (in my belief) untrue reports of Verant scooping up IE cache data and sending it back to their servers. The problem isn't that Verant is doing this -- the problem is that, with the proposed general "we can do what we want" language proposed, they could, legally. Say, to check eBay cookies to look for people violating Verant's EULA in that fashion.

In any event, Verant came to their senses, and no doubt will actually try to modify their client-server protocol to not send everything blindly to the client instead of mauling the privacy rights of their users. Sorry, Gordon.

VERANT CHANGES THEIR MIND; EULA NOT TO BE MODIFIED
Wednesday, April 5, 2000 - 3:09 PM [Filed by lum]

John Smedley, Verant's President, sent this in:

Ok. We put the poll in, and with roughly 15,000 people participating the poll came up with 83% of the people being fine with us running the check for cheating.

DESPITE THIS POLL we have decided that it's the wrong thing to do. Enough people have convinced us that it's chipping away a little too much at people's privacy EVEN if they do consent for us to implement this policy.

Therefore, the change to the EULA will read as follows:

Solely for the purpose of patching and updating the Game, you hereby grant us permission to (i) upload Game file information from the Everquest directory and (ii) download Game files to you.

Now, before anyone wonders exactly what this is, let me explain. Technically speaking we probably should have had this language in there from day one for you to consent us to even download new game files to you in the first place. We apologize for not realizing that we should have gotten this consent, but live and learn.

We can admit when we make mistakes, and I believe this is a case where we owe an apology to our Playerbase. In our haste to try and thwart people from damaging the game we went overboard.

There will be absolutely no scanning of anyones computer for any reason other than the normal patching process (which won't do any sort of checking on what you have running).

Regards,

John Smedley
President and CEO
Verant Interactive, Inc.

VERANT TO BACKPEDAL?
Wednesday, April 5, 2000 - 4:00 AM [Filed by lum]

John Smedley, Verant's President, appeared in an IRC channel early this morning to try to justify the recent EULA changes announced yesterday. (The complete log is here).

I want to let everyone know we are reconsidering this decision. We're doing an in-game poll tomorrow.

We do NOT scan people's hard drives. We never have. All we do is check to see if they are running things like WinShowEQ.

I'm actually surprised about this to tell the truth.. UO has the same thing in their agreement. 5 (e) of their agreement.

Clearly I underestimated people's reaction to this.

Lets see what tomorrow brings. I'm reconsidering even putting it in at all.

Later, Brad McQuaid, Everquest's lead designer and Holder of the Vision, and Sony/SCEA's director of legal and business affairs, who drafted the EULA changes were in the same channel. I got the opportunity to fire off a few questions (here's the complete log of that session). Sony's lawyer, on as "SonyEsq", had this to say:

What protections do users have against Verant unilaterally declaring what is "appropriate" for monitoring?

The limitations are twofold. First, the information that is examined is strictly limited to that which is game related. For instance, no one would insist that Verant not examine the EQ directories during its patch routine. Because, if Verant did not peek, then it would have to patch the entire game every time it patches. So, it looks to see what needs updating, and updates.

Now, secondly, there is a reasonableness standard. The standard of reasonableness is not a subjective one, it is an objective one. That means, (this is gonna sound like a pile of legalese, but I didn't write 200 years of common law). That means that it is what a "reasonable person, acting with knowledge of professionals in the industry, would adjudge as fair."

So it is Verant's intention, in taking any of these steps, to only do what is necessary and reasonable to protect the integrity of its game. Certainly, no more.

The poll question is already on the EQ Gameplay board. It reads as follows:

Are you comfortable with the EverQuest client checking to see if you are running programs that are capable of cheating in EverQuest and reporting that back to our servers?

Um, sorry, that's not the issue. Everyone agrees that Verant has a right to defend the integrity of their game against third-party programs; everyone agrees that a more tightly-worded EULA would cause far less of a firestorm. The problem is that, at first glance, Verant is giving themselves the right to determine what is appropriate to examine on your PC. That is the key problem here, and a question that is phrased to elicit a "ooh, cheaters bad" response isn't quite kosher. (To Smedley's credit, in the thread he did promise to continue alternate wording.)

Also, Smedley noted that UO's EULA had the same sort of we-can-snoop-whatever-we-want clause. The clause he refers to is:

(e) Privacy. You understand that you have no expectation of privacy regarding the communications you make on the Service, and that all communications made by or received from you may be monitored by Origin Systems representatives. You hereby consent to the extraction of hardware system profile data and any data related to operation of the Software through the Service from any computer that logs on to the Service using your Account.

Not quite the same thing. And, as any UOE user over the past two years can tell you, OSI hasn't done any process checking in their client.

But then again, when you're caught with your hands in the cookie jar, the first impulse is usually to point at someone else.
