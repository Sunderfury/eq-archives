Preview/Interview: Shadowbane
Developer: Wolfpack Studios
Publisher: Gathering of Developers

Eagerly awaited by a rabid fan "underground", Shadowbane made its public debut at e3 this week. We took a look at a demonstration, and liked what we saw.

Unlike other games that try to get as many customers - and satisfy as many playstyles - as possible, Shadowbane is focused on one thing - player conflict. Players who have cut their teeth on Ultima Online and other "PK" worlds may be in for a rude shock, though, when they discover exactly what the developers, Wolfpack Studios, mean by player conflict.

Basically, Shadowbane models the seizure of territory. Players can take and build up a town (the mechanics of which seem to be still under wraps, but involve "choking off the town's resources"), which then becomes their home base, their point of recall/respawn, etc. Wolfpack believes that since players have a geographic point of origin that can be easily traced, if someone decides to kill someone else, their victim knows where to find them. And, presumably, bring along some friends.

It gets better. Cities combine to form nations (the upkeep needs are greater, requiring ever larger guilds to hold them) and those nations can form alliances - and break them when circumstances warrant. ("Hey! Why should I pay you taxes, when I can keep all this money for myself!") Concievably, a nation can even seize control of an entire world - a feat which Todd "Warden" Coleman speculated might even be rewarded by Wolfpack with a coronation ceremony!

All this is surrounded by an ongoing plot, maintained by on-site "feature characters" that move along the plot by taking the part of various deities, heroes and rulers. They set the stage for possible conflict by, say, asking three guilds to obtain a scepter from a temple, while a fourth guild may be called upon to protect it.

One question that often comes up when discussing a PvP-intensive game like Shadowbane - well, Shadowbane is the only one in development, actually - is the problem of grief PKers, or as Warden terms them, "roving bands of thugs". It is possible to be in a roving band of thugs, but the game won't reward you for it. True power in Shadowbane comes from holding on to territory - and territory implies responsibility, in the most feudal sense. And since true power comes from this, the grief PKers - who are also usually powergamers to the nth order - will pick up on this, and Play Nice. Without a Play Nice Policy, at that. It'll be interesting to see how this works out, and is certainly a unique concept.

Shadowbane is a gutsy game in a number of ways. The game rewards nation building, diplomacy, and cooperation, something that PvPers in online games are not universally known for. And the game promises to be heavily influenced by its plotline, something which could cause its customers to actually utter the dreaded "RP" word. The engine itself seems fairly far along in development; we weren't able to see an actual running copy, but an "in-flight film" of a session, a feature of Shadowbane that allows you to save movies of your greatest victories in a small file format and play them within the engine. Although surely some enterprising hacker will discover the format and "rewrite" history, it does give new meaning to the kill shot.

Does Shadowbane itself have a kill shot in its future? We'll find out soon enough. The question really is, are the players ready for it?

Like a web browser, as you run around the world, things get streamed down to you, on the fly. L: Are you using a zone-based architecture?

JH: No, it’s actually a contiguous world.

TC: Underneath the covers, though, we have a grouping object we call a zone, just for repop purposes. For example, “the orc fort” needs to reload all its monsters and items.

JH: Yes. This is a computer generated world. It’s a multi-fractal system, actually, we give a random seed value to the client, none of this comes from the server. The server can say, “This is the Snow World, or the Ice World, and it has this tileset.” It can completely generate the entire world, as you run around.

James Nance: So there’s no download time on new areas.

JH: That’s right.

L: Because it uses the same seed.

TC: Exactly. It uses the same seed, so instead of downloading an entire new world, you get the seed value – a couple of bytes – and all the god-power. The keeps, the people, and all that kind of stuff. But basically, the world is already set. All the human adds are on top of that.

LoH: How does the skill system work?

TC: Let me back up before I hit skills, because there’s a base character system , character creation, things like that, that it makes more sense. You start with character creation. It’s a point based character creation system, very similar to GURPS, or Champions, or Hero, or something like that. Obviously different stats and those kind of things, but very similar in nature. So I start out by choosing a class. I choose a base class, and there’s four basics. Fighter, Healer, Mage and Rogue. From there, once I get in game, I can go around and learn the ropes a little bit, I can go to newbie areas and be protected.

L: Like newbie school in a MUD.

TC: Yeah, pretty much. So what happens at that point, is right about the time I hit fifth level-ish, that’s when I start looking for an organization to join. There’s a number of specialization classes, I think there’s like 22 of them, and those specialization classes, you can join them depending on your pre-reqs. For example, to be a ranger, I could start as either a fighter or as a rogue. Both will allow me to get into the ranger’s organization, once I join a rangers-type organization, I can become a ranger. So that’s my specialization. Now I’m a ranger for the rest of my levels. At various points in the game, I can take a sub class, if I choose to do so, called a discipline. I don’t have to. If I do, it’s giving me new powers. Like, alchemy is a discipline, blacksmithing is a discipline, that’s the closest we get to trade skills. You have other disciplines, like blade mastery is a discipline. There are something like 40 disciplines. I can give you the exact numbers later, it’s hard for me to remember the exact numbers off the top of my head. That allows me to make a ranger-blademaster-beastmaster…

L: What kind of feedback do you have during this process? I assume you’re using experience points for advancement.


TC: Yes.

L: Can you see how many experience points you have, how many you need to get to the next level…

TC: Instead of gaining towards experience, every level is 10,000 experience marching down to zero. So what happens is, if I kill a raven at level 1, it may give me 25 experience. Now at level 2, if I go kill that raven, I’m only going to get 15. Eventually I get zero for it. So it’s not going to be worth my time.

L: But you have feedback for all this.

TC: Yes. It’ll say “Experience to next level: 8,732.”

L: Do you have the same kind of feedback for skill gain?

TC: Yes. It’ll say, “To get your skill to the next level, you put in 3 points”. There’s actually two different things that you get for taking either a class or a discipline. You get skills, on one side, and you get powers on the other side. So for an example, a fighter might get a lot of skills, whereas a mage would end up getting a lot of powers. And within powers you’ll have a whole bunch of types as well -  you’ll have faith-based powers, lore-based powers and special maneuvers. So a blademaster may have a whirlwind attack that’s a special maneuver power. And he kicks it off, and it does some special effect.

L: Do these come automatically, or through some quest in the game?

TC: You get them based on level, on other things that you already have. For example you might have Air Storm, and you may have to have Whirlwind at rank 3 to be able to learn it already, and you get it based on your class and discipline.  So I get Blade Mastery, and that opens up 7 or 15 new powers that become available to me through the course of gameplay.

L: But everyone has access to these, it’s not like something, say, like one guild has access to this one part of the world so they can monopolize this one special power.

TC: No, that won’t happen. It’ll be world to world. We’ll have certain worlds later down the line, we hope to add additional worlds with different races, things like that, as an expansion pack . But when the game goes release, everything will be available to everybody, based on the pre-reqs. So for example, if I want to get Elven Healing. I have to be an Elf. Or I want to get Blade Singing. I can’t get Blade Singing if I’m a Dwarf. So there are pre-reqs built into it. But it’s available to any player, provided they have the right pre-reqs.

LoH: Since you’re level based, aren’t you afraid there’s going to be an “end” to the game?

TC: Well, the game is on two levels, so there’s a yes and a no. The yes is, yes the RPG, eventually you’ll level out. You’ll have the highest level, the best equipment.

L: What is the highest level?

TC: That’s interesting. Everything is a little bit different with us. There’s no max max level, what there is is a soft cap. You hit a point at about 50th or 60th level, where it becomes exponentially harder to gain a level, and you gain so little for it it’s not worth it any longer.

JH: We’ll have lifetime players with levels and people will go “oh my god, I never thought anyone could be that level.”

TC: That’s the basic game. Keep in mind we have another layer, the strategy game. And you can be first level, and still take part in the strategy game. I want to be a diplomat, say, if  I’m really good with people and get an army underneath me, and get them to take a city, I can be king of a city-state and still be first level. We’ve got a bunch of different organization types, and depending on your organization type, that determines, at least a little bit on a roleplaying level, the type of your nation. Some people will play that and actually enjoy that, some people won’t. You’ll see some people that take over a city, be a Templar’s Guild consisting only of Templars and we actually roleplay it. Some people won’t. But you know, it’s still, by putting it into the game and allowing it to have an influence on gameplay, we still expect it’ll add to the overall atmosphere. I guess that’s my longwinded answer to your question.

L: Brian had mentioned outside that players can build towns, and build buildings based on certain criteria…

TC: Yes, they can.

L: How does that work? How would a high level guild that wants to own a particular patch of real estate go about building a town?

TC: You don’t build a town so much as you take a town. So, initially, there’s going to be a land rush. People run out and find the town areas and take them. You’re going to have a lot of turmoil just in the beginning, until things start to settle out. Beyond that, I can either go and acquire towns through diplomacy – meaning if I have a town and you’re my neighbor, and I’ve got a really big army, I can go and say “hey, swear fealty to me, and we’ll never attack you.”

L: So these towns are pre-placed?

JH: Right now the towns are preplaced, fully built. There is a guildstone that is preplaced, and those are centers of power. You can claim your stone if it hasn’t been claimed and build your town up around that. But there has been some internal questioning as to whether it’d be really cool if people could place the guildstones and spawn a town in a particular area. Our initial concern is you have these big clusters of towns, and we really want people to spread out.

L: How would an opposing guild take over a town?

TC: I’ll give you some of the information, but we haven’t released all of the guild stuff. Basically the process involves choking off the resources to the city, over a period of 24 to 78 hours. That makes the burden of work on the attacker, by far. We want that on purpose. If you own a city, it should be very hard for someone to displace you and kick you out.

L: How would you choke off resources in an engine like this?

TC: That’s the one thing I can’t talk about. There’s a really cool way in which you actually have to lay siege. And the defenders, their burden of work is to go out and break your siege, and send you packing back home. So it’ll be a really interesting phenomenon, seeing people besiege towns. I think you’ll end up having in the game – this is our suspicion, but we don’t know – we’ll end up having certain areas that have longstanding guilds, that because of the way alliances form and because of the way they are set out, they are basically stable, and they won’t change for years, conceivably. You’ll have other areas that are more like the badlands, and they will be toppled almost constantly.

L: Do you have some areas that are more valuable then others? More resources, for example.

TC: Not right now. Typically, not in game terms.

L: There’s no advantage to having one guildstone over another, then.

TC: The only ones are the ones that come naturally out of the game based on terrain. If I have one that was, say, on an island, because it was randomly generated, as part of the world generation, that’s pretty cool, and it might have some intrinsic value. It’s better than other places. But it’s nothing like, this one has a 17 rank,  and this one has a 42 rank, therefore I’d rather have one other the other.

L: Can guilds control more than one town?

TC: Yes, they can. Nations can grow, not just through warfare, but also through diplomacy. If I go, and I convince you that it’s in your best interest to join the city with the three other city-states, the small hegemony that I’ve built, you can actually swear fealty to me and then you’re now a part of that overall nation.

L: Is that enforced in-game?

TC: Yes, it’s enforced in-game in terms of the hierarchy. If you come and swear fealty to me, then when someone else comes in our town, they’ll see the nation guildcrest and they’ll see our guildcrest. Our sub-guildcrest. You’ll still hold the town, you’ll have control of the resources, that allow you to build the buildings and things like that, there is an ingame mechanics.

JN: There’s also points depending on the size of the guild. A tiny tiny guild taking over a town won’t be able to maintain it very long. And at the same time it would take a huge guild  to hold multiple towns. So we try to keep people from becoming too powerful that way.

TC: The way it’s engineered is that, because we get this question all the time, is it possible for one guild to take over a world? It is, we don’t see it happening, but if it does, we’re cool with it! In fact, we’ve talked about having a coronation ceremony –

JH: Flying the guy down to Austin…

TC: …whoever the first King or Queen is of a single world. The reason is that…

(laughter)

TC: …having that much land is going to require more and more players. And if we hit a point where 90% or 80% of the players want to be under one king, it’s the will of the players. So we’re not going to stop it – great! That’s what they want to pay for, that’s what they want to do.

JH: Someone really charismatic…

JH: It’s gonna all come crumbling down!

L: Now, how easy it is using the diplomatic system, for one guild to stab another guild in the back?

JN: It’s reeeealy easy.

JH: Really, really, really easy.

TC: You totally know what we’re getting at here. Not only is it easy, you are enticed to do it. At any given moment, let’s say, you decide to build a nation. You take a city, then take another one, and I’m your lieutenant. You can’t hold both cities. You have to have someone else take over the second city, swearing fealty to you.

L: And you have to trust them.

TC: You have to trust them. At any given moment, I can turn on you like that. (snaps fingers)

JN: Why would I keep paying taxes to Todd, when I could just keep them for myself?

TC: Exactly. So you are very much enticed, at any given moment. And it was done on purpose. Both human nature and our systems make it to where the higher you get in terms of nation building, it’s like a deck of cards. Every additional card you put on is a little bit more strain, and could cause the whole thing to fall down.

L: Now what reason is there for a guild to take over a town, and hold it, and do nation-building, as opposed to just being a blight on the landscape, running around and killing everything they see?

TC: A lot of the ingame, in fact, most of the power gained in game, requires you to be a part of a city. At a minimum, you have to join a GM-run guild which is in the safe haven area, but the safe haven areas only let you get up to about half level. You want to do that, you’re certainly welcome to. But if you want to be a hero in the world, you’re eventually going to have to take a little bit of risk and venture out into the dangerous areas.

LoH: So there are safe areas.

TC: There are safe areas. There are cities that can’t go to war. They are GM run cities. So they basically are preset at about mid-level, and mid-level training facilities, mid-level shops.

JH: We have a good vision for the game we’re trying to build, but we want to give people a chance to go into a GM-run town, and enjoy the game, because we think it’s going to be really rich.

LoH: The more complicated a game is, the more frustrating it is to just get whacked, you don’t even know how to walk around, and here’s this idiot attacking you.

TC: There’s also another thing, which is penalty of death. If you take a game like Duke Nuke’Em, something like that, deathmatch. You don’t care if you die, it doesn’t matter. You just run back into the fray. Obviously that’s one end of the spectrum and some of the other massively multiplayer games have gone to the other end of the spectrum. If you die, it hurts you pretty badly. It puts you back a week’s worth of game play. We decided we’d skew more towards this side of the spectrum. What we don’t want is people running into the fray, and dying, and running right back into the fray. We don’t want a shoot-em-up. But we don’t want the penalty to be so severe that if someone does kills you and it had nothing to do with you, you just happened to be in the wrong place at the wrong time, it shouldn’t be that severe. The penalty also scales based on the challenge it was for that player. If I’m 10th level, and James is 70th level, and he kills me, it’s basically a free teleport back to town. It doesn’t hurt me at all, because I could never possibly beat him.

JN: And I get nothing for it.

TC: And he gets nothing for it, moreover. I keep all my stuff, I keep all my experience, he’s gained pretty much nothing.

JH: We’re also thinking the guild system will add a layer of accountability to the entire system. If somebody kills me, and I see their guild crest, which I will see before I die, I know that the Band of the Hand was the group that killed me. And I’m going to tell the rest of my guildmates, the Band of the Hand has been picking on us. Killing us. We can form a treaty or do whatever needs to be done to make sure that doesn’t happen.

TC: The big problem I have with the, I call them “roving bands of thugs”. The roving bands of thugs syndrome that you see in some of the other games, is because there’s absolutely no accountability. If I kill you, I don’t have to worry about you seeing me ever again, you don’t know my name, you can’t find me, I can go to another city, I can teleport across the world without even thinking about it, it’s no big deal. In ours, they know where to find you, because you can only recall back to one spot, the middle of your town. That’s where you train, that’s where you heal, that’s where you buy and sell your equipment, everything revolves around that town. You want to form a roving band of thugs, you can do it, but you won’t be very effective in the game. The game system doesn’t make it a very effective way to play. And that’s the way I think you deal with problem players, the playerkillers and things like that, if you want to entice them, don’t tell them “don’t do this or we’ll ban you”,  don’t tell them “don’t do this because you signed a code of conduct”, you tell them “if you want to do that, you can, you just won’t be very powerful in the game.” Because that’s the one thing they listen to – what’s going to make me powerful in this game. So that was our design philosophy on everything we did.

LoH: Never underestimate a snert.

JH: Oh, the years we ran MUDs, we knew you just can’t underestimate them. It’s amazing.

LoH: They will go to any length to ruin your day.

TC: Yes. Absolutely.

LoH: That’s their goal – to ruin your day. It’s not for power or goodies, but to piss someone else off.

TC: But if the penalty is so severe, that my doing it will actually hurt my character overall, they probably won’t.

JH: If you have refuge in your guild, if you’re a member of your guild, and they want to come in, they can’t, especially if the guards start attacking them every time. They just won’t be able to get to you unless they go through your friends.

TC: And if you’re in a GM held town they can’t get to you at all.

JN: The neat thing about accountability with the guildcrests is that it gives you multiple avenues to resolve a problem.

LoH: So what keeps someone from just resigning from a guild? Changing and going to another?

TC: They will never level again, they can’t get their items repaired, they have nowhere to hide.

L: Well, they could join another guild, couldn’t they?

TC: They could join another guild. And now that guild is going to have to deal with that person.

JN: But the thing is, since the guild leaders have invested the time to build up their guild, they may not want those people in their organization. It’s not just a matter of “now I know who’s butt to go kick, and I can get my friends to do that”, but I can go through channels. My guild leader can talk to his guild leader. Tell him “you guys don’t’ want a guild war. It’s bad for business.

It’s a very feudal system. It’s basically in many ways mirroring feudal society.

L: How is the plot in Shadowbane handled? Do you have an overarching storyline?

TC: Yes. We also have feature characters. We hire roleplayers who are actors to basically play roles in the game. Range anywhere from if I have a GM run city, I might have a noble to run it, hire someone to play and basically cause political strife and intrigue in that city, all the way to demigods.  So someone would show up like Ares shows up on Hercules.

L: Do you have a pre-written storyline already that you’re planning on running with?

TC: We have the basic outline of what the story’s going to be, but one of our goals is between now and release to set up what we call the storyboard. It’s going to be a group of individuals, kind of like a writing staff of a TV show, who basically build scripts and pass them out to their actors and say, here’s what’s going to happen over the next month. It’s a little bit more open then that, because the players are going to dictate it.

L: So players could be part of the storyline.

TC: Absolutely. That’s the goal. But we certainly can’t provide enough feature characters that every player will have their own individual mentor hired by our company. So instead what we are doing is engineering it that our feature characters work at the guild level, and it has a trickle-down effect. So let’s say I’m a god of thunder, and I show up at a city that built a temple to the god of thunder, and I say there’s an artifact lost in the Northlands. You must find it and bring it to me. At the same time there’s the god of deception and the god of wind saying the same thing…

JN: Or you need to block someone from taking this artifact.

TC: Now I’ve got a quest. Obviously they can’t all succeed, but it’ll be a lot of fun seeing how it turns out.
