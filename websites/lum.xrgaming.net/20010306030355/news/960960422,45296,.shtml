HEY, LET’S FORM A COMMUNITY. WHO SHOULD WE HATE?
Tuesday, June 13, 2000 - 10:27 PM [Filed by hedron]

Well, I haven’t been stuck in any airports lately, but I haven’t let that stop me. Swell, right?

Anyway, consider the following quote:

“Someday we WILL be able to hand over the reins of policing to players. It will be seen as just a meta-game for those who are interested in it (and what's more, I bet the cops will be the same people we're currently turning into grief players with our limited mechanics). But right now, neither players nor developers are ready. I can't tell you how much I wish that in UO we had found a way to make the players able to do this, actually able to win against the bad guys--because I do regard those grief players and those rampant PKers (be they the "good" ones or the griefy ones) as the bad guys in this virtual world, far more so than the monsters. One of my biggest disappointments in UO is that we never found a way to have the good guys win.” – Raph


So let me just start by saying that I derive a certain amount of joy from the fact that in UO Raph “never found a way to have the good guys win.”

I consider it poetic justice.

UO was developed as a game that had somewhere near its roots this idea of “diversity”. PVPers and PKers were allowed into the world under the banner that the diversity was what would bring life and excitement to the game play. That was part of the general idea, anyway.

But Raph has pretty much confirmed here what every PVPer and PKer in the game already knew. There was from almost day one an undeclared war on the “diverse” PVP and PK segments of the audience.

Fine. But there’s something here that I find slimy. Pay attention to the social dynamics of what was really going on. First, player hunters are empowered in the game to follow their play style. Then they’re classified as the “enemy” of society. In fact, the game mechanics are set up in such a way as to forcibly classify player hunters as not only the foe, but as a truly damaging foe – they can loot, and loot hard. Not only that, but it’s hands-down the most profitable way to exist in the game.

Then, having created the player hunter menace, UO proceeds to try and control this new animal. Why? And how? Well, the why is easy. Raph tells us.

“Simply put, I think that the things that drive community are: shared interest to get everyone in the same place; limited resources that you need to cooperate over so everyone gets enough; and an enemy you have to fight to keep out.”-Raph


So what were the player hunters? They were the enemy, the terrible soulless scourge that was to forge a community by bathing it in fire. But not too much fire. Not too much opposition. Not too big of an enemy.

What Raph wanted was a controlled tool. A segment of society that he could set up to be the “victims” so-to-speak of a social experiment whose out come was SUPPOSEDLY already pre-determined. Here was the script: anarchy rules, then all good and decent folk react, communities form as civilized people band together and fight off the menace of immoral player hunters, over time player hunters realize the evil of their ways and of the futility of fighting against the valiant spirit of cooperative humanity. Oh how gushingly sweet. Happiness rules, lasting friendships form, and everyone settles down to a nice cozy knitting session at their hearths while sharing a warm cup of tea among their warmly regarded companions.

Except the player hunters. They’re either dead, powerless, quit or were forcibly converted.

But things didn’t quite work out as planned. Instead, the player hunters basically beat on everyone mercilessly. It was at this point that something HAD to be done. The war machine went into full tilt. A new alliance was formed: 90% of the player base, plus the designers, plus the game code. This coalition would muster its troops and smash the player hunter menace into the tame little community building tool that it was supposed to be.

BAM! Visible marking of the “enemy”, notoriety, banishment from towns, constant nerfing of powerful player killing tactics, stat loss, bounty systems, blah blah blah. Whoooo!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!! GIT ‘EM!!!!!! SICK THE CROTCH CRICKETS ON ‘EM!!

(slow smile) To repeat:

“One of my biggest disappointments in UO is that we never found a way to have the good guys win.” – Raph


A moment of silence for the effort? Sure. Why not. (pausing a few beats in a solemn hush)

Why did it fail? What happened? How could the mighty forces arrayed against the player hunters fail to achieve victory? We could speculate on a thousand reasons. Accountability, instant travel, better and smarter players preferring the challenge of player hunting - whatever your favorite explanation is, you can have it.

I’ll say it again. Poetic justice.

Think about what was actually being done from a social perspective. One portion of the gaming community was being hurled down on the Tarpeian rocks of the game mechanics for the moral edification of another part.

Of course, you could argue that the player hunters deserved whatever they got due to their antagonistic behavior. But I would argue that the point of a game like this is to conflict just as much as it is to cooperate. I’d argue that it was only bad decision making about death penalties that made player hunters such a rabid issue instead of the generally welcome added thrill that it should have been. I’d argue that it was the decision to try and set up the conflict as “All of Society” vs. “The Aggressive Player Hunters” that screwed things up, instead of setting it up as Community A vs. Community B, each with their own band of merry ultra-efficient player hunters.

The game took players who signed up thinking that rising up and smashing down their enemies (or at least trying) would be some damn good fun, and demonized them. Not that all of us minded being demonized. But the mere desire to attack and defeat other players in A GAME is not inherently evil. Part of the fun of a game is that you have a harmless forum in which to conflict. It was the choice of the designers to give the act of player killing the sting that converted it from harmless conflict to “damn that pissed me off”.

Although there was more to it all than simply teaching players that X action is good and approved, and Y action is evil and penalized. There was the purpose of forming the populace into a community. But it needed to be more than a community, it needed to be a PLANNED community, one where spoon-fed morals are enforced. Sort of Brave New World-ish, isn’t it? Raph Huxley? What do ya think?

Or maybe there’s a better parallel. (Trying to think of an historical example where the law makers/government singled out a distinct group of people and used their presence as a menace to encourage the unification of the majority of the population under a new order, eventually even visibly marking that small group for easier identification.)

Anyway, it’s an interesting irony to have tried to teach ethical behavior through the device of setting up a group of people as an expendable teaching resource. And then to have that expendable resource become a sort of anti-lesson? Now that’s rich.

Damn. I just can’t think of that better parallel. Can you?

Hedron
