MIXED NUTS, Part II
Wednesday, January 19, 2000 - 8:06 AM [Filed by myschyf]

After my last update many of you wrote in (or posted) to say either that you thought incentives were a good idea or that the very idea of OSI offering incentives made you ill. Incentives, as many proposed them, could be anything from amnesty for past wrongdoings to a novelty item without substance. However I have a third option for you. What if incentives could be something far less tangible? What if offering incentives was as simple (and complex) as establishing a good relationship with the player base?

Lets talk about Twister for a sec. Twister posted bugs but he actually found very few of them by himself. Yet people sent him bugs all the time. They knew these bugs would be posted openly. They probably realized that OSI themselves would read the site for information about bugs. So since they would be posted openly and since they knew OSI would read about them there, why not send them to OSI? In other words, what made Twister so special to them and OSI not so special? He created a relationship with the portion of the player base that is adept at finding and testing bugs. OSI has failed to do this. As a matter of fact, they've done the opposite. They have shunned these people.

But they've even done worse than that. IMHO they've failed to create a relationship with the majority of their player base. Oh sure those of us who post on boards and run events have some sort of relationship with some of the game staff, whether it be Counselors, GMs, Cal, or whomever. But we are a very small minority of the actual player base. I doubt we comprise even a tenth. What about the rest of the people?

About a month ago I started seeing posts for Counselor of the Month contests on the European shards. These were publicized on COB and on the Vault. My first thought on seeing these was "OMG how unbelievably hokey." Then after I considered it a bit longer I thought "You know, this is kind of neat." As I continued thinking about it I thought that I'd never seen anything like this done on Great Lakes, Atlantic, or SP (the other shards I play on) nor would I be able to make an informed vote if there was such a contest. I don't have the foggiest idea which counselors or GMs are assigned to my shards. Do you? I'll bet most people don't and that's a damn shame. How much effort would it take to put up a page on the UO website that listed the counselors and GMs assigned to each shard? If it had the hours they'd be working that would be even greater.

Lets take this even further. When was the last time you saw a GM or a counselor in the game just walking around, seeing how people were doing and stopping to answer a question if they could? So you say they'd be mobbed? Why? Because they only show up once in a blue moon and when they do show up they are standing outside the west Brit bank? Well no shit, Sherlock. This might come as a huge surprise but players do frequent areas other than Britain and banks in general. Walk the road from Minoc to Trinsic sometime. You'll see lots of people. Never a counselor though. Or a GM. These people only come when you have a problem. Welp folks, I think this is a bad way to build a relationship with your customers. I'm not talking about sitting around in the Counselor's Guild either. That's stupid. I'm talking about getting out there and meeting and talking to people. But while we are talking about the Counselor's Guild -- wouldn't it be nice if there was always a counselor on duty in the guild to answer a question? Wouldn't it be nice to know that if you had a quick question you could just bop over to the Counselor's Guild and you could COUNT on a counselor actually being there? God forbid a representative of OSI should really be in that empty building awaiting the chance to help a customer.

I'll take this even further. Counselors and GMs should know who the veteran players on their shards are. If you live in a neighborhood for 2.5 years you get to know the owners of the corner store. And they get to know you. Because you've been a regular customer for 2.5 years. They let you slide if you are a dollar short because they know you'll be back tomorrow or the next day. They greet you by name when you walk in the door. They know what brand of cigarettes you smoke, if you will buy beer or diet coke or whatever. They know you. You have been a long-time customer and you are treated accordingly. With OSI you are a number. Gee thanks folks, don't do me any favors.

Once upon a time I played a game called Legends of Kesmai (LOK). The beta ran for almost a year and had been in production for about a year when it was announced that the game would run on AOL as a separate game. So of course we all ran over to AOL to make new accounts and new characters. Don't ask me why -- it seemed like a good idea at the time. Anyway when we got to AOL I discovered that some little twerp had gone and taken MY name. Myschyf is a game name I've been using for 7 or 8 years now (maybe 5 years at the time) and all names had to be unique in LOK. To say I went ballistic would not give you the true depth and feeling of the wrath I unleashed on the LOK boards. I had a good idea of who had done it and I threatened this person with everything from personal dismemberment to continual harassment. So I was out hunting one day in the game trying to build enough skill on my character to eventually wipe out whoever had done this heinous misdeed when who should appear beside me at that moment but the player-liaison person for LOK. She had seen me enter the game and wanted to talk to me about the person who had 'stolen' my name and what KesCo could do to help me. She knew I was pissed, knew why, empathized and asked me not to do anything too horribly stupid until she could check into it. Now THAT'S customer service! They didn't solve it. They couldn't. There was absolutely nothing that KesCo could do about it and she knew it and I knew it. But the simple fact that they cared enough to come talk to me about it helped more than words can tell you. THIS is the level of customer service and player relationships OSI should aspire to.

When someone tries to contribute to this game -- whether it be an idea for a quest, an idea to make the game better, or simply reporting a bug, OSI should at least send them a letter thanking them for their contribution and not some lame-assed form letter either. A real, honest-to-goodness letter written by someone who can take the time and trouble to at least make the player believe that OSI really does give a shit.

OK enough for today. This thing is too long already. As always, feel free to post your comments here. More tomorrow.
