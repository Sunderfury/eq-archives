UItima Online: Triumphant
Ultima Online: Renaissance review
Developer: Origin Systems
Publisher: Electronic Arts
reviewed by Lum the Mad

Finally, Ultima Online is done.

I'm certain we'll see new features and whatnot in the future (there's a whole team of people who need to remain gainfully employed, after all), but after three years of trying almost everything and anything to "fix" UO's release... it's fixed. UO is a finished product. You can play now.


Everyone was young once.

So what caused this epiphany? Some very basic, very simple design decisions that should have been made long ago, that go a long way towards repairing the damage that three years of misguided efforts and outright neglect have caused.

First off, there's a PK switch now. Oh, it's called something different, and is probably the most controversial part of UO:R among its current playerbase, but the simple fact is that if you so choose, you will never be killed by another player. Ever. It's a choice Everquest and Asheron's Call had no problem giving their users; and it's a choice that the developers of Ultima Online have been drug to, kicking and screaming the entire way. But it's there now, and it works.

Don't like PK switches? Neither do I. The key word here is "choice". Every server in UO has been exactly doubled in size, with two basically identical worlds ("Felucca" and "Trammel" in the game fiction, "Teenage Wasteland" and "Carebear Land" for short). One world has a prohibition against attacking other players, one world doesn't. The new PK- land is also ready and waiting for colonization - the target of every player being able to own virtual property is feasible now. Switching between the two isn't trivial, but is possible. It is possible, in fact recommended, to train a new character in the PK- side of the world and then, once at an appropriate level of skll, graduate to the PK+ side.

That skill advancement, by the way, has been significantly redone. In pursuit of two objectives - making skill gain accessible to the casual player and removing the incentive to "macro" your character while not actually playing - building a character in Ultima Online is so much less tedious now it's enough to make a veteran of earlier UO systems weep. Every player has a "burst hour", the first hour of every day they play, where skill gain is greatly accelerated. Characters can still gain skills afterwards, but at normal, slower rates. Combined with the decoupling of skill gain difficulty from the number of people using a skill (a truly bizarre design that assumed that, given many blacksmiths in the world, blacksmithy would become much more difficult for some reason) this gives players the freedom to quickly assume their desired role in the world. Many UO veterans complain that this somehow cheapens their accomplishment - "It took me six months of painful macroing, monotonous clicking, and carpal-tunnel syndrome to become a grandmaster smith and it only took this newbie two weeks of enjoying himself!" - but the simple fact of the matter is that UO as a game really only matures when its characters do. There is nothing inherently fun about being a weak pushover. Massive kudos to the UO dev team for recognizing that UO is in fact a multiplayer game and the fun of the game is in interacting with those players, not just in building a character. Another welcome, welcome feature introduces "skill locks", or the ability to designate which skills to gain or lose skill in. A fiction-breaking device? Sure. But in a game where most of the player skill involves choosing from a limited skill point pool to develop skill in, absolutely crucial.


I often find men in glowng bear hats by my smithy.

Much work has also been done in making the game accessible to new characters. On the PK- side, the city of Occlo has been repurposed into the newbie-friendly city of Haven. After going through an exhaustive tutorial explaining how to perform such basic functions as opening doors, eating bananas and putting on a hat, new players are sent to Haven, where they are automatically paired with a Companion, a volunteer that guides the new player through the UO game system. (There are also supposedly quests for new players that help teach them how to do various trade skills and combat tasks, but they weren't in place or were broken during my visit.) After up to a month of nest-egg building, they then can be sent via gate to the other cities of the PK- lands (and presumably the PK+ world as well, eventually.) Although the UO volunteer program has been liberally laced with problems and scandal, the Companion system works well, and new players should have a smooth transition into veteran status.

Once a player reaches that veteran status, most of them will probably want to beat up other players at some point. On the Player vs Player side, the combat system has undergone some radical revisions as well. It's too early to judge their impact (although, as always, the unofficial Dev Board is full of thousands of folks who disagree that it's too early) the intent is obviously to break the Tank Mage mold of character design. Players can be nimble fencers that stun opponents, or physically weak mages, or, if they are so inclined, the Tank Mage archetype still works as well. UO Powergamers is doing the yeoman work of actually determining what works and what doesn't, and those looking at PvP would be well served to visit. Call it Stratics For A New Generation.

Another new feature of UO:R is an upgrade of the monster AI, from "Help, I'm trapped behind these little twigs" to "I'm gonna kick your ass and feed it to your momma" status. Monsters in UO aren't ridiculous pushovers any more, but now will actually *gasp* cooperate and cure each other and basically be somewhat more of a challenge. Although there are some "cheese areas" with easily harvestable loot, the wilderness of UO is crawling with things waiting to target your little icon. Veterans of UO may remember that one reason spawn used to be rare was that it would pop up in places like mountain tops, oceans, and trapped within player houses. All of these have been fixed; now if you want to find monsters you don't have far to look any more. Monster bashing in UO is still an inferior experience to games like Everquest and Asheron's Call which basically dedicate themselves to the monster-bashing "gestalt", but it's there if you want it and most seem to.

UO also has very basic grouping features now. Group members have a display that shows each member's health and mana, and have buttons by each one that will automatically heal and cure them. Functions such as adding group members and especially communicating within a group seem unnecessarily arcane, however, and the whole system feels somewhat tacked-on. It could use more refinement.

Many UO veterans may also remember horror stories about UO's technical support staff ("GMs", counselors, and others). One of UO:R's stated objectives was in improving UO's support system, and it has noticeably improved in quality if not quantity. Over the past six months Origin has gone to great lengths to clean up UO, in some cases booting entire guilds over system exploits and harrassment complaints, and just recently forcing people with wildly offensive names to simply delete the offending character. The system is draconian (the person in charge of it probably chose the handle "Tyrant" for a reason) and many complain that innocent people have been victimized by wide-ranging dragnets, but in the end it works. UO is a noticeably more pleasant place to visit than it was. Rumors and whispers of GM abuse have, although not disappeared entirely, decreased from a thundering roar of years past. Although recent cutbacks by Origin seem to have reduced the number of paid staff available (GMs now work all shards from a list instead of being assigned to a particular shard, for example), thanks in large part to a huge corps of volunteers calls for help are answered quickly and usually professionally. Another in a long list flaws of UO finally being repaired.

Given all of the above, what sort of game is UO? How do these improvements change the game for the average player?

UO is best looked at as a virtual world. It goes the farthest of any MMORPG currently in putting the mechanics of the game in the hands of its players - almost anything in UO is player-craftable, and the economy of UO is one of its strongest creatures. Players can, and often do, play UO without ever lifting a sword arm against another entity. It is no accident that UO is also the game with the most actual role-playing going on. So much so, in fact, that "roleplayers" are an actual political faction within UO: The Metagame that I so enjoy writing about. UO is such a mature, self-contained society that pressure groups within it already exist, organically grown. That more than anything else is testament to its success. It's weird, but it's true - players care about it enough to bicker endlessly about it, both in and out of game. And whereas most bickering in games like Everquest involve bickering with developers over how class such-and-such is broken, players in UO are bickering with each other. It really is a virtual world. Believe the hype.

So far this review of UO:R has been glowing, but there are some clouds on the horizon. Origin has played fast and loose with the truth on occasion; the most glaring example was releasing the UO:R boxes to stores weeks before the expansion was actually finished. Yes, refunds were offered to offended buyers, and yes, the expansion itself was a free download to existing players, but still, this would be absolutely reprehensible conduct from any company. You don't send out an empty box with the words "game to be patched in later", but in a package where the main selling point was in the above-detailed new features to UO, that's exactly what Origin did. Other examples of "broken promises" include revamped magic and alchemy subsystems that have been promised to the playerbase for years and are now a consistent joke, and the somewhat newer "it's not a mirror" joke where a Dev Team member posted on a message board that the "new lands" of UO:R would not be an exact duplicate of the old ones, when in fact they functionally are. Communication between players and developers is good, but only when those developers can be trusted to actually tell the truth. Origin has tried hardest of any gaming company in recent memory to be open and forthright with its users, which makes these lapses in judgement all the more painful. A large percentage of UO users simply don't trust Origin. That percentage has dropped, but it's still there.

UO's development has also been marred by haphazard quality assurance, which has been the bugaboo of every Origin product for the past ten years. UO:R was no exception; the introduction of UO:R was a complete disaster, and even when deployed to production shards game-crippling bugs such as randomly disappearing items in the inventory of players caused server warps that would basically invalidate anything a player did that day. With every such disastrous major patch promises are made from Origin that lessons have been learned and that future patches will not be such a fearsome occurance, and those promises are never kept. It's time for Origin to learn how to bug test, or perhaps hire enough people to actually debug their code. Players simply should not have to deal with wondering what bugs have been introduced when the UO Autopatcher fires off.

Still, the above two paragraphs shouldn't scare new users. (Make them wary, maybe.) Ultima Online, for its dated graphics and occasionally psychotic userbase, is still the most advanced persistent world out there. Everquest may have superior graphics and more to offer the virtual explorer, and Asheron's Call may have the better game system and more responsive dev team, but Ultima Online alone has nailed the many economic and physical interactions that make up an immersive, addictive world. And as I said, with this expansion, Ultima Online is finally done. What follows isn't the efforts to fix a broken product, but to improve a good one.

And I think I speak for every long-suffering, but still loyal UO user, when I say that it's about damned time.
