07/16/99 7:00 AM OSI UNDER SIEGE

According to Sunsword on COB's dev board, Siege Perilous will be up at 3:00P Central today. According to Lum, it will then immediately crash from 3,000 people logging in at once.

Here again, from UOHoC, the various owo.com postings and what I've been able to ferret out from constant nagging and whining, is what you can expect on this new l33t shard --

  • Are you a PK? Well, the good news - no stat loss for murderers, ever. Also, you can be finally be a Dread Lord red instead of "The Murderer" as that is going away. The bad news - well, with one character slot per player you had better either be one hard core PK or have friends. Actually with the other changes, solo PKs are going to be almost impossible to play. Other players, too, but it's going to be a while before anyone FINDS the Res spell, much less has it ready for the casting. The only town you can go to as a red, as before, is Buc's Den, and the gate from Vesper has been closed. Better save for a boat. Speaking of towns, magic works in towns now, so don't bother making those gimped up townfighters.

Better find a new shard -- provocation has been gimped and is now difficulty based, along with the previous barding gimps. I don't think we'll see too many bards on SP. Blade Spirit and Energy Vortex have also been gimped. Oh, and you can't cast Gate in dungeons either, or mark there. Hmm. Dungeons are going to be fun to hunt in now. Speaking as someone who likes to hunt the two legged creatures therein.

And NPCs won't buy a damn thing. So give up on that tailor mule. Oh, and give up on that fisherman mule too, since you won't be able to fish up treasure maps or SOS ("You'll find an SOS once a week"). No more seeing people yell "I WILL TAKE THEE" trailed by 50 escorts either -- only one escort per customer. NPCs don't carry money on them, so slaughtering them is useless (and it gives you a murder count).

  • Tailoring still has one use - leather armor. The skill requirement for making leather will be lowered, and players will be able to wear leather without any penalty to Meditation or Stealth. Plus, if you wear a leather cap, you will look very funky.

  • Lots of skills won't unhide you when you use them, such as Detect Hidden, Item Eval, Arms Lore, Snooping, Poisoning, Eval Int and Anatomy. You may begin macroing on my mark.... GO.

  • Thieves are somewhat less gimped on SP. Although you still have to be in the Thieves Guild to steal from players (and like everything else it costs 3X as much), you won't be booted from it for taking a murder count, and the chance of being revealed while stealthing/snooping has been significantly lowered. However random steal is gone... you have to snoop first. NPCs will attack anyone who steals from them outside of town.

    So far so good.

07/14/99 4:00 PM SIEGE TRIUMPHANT

Some more thoughts on the topic of the day.

EVERYONE I talk to is moving to Siege Perilous. People are talking about selling established characters on other shards, knowing that they are gonna be on SP full time. BEFORE THE SHARD IS EVEN UP. In fact, I know quite a few folks who are re-activating their accounts to start on this shard. Why is it so popular?

Simple - no twinks.

This shard is going to be hard as hell to get started on. Think about it folks - how are you going to get money? How are you going to go bash on your first orc? With that practice sword? I don't think so. So go buy a sword from the NPC vendors... I dare you. What? It costs 190gp and you only started with 100gp? Gee. Guess someone has a problem.

So basically, the only folks who are going to be established here are the hardest of the hardcore. No more macroing tailoring skullcaps. No more going to your buds who have castles chock full of platemail for outfitting. Hell, no more castles - those deeds cost a cool THREE MILLION GOLD. Guild membership is a requirement, they may as well ask what guild you're in when you roll up a new character.

You going to be a merchant? Fine (and they will rule this shard), but that's what you are... no mules, unless you have a second or third account (and truthfully, not many of us are that ate up, including myself). You going to be a mage or alchy? Fine, but I hope you have a LOT of money saved up for reagents.

You going to be a PK? Heh heh heh. Hope you can run when the tables turn, boy, because Mister Kal Ort Por is out of business.

I predict the server will be FLOODED the first couple of days -- then, as it sets in exactly how hard it will be to get started, the total number of people on will drop tremendously. But those who are left are going to have a riot.

This is a pretty shrewd move for the UO dev team. UO's strengths over EQ lie in its cooperative/PvP environment and in its economy. Siege Perilous is the undiluted-heroin version of both of those. The economy will be 100% player driven... the ONLY way to make money in bulk will be from other players, or 200gp at a time from monsters. That's it, folks. Everything else is up to you. Player vendors will be HUGE. Selling everything from equipment (and finally, non-exceptional equipment will have a player market now) to reagents to the little things... like backpacks. You really want to pay an NPC 250gp for a backpack? Didn't think so.

And this server will be the PvP equivalent of the major leagues. Simply put, if you think you're a badass in UO, you are going to be on Siege Perilous. If you aren't on SP, you won't be shit. Because you can't compete. And everyone will know it.

Want to know why OSI put Siege Perilous up? Simple... newbies.

When all of us hardcore PK assassins flock to SP like bees to honey, the production shards become a much safer, friendlier place. It's a better environment for novices. It's always kind of intimidating when you show up in Minoc as a newbie and there's 50 corpses laying around from the latest townwar.

Again, it's a shrewd move by OSI, and I salute them. Verant has some catching up to do, and the Racewar server isn't gonna cut it.

07/13/99 1:30 PM GUESS WHAT LUM IS DOING WHILE WAITING FOR A JOB TO COMPILE

Here's another interesting post from Raph Koster on rec.games.computer.ultima.online, in response to a plea from a poster for some sort of virtue/Virtues in UO. (Hey, am I a cheat-oriented site too?)

Jim, Let me start by saying that I completely understand where you are coming from, and admire your dedication to the virtues.

I think that the Virtues serve as a powerful lesson, but only in reaction to evil. In other words, all the standalone Ultimas started out with a world in turmoil and succumbing to evil. It was the task of the player to set it right, and it (presumably) stayed right until the next game.

In your post I sense a tension between a desire to be the one who enters a land gone evil and sets it right, and a desire to run around in a land where things ARE right. But in a land where everything already embodies the Virtues, when do you actually exercise them? When do you actually face ethical challenges? There are no beggars to be compassionate towards. There are no misleadingly smooth promises by a Fellowship. There are no tensions and misunderstandings between two races. Because everyone is already on the right path.

To make a successful game, we can't make it completely peaceful. People do crave some extent of that struggle, I think.

That said, I know that not everyone wants to log into the game to struggle against the forces of evil. And a lot of them want to BE the forces of evil. The thing is, UO is no longer teaching the lesson you have learned. It is instead asking you the player to put it into practice in your dealings with other players.

It is amply evident that a sizable number of the players of UO don't, can't, or won't. I'd welcome ideas on how to get them to--but I don't think the answer lies is either getting rid of those who are ethically challenged (that in itself is unethical IMHO--you have to teach them the right way to behave) nor in preventing them from acting (if you have no choice but to act ethically then you are little more than a lab rat pressing prescribed buttons). I'd welcome ideas on game mechanics that reward those who act according to the Virtues. But they can't be just Pavlovian game mechanic levers that one can push, or they will fail in that ultimate goal.

As far as cracking down on cheating, harassing, and other such extra-fictional forms of unethical behavior--we do punish people. In fact, right now we're on a bit of a crusade about it, and we're seeing quite a lot of whining from those who have been caught (visit any of the cheat-oriented sites like Dr. Twister's for evidence of this).

-Raph Koster
Lead Designer, Ultima Online

07/13/99 12:30 AM IN THIS CORNER - THE PK TANK MAGE! AND IN THIS CORNER - THE TWINKED KITING DRUID!

Here's excerpts from an interesting dialogue between Raph Koster (lead designer for UO) and Brad McQuaid (lead designer for EQ) on PvP/PvE skills, player levels, and gaming systems from alt.games.everquest. (This is as part of a continuing dialogue, thus may seem a bit disjointed without posting 30 pages of text. I've included quoted text from some previous posters to try to maintain some semblance of context.)

It is a common phenomenon in PvP for a relatively low level character to take out a higher level one through the application of good tactics...

Depends on the game... in a system where the difference between a newbie and a high level character is great (regardless of whether it is a level based or skill based system) PvP tactics mean less. Obviously in a system where the difference in stat/skill/weapon/etc. based power between a newbie and a high level character isn't so great, PvP tactics mean more. It could be argued that the latter is better suited for a PvP environment, and the former for PvE.

>>>You are equating intelligence with combat effective. Intelligence only
>>>goes so far restricted by game mechanics.

>>Intelligence will always outweigh the game mechanics. Give it
>>time--everyone is still very new at EQ. :)

>That is simply not true. It does not outweigh ALL game mechanics.
>Certainly giving the same stats, same spells, same armor and generally
>same character, the one with a human behind is always better.
>But a game master *can* use game mechanics to even the odds. Look at a
>Sand Giant. It does not matter how clever you are, a L16 has no chance
>against it.

Give it time--I am telling you that in time, players will find tactics to defeat these monsters. Yes, the support staff will be right behind them scrambling to correct the method used, but nonetheless it will happen. I would be very surprised if there had not already been approximately a 5 level decrease in the capability of mobs in EQ relative to players of the same level (in fact, I don't need to be surprised, I've already heard of methods specific classes use to kill 5 levels above their capability).

>And a GM *can* (and probably has in the case of EQ) make mobs that no
>L50 player can defeat. Look at lady Vox, it takes 30 high level
>players to kill.

My magic crystal ball says that in a year, Lady Vox will be soloed or at the least taken by a group of 3 players. ;)

My magic crystal ball says that we'll upgrade Lady Vox before that occurs :)

This is a very common thing in muds. Let me describe a "Lady Vox" I put in a mud once: it was a vampire that resided in a closed room with a locked door. The instant you walked in, it slammed and locked the door behind you, blinded you, summoned helpers to take you out, and attacked with a full arsenal of magic spells.

When it first went in, it was utterly deadly. It took a carefully coordinated group of 10 people to kill it.

Today, it is routinely soloed. Despite its capabilities having been *upgraded* in the meantime, and player capabilities being downgraded to match. Players figured out tactics to deal with it, and soon were superior in organization and execution.

I pretty much agree with this, and think it's a very important part of an evolving game. Players get better, obtain more knowledge, more items of power, etc. and can over time accomplish that which their predecessors couldn't. This is, imho, a good thing -- it creates a sense of accomplishment, and even nostalgia, for long time players.

>See, the designer can ALWAYS add tougher mobs.

There are massive pitfalls here. To list the two most important ones:

1) this means (mathematically) that the game as a whole loses a degree of accessibility to newbies. Features and things to do will tend to be added at the high level in order to satisfy the high level players demanding greater challenge. It is a very common thing to see muds where 90% of the zones are intended for groups of maxxed out characters only. Newbies are reduced to one or two areas per level range, and the entire process of levelling up is seen as just "the prelude to the real game." This renders the game less friendly. UO suffers from this ("build your character to GM, then go play") and so does EQ ("the game doesn't really start until level 10"). It is a bad thing.

2) It runs the risk of unbalancing the entire economy and creating a Monty Haul system. The addition of higher level areas implies the addition of cooler and more powerful items to serve as rewards in those areas. Over time, these items filter down into an economy that has insufficient drains. The average standard of play rises as a result, and the previously difficult areas are rendered less challenging. So, the designer can ALWAYS add tougher mobs, which means even more powerful items, which...

I think #2 balances out #1 in that the lower level player will become more powerful over time just as the higher level player does, as more items and areas are added, and existing areas and items are upgraded. It's merely the responsibility of the designers and maintainers to remain vigilant and monitor and react to this normal and natural game evolution.

>But MUDs have very limited resources. How many MUDs you know have a
>team of 30 people looking after it?

Er, pretty much ALL the established muds do.

Yes, but typically they aren't paid and aren't able to devote 40+ hours a week to maintaining their MUD.

>I do admit that the designers will never catch up with the most rabid
>power levelers. But look at the average.

The average will take up the tools of the highest achievers. This is normal. They want to feel "competitive."

It does take time, though, for these 'tools', whether it be knowledge or items or what have you, to trickle down.

>I am a L24 now after about 3 months of play. Let's say I average a
>level a week (which probably is not possible after L30), I will make
>Level 50 in about 26 week, 6 months from now.

>The EQ expansion set will be out by then. And btw, I am assuming I
>ONLY play this one character without making another one.

There are big assumptions implicit in that statement which I don't want to uncover for fear that I'd be seen as bashing EQ (which really isn't my intention). Suffice it to say that it assumes that the actual form of play is altered by addition of character types or expansions.

You lost me on this one Raph -- and don't worry, I know you're not bashing EQ :)

Why does the 'form of play' have to be altered by additional character types or world expansions? Did T2A do this?

>Brad once made an estimate that an average player (20-30 hrs per week)
>will take a year to 18 month to reach level 50. That is more than
>ample time to put on an expansion that extend the life of the game for
>another 6 months. Notice that since a lot of mechanics are already in
>place, adding new areas, spells, abiliities and mobs will be a lot
>resource efficient.

I predict that the longer the game runs, the less time it will take for people to reach level 50 from scratch; I also predict that the longer the game runs, the less time it will take to exhaust a given expansion.

If the world was left alone, yes. If the world is modified such that it keeps up with the deflation we are talking about, then not necessarily. The rate of deflation, of course, must be monitored and controlled, but it is not in and of itself a bad thing. The alternative is a totally closed entropic system, which I'm not sure I've ever seen work, and I'm pretty darn sure wouldn't be too fun even if it did work.

*sigh*

I also predict that this reply will be taken as EQ bashing in some form, and that I probably shouldn't post it. :P But I'm really just trying to discuss general design factors that apply equally as much to UO. All the above applies just as much to UO as to EQ.

-Raph Koster
Lead Designer, Ultima Online
