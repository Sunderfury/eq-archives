Guidelines For Loot Distribution

Part of the fun of being in a closely knit family like Aquilo Phoenix is joining together with guildmates to engage in battle against powerful enemies. While the true value of such experiences is found in the bonds of freindship and trust that grow between members, a nice side benefit lies in the trophies that can be carried away from these encounters.

Unfortunately, sometimes the desire for valuable items leads to conflict, even between close members of a family. For this reason, Aquilo Phoenix has established a guildline for how loot from guild activities will be handled. Please note that this guideline applies only to raids originated by Aquilo Phoenix. When our guild members are invited to participate in activities lead by other guilds, we must abide by the rules established by their leaders.

Note: It is good planning for a raid leader to be clear about who can roll for known drops before the raid begins. This will help avert hard feelings later on, and also speeds up the loot distribution process.

Our loot rule is very simple and clear:

If you are able to use the item, will use the item, have a certain need for the item, and feel you have done enough to deserve the item, then you may roll for the item.

This hardly sounds like a rule, especially compared to some of the elaborate looting guidelines established by many powerful guilds. The important difference in our rule is that it is based upon the priciples of honor, honesty, humility, and sacrifice. These are all virtues that Aquilo Phoenix members are expected to possess in abundance.

Let us examine the individual segments of the loot rule.

Able to use the item - This means that the race and class of the character that you are playing on the raid has the ability to use the item. Example: Melee types can't wear a Robe of the Ishva, so they should not try to win it when a caster who can use it is in the group. Loot from raids is meant to go to the characters who are actually present, and should not be used to twink another character who is not in attendance.

Will use the item. - This means that you will actively use the item on the character participating in the raid. Example: Monks can wear golden efreeti boots, but they are too heavy and have no useful stat boosts for the monk class. Therefore, since the monk wouldn't actually wear the boots, she should not roll against a class who would make use of them.

Have a certain need for the item - This means that the item in question is better than an item for the same slot or weapon type that the character on the raid currently uses. If you have a better item than the one that dropped, and someone else on the raid who can use it does not, you should not roll for the item.

Have done enough to deserve the item - This is the trickiest principle to quantify, and is the most dependent upon the individual honor of our guild members. It is a fact that on certain raids, especially in the planes, some guild members will have put in more time and effort than others have. Ideally, the people who have put in the most time and effort, and who have made the most significant contribution to the raid, will reap the rewards of their labor. Example: A level 47 ranger cannot join a Fear raid at the break, but arrives some hours later. After he has been there a few hours, a breastplate drops which this ranger needs, but so does the level 50 ranger who helped break the plane. While the level 47 ranger has made a significant contribution in the last few hours, and by most players' standards would certainly be entitled to roll for the item, it would be in the intersts of humility and sacrifice for him to bow out of the roll so that the level 50 ranger could have it. This would be an especially nice thing to do if the level 50 ranger was a veteran of multiple Fear raids, and had missed (or declined) previous opportunities at the breastplate.

We realize that there are many factors that determine what is a "fair" way to divide loot, and that special circumstances will arise which will require decisions that are far from clear cut. In situations where the parties involved cannot reach an agreement, the dispute will be settled by the raid leader. But if all guild members put aside personal interests and abide by the priciples of honor, honesty, humility, and sacrifice, the path to take will be made clear.
