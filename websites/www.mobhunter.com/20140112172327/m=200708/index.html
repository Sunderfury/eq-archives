It All Comes Down to a Bjork Song

Over at Eating Bees, Sanya discusses human behavior and sums it up rather nicely:

“We need challenges to feel alive, and we need to meet and defeat those challenges to feel like we matter. Some writers have claimed that players have changed, or somehow evolved. This is nonsense. Human motivations donâ€™t change.”

This is exactly why I loved the show Deadwood even though I typically don’t enjoy westerns. What did people in that old west town care about? Eating, drinking, screwing, and making money — same as people do today. Apart from the style of the dialogue, the interactions between the characters were just as easy to identify with as stories set in our own era.

If you satisfy the base motivation, context is meaningless. The greatest books, movies, and games would be great regardless of the genre they’re set in. Fantasy? Sci-fi? Victorian romance? Who cares. Genre is merely a conceit which attracts an inital batch of eyeballs. You’ll only keep consumers coming back if you satisfy their base motivations.

Game design isn’t just about coming up with good ideas. It’s about coming up with good ideas that achieve the goals you set on a product and/or company level, and making them fit seamlessly with all the other good ideas that meet that same objectives. And your objectives had better address the core motivations of your audience, or else all the good ideas in the universe aren’t going to matter.

Shocking Disaster

Though I’m living in Boston now, the news about the bridge collapse in Minneapolis hit close to home. I’ve driven over that bridge many times, and it’s simply unbelievable to see the images appearing in the news. It’s just something that almost defies description because it’s so unexpected.

My thoughts and prayers go out to all those who were affected, especially to those who lost loved ones. Though any such death is a tragedy, it’s a credit to all the rescue workers on the scene that more lives were not lost.
