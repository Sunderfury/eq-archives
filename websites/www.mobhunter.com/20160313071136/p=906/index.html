The Blog Awakens

Like pretty much everyone else I know, I went to see Star Wars: The Force Awakens. Twice so far, and it’s been an awfully long time since I’ve paid to see the same movie in the theater more than a single time. Like pretty much everyone else I know, I loved the movie.

And yet…

Look, don’t get me wrong. This is a fantastic piece of entertainment, regardless of acknowledged plot holes that should have been avoided. It is the Star Wars movie that my generation–the one who saw the original trilogy as kids and had our lives shaped by it–asked for as adults who still love the franchise and felt let down by the prequel trilogy. It’s Star Wars for grownups. J.J. Abrams and his Disney overlords gave us what we wanted.

And yet…

I promise, I’m not one of those old coots waving his fist in the air demanding that his precious Star Wars never changes. It’s a new age, and The Force Awakens needed to push the franchise forward to make the brand relevant for future generations. I get that, and approve. Abrams faced a tall order and delivered with flying colors.

And yet…

Call me crazy, but this movie has left me nostalgic for George Lucas.

No, I’m not talking about the stilted dialogue, wooden performances, or the over-reliance on CGI that marred the prequel trilogy. Clearly Lucas had his weaknesses, and most of them I will not dispute. But I also think he brought a childlike innocence to the franchise that the new movie fails–or perhaps decided not to try–to capture.

Oh sure, it’s huge on nostalgia moments: beloved characters echoing iconic lines, familiar props and settings, the holographic chess set, on and on and on. But at times it felt to me like Abrams was checking boxes on a list of fan appeasement; some of it came off as more than a little forced.

I think many of the common complaints about the prequel trilogy stem from the fact that Lucas was not trying to serve the grown-up fans of the franchise; he still wanted to make movies for kids. Or perhaps he believed that the same style of charm and humor would still resonate with adults… I can’t say for sure. But I think I walked into The Phantom Menace ready to see a movie that showed me a more mature side of the Star Wars universe, and that’s not what Lucas set out to give me. And he held fast to that vision through the next two movies.

Again, don’t get me wrong here. I’m in no way saying any of the prequel films are better movies than The Force Awakens. But whatever their flaws, I felt like they were lovingly crafted by their universe’s creator. I knew how protective he was of those characters, those settings. And I felt like there was a genuine love for that universe coming through, warts and all, even when we rolled our eyes at the jokes.

Yes, even in Jar Jar, whom I hate as much as you do.

The Force Awakens is a modern, well-crafted movie set in the Star Wars universe. I loved it, and I plan to watch it many more times in the years to come.

And yet…

Some part of me misses the old master’s touch, and wishes that instead of all those epic space battles and fight scenes, we’d been given something else. An elegant movie for a more civilized age.

Maybe we should have been more careful about what we asked for.
