Whose Game Is This, Anyway?

Ownership of virtual assets has come under the spotlight once again thanks to a lawsuit against IGE on behalf of those who believe gold farming is a disruption to the environment of paying customers. Along with questions about Second Life’s EULA, we have some new twists on the old argument about who really owns virtual goods.

Remember the sound and fury when SOE announced their Station Exchange servers? Remember mouthpieces like me throwing around phrases such as “the right to use”? Remember the outcry about how it’s really the players who own their avatars in MMOs, no matter what soulless corporations try to claim?

Well, the case against IGE is without question the best argument for why you really do want the MMO creator to own everything in the game instead of you. And I’m going to tell you why.

Companies that make MMOs want your business. They want you to fall in love with your characters, playing them for years inside the game worlds the company provides. They want you to identify with your character, daydreaming about all the loot you will one day acquire and the godlike beings you will one day defeat.

Of course, as you enter the game world you are asked to click on the Accept button of a long End User License Agreement that you never actually read. It says, in essence, that you intend to play nice within the game world and that you realize it is the company that owns your character assets, not you.

Some players balk at that notion. After all, they are the ones investing the time and creativity to make those hash marks in a database actually mean something. Shouldn’t the playerÂ have the right to sell their creation if they want to?

No. Because the EULA is not there to hang like a sword of Damocles over the player’s head — believe it or not, it’s there to protect them.

As long as you play by the spirit of the rules, game companies aren’t going to use the EULA against you. It’s in their best interests not to, because after all their goal is to get you to keep playing their game for, like, ever. No matter what you think of a given game company’s choices or decisions, I can pretty much guarantee you that they never, ever, ever try to drive their good customers away. That would be dumb.

So who is the EULA meant to target? The gold farmers, the disruptive players, the hackers, and those who generally engage in asshattery outside the spirit of the rules. Thanks to the EULA, game makers get to tell jackasses that they need to hand over their toys (in this case, virtual assets) and get the hell off the playground.

If honest players owned in-game assets, guess who else would? The gold farmers, the disruptive players, the hackers… you know, the asshats. And if the IGEs of the world can claim that they have every right to sell the assets they own, then there’s no defense against in-game spam and a dozen other types of gameplay disruption they’ve thought of but are currently reluctant to try.

Seriously, let Blizzard, SOE, NCsoft, and the rest own your characters. As long as you play nice, they’ll let you do whatever you want with them. Challenge that notion for some sort of meaningless intellectual property victory and you open the floodgates for IGE and its minions to run MMOs into the ground.
