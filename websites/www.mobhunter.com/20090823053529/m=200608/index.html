Hardcore vs. Casual: It’s On! (Or Is It?)

When I first started playing UO, I didn’t even think about it. Gamers were gamers. Sure, that PK who had me dead in a few hits was obviously better at it than I was, but that just seemed like part of the deal.

After EQ launched,Â I watched websites devoted to the game spring up all around. It quickly became clear that a lot of people were devoting a hell of a lot of time to this MMO thing, and I became fascinated by the degree to which some expressed their passion. Those were heady times, back when someone called Abashi blazed the trail known as community management, often setting the nearby grasslands ablaze in the process.

The MMO community has evolved since then. Developers have (hopefully) learned a lot, and many players know more about what goes into making a game of this scope. But amidst all this growth, one conflict that was around from the beginning refuses to go away. In fact, the sides are more divided now than ever, and the hostility has never been stronger.

O Death, Where Is Thy Sting?

One of the things that makes a game a game is the possibility that you can win or lose. This is true of everything from multiplayer sports to single-player games like Solitaire. In order to achieve victory, there must be the chance for defeat.

In video games (at least those which focus on combat), the most obvious way to illustrate defeat is death. You fight an opponent and he/she/it kills you. The goal of the game is to win, but you blew it. You paid the debt which cancels all others.

The extent to which death should impact your play experience, of course, is a matter of considerable debate among those who discuss MMOs. It’s a topic that tends to solicit strong opinions, in large part because of the reactions to theÂ penalties players have experienced in previous games they played.

I Picked a Hell of a Time to Fly

I’m off work for about a week on a trip to the east coast, visiting Boston and Maine. Hadn’t turned on the TV before going to the airport this morning. It seemed odd that there were extra guards on hand telling everyone to remove shampoo, toothpaste, and other liquids from their carry-on bags. When I arrived at the gate and saw the CNN report on the terrorist plot in the UK, of course, it made sense.

All in all, the extra security didn’t delay the flight and everything went smoothly. Still, it’s a bit spooky flying on a day like this, and frightening to realize what lengths some will go to inspire terror. We live in a pretty messed-up world, and it can make working in the gamesÂ industry feel pretty unimportant. But hey, we all have to live our lives and we can’t let the crazy bastards win.

It will suck if carry-on bags end up being banned from flights. I’ve become utterly dependent upon my PSP to get me through those hours spent in a cramped seat with all the germ-ridden people around me. But if it makes planes less likely to be blown apart by psychopaths, I’ll find a way to get by.
