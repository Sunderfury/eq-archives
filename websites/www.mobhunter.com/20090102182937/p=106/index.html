PvP Is for Suckers! (Prove Me Wrong, Suckers!)

I noticed that Lum got an awful lot of traffic when he wrote about PvP philosophy, and Psychochild cashed in as well. This lends some weight to the notion that those who love PvP are a vocal lot who enjoy talking about their favorite style of gameplay.

So even though I really don’t think PvP is for suckers, I figureÂ a controversial title will get me lots of traffic and comments. Oh, what a devious blogger I have become!

I enjoy competition with other humans when there’s a reasonable chance that either side could win. A fastball competition against Schilling would not be fun because I know I’d have no hope; playing Archon against him is another story. I’m not saying everything has to be perfectly equal, but to me there’s not a lot of fun to be had if I know I’m either guaranteed to lose or guaranteed to win.

There really hasn’t been a lot of MMO PvP that I’ve enjoyed. Back in the days of Ultima Online, I used to dread running into the random PK deep within a dungeon when I was trying to hunt. I felt like I wasÂ at the mercy of a dialup connection as my reagents were stolen and prize armor ganked, followed by the inevitable trash talk as I stumbled around in ghost form. I dabbled with PvP in EQ and EQ2, but I ended up staying almost exclusively PvE in both games.

I think Lum’s take is closest to my experience. I felt like PvP was gang warfare, and since my in-game circles tend to stay pretty small, I was a target more often than I was a participant. Though such situations can be overcome if you stick with it, I frankly didn’t enjoy the playstyle enough to mold myself into a badass PK.

WoW’s battlegrounds probably come closest to the type of PvP I like, because you’re not at the mercy of some jackass griefing you until you give up and log out. Battlegrounds are easy in, easy out, with little mess.

That said, where battlegrounds miss the mark is giving players a sense that their fights matter in the big picture. A relevant part of the PvP population wants the action to have a significant role in the story and makeup of the world. DAoC did a good job with this aspect, staging RvR combat as the crux of the game.

While one could debate whether MMO PvP can ever be mainstream, clearly there is a large audience that appreciates exciting, well-thought-out PvP combat. Such PvP needs to be at the core of the systems you are designing, not something squished into a game that was never designed for it.

The ideal solution would be an MMO design that worked equally well (but very differently) for PvP and PvE. That is, everything would feel completely cohesive and complete no matter which style you played.

Can the perfect bridge between the two playstyles be achieved? Or must the PvP flag be an all-or-nothing deal in order to be truly satisfying to either side?

Come on, suckers… I mean, gentle readers. Your ideas are welcome.

    I believe it can be done through resource management.

    Here’s my idea. Consider an MMO that consists of two major opposing factions, similar in concept to WoW. I’ll call these sides Red and Blue.

    We’ll start from a close-up perspective. There is a stretch of neutral territory between a Red base and a Blue base. Each base contains a smithy. The neutral territory contains an iron ore mine, which may be owned by either party. Every hour, this mine will send an iron ore caravan to travel to the relevant base, depending on who owns the mine at that time.

    Should the caravan get to the military base, the smithy gains an ability. For the next hour, all NPCs that spawn at the base receive weapon and armor bonuses. Also, all PCs that go to the smithy may receive similar bonuses that will last until the next time they have to repair said weapons and armor.

    NPC guards will not spawn at the mine to defend it, so the owner’s base must send in squads of reinforcements every so often. Likewise, the enemy’s base will send in squads of NPCs to raid the mine on a similarly regular basis. The timing of this should be such that without player interference, the ownership of the mine will never change from one side to the other, but that its always a near thing.

    Already, we have two quest options available depending on the environment. Basically, “Travel with the next NPC escort to defend the mine”, or “Travel with the next NPC escort to raid the mine.” This allows for PCs from both sides to engage each other in a way that actually influences the environment.

    Also, with all those NPCs running around tactics comes into play much more than just traveling around in a high-level pack hero killing. The game mechanics should be tweaked so that, much like in the average game of Dota (if you’re familiar), travelling behind enemy lines to attack the enemy heros directly will usually result in being mass-attacked by every enemy NPC in the area. So it makes more sense to hang back and mop up those NPCs first, giving the enemy PCs a chance to turn tail if the battle isn’t going their way.

    The focus of PvP in this way becomes changing the consequences in the environment to gain an advantage, instead of just racking up killscores.

    The concept above becomes even more interesting when you consider other possibilities. “Travel with these NPCs to escort the caravan back to base” is one, where the reward - both environmental and personal to the PCs involved - would depend on how damaged the caravan was by the time it got to the smithy. If unharmed, a very strong armor upgrade at the smithy, and a lot of cash/a nice weapon/whatever. If highly damaged, less of an upgrade and less cash.

    Then there’s “Raid the enemy caravan with these NPCs to hijack or destroy it” is another. You get a cash payment for destroying the caravan, but if you, your PC mates and your NPC escort manage to drive it back to your base, you get the armor bonus at *your* smithy, and some Blue-reward tokens that may be traded for something nice once you accumulate a whole bunch of them.

    The idea really takes off if you give an overall arc-quest of “If we receive 20 iron ore shipments at our smithy in the space of 24 hours, we will launch a sizable NPC raid on the enemy base which will, given our superior arms and armor, have a good chance of succeeding.” If it succeeds, the Blues can actually take over the base which used to belong to the Reds. Then, the lumber mill between that base and the NEXT Red base becomes the relevant resource to manage, and the Blues may then eventually raid and take over that Red base. And so on and so forth.

    That’s all well and good for PvP, but what about PvE? Well, lets go back to when the Reds and Blues were fighting over the iron ore mine. Behind the Red military base there’s a lumber mill. This lumber mill sends wood shipments to repair the walls of the Red base with the smithy, strengthening defenses. Since this lumber mill is well within Red territory, there’s no Blues to fight. Enter the Bandit Chief. So long as the area isn’t in contention, there is a base of NPC bandits that sends out raids on the wood shipments. Without player interference, the bandit raids will always succeed over the caravan’s NPC escorts. But players that are too low level for PvP or players who just prefer PvE can tip the scales, taking the wood transport to the Red base at the front line for a reward, and ALSO BOLSTERING THE TOWN’S DEFENCES AGAINST THE BLUE ARMY ATTACKING. So even PvE has an environmental influence on the tides of battle between the Reds and the Blues.

    There’s problems with the idea, I grant you. If the Blue Army gets on a roll and winds up taking over the whole world, the playing area for the Reds would get smaller and smaller. This would need its own solutions. But I think it would be an intelligent way of allowing for PvE or PvP in a way that influences the playing environment and that also takes to focus away from mere hero killing in the latter.

    I stopped playing WoW a few months ago. If a system like this had been implemented, I would have kept on playing. I’m led to understand that player retention is an issue for developers. This is one potential solution that would have worked on me, and others like me.

I think mainly because even hardcore PvPers like to occasionally take some time out and see that yes, there is all sorts of other stuff in the game they can do.

    So we want PvP to have a purpose and an impact on the game world. But if we do that, we also want PvE to have a purpose and an impact. And then we’ve got people who want to craft and they want that to have meaning. And we’ve also got people who want to own houses, and because of that we’ve got people who want to set up taverns, and because of that we’ve got folks who want to sing and dance and entertain other players, and people who want to take all the houses and make them into a town, and raise an army to wage war on other cities and….hey we’re back at PvP. Imagine that

    Possibly the key here is designing a system in such a way that it creates, supports, and fulfills the above.

    What I see as a major problem is what you’ve identified in your third paragraph, Moorgard. PvP is by nature unpredicatable, and so gives you a different experience each time you enter into it.

    Items? Make them communal. Make your strategy and knowledge of how to use each item your weapon. Avid players of strategy games like Command and Conquer know that it’s that what you think that matters, not what you have. Lord knows I’ve defeated enough WoW players who had uber loot and no knowledge of how to use their skills. It’s the same in Eve, where you can shoot down any ships regardless of the equipment it has; you just need to think and bring a few friends (or a couple hundred).
