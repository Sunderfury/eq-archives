Format War Over… For Now

It was a war that never should have happened: two competing standards for the high definition media crown. In one corner, HD DVD, the format backed largely by Toshiba which could have won based solely on its logical name (people know what HD is, and they know what a DVD is… a no-brainer). In the other corner, Blu-ray Disc, the oddly named format championed by Sony.

For a while there, it looked like the VHS vs. Betamax battle all over again. Stores were stocking both formats, confusing customers and causing most of them to stick with the familiar DVD. Add in the fact that you needed an HDTV to even care, as well as the introduction of “upconverting” players which made regular DVDs look great on high-def TVs for far less money than either of the new formats, and you had a recipe for a technology meltdown.

So it was with an almost palpable sense of relief in the industry that, at this year’s CES, the dominoes destined to end the war started to fall. Warner Bros. announced that they would stop releasing videos on HD DVD and would support Blu-ray exclusively. As retailers began announcing that they would either carry Blu-ray exclusively or encourage customers to choose it over its rival, Toshiba conceded defeat and dropped HD DVD. Just like that, it was over.

The winners? People like me who rolled the dice on Blu-ray. I bought a PS3 almost unintentionally, and despite troubles early on I think it’s shaping up to be a great media console. The losers? All the people who got suckered into buying HD DVD players and movies last Christmas without knowing their brand new system was about to be orphaned.

And guess who doesn’t care: Microsoft. Though they offered an HD DVD module for the 360, none of their console’s titles shipped in that format. When HD DVD’s future looked shaky after CES, Microsoft responded with an unqualified “Meh.” Why would that be? Because, like every cable company in America, Microsoft wants the future of media to be downloadable–and they want to be the ones serving that future up to you.

As a faster data infrastructure is being built across the globe, the reality of downloading full high def movies in a matter of minutes is almost here. When that’s the case, how many people are going to waste time, effort, and shelf space buying movies on pieces of plastic? Go ahead and ask the record industry what happens when consumers have the choice between downloading their media or buying it in a store.

It also doesn’t help that Blu-ray movies retail for significantly more than their DVD counterparts. For mass adoption to really kick in, pricing will need to be a bit more realistic. A slight premium is fine because the format does offer some slick features that DVD doesn’t, but the pricing gap needs to narrow.

So while round one of the HD media war is over, the real battle is still to come. And the way it’s looking, round two will be even bloodier than the first.
