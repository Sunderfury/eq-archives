Patch Notes Are the Key to Wisdom

Actual patch note:

Longer combat times inherently sharpen archetype roles and there is more opportunity to enjoy your class play experience both solo and in groups.

Ah, so that’s how you get players toÂ enjoy their classes! Turns out there are a few other things in the universe I hadn’t realized before:

  • Longer times in the dentist chair give you more opportunity to enjoy your dental experience.
  • It’s a positive thing when your car breaks down because it sharpens the role of your shoes.

IÂ make this post not to beÂ glib or to pick on any particular design team. Rather, I wish to underscore two key points: 1) patchÂ notesÂ make a poor vehicle for advancing game design theory, and 2) you shouldn’t make design decisions sound like dogma.

Whether lengthy combat adds to enjoyment or class distinctiveness is a matter of opinion, not an absolute. Simply adding “We feel that…” to the above patch note avoids that pitfall, and a direct explanation to the players in a different setting (i.e. blogs or forums) is an even better venue for explaining why things are the way they are.

Ideally, of course, your systems simply feel right within the game you’ve created so no explanation is necessary, but given the scrutiny afforded to all MMOs these days it may be impossible to release a major game without being forced to justify such decisions and directions.

A New Year and Whatnot

So let me just get this out of the way right up front: I bought a PS3. I really didn’t intend to — I was more interested inÂ finding a Wii — but there’s something about being in a Walmart after Christmas and seeing the PS3 on the shelves that I just couldn’t resist. I felt giddy and dirty simultaneously, which is always a sexy combination.

Being over at Kohath’s house and seeing the PS3 hooked up to his 52″ TV a few nights before was sort of inspiring, despite the fact that I didn’t care for the games he had. That whole new technology thing is just intoxicating, and I’m nothing if not a pawn of overhyped commercialism.

Plus, here’s my justification: Once I settle in and buy a house here in the Boston area I will be picking up a new HDTV. I’ll want to watch high-def movies on it. Blu-Ray delivers the goods, and the PS3 plays games too. See how easy it is to convince myself to spend lots of money?

The Bluetooth remote is pretty sweet (sold separately, of course), and the console itself looks cool. One of these days I’ll even buy some games for it.
