A Look into the Vast Wasteland

Q: Are We Not Men? A: We Are TiVo!

I said last time I wrote one of these columns that I would reveal what ends up on my TiVo. First, though, a moment of clarification.

I don’t own a TiVo. I own a ReplayTV. Technically speaking, I don’t really own it; the thing actually belongs to my buddy Kohath, and even though I’ve been using it for several years, I’ve never paid him a dime for it. But since possession is nine tenths of the law, let’s just agree to the premise that I own it.

So why am I not talking about what’s on my ReplayTV? Because TiVo won the verbification war.

What the heck is “verbification”? I’ll tell you, since I made it up.

Every so often, a brand name becomes so engrained in our culture that it gets turned into a verb. For example, the search engine Google is so widely known that you don’t hear people saying “Did you search online for that?” You hear them say “Did you Google that?” I’m sure you can think of other brand names that have been verbified.

Although TiVo and ReplayTV hit the market at about the same time, the latter didn’t catch on nearly as much as the former. In addition, DVR–the generic term for digital video recording devices–just doesn’t sound sexy. As a result, you hear all kinds of people mentioning what they’ve got on their TiVo, just because most everyone understands what they mean.

Anyway, here is a brief dip into my psyche, courtesy of my recording habits. I’ll just stick to network/basic cable stuff for now and leave HBO for another time.

24 – I’ve watched this show from the beginning, and whether you like it or not, you must admit it was a unique concept. Now in its fourth season, it feels far less innovative, mostly since we’re all just waiting for the next twist or turn. I mean, what more can Jack do that would really shock us? How much more extreme can the threat to the country get? Despite that, it’s still an entertaining show, and this season is all the better since it’s no longer saddled with ridiculous plotlines such as Kim getting chased by mountain lions.

Lost – I never spent much time watching Alias, despite all of Jennifer Garner’s compelling disguises. But this series, by the same creator, had me interested when I first read about it. What’s kept me watching, though, is a combination of a talented cast and some terrific writing that is constantly playing with your expectations about the characters. Just when you think you know why someone acts the way they do, a flashback scene comes along to challenge your perceptions. Those kind of moments are infinitely more intriguing than a spooky island with polar bears.

The Shield – Those of you who read my Mobhunter stuff may remember my homage to the late, great cop show Homicide: Life on the Streets. To me, that was the best police drama ever to air on television, and I still maintain that belief. The Shield, though, is Homicide’s offspring in many ways, albeit with the violence and foul language cranked up a few notches. Its characterizations are extreme at times (if there really are cops like Vic, I’d rather not know about it), but the acting is first-rate and the stories superb. With Glenn Close joining the cast, the show is even more promising this season.

Nip/Tuck – Yet another FX show that doesn’t pull any punches. Plastic surgeons must cringe at the portrayal of these characters at times, but this is just great television. The operating room scenes look real enough to make you queasy, and the personal drama often packs a similar wallop. Last season’s cliffhanger was a bona fide shocker, and I can’t wait to see what season three will bring.

Survivor – Yeah, I was one of those people who poo-pooed the whole reality TV concept. I was sure this show was going to be absolute crap, and only watched it to prove my own point. Needless to say, I fell into the silky web of Jeff Probst and haven’t gotten out since. Is it melodramatic? Yes. And often ridiculous and oversexed, too. But I can’t help but tune in every week to see who gets voted off. If it helps salvage any of the respect you just lost for me, at least you can feel better to know that this is my only reality show guilty pleasure. Honest.

Arrested Development – I’m of the opinion that this is the funniest network television show since Seinfeld. The writing is top-notch, the ensemble cast is magnificent, and the directorial pacing of the show matches its quick wit. It’s deliberate absurdity at its finest, which is made all more hilarious by Ron Howard’s deadpan narration. Like Seinfeld did at first, this show suffers a bit in the ratings. Let’s hope Fox shows the same level of commitment that NBC did for Jerry, because Arrested Development has the potential to be known as one of television’s great comedies.

Battlestar Galactica – I am going to give this show its own column, both because I’ve rambled on long enough for one day and because I think this is the best science fiction show to come along in many years. I’ve received enough PMs to indicate that a lot of you enjoy it as well, so we’ll save my analysis for next time.

Aside from a few strays here and there, the programs above are the ones I watch on a regular basis. They, along with allusions to some of my favorites from the past, may give you some insight into the twists and turns of my odd little mind. But then again, it’s only TV, so take it as you will.
