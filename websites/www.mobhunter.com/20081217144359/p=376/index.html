The Knight Who Was Death

Like so many others, I have been lured by the call of the Lich King. After drifting off into AoC and WAR for a while, one appreciates just how polished and solid the act of playing WoW is. That’s not to say it’s perfect; rather, I mean the fact that there usually aren’t stupid little annoyances that gnaw away at your urge to play like so many rabid chipmunks.

My main in WoW has been an undead warrior, a character of which I’ve never been especially fond. As a DPS build, I pretended he was a monk in order to get through the day. As an undead, I pretended he used to be an attractive human (unlike the human models in WoW, which I can’t stand). You might guess based upon my general malaise that I was a prime candidate to try out the death knight, and you’d be right.

General impressions and a couple spoilers after the break.

First off, the death knight intro quests are cooler than anything that I’ve seen in WoW (or most any MMOG) so far. Built upon their phasing technology, you move back and forth through the zone and see real changes based on the quests you’ve done. That’s extremely awesome!

Secondly, the armor that you get looks absolutely badass. I made a blood elf male DK and am completely sold on how he looks. Extra awesome!

Thirdly, the death knight cuts through mobs like a hot knife through butter. There’s something scandalously appealing about a class that makes you feel like you’re exploiting the system just by playing it. You can just about mash random buttons and be extremely successful. Super duper awesome!

However, there are negatives that go along with each of these positives. Here’s where the spoilers come in:

  • After playing through all these cool, dynamic, zone-changing quests, you get dumped into the static, never-changing Outland. Ugh! Give me one freaking 60-70 zone in Wrath so I don’t have to relive this grind!
  • The quest line builds up the notion that you really don’t want to be working for mean old Arthas. You can almost taste your betrayal moment coming… except it doesn’t. Instead, your boss decides that he will no longer work for the Lich King, so now you won’t be working for him either. How rebellious of you–not!
  • That awesome DK armor? Yeah, prepare to trade it in for a clown suit as you level through Outland. Personally, I’m determined to wear my quest gear until I absolutely can’t anymore, but even so it’s a bummer to lose out on that badass look. Blizzard, give us vanity slots already!

The only saving grace with playing through Outland again is that the overpowered death knight completes quests so much faster than characters did the first time around (especially compared to my cumbersome warrior). Besides just killing quickly, you also get a fast horse with access to a talent that increases your speed to godlike levels. It should take far less time to get to a reasonable level in the BC zones, at which point you can jump over to Wrath and get to the new stuff.

Will the death knight be my new main character? In all likelihood, yes. I already feel more of a personal connection to it than I did to my undead warrior, and the class is just plain fun to play. You can’t throw a rock in Outland without hitting a dozen death knights, and I’m sure there will be even more people trying them out once they get their main characters to 80.

Whether you’re a raider or a soloer or anywhere in between, I recommend playing through the death knight starting experience at least once. It features some of the best quests in WoW so far and leaves you with a very intriguing new character to play around with. As for yours truly, you will find me in Outland for a while, but eventually Moordeth will be making his way to the frozen north.

After all, I owe Arthas a visit.

  • Chris Holmes
    November 24th, 2008 at 3:56 pm

    The question is though: what is fun?

    It’s all relative. We used to have these discussions on various message boards and I’ve always believed you could boil MMO gamers down into two main camps.

    The first camp is the group of people want to be entertained. To them, an MMO is no different than a game of chess, a hand of poker, or a match against a buddy in Madden when it comes to entertainment value. It’s a GAME to them, in the purest sense of the word. Unvarnished entertainment. Fun. And for those people, fun is THE keyword. It’s not anything else. So the whole risk/reward aspect, or the raid tactics, or whatever, none of those things have nearly as much impact on them as the raw entertainment value of the thing. And the minute their fun gets diminished because of obstacles, they’re gone.

    The second group of people are more carrot-oriented. It’s not so much about being entertained as it is working toward a goal. They’re goal-oriented people. They enjoy overcoming challenges, obstacles; they like feeling like they’re earning something. They don’t like tedious tasks and repetitive quests, but they’ll do it if it means something bigger and grander at the end. These are the people who put together the dragon-slaying guilds and push the limits of the content.

    Now, you could put a game of checkers down in front of the two camps and ask them to play each other, and they could potentially have ‘fun’, but for vastly different reasons. A camp 1 player is going to have fun if he just enjoys the act of playing checkers; if the checkers look neat and he feels like he’s “winning” most of the time. A camp 2 player could have fun too - if he’s achieving his objectives. If there are no objectives, he’ll make up his own. Like, “I need to the next six games in a row, or I start over”. That’s just the way they operate.

    Over the years, I’ve come to believe there are a lot more camp 1 folks than camp 2. And I don’t think that’s good or bad; it’s just how it is.

    I’m a camp 2 guy myself. I like challenge, I like risk/reward, I enjoy goal-setting and achievement. I’m wired that way. But I totally get that there is a another camp of gamers, and they’re numbers are bigger than mine. And I don’t have a problem with that.

    The smart developers are going to build content for everyone anyway. And that’s what Blizzard has done.
