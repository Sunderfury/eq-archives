Shadows of Luclin Preview
 12:56 EST - jcsmith

There is a brief preview of the upcoming Everquest expansion: Shadows of Luclin up at CGO today. Peep this...

But it seems that most of the work being put into Luclin is going into new character models. Characters are built from more polygons and bigger textures, which gives them a lot more detail. A new skeletal animation system is being used for combat animations specific to weapon configurations; a character with a shield and a sword will move differently from someone with a spear or axe. The most dramatic change is ironically the smallest. Characters' faces are now 3D models with bones moving their brows and mouths. Eyes are separate objects that can roll and glance. Characters in combat will grimace while they fight. They'll get a pained look when they're damaged. Players can smile and frown. These new animations and facial models give characters in Luclin more, well, character.

Regarding Runnyeye
 12:43 EST - jcsmith

Absor made a huge post at the Developer's Corner where he covers a ton of Runnyeye related issues. Here's a piece of it...

We were aware that there would be a stampede to the zone once we announced it in the patch message, and some thought had been given to implementing the ABF (Anti-Bottom Feeding) code there, but we decided against it.

We had promised that we would not be doing this to existing zones, and despite the fact that 99.9% of the population of Runnyeye is new, most of the old items from the zone were retained, (alongside the new items to be found there) to allow existing quests dependant on them to remain functional.

Turning on the ABF code there could have had detrimental effects on those trying to complete quests.

I feel that after the "shiny" wears off, the over-level players will leave the zone, and those of the appropriate level can begin to really enjoy the zone.

Kill Me, Please
 0:51 EST - jcsmith

There is a new editorial at RPGDot by the name of Kill Me,Please. It takes a look at death in computer RPGs. Here's a taste test...

The key in all these cases is game balance. Regardless of which approach the designers take, death has to be balanced as part of the overall game. For MMORPGs this is perhaps trickier, since death can occur due to circumstances beyond a player’s control, such as lag. It is this, and the ever-present grief players, that lead me away from thinking perma-death would be right for MMORPGs are they are today. Though if unlimited resurrection is the way, then hard level limits, and levels themselves, should really go. There is little point in having a ceiling for advancement I these games because everyone will get there, sooner or later, it’s just a matter of time. Skill based advancements are better, since these also allow players to give their characters real personality, becoming Master Smith Champion Archer Jenna, instead of Sorcerer 56 Jenna with all the best items. If you decide to pursue swordplay over magecraft, you should still have the option of learning both later, if you put the time in. After all, isn’t that what these games are all about?
