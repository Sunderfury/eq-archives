Why Aren't Damage Shields Resistable?
 13:39 EST - jcsmith

Absor responded to the question of why damage shields are not resistable in Everquest. Here's the real deal...

Thistle coat is easy, it's a physical object. If the golem could resist it, then it would be resisting sword blades and arrows.

The fire one is different. I could, I suppose, come up with a good enough explanation for it. Like perhaps the fact that the shield resides within the aura of the person being hit, that shield is much stronger than normal fire spells. Or that by actively reaching into the fire the opponent is sacrificing his ability to resist the fire.

The reality is that those spells just don't give resists, and their power and effectiveness is based on that fact. If they were resistible they might be more powerful.

Make Norrath Talk
 13:32 EST - jcsmith

Eno Khaon dropped us a mail to inform us about something that's new to me at least. Here's the scoop...

A few RL friends of mine and myself read at a message board that someone made a program that makes EverQuest talk. So we checked it for ourselves, and sure enough, someone did! The program is called EverTalk and after much virus scanning and then eventual testing, we found it to be legit and not a scam. The website for it is
http://DysassymblerX.tripod.com/

The installation process is a bit rough (you have to follow directions at the website very carefully) but it works quite well and is, so far, pretty free from bugs other than an occasional mispronounciation. It even supports some in game commands that are entered as emotes. "/em doesn't hear voices." disables the speech and "/em hears voices." re-enabled speech.

Hybrid Mana Costs
 1:05 EST - jcsmith

Lawrence Poe put out the word on Hybrid Mana costs at the Developer's Corner earlier today. Here's the real deal...

We have made several changes to Hybrid mana pools/Spell costs. Hybrids now have the same mana pool as a pure caster, meaning if a Paladin and a Cleric are in the same equipment with the same stats, and were the same level they will have the same amount of mana.

There has always been an artificial spell cost increase for Hybrids casting spells, meaning for a Hybrid to cast a spell it would cost more mana than if their parent class were to cast the same spell. The reason the spells in game do not reflect this is that the artificial cost increase is done in code, instead of the spdat. Recently this artificial cost increase has been lessened. Also starting at level 51 the added cost starts to gradually go down, eventually reaching almost zero additional cost when the character reaches level 60.

Critical Hits With Bolts?
 13:03 EST - jcsmith

Absor responded on the Developer's Corner yesterday regarding the oddity of what appears to be a critical hit with bolt spells in EQ. Here's the dope...

We have not added critical hits to bolt spells. I've read a couple emails about this phenomenon. I spoke with Steve Clover about this, what appears to be happening is that the mage will cast a bolt spell and due to lag or the zone server not updating it will miss and fly off. The caster of the bolt will cast another bolt spell as the 2nd bolt spell is hitting the first one will correct itself and also strike the target, giving the appearance of a "Double attack" or "critical hit".

MMORPG Rumors
 12:32 EST - jcsmith

A new edition of the Gamespin Editorials was posted to Gamespy yesterday. This one examines some rumors in the MMORPG community and such games as Middle Earth Online, Shadowbane and more. Here's a taste test...

My feeling is that companies are starting to shy away from MMORPGs and Take 2 just decided that it would be too expensive to run the server farm. The servers cost a lot and the staff to administer them and handle customer relations isn't cheap either. The generally accepted break-even point for these games seems to be 100,000 subscribers, and that's a number that Turbine's Asheron's Call has struggled to get to, despite being backed by Microsoft -- and Turbine just laid off some staff. Then Take 2 probably looked at the big picture and saw Star Wars Galaxies, the unannounced EverQuest 2, and other MMORPGs in development and decided that Shadowbane might not make financial sense. It probably doesn't help that Shadowbane will emphasize player vs. player combat, something that has proven to be unpopular for the industry-leading EverQuest.

This article makes for a good read and has some good information inside. The editor later goes on to say that he thinks the market might be close to saturation with 700,000 or so subscribers between UO, AC and EQ. I feel that is a gross miscalculation on his part. That market is still growing at astronomical rates between those three titles and they are offering much less than this genre can being first generation titles. I think that one thing a lot of people don't understand is many MMORPG fans aren't traditional RPG fans. They estimate the MMORPG market by the traditional RPG market. I have three real life friends who began playing these games simply because I turned them on to them. They watched me play a few times, then tried the games out here then bought their own accounts. None of these people even enjoyed or played RPG's before UO or Everquest.

Despite that disagreement I really enjoyed the article.

Warden on EQ Expansion
 13:38 EST - jcsmith

Warden made a response on the Shadowbane forums to give his thoughts on the Shadows of Luclin expansion for Everquest and about EQ and SB competition. Here's the scoop...

EQ and SB, we think, will likely end up being more "indirect" competition than anything else.. sure, we'll have players migrate back and forth -- but our goal with SB was to expand the current market, not just "steal some players away from pre-existing games and hope for the best."

If you think about it, EQ and SB may look similar on the surface but they're actually very, very different visions : EQ is a static model that concentrates on enhancing and extending the "dungeon adventure" style of gaming. (by "static" I mean the world doesn't really change. Log off for a few months and come back in.. other than some new players faces, your game experience remains pretty much the same.) SB on the other hand uses a very dynamic model, emphasising group and guild interaction, and a constantly-changing political landscape. From month to month new citystates will form, kingdoms will crumble, and some players will rise up as heroes (or villains) and affect the world in a very real way. So while we both use similar technology, they're aiming for a very different "core experience".

This isn't to say they won't flesh out their PvP experience to some degree (more likely by using a model similar to what DaoC has planned) but I'm doubtful that you could "tack on" a truly dynamic model to what is, at its core, a static game... And, moreover, I don't even think I'd want to! Why rock the already-profitable boat with a bunch of new functionality that isn't aimed at your core market?

.. so, anyway, yes, we've heard the rumors, but we aren't terribly concerned. Even our staunches critics have to grant us that we're novel and different (and therefore "risky" as far as these games go.)

A Rebuttal!
 13:07 EST - jcsmith

Last week we linked to the Telling Stories Without Telling Them editorial at Gamespy. A week has gone by and Monolith's Craig Hubbard has posted a Rebuttal. Here's a snippet...

Maybe now that Mr. Osborne has played Half-Life, he can no longer sit through The Godfather without fidgeting, but for those of us who can, "interruptions" like cutscenes, linear narratives, and even printed text are all just facets of an incredibly flexible art form. When used appropriately, they add context and dimension to a game. They're a tool to provide a player with motives and history beyond what he or she brings to the game. They aren't necessary for all games, obviously, but neither are they anathema. Otherwise, Final Fantasy IX wouldn't have been a smash hit and Metal Gear Solid 2 wouldn't be on so many people's "must have" lists.

On Plane of Mischief No Drop Items
 13:00 EST - jcsmith

Absor made a response yesterday on the Developer's Corner. He discusses the need to change certain Plane of Mischief items to NO DROP. Here's the 411...

Yup, we're very aware of how the quests work. I announced those changes the second I heard them so that you would have as much warning as possible.

Since then we've looked into other, more interesting ways of fixing things that we deemed wrong with this area (I'm not going into details, I love the fact that this zone is complicated, and I'm not spoiling things). I'm not sure how, exactly, we're going to make these changes yet. And it might very well include making many of these items No-Drop, as I mentioned. But I think that you'll see that it works out well in the end, and making those items No-Drop shouldn't actually be a problem for those that are playing in the zone. We're adding levels of convolution to the zone.

I can't explain it further, but if the items do become No-Drop, we are aware of the concerns that folks have brought up, and we know that it's nearly impossible to tell what's what up there. We're not going to make it so that those that do the experimentation will get the least reward. As to how that will be accomplished, you'll have to trust us.
