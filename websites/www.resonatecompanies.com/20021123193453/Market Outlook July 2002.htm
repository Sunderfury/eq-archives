Market Outlook July 2002

Thomas A. Giles, CFA

Chief Investment Officer and Director of Research

Dean Investment Associates

 

 

This Time is Different (But it’s really the same)

 

          Historically, this year’s first half was the worst first half for the S&P 500 since 1970. The good news is the S&P 500 rose very strongly in the second half of 1970 because that recession ended and the economy grew. The 1970 market environment was similar to today because both recessions saw unemployment rise to 6% after declining for 9 years. Today, we face a war on terrorism, but in 1970 we faced acceleration in the Viet Nam War.

          The NASDAQ was not created until 1971, but investors had begun to become aware of it by the Crash in 1987. The recession associated with the Gulf War of 1990-91 saw a bull market begin for the S&P 500 and the NASDAQ in the wake of that recession, war and bear market.

          This year, the S&P 500 and the NASDAQ have retraced to near their lows of September 2001 despite today’s economy being better than last September’s economy. The recovery doesn’t feel like the economic recoveries of the last 30 years despite GDP officially growing over 6% in the first quarter, because of the temporary boosts from government spending and unseasonably strong housing construction.

          But there are many positives in this atypical recovery that eventually should be reflected in higher stock prices. Industrial production is rising and manufacturing activity has increased every month since February. Unemployment declined in May and the government’s Index of Leading Economic Indicators has risen for 7 out of the last 8 months. Inflation, other than from health care, remains under control as the CPI is up only 1.2% over the last reported 12 months. Inventories are low especially in relation to sales as any increase in final demand would require an increase in production.

          The Federal Reserve at its meeting in late June did not raise interest rates, but stated that it “expects the rate of increase of final demand to pick up over coming quarters, supported in part by robust underlying growth in productivity, but the degree of the strengthening remains uncertain.” This is an implicit recognition that recent growth has slowed in the second quarter, but should reaccelerate. First Call is reporting that corporate negative earnings preannouncements are fewer and lighter than in previous periods, implying the economic recovery can be reflected in the EPS of most companies being in line or better than expected.

          Each economic and stock market cycle is different, but history shows how the market can overcome concerns when the economy improves following a bear market. Every bull market has climbed a wall of worry whether the worry was the end of a recession, a war, unemployment, inflation, or a crisis in confidence such as Watergate.

          The current market is suffering from a crisis in confidence stemming from three things. The opinions of Wall Street analysts influenced by investment banking relationships, the accuracy of the accounting statements and the credibility of corporate officers and Boards of Directors are all under clouds of suspicion. Last September, following the emotional sell-off after the terrorist attacks, investors correctly anticipated the economic recovery that exists today, only to see the rally be short circuited by the current crisis in confidence.

          Stocks rallied last fall as they had in previous times of difficulty because the right set of policy actions were taken to address the problems at hand. The government cut taxes and lowered interest rates to stimulate the economy. The government mobilized to combat terrorism. Corporations cut costs to improve profitability.

          Today, the right set of policy actions are being taken to address the new problems at hand. Wall Street brokerage houses are separating investment banking influences from an analyst’s recommendations. CPA firms are tightening their audit process and review function in order to retain their licenses. The internal auditors and the Board of Directors at public companies are becoming more proactive at a time when the government is probably going to enforce vigorously the laws against white collar crime.

          Investor confidence has been shaken and may take a while to be restored fully. The risk this year has turned out to be in the stock market, but we think the risk for the second half of this year is to be out of the stock market.

          Investor psychology is very bearish, just as we have seen at every major stock market bottom. This time is different a lot of people say, but it’s really the same. By the time the crisis in confidence passes, the stock market should be significantly higher.

          It is extremely difficult to fund a retirement or pay for a child’s education with a money market return of less than 2% or with a bond yielding less than 5%.

          Long term investing says to buy low and sell high. Prices are clearly low, but can they go lower? History has shown that an improving economy following a bear market can overcome real concerns and we are bullish on the economy and the stock market.

          Long term investing succeeds because long term fundamentals have more staying power than short term concerns. We think that proven principles of successful investing are going to be proven again to be successful.
